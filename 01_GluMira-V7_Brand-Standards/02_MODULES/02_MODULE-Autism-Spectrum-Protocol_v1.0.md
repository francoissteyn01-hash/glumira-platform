Part 1 — The Intersection: Why ASD + T1D Is Different
Type 1 Diabetes management demands over 8,000 hours of self-management per year outside clinical settings. For autistic individuals, every one of those hours is filtered through a fundamentally different neurological experience: different sensory processing, different executive functioning, different interoceptive awareness, and different social-communication patterns.
Research published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders (2024) confirms that healthcare providers routinely treat autism and diabetes as separate, unrelated conditions. This separation means autistic-specific challenges — such as injection site sensory aversion, food texture refusal during hypo treatment, or inability to communicate glucose symptoms during shutdown — go unaddressed.
A PMC study (2020) on families raising children with comorbid T1D and ASD found that caregivers face unique challenges not understood by families with only one condition or by their healthcare providers. Themes consistently emerged around coping techniques, technology sensory issues, and the inadequacy of healthcare experiences.
1.1 Key Statistics
Autism prevalence: approximately 1 in 36 children (CDC, 2023)
T1D paediatric population: approximately 540,000 children worldwide under 15
Estimated ASD + T1D comorbidity: 2.5-4% of T1D population
9 out of 15 studies found higher diabetes prevalence in autistic populations
82% of caregivers in interview studies reported autism significantly complicates T1D management
74% of autistic adults report significant interoceptive confusion unless bodily signals are extreme
Insulin pump use is lower in ASD+T1D children vs T1D-only children due to sensory barriers
83% of ASD+T1D pump users prefer tubeless pumps

Part 2 — Interoception: The Hidden Danger
Interoception — the eighth sensory system — is the body's ability to sense internal states: hunger, thirst, pain, temperature, heart rate, bladder fullness, and critically for T1D, the symptoms of hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia.
Research from PMC (2024) demonstrates that autistic individuals may have altered interoceptive processing: heightened awareness of internal signals but significant difficulty interpreting and integrating them. A person may feel 'something wrong' but be unable to identify it as low blood sugar.
2.1 Interoception and Hypo Awareness
⚠ CRITICAL SAFETY CONCERN: Autistic individuals may not recognise hypoglycaemia symptoms the way neurotypical individuals do.
Breakthrough T1D UK reports that interoceptive differences in autistic children with T1D directly affect how they recognise both high and low blood glucose. If the child also has alexithymia (difficulty identifying emotions and body states — present in approximately 50% of autistic individuals), they may be unable to both recognise AND communicate how they feel.

✓ PRACTICAL TIP: For autistic children with T1D, never rely on symptom reporting alone. CGM with remote monitoring is strongly recommended. If CGM is not available, schedule glucose checks at fixed intervals — do not wait for the child to 'feel low'.
2.2 Interoception Training for T1D
Occupational therapists specialising in interoception (e.g., Kelly Mahler's Interoception Curriculum) can help autistic individuals learn to recognise internal body signals. For T1D specifically:
Use a body check chart before each glucose check: 'How does your tummy feel? How do your hands feel? Is your head buzzy?' — then compare with the actual glucose reading
Build a personal 'hypo dictionary': over time, identify what THIS person's hypo feels like — it may not match textbook symptoms
Create a visual scale: 1-5 body feelings chart with faces/colours, paired with glucose ranges
Practice during stable glucose: 'Your glucose is 6.5 right now. How does your body feel at 6.5?' — building the mind-body connection


Part 3 — Age-Banded Protocol
3.1 Ages 0-5: The Smallest Patients
Injection Protocol
Establish a predictable injection routine: same order of steps, same location, same time cues
Use numbing cream (EMLA/Ametop) for every injection if sensory sensitivity is high — this is not 'giving in', it is a medical accommodation
Offer a sensory tool during injection: preferred texture toy, chewy, or weighted lap pad
Never restrain or hold down — this creates injection trauma that compounds over years
If child refuses: pause. Attempt again in 5 minutes with a modified sensory approach. If continued refusal for bolus: contact care team for guidance on delayed dosing
Log which sites are tolerated — build a body map over time
Feeding Protocol
Accept that food aversions are neurological, not behavioural. Do not force 'healthy' foods
Work WITH preferred foods: calculate carbs for the foods they actually eat
Have 3 confirmed hypo treatments that the child will accept — test BEFORE an emergency
For tube-fed or severely food-restricted children: work with a paediatric dietitian experienced in both ASD and T1D
If the child eats the same foods daily: this is a T1D STRENGTH — carb counting becomes highly predictable
Communication
Use visual schedules for the daily T1D routine — picture cards showing each step
Create a 'feeling board' with faces/colours that the child can point to
Teach one key phrase: 'I feel funny' = check glucose NOW
3.2 Ages 5-10: School Years
School Protocol
Provide school staff with ASD+T1D one-page guide (see Part 9)
Insist on a named diabetes contact who also understands ASD needs
Establish a quiet, designated space for glucose checks and injections — not in the classroom, not in public
Request that glucose checks are built into the class schedule (predictable = less anxiety)
Pack hypo treatments that the child will actually eat — inform school of specific brands/types
If the child masks at school: they may appear 'fine' while hypoglycaemic. School staff MUST check on schedule, not on reported symptoms
Independence Building
Introduce self-monitoring with CGM: the child watches their own numbers on a screen (visual, predictable, pattern-oriented — plays to autistic strengths)
Teach carb counting as a maths exercise — many autistic children excel at this if it is structured and rule-based
Create a 'diabetes kit bag' that the child packs themselves — same items, same order, every day
3.3 Ages 10-14: Pre-Teen Transition
Puberty Impact
Puberty hormones cause insulin resistance → doses increase → more injections or larger doses → sensory load increases
Prepare the child BEFORE puberty hits: 'Your body is going to change how it uses insulin. We will adjust together.'
Growth spurts can cause sudden glucose instability — explain the science (autistic children often respond well to logical explanations)
Menstrual cycle onset adds another variable — see Menstrual Cycle Module for hormonal insulin sensitivity mapping
Self-Injection Readiness
Do NOT pressure self-injection based on age. Readiness is individual.
Signs of readiness: child asks to try, watches the process with interest, handles the pen/syringe without distress
Teach with the child's preferred learning style: some need to watch 20 times, some need a written procedure, some need to inject an orange first
Celebrate the first self-injection with dignity, not fanfare — many autistic children dislike unexpected attention
3.4 Ages 14-18: Teen Independence
Autonomy Transfer
Transfer management tasks ONE AT A TIME, not all at once
Create a written checklist that the teen follows independently — not verbal instructions
Use phone reminders/alarms for dose times — external cues compensate for executive function challenges
Expect regression during stressful periods (exams, social conflict, autistic burnout) — temporarily resume parental support without judgment
Social Challenges
Provide scripts for disclosing T1D to friends: 'I have diabetes. Sometimes I need to check my blood and eat something. It is not a big deal.'
Address alcohol with honesty: alcohol lowers blood sugar, combined with executive function challenges this is high risk
Driving: check glucose before driving, always carry fast-acting glucose, set CGM alarm for 5.0 mmol/L while driving
3.5 Ages 18+: Autistic Adults
Burnout Protocol
Autistic burnout is a documented phenomenon where the psychosocial demands of navigating neurotypical systems exhaust internal resources. During burnout, diabetes self-management often drops significantly.
The platform detects burnout through logging pattern changes: if regular logging drops for 3+ days, Mira checks in gently
Burnout mode: simplify ALL platform interactions to absolute essentials — 'Did you take your insulin? Yes/No'
Do not lecture about missed doses during burnout. Acknowledge: 'This is hard. Let us keep you safe with the minimum.'
Healthcare Navigation
Pre-appointment preparation: generate a question list in GluMira™, print IOB Hunter™ report, list any changes since last visit
Sensory preparation: call ahead to ask about waiting room environment, bring noise-cancelling headphones, request first appointment of the day (least crowded)
If you mask with your endocrinologist: consider showing them this protocol. They cannot help with what they cannot see.

4.2 Adhesive Sensitivity Protocol
Test adhesives BEFORE medical need: apply a small patch to forearm for 24h
Barrier films (Skin-Tac, Cavilon) can reduce skin reaction
Hypoallergenic tape alternatives exist — trial multiple brands
If all adhesives are refused: MDI (multiple daily injections) is a valid choice. Pump refusal is not non-compliance.
4.3 CGM Alarm Management
CGM alarms can trigger sound sensitivity — adjust volume, use vibration only, or route to caregiver phone
Visual-only alerts on smartwatch may be better tolerated than audible alarms
Set alarm thresholds wider to reduce frequency, tighten gradually as tolerance builds
Night alarms: route ALL to caregiver device, silent on child's device


Part 5 — Executive Function Support Protocol
Executive function encompasses planning, organisation, working memory, flexible thinking, and self-monitoring — all essential for T1D management, all potentially affected by autism.
5.1 External Structure Systems
Visual schedule: daily T1D tasks in picture/icon sequence (see VisualSchedule.tsx)
Timer-based prompts: phone alarm at dose times, glucose check times, meal times
Checklist systems: laminated daily card, tick off each task, reset each morning
Colour-coded insulin storage: different colour caps/labels for different insulins
'If-then' cards: 'IF glucose < 4 → THEN eat 15g glucose → WAIT 15 min → RECHECK'
5.2 Decision Simplification
Reduce the number of decisions per event: pre-calculate doses for standard meals
Create a 'meal-dose card' for the 5 most common meals with pre-set insulin doses (approved by care team)
Use the IOB Hunter™ what-if tool to pre-plan: see the expected curve before dosing
For correction doses: provide a simple lookup table, not a formula to calculate in the moment
5.3 Working Memory Compensation
Insulin pen memory cap: tracks last dose time and amount
GluMira™ simplified logger: one-tap 'Dosed' with auto-timestamp
'Did I dose?' anxiety: CGM + dose logging creates a checkable record — no need to rely on memory
Keep insulin and testing supplies in ONE location — never split across rooms/bags


Part 6 — Meltdown & Shutdown Emergency Protocol
⚠ DURING A MELTDOWN OR SHUTDOWN, THE PERSON CANNOT ENGAGE WITH T1D MANAGEMENT
6.1 Priority Order
1. SAFETY FIRST — protect the person from physical harm during meltdown
2. DO NOT attempt finger pricks, injections, or force-feeding during active meltdown
3. CHECK glucose remotely (CGM app on caregiver phone) if possible
4. IF glucose dropping + person cannot eat: prepare glucose gel. Apply to inside of cheek when safe to approach
5. IF person is unconscious or seizing: GLUCAGON + CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES IMMEDIATELY
6. AFTER meltdown resolves: check glucose, offer preferred hypo treatment in calm environment
7. LOG the event: time, trigger, glucose, treatment, recovery time
6.2 Glucose-Triggered Meltdowns
Breakthrough T1D UK confirms that high and low glucose levels can directly trigger or worsen meltdowns by adding to sensory overload. This creates a dangerous cycle:
Low glucose → irritability/confusion → sensory threshold drops → meltdown → cannot treat hypo → glucose drops further
High glucose → nausea/headache → sensory overload → meltdown → cannot check or dose → glucose stays high
✓ PRACTICAL TIP: If meltdowns cluster at specific times of day, check if those times coincide with insulin peaks or glucose drops. IOB Hunter™ can reveal hidden patterns.
6.3 Prevention Strategies
Maintain glucose stability as much as possible — fewer spikes and drops = fewer sensory triggers
Identify the child's 'pre-meltdown signs' — many autistic children have identifiable precursors (stimming changes, withdrawal, covering ears)
When pre-meltdown signs appear AND glucose is unknown: check first, intervene for glucose, THEN address sensory environment
Keep glucose gel in every room of the house and in every bag — it must be instantly accessible


Part 7 — Food & Nutrition Protocol
7.1 Working With Food Aversions
Food aversions in autism are neurological responses, not preferences. They cannot be overridden by willpower.
NEVER withhold preferred foods as punishment or force non-preferred foods — this creates food trauma
Document all accepted foods with exact carb counts. This becomes the child's personal formulary.
If accepted foods change (they can, suddenly and without warning): update the carb database immediately
7.2 ARFID and T1D
Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) is common in autism and profoundly affects T1D management. If a child eats fewer than 10 foods consistently, consider ARFID screening.
A child with ARFID + T1D may eat only white foods, only crunchy foods, or only room-temperature foods
Work with a specialist dietitian experienced in BOTH conditions — standard diabetes nutrition advice will fail
Insulin doses must be calculated for what the child ACTUALLY eats, not what they 'should' eat
7.3 Hypo Treatment Aversion
⚠ CRITICAL: If a child refuses all standard hypo treatments due to sensory aversion, this is a medical emergency plan item.
Test at least 5 different glucose sources during STABLE glucose — not during a crisis
Find the ONE treatment they will accept and STOCK IT. Buy in bulk. Never run out.
If no oral treatment is accepted: discuss glucagon mini-dosing with your care team
Keep glucose gel as backup — it can be applied to the inside of the cheek without chewing or swallowing


Part 8 — Technology Adaptation Protocol
8.1 CGM Adaptation
Introduce the sensor concept with pictures and social stories BEFORE the first insertion
Let the child hold the sensor, feel the adhesive, examine the inserter
First insertion: quiet room, preferred sensory tools available, no audience
Placement: let the child choose from approved sites — giving choice reduces resistance
If adhesive causes distress: try different body locations, barrier films, or tegaderm alternatives
For sound-sensitive children: mute all audible alarms, use vibration or visual alerts only
8.2 Pump Adaptation
Tubeless pumps (Omnipod) are preferred by 83% of ASD+T1D families who use pumps
Allow an extended trial period with a non-active pod attached — wear it for days before filling it
If the pump is refused: MDI is equally effective. The child is not 'failing' — the technology does not fit their sensory needs
Automated insulin delivery (AID/closed loop) can significantly reduce the management burden during meltdowns and burnout — fewer decisions required
8.3 GluMira™ Platform Adaptations
Sensory mode selector: Standard / Low Stimulation / Minimal
Simplified logger: one-tap actions with progressive disclosure
Visual schedule: picture-supported daily routine
Mira autism-aware mode: literal language, one idea per message, no idioms
Burnout detection: gentle check-in after 3+ days of reduced logging

Part 10 — Caregiver Wellbeing Protocol
Caregivers of children with ASD+T1D carry a dual-condition burden that is qualitatively different from either condition alone. Research shows they experience higher rates of burnout, anxiety, and isolation than caregivers of children with T1D alone.
10.1 Recognising Caregiver Burnout
You stop checking the CGM because you 'just can't look at another number'
You feel rage instead of concern when the glucose alarm sounds
You withdraw from the ASD+T1D support community because 'nobody understands anyway'
You notice your own health declining — missed meals, poor sleep, no exercise
You feel guilty for resenting the demands of two conditions that your child did not choose
All of these are normal responses to an abnormal burden. You are not failing.
10.2 Practical Caregiver Support
Divide the T1D and ASD management roles between caregivers if possible — no one person should carry everything
GluMira™ caregiver multi-access: up to 4 caregivers per child, editor/viewer roles — share the monitoring
Accept 'good enough' management during crisis periods. 80% adherence while surviving burnout is better than 100% adherence followed by total collapse
Connect with other ASD+T1D families — the Children with Diabetes 'Type Au-Some' community, Breakthrough T1D UK forums
Request respite care specifically trained in both conditions — this may require training someone yourself


Part 11 — Clinical Guidance for Healthcare Providers
11.1 What Clinicians Miss
Autistic patients who mask in clinical settings appear 'fine' — you may be treating a performance, not a reality
Injection refusal is not non-compliance — it is a sensory barrier. Respond with accommodation, not judgment.
Food 'pickiness' that affects T1D nutrition is likely ARFID — a clinical condition, not a parenting failure
If glucose patterns worsen suddenly: ask about autistic burnout, sensory environment changes, or new stressors before adjusting insulin
Glucose fluctuations can directly trigger meltdowns — consider glucose stability as a sensory management tool
11.2 Communication Adaptations
Use clear, literal, concrete language. Avoid idioms, metaphors, and vague instructions.
Instead of 'Keep an eye on your glucose': say 'Check your glucose at 10am, 1pm, 4pm, and 8pm'
Instead of 'Eat something if you feel low': say 'If your glucose is below 4.0, eat 15g of [specific treatment]'
Provide written instructions — verbal instructions may not be retained under the cognitive load of a medical appointment
Ask about sensory needs at the start of every appointment: 'Is there anything about this room that is uncomfortable for you?'
11.3 Using GluMira™ IOB Hunter™ in ASD+T1D Consultations
The IOB curve can explain to autistic patients WHY dose timing matters — visual, logical, evidence-based
The 60-second interpretation panel provides pattern context without requiring the patient to explain
The injection site tracker shows which sites are tolerated — use this data for site rotation planning
Meltdown logs with glucose correlation can reveal patterns invisible to standard clinic data


Part 12 — Practical Tips Quick-Reference Cards
Print these cards. Put them on the fridge. Keep them in the diabetes kit bag.
CARD 1: Daily Routine
✓ Same order, same time, same place — every day. Predictability is safety.
✓ Wake → Check → Breakfast → Dose → [Day activities] → Lunch check → Snack → Dinner → Dose → Bedtime check
✓ If the routine must change: warn in advance, explain why, show new routine visually
CARD 2: Hypo Response
✓ Check glucose. If below 4.0 (72 mg/dL):
✓ Give 15g of THEIR preferred treatment (not what is convenient for you)
✓ Wait 15 minutes. Recheck. If still low, repeat.
✓ If they refuse to eat: glucose gel to inside of cheek.
✓ If unconscious: GLUCAGON. Call emergency services.
CARD 3: Meltdown + Low Glucose
✓ STOP all diabetes tasks. Ensure physical safety.
✓ Check glucose REMOTELY (CGM app).
✓ If glucose dropping: prepare glucose gel. Wait for safe moment.
✓ After meltdown: check glucose. Offer preferred treatment. Log event.
CARD 4: School Morning Checklist
✓ ☐ Glucose checked and in range
✓ ☐ Insulin dosed for breakfast
✓ ☐ Diabetes kit bag packed (meter, strips, hypo treatment, glucagon)
✓ ☐ Hypo treatment is the specific brand/type they will eat
✓ ☐ School contact knows today's schedule
CARD 5: Strengths to Leverage
✓ Routine preference → consistent meal timing, reliable dose schedule
✓ Detail orientation → accurate carb counting, pattern spotting
✓ Rule following → clear protocols are followed precisely
✓ Visual learning → CGM graphs, IOB curves, visual schedules
✓ Honesty → will tell you exactly how they feel (if they can identify it)
