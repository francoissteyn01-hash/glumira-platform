# "I Forgot My Insulin" — Damage Control and Prevention

**Topic ID:** 49
**Age Range:** 13–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Self-Management & Problem Solving
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17

---

## It Happens. Now What?

Let us get this out of the way first: forgetting your insulin does not make you irresponsible, careless, or bad at managing your diabetes. It makes you human. It happens to adults who have had T1D for decades. It happens to endocrinologists with T1D. It happens.

But here is where honesty matters: what you do next — in the hours after you realise — genuinely matters. A missed dose handled promptly and calmly is usually manageable. A missed dose ignored or hidden can spiral into a medical emergency. This topic covers both parts: what to do right now, and how to make it less likely to happen again.

## Right Now — The First Hour

You have realised you do not have your insulin. Maybe you left your pen at home. Maybe your pump ran out and you do not have a spare cartridge. Maybe you are at a friend's house, at school, at a party, and your insulin is somewhere else.

**Step one: Do not panic, but do not ignore it.**

Missing a single rapid-acting (mealtime) dose will cause your blood glucose to rise. How high depends on what you ate, how active you are, and how long it has been. Missing a single dose of long-acting (basal) insulin has a more gradual but broader effect — your background glucose regulation will be impaired for hours.

**Step two: Check your blood glucose.** Whether you have a meter or a CGM, check now and keep checking frequently (every 30–60 minutes).

**Step three: Can you get your insulin within a reasonable timeframe?**

- If someone can bring it to you within an hour or two — call them. Ask. This is not an overreaction. A parent, a sibling, a friend — whoever can get it to you fastest.
- If you can get home or to where your insulin is within a couple of hours — go. Rearrange your plans if needed.
- If you are stuck somewhere without insulin for an extended period (a school trip, a sleepover, a festival), you need to escalate. Call your parents. Call your diabetes team. If you cannot reach them, you may need to visit a pharmacy or, in a worst case, an urgent care centre or A&E.

**Step four: While you wait, reduce the demand on what insulin you do have.**

- Do not eat carbohydrates. If you must eat, choose foods that are very low in carbs — cheese, nuts, plain meat, eggs. Every gram of carbohydrate you eat without insulin coverage will push your BG higher.
- Stay hydrated. Drink water. High blood glucose causes dehydration, and dehydration makes everything worse.
- Light physical activity (walking, not intense exercise) can help lower BG slightly by increasing glucose uptake into muscles. But do not exercise intensely without insulin — this can paradoxically raise BG further and accelerate ketone production.

**Step five: Check for ketones.**

This is important. If your blood glucose is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) and you have missed insulin — particularly basal insulin — check for ketones. Use blood ketone strips if you have them (more accurate) or urine strips.

Ketones above 0.6 mmol/L on a blood meter, or moderate/large on a urine strip, indicate that your body is starting to break down fat for energy because it cannot use glucose. This is the road to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), which is a medical emergency.

If your ketones are elevated:
- You need insulin. This is no longer a "wait and see" situation.
- Drink plenty of water.
- If ketones are above 1.5 mmol/L or you are feeling unwell (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, heavy breathing, confusion), go to A&E / the emergency department immediately.

**DKA can develop within hours in someone without basal insulin.** This is not theoretical. It is the mechanism by which missed insulin can become life-threatening.

## Pump-Specific Situations

If you are on an insulin pump and it fails, runs out, or gets disconnected for an extended period, the situation escalates faster than with injections. This is because pumps deliver only rapid-acting insulin — there is no long-acting insulin in your system as a buffer. When the pump stops, all insulin delivery stops.

If your pump fails and you do not have a backup:
- You have a window of roughly 1–2 hours before BG starts rising significantly.
- After 3–4 hours without any insulin, ketone production can begin.
- This is an urgent situation. Contact your diabetes team, get to a pharmacy for an insulin pen if possible, or go to urgent care/A&E.

This is precisely why carrying a backup insulin pen and needles — even if you are on a pump — is strongly recommended by every major diabetes guideline. More on that below.

## Once You Have Your Insulin Back

When you get your insulin:
- Check your BG.
- Take your insulin as discussed with your diabetes team for correction in these situations. If you are unsure what to do, call them.
- Continue checking your BG every 1–2 hours until it stabilises.
- Drink plenty of water.
- If you had elevated ketones, continue checking ketones until they clear.

Do not try to "make up for it" by taking extra insulin beyond what you know is appropriate. Over-correcting can cause a severe hypo, which is a different emergency. Follow your care team's guidance for correction.

## Prevention — Making It Less Likely to Happen Again

Forgetting is human. But there are systems that catch human error. The goal is not to never forget — it is to have backup systems that mean forgetting does not become a crisis.

**Phone alarms and reminders.** Set daily alarms for your basal insulin. Not a single alarm — set two, five minutes apart. Make the alarm tone something distinctive and slightly annoying. It should be impossible to dismiss and forget.

**A backup pen.** If you use a pump, carry a rapid-acting insulin pen and needles as a backup. Always. Not in your pump bag that you might leave behind — on your person or in a separate bag. If you use pens as your primary delivery, consider keeping a spare pen at school, at work, or at a friend's house where you spend a lot of time.

**Spare supplies at school.** Many teens keep a small diabetes kit at school — a spare pen, glucose, test strips, ketone strips. Speak to the school nurse or your form tutor about storing supplies in a safe, accessible location.

**The "keys, wallet, insulin" check.** Before you leave the house, build a mental checklist that includes your insulin. Some people physically keep their insulin pen with their keys or wallet so they cannot grab one without seeing the other.

**Visible storage at home.** If your insulin lives in a drawer you never open or the back of the fridge, you are more likely to forget it. Keep it somewhere visible in your routine path — next to your toothbrush, by the front door, wherever you will see it at the right time.

**Travel and event planning.** Before any trip — school trip, holiday, festival, sleepover — pack your diabetes supplies first, before anything else. Make a checklist if needed. And pack more than you think you will need. Getting stuck somewhere with insufficient insulin is a solvable problem — if you have a backup.

**Tell someone.** If you realise you have forgotten your insulin, tell someone immediately. A parent, a friend, a teacher. Do not try to quietly manage it alone, especially if you might not be able to get insulin for several hours. Pride is not worth a DKA admission.

## The Shame Factor — Let Us Address It Directly

Many teens who forget their insulin feel embarrassed or ashamed, especially if they have to tell a parent or their diabetes team. This shame can lead to hiding the mistake, which delays treatment, which makes the situation worse.

Here is the truth: your diabetes team has seen thousands of missed doses. They are not going to judge you. Your parents might worry, but worry is not the same as blame. And your friends, if they are decent friends, will help you sort it out.

The most dangerous thing about forgetting your insulin is not the forgetting itself — it is the silence that sometimes follows it.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Forgetting insulin happens to everyone** — what matters is acting quickly. Check your BG, avoid carbohydrates, stay hydrated, and get your insulin as soon as possible.
2. **Check for ketones if your BG is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL)** — elevated ketones mean your body is heading toward DKA, which requires urgent insulin and possibly emergency care.
3. **Pump users are at higher risk** because there is no background long-acting insulin. Carry a backup pen at all times, as recommended by all major guidelines.
4. **Build systems to prevent forgetting:** phone alarms for basal insulin, a "keys-wallet-insulin" exit check, spare supplies at school, and a backup pen in a separate bag.
5. **Tell someone immediately** if you have missed insulin and cannot access it. Do not hide it — silence turns a manageable situation into a dangerous one.
