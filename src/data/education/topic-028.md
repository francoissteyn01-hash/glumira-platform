# The School Nurse Relationship — What to Insist On

**Topic ID:** 028
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. Of all the relationships you build around your child's diabetes management, the one with the school nurse — or whoever fills that role at your child's school — may be the most important during school hours. A strong, collaborative relationship with school health staff can make the difference between a child who thrives at school and one who is constantly pulled out of class, sent to the office, or made to feel different. Let's talk about how to build that relationship, what you have the right to expect, and what the law says in different regions.

## Not Every School Has a Nurse

Let's start with reality. In many schools — particularly in South Africa and parts of the UK — there is no dedicated school nurse on site every day. The "school nurse" role might be filled by:

- A school health service nurse who visits weekly or termly (common in the UK and SA)
- A school secretary or administrator who has been designated as the health contact
- A class teacher or teaching assistant who has volunteered or been assigned
- In some US schools, a full-time registered nurse or licensed practical nurse

Whoever fills this role, the principles are the same. They need training, they need a clear care plan, and they need your support.

## The Care Plan Meeting

The foundation of the school nurse relationship is the care plan meeting. This should happen before the school year starts — or as soon as possible after your child's diagnosis — and should be reviewed at least annually, or whenever your child's management changes significantly.

### Who Should Be at the Meeting

- You (and your partner, if possible)
- The school nurse or designated health staff member
- Your child's class teacher
- The head teacher or a senior leader with decision-making authority
- Your child's diabetes specialist nurse (DSN) or diabetes educator, if they are able to attend (even by phone)
- Your child, if they are old enough to contribute (typically age 8+)

### What the Meeting Should Cover

1. **Your child's current management plan:** What type of insulin regimen (MDI or pump), what monitoring system (CGM or meter), what the target ranges are, and what the hypo and hyperglycaemia protocols are.

2. **Who does what:** Clearly define responsibilities. Who checks blood glucose? Who supervises or administers insulin? Who has access to the diabetes kit? Who calls you if there is a problem? For children aged 5–7, an adult will need to handle most tasks. For children aged 8–10, they may manage some tasks independently with adult supervision.

3. **Emergency procedures:** What to do for a severe hypo (glucagon), what to do if the child is unwell with high glucose and ketones, and when to call an ambulance.

4. **Daily routines:** When does your child check glucose? Before lunch? Before PE? Is there a mid-morning snack? Can your child eat in the classroom if blood glucose is low? Can they check their CGM during lessons without asking permission?

5. **Communication:** How will you and the school stay in touch? A daily logbook? A communication app? Texts? Agree on the method and frequency.

6. **Training:** What training do staff need, and who will provide it? (See below.)

### Insist on a Written Plan

The care plan should be a written, signed document. Not a verbal agreement, not a casual chat in the corridor. A written plan protects your child, protects the school, and provides clarity for everyone — including supply teachers, after-school staff, and anyone who is covering for the usual staff.

## What You Have the Right to Expect

### In the United Kingdom

Under the Children and Families Act 2014 (England) and equivalent legislation in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, schools have a legal duty to support pupils with medical conditions. The statutory guidance "Supporting Pupils at School with Medical Conditions" (Department for Education, 2014, updated 2017) requires schools to:

- Ensure that children with medical conditions are properly supported so they can have full access to education
- Develop and implement an Individual Healthcare Plan (IHP) for any pupil with a significant medical condition
- Ensure that school staff are adequately trained
- Not require parents to attend school to administer medication or provide care

NICE guideline NG18 (Diabetes in children and young people, 2015, updated 2022) specifically recommends that schools should have at least one trained member of staff who can support the child with diabetes management during the school day.

### In the United States

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) protect children with diabetes from discrimination in schools. Under Section 504:

- Schools must develop a Section 504 plan or an Individualized Health Plan (IHP) for students with diabetes
- Schools must ensure that trained staff are available to provide diabetes care during school hours, on field trips, and at school-sponsored events
- Children cannot be excluded from any school activity because of their diabetes
- The school must accommodate the child's need to check glucose, eat snacks, access water, and use the bathroom as needed

The ADA's position statement on diabetes management in the school setting (and the complementary Safe at School campaign) provides a detailed framework for school-based care.

### In South Africa

South African law does not have legislation as specific as the UK or US frameworks, but the South African Schools Act (SASA) 84 of 1996 and the Constitution's Bill of Rights (Section 29 — right to education) provide a foundation. The National Department of Health's Integrated School Health Policy (ISHP) provides for school health services, though implementation varies. Parents may need to be more proactive in negotiating care plans and training, and involving the child's diabetes team in school communications can be very helpful.

## Training — What to Insist On

Training is not optional. An untrained staff member cannot be expected to manage a diabetes emergency safely. At a minimum, the following people should receive basic diabetes training:

- The designated health staff member (school nurse or equivalent)
- Your child's class teacher
- Any teaching assistant or support worker who is regularly with your child
- PE teachers and coaches
- After-school club staff, if your child attends

### What Basic Training Should Cover

1. What Type 1 diabetes is (and what it is not — it is not caused by eating sugar)
2. How to recognise signs of low blood glucose (hypoglycaemia)
3. How to treat a hypo: fast-acting glucose first, follow-up snack, when to use glucagon, when to call an ambulance
4. How to recognise signs of high blood glucose (hyperglycaemia) and when to call a parent
5. How to support the child with blood glucose checking and meal-time routines
6. Where the diabetes kit is stored and what is in it

### Who Provides the Training

In the UK, your child's Diabetes Specialist Nurse (DSN) can often visit the school or provide virtual training. In the US, the school nurse or your child's diabetes educator can lead training. In South Africa, your child's diabetes team or a local diabetes association (such as Diabetes South Africa) may be able to assist.

If professional training is not available, you can provide the training yourself using resources from reputable organisations: JDRF, Diabetes UK, ADA, or ISPAD. Some organisations provide free online training modules specifically designed for school staff.

## Ongoing Communication

The care plan meeting is the start, not the end. Ongoing communication keeps the relationship strong:

- **Start of each term:** A quick email or meeting to review any changes in your child's management, new supplies needed, or staff changes.
- **After any significant event:** If your child had a severe hypo at school, schedule a debrief. Discuss what happened, what went well, and what could be improved. Keep the tone collaborative, not blaming.
- **When things change:** New insulin, new pump, new CGM sensor, new target ranges — update the school promptly and in writing.
- **Say thank you.** School staff who support your child's diabetes are doing work that goes beyond their job description. A genuine thank-you note at the end of term goes a long way toward maintaining goodwill.

## When Things Go Wrong

If the school is not meeting its obligations — if staff refuse to administer glucagon, if your child is excluded from trips, if no one will check blood glucose — escalate firmly but constructively:

1. Put your concern in writing to the head teacher.
2. Reference the relevant legislation (Section 504, Children and Families Act, or SASA).
3. Involve your child's diabetes team — a letter from a consultant paediatric diabetologist carries weight.
4. Contact the relevant advocacy organisation: JDRF, Diabetes UK, or ADA's Safe at School programme.
5. As a last resort, file a formal complaint with the school's governing body or your local education authority.

Most issues are resolved at step 1 or 2. Schools generally want to do the right thing — they just need clear guidance on what the right thing is.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Request a formal care plan meeting** before the school year starts, with your child's teacher, the school nurse or health designate, and a senior leader present.
2. **Insist on a written, signed Individual Healthcare Plan** — verbal agreements are not sufficient to ensure consistent, safe care.
3. **Know your legal rights:** UK (Children and Families Act 2014), US (Section 504, ADA), and SA (SASA, Constitutional right to education) all protect your child's right to support at school.
4. **Ensure that all staff who supervise your child receive basic diabetes training**, including hypo recognition, treatment, and glucagon administration.
5. **Maintain ongoing, collaborative communication** with school health staff — regular updates, post-event debriefs, and genuine appreciation keep the relationship strong.
