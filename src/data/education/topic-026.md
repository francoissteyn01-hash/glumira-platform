# After-School Snack Protocols — Bridging the Gap to Dinner

**Topic ID:** 026
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. The hours between school pick-up and dinner are a surprisingly tricky window for blood glucose management. Your child arrives home hungry, often after an active afternoon, with dinner still an hour or two away. The after-school snack is not just about hunger — it is a genuine management tool that bridges the gap between lunch and the evening meal, and getting it right can smooth out those late-afternoon glucose dips or spikes that so many families struggle with.

## Why the After-School Window Matters

By the time school ends, several hours have passed since lunch. If your child had PE or was active at break time, their muscles may have used up a significant amount of glucose. Insulin from lunch may still be active (this is called "insulin on board"), particularly if lunch was later in the day. And if dinner is not until 6 or 7 p.m., that is a long stretch with nothing to stabilise blood glucose.

ISPAD guidelines recognise planned snacking as a legitimate part of paediatric diabetes management, particularly for children on fixed insulin regimens. Even for children on flexible regimens (basal-bolus or pump therapy), a well-chosen after-school snack can prevent the late-afternoon low that leads to overtreating, which then leads to a high before dinner, which then leads to a frustrating evening.

Breaking that cycle starts with a plan.

## Setting Carb Targets for the After-School Snack

The right amount of carbohydrate depends on several factors:

- **Time since lunch.** If your child ate lunch at noon and gets home at 3:30 p.m., they have had 3.5 hours of digestion and activity. A moderate carb snack (15–25 g) is often appropriate.
- **Activity level.** A child who had PE or played sport will need more carbohydrate to replenish glycogen stores than a child who had a quiet afternoon of art and reading.
- **Current blood glucose.** Check glucose (or look at the CGM) when your child arrives home. This reading is your decision point.
- **Time until dinner.** If dinner is in 45 minutes, a small snack (10–15 g) may suffice. If dinner is 2 hours away, a more substantial snack (20–30 g) with some protein and fat may be needed.

Here is a simple framework:

| Scenario | Suggested Carb Range | Example Snack |
|---|---|---|
| Glucose in range, dinner in 1–2 hours, no PE today | 15–20 g | Apple slices with a tablespoon of peanut butter |
| Glucose in range, dinner in 1–2 hours, had PE today | 20–30 g | Wholemeal toast with cheese |
| Glucose below target, dinner in 1+ hours | 15 g fast-acting (treat the low first), then 15–20 g sustained snack | Juice box first, then crackers with hummus |
| Glucose above target, dinner in 1 hour | 0–10 g, or skip the carb snack | Cheese cubes, cucumber sticks, a boiled egg |
| Glucose above target, dinner in 2+ hours | Small snack (10–15 g) — glucose may come down on its own | Half a banana with a few nuts |

These are starting points. Your child's diabetes team will help you refine the numbers based on your child's individual insulin regimen and glucose patterns.

## Activity-Adjusted Snacks

One of the most useful habits you can build is adjusting the after-school snack based on how active your child was. This does not need to be complicated:

- **Low-activity day (classroom-based, minimal break-time play):** Stick to your standard snack size and composition. This is the baseline.
- **Moderate-activity day (regular break-time play, walked home):** Add 5–10 g of carbohydrate to the standard snack, or choose a snack that includes slightly more carbohydrate naturally.
- **High-activity day (PE, sports practice, sports day):** Increase the snack by 10–20 g of carbohydrate and include protein and fat to sustain blood glucose through the extended post-exercise period. Remember the delayed hypo risk from Topic 23 — after intense exercise, blood glucose can continue to drop for several hours.

A simple communication tool: ask your child after school, "Did you do PE today?" or "Were you running around at break?" Their answer — even a vague "yes" or "a bit" — gives you enough information to adjust.

## Snack Ideas That Work

The best after-school snacks combine carbohydrate for energy with protein or fat for staying power. Here are some family-tested options:

### 15–20 g Carb Snacks (Standard)

- 1 medium apple (~15 g) with a tablespoon of peanut butter (~2 g)
- 4–5 whole wheat crackers (~16 g) with cheese
- 1 small banana (~20 g)
- 150 ml plain yoghurt (~10 g) with a handful of berries (~5 g)
- 1 slice of toast (~15 g) with butter and Marmite

### 20–30 g Carb Snacks (Active Day)

- Wholemeal sandwich with ham and cheese (~28 g)
- 1 cup of milk (~12 g) and a small muesli bar (~15 g)
- Hummus with pitta bread (~25 g for half a pitta)
- Small bowl of cereal with milk (~25 g)
- Crackers (~16 g) with peanut butter and a small fruit (~12 g)

### Low-Carb Snacks (High Glucose)

- Cheese cubes or string cheese (~1 g)
- Boiled egg (0 g)
- Cucumber and carrot sticks with cream cheese (~3 g)
- A small handful of nuts (~3–5 g)
- Biltong or jerky (~1–2 g)

## Building Consistency Without Rigidity

Children thrive on routine, and a consistent after-school snack habit helps both glucose management and family sanity. But consistency does not mean rigidity. You are not failing if Tuesday's snack is different from Monday's. The goal is a reliable pattern — "we come home, we check glucose, we have a snack" — with flexibility in what that snack looks like based on the day's numbers and activity.

Some families find it helpful to prepare a "snack station" — a shelf in the fridge and a basket in the cupboard with pre-portioned, pre-counted options that the child (or an after-school carer) can choose from. Label each option with the carb count. This is especially useful if your child goes to after-school care or is looked after by a grandparent who is still learning carb counting.

## The After-School Carer or Grandparent

If someone other than you handles the after-school period, give them the same clarity you would give a school nurse:

- A simple written guide: "Check glucose. If below X, give juice first. Then offer one of these snacks [list]. If above Y, offer one of these low-carb options [list]. Text me the glucose number."
- Pre-portioned snacks in labelled bags
- Your phone number for any questions

Keep it friendly and supportive. After-school carers who feel confident are more likely to get it right consistently.

## Avoiding the "Graze Until Dinner" Trap

One common challenge is the child who nibbles continuously from school pick-up until dinner. Continuous grazing makes it very difficult to count carbs accurately and can lead to stacking insulin corrections on top of each other.

The fix: one defined snack, at a defined time, at the table. After that, if your child is still hungry, offer low-carb options (carrot sticks, cheese, cucumber) that have minimal glucose impact. Save the next significant carbohydrate intake for dinner.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The after-school snack is a management tool, not just a treat** — it bridges the gap between lunch and dinner and prevents late-afternoon glucose dips.
2. **Adjust the snack based on activity level** — add 10–20 g of carbohydrate on PE or high-activity days to account for increased glucose uptake by muscles.
3. **Check blood glucose on arrival home** and use the reading to guide snack size: more carbs if low or dropping, less if high or stable.
4. **Combine carbohydrate with protein or fat** for sustained energy — apple with peanut butter, crackers with cheese, toast with ham.
5. **Avoid continuous grazing** by offering one defined snack at a set time, then low-carb options if hunger persists before dinner.
