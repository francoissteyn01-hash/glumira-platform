# "Why Me?" — Answering the Hardest Question

**Topic ID:** 040
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** All (children, parents, and caregivers)
**Category:** Emotional Wellbeing
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## The Question That Doesn't Have a Simple Answer

It might come at bedtime, whispered into a pillow. It might come as a shout after a frustrating blood glucose check. It might come quietly, in the car, staring out the window. Or it might come as rage — at the injections, at the counting, at all of it.

"Why me?"

If you're a parent, hearing this question from your child can break your heart. If you're the young person asking it, the feeling behind it — the grief, the anger, the unfairness — is as real and valid as anything you'll ever feel.

Mira can't give you a neat answer to "Why me?" because there isn't one. But what Mira can do is sit with you in this question, help you understand where it comes from, and offer some honest ways to hold it.

## The Grief Nobody Talks About

When a child is diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes, the family grieves. There's shock, fear, and a frantic learning curve. But the initial grief tends to focus on the diagnosis itself — the hospital stay, the new routine, the overwhelming information.

What often gets missed is the grief that comes later. The slow-burn grief that surfaces months or years after diagnosis, when the reality of "this is forever" truly settles in.

For pre-teens, this realisation often hits between ages 10 and 13. They're old enough to understand permanence. They're old enough to compare their daily reality with their friends' daily reality. They're old enough to feel the weight of a condition that demands constant attention in a life that's already changing in a hundred other ways.

"Why me?" is grief talking. It's the sound of a young person processing an enormous truth about their life. And it deserves to be heard with the seriousness it carries.

## What "Why Me?" Really Means

When your child asks "Why me?", they might be asking several things at once:

**"Did I do something to cause this?"** No. Absolutely, categorically no. Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition. The immune system mistakenly attacks the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. Nothing your child ate, did, or didn't do caused this. Nothing you as a parent did or didn't do caused this. It is not a consequence of diet, lifestyle, behaviour, or any choice anyone made.

This message cannot be stated too clearly or too often. Children — especially pre-teens, who are developing a stronger sense of cause and effect — can carry secret guilt about their diagnosis. They may never voice it. Ask gently. Reassure directly.

**"Is this fair?"** No. It isn't fair. And pretending it is doesn't help. It's not fair that they have to check their blood glucose when their friends don't. It's not fair that they have to think about insulin with every meal. It's not fair that a trip to a birthday party requires planning that other families don't have to do.

Acknowledging the unfairness is not self-pity — it's honesty. And children need the adults in their lives to be honest with them about this. "You're right. It's not fair. And I'm sorry."

**"Will it always be like this?"** The honest answer is that T1D is currently a lifelong condition. There is no cure today. Research is ongoing, and there is real progress in areas like beta cell replacement, immunotherapy, and advanced closed-loop pump systems. But promising your child a cure that hasn't arrived is a form of hope that can backfire. Instead, you might say: "Right now, this is how we manage it. Scientists are working hard on better treatments and one day maybe a cure. And in the meantime, we'll manage it together — and you're getting better at it all the time."

**"Why can't I just be normal?"** This one cuts deep. The desire to be like everyone else is at the very core of pre-teen identity. The answer isn't "you are normal" — because they know their experience is different. The answer is something more like: "You are yourself. You have T1D, and you also have everything else that makes you who you are. Diabetes is part of your life, but it is not all of your life. And being different in this one way does not make you less."

## It's OK to Be Angry

Some children express "Why me?" through tears. Others express it through anger. And anger, while harder to sit with as a parent, is a completely healthy emotional response to an unjust situation.

A child who slams their meter on the table and yells "I hate this!" is not misbehaving. They're communicating. The behaviour might need gentle boundaries — "I hear that you're angry, and you're allowed to be angry, but the meter still needs to work tomorrow" — but the emotion underneath is valid and should be acknowledged.

Suppressing anger doesn't make it go away. It drives it underground, where it can emerge as withdrawal, non-compliance with diabetes care, or depression. Giving your child permission to be angry — and showing them that anger doesn't frighten you or push you away — is one of the most powerful things you can do.

"You're allowed to be furious about this. I'm furious about it sometimes too."

## How to Respond: A Guide for Parents

There is no script that will make this question painless. But there are approaches that help:

**Don't rush to fix it.** The instinct to make your child feel better is strong. But "Why me?" isn't a problem to solve — it's a feeling to hold. Sometimes the best response is simply sitting with them in silence, a hand on their back, letting them know you're there.

**Don't compare.** "Other people have it worse" is factually true and emotionally useless. A child in pain doesn't need perspective — they need presence.

**Don't minimise.** "It could be worse" or "At least it's manageable" may feel reassuring to you, but to a child in the grip of grief, it sounds like their feelings don't matter. They do.

**Do validate.** "This is really hard. I see how much you're carrying. Your feelings make complete sense."

**Do share your own feelings (carefully).** "I wish this wasn't part of your life too. Sometimes I feel angry about it as well." This shows your child they're not alone in their grief — and that strong emotions are a family affair, not a personal weakness.

**Do keep the door open.** "You can talk to me about this any time. Even at 2 AM. Even if you just want to say 'I hate diabetes' and nothing else. I'm here."

## A Word Directly to You

If you're the young person reading this — the one asking "Why me?" or feeling it without saying it — Mira wants you to hear this:

You didn't cause this. You didn't deserve this. You're not being punished. The universe is not picking on you. Sometimes, a body's immune system makes a mistake, and a person ends up with Type 1 diabetes. It happened to you. It's not fair.

And you're allowed to grieve that. You're allowed to be angry. You're allowed to have days where diabetes feels like the heaviest thing in the world. Those feelings are not weakness. They are proof that you understand the weight of what you carry — and that understanding is a kind of courage.

But hear this too: you are not only your diabetes. You are your laugh, your interests, your friendships, your dreams, your weird jokes, your favourite songs, your plans for the future. T1D is something you live with. It is not who you are.

And on the days when it feels like too much, reach out. To a parent, a friend, a counsellor, a diabetes nurse, an online community — anyone. You don't have to carry this alone.

You never did.

## Key Takeaways

1. **"Why me?" is a grief response, not a behavioural problem** — it reflects a young person processing the permanence and unfairness of their condition.
2. **Be absolutely clear: your child did not cause their T1D** — Type 1 diabetes is autoimmune, not caused by diet, lifestyle, or any choice.
3. **Acknowledge the unfairness honestly** — minimising or comparing doesn't help; validation and presence do.
4. **Anger is a healthy and expected response** — give your child permission to feel it, with gentle boundaries around how it's expressed.
5. **Keep the door open for ongoing conversation** — this question doesn't get asked once and resolved; it resurfaces, and your consistent availability matters more than any single perfect answer.
