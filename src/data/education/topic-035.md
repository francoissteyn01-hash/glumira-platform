# Social Media and T1D — What Your Child Sees Online

**Topic ID:** 035
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Category:** Digital Wellbeing & Safety
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## The Screen They Carry Everywhere

Your child's relationship with social media is part of their daily life — and increasingly, it's part of their diabetes life too. Whether they're on TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, or a platform that didn't exist when you started reading this sentence, they are encountering content about health, food, bodies, and chronic illness. Some of that content is helpful. Some of it is dangerously wrong.

As a parent of a child with T1D, understanding the social media landscape isn't optional — it's part of keeping your child safe and well-informed. This topic is about knowing what's out there, recognising the risks, finding the value, and supporting your child without becoming the internet police.

## The Misinformation Problem

Let's start with the hard truth: social media platforms are full of inaccurate health information, and diabetes is one of the most misrepresented conditions online.

**"Cure" claims.** Your child may encounter posts claiming that T1D can be reversed through diet, supplements, essential oils, alkaline water, or various other interventions. These claims are false. Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system has destroyed insulin-producing beta cells. No lifestyle change reverses this. But a twelve-year-old scrolling at midnight may not know that — and the hope these posts generate can be followed by crushing disappointment.

**Type confusion.** The general public — and much of social media — conflates Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. Your child may see content blaming diabetes on sugar consumption, poor diet, or lack of exercise. This is harmful. It can cause shame, guilt, and a fundamental misunderstanding of their own condition. A pre-teen who already feels "different" doesn't need the internet telling them their disease is their fault.

**Dangerous challenges and trends.** From time to time, social media trends emerge that involve food restriction, extreme exercise, or deliberate manipulation of insulin. These are rare but genuinely dangerous. Awareness means you can address them if they surface.

**Influencer health advice.** Not everyone with a large following and a confident tone has medical qualifications. Some diabetes influencers share excellent, evidence-based content. Others share personal experiences as though they're universal truths — and for a young person, the distinction between anecdote and evidence can be hard to spot.

## The Value That's Also There

It's not all bad. In fact, some of what your child finds online may be genuinely beneficial.

**T1D peer communities.** For a pre-teen who feels like the only kid at school with diabetes, finding an online community of young people who understand the daily reality of T1D can be profoundly reassuring. Seeing someone their age confidently managing a pump at a party, laughing about a CGM alarm going off in class, or simply saying "today was hard" can reduce isolation in ways that adult reassurance sometimes can't.

**Normalisation.** Visibility matters. When your child sees T1D represented in content they consume — by creators who are thriving, not defined by their condition — it sends a powerful message: diabetes is part of my life, but it is not all of my life.

**Practical tips from lived experience.** Some creators share genuinely useful real-world strategies — how to wear a CGM at the beach, how to talk to friends about lows, how to handle school situations. These tips, while not medical advice, can complement clinical guidance with the practical texture of daily life.

## Teaching Critical Thinking, Not Just Rules

Banning your child from all health-related content online is neither realistic nor helpful. What you can do — and what will serve them far beyond diabetes — is help them develop critical thinking skills about what they see.

**Ask questions together.** When your child shares something they saw online, resist the urge to immediately correct or dismiss. Instead, explore it with them. "That's interesting — where did they get that information? Are they a doctor, or are they sharing their own experience? Does that match what your diabetes team has told you?"

**Teach the source check.** Help your child understand the difference between a peer-reviewed medical source, a reputable diabetes organisation, a personal blog, and a sponsored post. They don't need to become researchers — they just need the habit of asking "who says so?"

**Create a safe space for questions.** If your child feels they can come to you with anything they've seen online — without judgement — they will. If they feel they'll be lectured or their phone will be taken away, they won't. The goal is to be the person they bring their questions to, not the person they hide their screens from.

**Share good sources proactively.** Rather than only reacting to bad content, introduce your child to reliable T1D resources. Your diabetes team can recommend age-appropriate accounts, websites, and communities. When your child has a baseline of trustworthy information, misinformation is easier to spot.

## Monitoring Without Spying

This is the delicate balance every parent of a pre-teen navigates — and diabetes adds another dimension to it.

**Be transparent about your involvement.** If you're going to follow their accounts, check their feeds occasionally, or use parental monitoring tools, tell them. Secrecy breeds mistrust. "I'm going to keep an eye on what you're seeing about health stuff online — not because I don't trust you, but because there's a lot of bad information out there and I want to help you sort through it."

**Focus on the content, not the control.** Your concern is about what they're absorbing about their health, not about policing their every click. Keep the conversation centred on information quality, not surveillance.

**Respect their need for private community.** If your child is part of a T1D peer group online and finds it supportive, that space has value. You don't need to read every message. You do need to know the group exists and that it's a healthy environment.

**Check in regularly but lightly.** "Seen anything interesting about diabetes online lately?" is a better opener than "Show me your phone." Regular, casual conversations about online content normalise the topic and keep communication lines open.

## When Something Harmful Gets Through

Despite your best efforts, your child may encounter content that upsets them, confuses them, or leads them to question their treatment. When that happens:

**Stay calm.** Your reaction sets the tone. If you panic or get angry at "the internet," your child may stop sharing what they find.

**Correct gently and clearly.** "I can see why that sounded convincing. Let me explain why your diabetes team would say something different." Then follow up with their clinical team if the misinformation has caused genuine anxiety.

**Acknowledge the emotion.** If your child watched a video claiming a cure exists and felt hopeful, the disappointment of learning it's false is real. Don't skip past that. "I wish it were true too. I really do."

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Social media contains significant diabetes misinformation** — from false cure claims to Type 1/Type 2 confusion to dangerous trends.
2. **Online T1D communities can also be genuinely valuable** — providing peer support, normalisation, and practical real-world tips.
3. **Teach critical thinking rather than imposing bans** — help your child question sources, check credentials, and compare online claims with clinical guidance.
4. **Monitor with transparency and respect** — be honest about your involvement, focus on content quality, and keep conversations open and non-judgemental.
5. **When harmful content gets through, respond with calm correction and emotional acknowledgement** — your child needs to feel safe bringing you what they've seen.
