## Hello from Mira

Everything we have covered in the emergency topics — severe hypos, DKA, seizures, sick-day management, travel — comes together in one document. It is not glamorous. It is not complicated. But it may be the most important piece of paper in your household.

Your **Emergency Action Plan** (EAP) is a concise, clear document that tells anyone — a babysitter, a teacher, a grandparent, a paramedic, a stranger in an airport — exactly what to do if something goes wrong. It takes the knowledge out of your head and puts it somewhere others can access it when you might not be there to explain.

I have seen too many families who carry everything in their minds — who know exactly what to do but have never written it down. And then the moment comes when someone else needs to act, and that knowledge is locked inside one person who is not in the room.

Let us build your plan together.

## Why a written plan matters

In an emergency, stress degrades thinking. Even people who have been trained can freeze, forget steps, or second-guess themselves under pressure. A written plan:

- **Removes the need to think from scratch** — the steps are right there, in order.
- **Empowers people who are not diabetes experts** to act correctly — a babysitter, a supply teacher, a sports coach.
- **Provides consistency** — the same plan is followed whether the parent, the teacher, or the grandparent is responding.
- **Gives paramedics critical information** quickly — insulin types, emergency contacts, relevant medical history.
- **Reduces fear** — for the person with T1D and for everyone around them. Knowing that a plan exists is itself reassuring.

ISPAD, ADA, and NICE all recommend that families have a written, regularly reviewed emergency plan as part of comprehensive diabetes management.

## What your Emergency Action Plan should contain

Your EAP should be **one to two pages maximum** — concise enough to be read and acted upon in under two minutes. It is not a manual. It is a quick-reference document for a crisis.

### Section 1 — Personal information

- **Full name** of the person with T1D
- **Date of birth**
- **Photo** (especially important for children — emergency responders can confirm they have the right person)
- **Type 1 Diabetes** — stated clearly and prominently
- **Date of diagnosis**
- **Other relevant medical conditions or allergies**
- **Hospital or clinic number** (if applicable)

### Section 2 — Insulin and device information

- **Insulin types** — list both basal and bolus insulin by generic AND brand name
- **Delivery method** — injections (pen or syringe) or insulin pump (specify pump model)
- **CGM** — whether one is used, the type, and how to access readings (follow app details if relevant)
- Note: Do NOT include specific doses in the EAP distributed broadly. Doses change frequently, and an outdated dose on a plan is dangerous. Instead, state: *"Insulin doses are managed by [name/parent/patient] and the diabetes care team. Do not administer insulin without their guidance."*

### Section 3 — Hypoglycaemia (low blood glucose) action steps

Write this as a simple, numbered list:

**Mild hypo — person is conscious and can swallow:**
1. Give fast-acting glucose immediately (specify what is available and where it is kept — e.g., "glucose tablets in the red pouch in their school bag").
2. Wait 10–15 minutes, recheck BG.
3. If still low, repeat fast-acting glucose.
4. Once above 4.0 mmol/L (72 mg/dL), give a longer-acting snack.
5. Notify [parent/carer name and phone number].

**Severe hypo — person is unconscious, confused, or seizing:**
1. Do NOT put anything in their mouth.
2. Administer glucagon — specify location (e.g., "nasal glucagon in the fridge door / in the emergency kit in the office") and basic instructions.
3. Place in the recovery position (on their side).
4. Call ambulance — [local emergency number].
5. Call [parent/carer — primary phone number].
6. Call [backup emergency contact — phone number].

### Section 4 — Hyperglycaemia and ketone action steps

**If blood glucose is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL):**
1. Encourage water or sugar-free fluids.
2. Check ketones if testing supplies are available.
3. Notify [parent/carer name and phone number].

**If ketones are above 1.5 mmol/L or the person is vomiting with high BG:**
1. Call [parent/carer] immediately.
2. If unable to reach parent/carer, call [diabetes team emergency number].
3. If the person is drowsy, breathing rapidly, or ketones are above 3.0 mmol/L — call ambulance.

### Section 5 — Emergency contacts (in order of priority)

| Contact | Name | Phone | Relationship |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary | | | Parent / Partner |
| Secondary | | | Parent / Partner / Family member |
| Diabetes team | | | Clinic / Hospital |
| Pump helpline | | | Manufacturer (if applicable) |
| Emergency services | | | Ambulance |

Include **international dialling codes** if the family travels frequently.

### Section 6 — Additional notes

- Any known triggers for severe hypos (e.g., after swimming, during illness)
- Impaired hypo awareness — yes or no
- Any relevant behavioural notes (e.g., "When low, may become aggressive — this is the hypo, not intentional")
- Food allergies or dietary restrictions relevant to emergency treatment
- Language spoken at home if different from the local language

## Who should have a copy of the plan

Think about every setting where the person with T1D spends time without their primary caregiver:

- **School** — class teacher, school nurse, PE teacher, front office
- **Childcare or nursery**
- **After-school clubs and sports coaches**
- **Grandparents and extended family**
- **Babysitters and au pairs**
- **Friends' parents** (for playdates and sleepovers)
- **Workplace** (for adults — a trusted colleague or first aider)
- **The person's own wallet, school bag, or backpack**
- **On the fridge at home** — visible and accessible to anyone in the house
- **In the car** — the glove compartment
- **Digitally** — a photo on the phone of anyone who may need it; a shared cloud document that can be updated centrally

When you update the plan, update ALL copies. Outdated plans can cause confusion.

## The hospital bag — packed and ready

If you live with T1D or care for a child with T1D, having a pre-packed **hospital bag** is not paranoia — it is practical. DKA, severe hypos, and sick-day crises can require urgent hospital trips, and you do not want to be gathering supplies while managing an emergency.

Your hospital bag should contain:

- **Diabetes supplies** — glucose meter, strips, lancets, ketone meter, ketone strips, spare CGM sensor, insulin pens or pump supplies, fast-acting glucose
- **A copy of the Emergency Action Plan**
- **A list of current medications** (including insulin types and any other prescriptions)
- **Insurance card or health system ID**
- **Phone charger**
- **Comfort items** — for a child, a favourite toy or blanket; for anyone, a book, headphones, something to make the wait more bearable
- **A change of clothes** — in case of vomiting or incontinence during a severe event
- **Snacks** — for the caregiver; you may be at the hospital for hours and need to eat to function

Keep this bag in a consistent, known location. Tell other family members where it is. Check and refresh its contents every three months.

## Review schedule — the plan is only good if it is current

An Emergency Action Plan is a living document. It must be reviewed and updated regularly:

- **Every 3 months** — or whenever there is a change in insulin regimen, school, care team, or contact numbers.
- **After any emergency event** — debrief with your diabetes team and update the plan based on what you learned.
- **At the start of each school year** — provide a fresh copy to the school with an in-person handover to the relevant staff.
- **Before travel** — add destination-specific information (local emergency number, nearest hospital, travel insurance details).
- **When the child is old enough** to participate, involve them in the review. Ownership of their plan is an important step toward self-management.

## A note on sharing the plan with your child

As children grow, they become increasingly aware of and involved in their own diabetes management. Sharing the Emergency Action Plan with them — in age-appropriate language — is not about burdening them. It is about empowering them. A teenager who knows what their plan says, who has practised with glucagon, who knows the emergency number, is a teenager who has confidence and agency.

For younger children, the plan is primarily for the adults around them. But even young children can learn: "If I feel shaky, I tell a grown-up and eat my glucose tablets." That is the beginning of self-advocacy, and it matters.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Write your Emergency Action Plan down** — the knowledge in your head cannot help someone who is not you; a written plan empowers anyone to act in a crisis.
2. **Keep it concise — one to two pages maximum** with numbered steps for hypo and DKA scenarios, emergency contacts, and personal medical details.
3. **Distribute copies to every setting** where the person with T1D spends time — school, family, friends, workplace, car, wallet, and digitally.
4. **Pack a hospital bag** and keep it ready — diabetes supplies, the action plan, medications list, charger, comfort items, and snacks for the caregiver.
5. **Review the plan every three months** and after any emergency — update all copies, and involve the person with T1D as they grow old enough to participate.
