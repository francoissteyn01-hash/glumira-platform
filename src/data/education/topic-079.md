## Hello from Mira

I want to speak directly to the people who care for someone with Type 1 Diabetes — parents, partners, grandparents, teachers, friends. This topic covers one of the most frightening things you may ever witness: a seizure caused by severe hypoglycaemia.

I will not soften this. Watching someone you love have a seizure is terrifying. But I have seen, time and again, that the people who know what to expect, who have rehearsed the steps in their minds, who understand what is happening and why — those people act. They do the right things. And their knowledge makes the difference.

This is not a topic to read and forget. Read it, understand it, and then share it with every person who spends time with your child or the person you care for.

## Why severe hypos can cause seizures

The brain depends almost entirely on glucose for fuel. Unlike muscles, which can use fat and other substrates, the brain has very limited energy reserves and requires a constant supply of glucose from the blood.

When blood glucose drops to dangerously low levels — typically below 2.5–3.0 mmol/L (45–54 mg/dL), though the threshold varies between individuals — the brain begins to malfunction. The earliest signs are confusion, slurred speech, and behavioural changes. If glucose continues to fall and is not corrected, brain function deteriorates further.

A **seizure** occurs when the brain's electrical activity becomes chaotic due to insufficient glucose. It is the brain's distress signal — a sign that it is running critically low on fuel.

Seizures from hypoglycaemia are classified as a **severe hypoglycaemic event** under ISPAD and ADA definitions. They require immediate treatment and constitute a medical emergency.

## What a hypoglycaemic seizure looks like

A seizure caused by severe hypo may present in different ways. Knowing what to look for helps you recognise it immediately.

### Tonic-clonic (generalised) seizure — the most recognisable type
- The body **stiffens** (tonic phase) — arms and legs become rigid, the jaw may clench.
- This is followed by **rhythmic jerking** (clonic phase) — arms, legs, and sometimes the whole body jerk repeatedly.
- The person may **cry out** at the onset — this is caused by air being forced through the vocal cords, not by conscious distress.
- Eyes may **roll upward** or stare blankly.
- Breathing may become **irregular or temporarily stop** — the skin may appear pale or bluish around the lips.
- The person is **unconscious** and will not respond to you during the seizure.
- Duration is typically **1–3 minutes**, though it can feel much longer.

### Other seizure presentations
- In some cases, particularly in young children, the seizure may be more subtle — staring, lip smacking, unusual repetitive movements, or sudden limpness.
- Some people experience only the tonic phase (stiffening) without the clonic phase (jerking).

### After the seizure (postictal state)
- The person will likely be **deeply drowsy, confused, and disoriented** for minutes to hours.
- They may not recognise you initially.
- Nausea and headache are common.
- They may have been incontinent (wet or soiled themselves) during the seizure — handle this with quiet dignity; they may be embarrassed when they become aware.

## What to do — step-by-step emergency response

When someone with T1D has a seizure, follow these steps:

### Step 1 — Protect them from injury
- **Clear the area** around them. Move furniture, sharp objects, and anything they could hit during convulsions.
- **Cushion their head** if possible — place something soft (a jacket, a pillow, your hands) under their head.
- **Do NOT restrain them.** Do not hold them down or try to stop the jerking. You cannot stop a seizure by force, and you may injure them or yourself.

### Step 2 — DO NOT put anything in their mouth
This is critical. There is a persistent myth that you should put something between the teeth to prevent them from biting their tongue. **Do not do this.** Placing fingers, a spoon, a wallet, or any object in the mouth of a seizing person can:
- Break their teeth
- Cause them to choke
- Injure their jaw
- Injure your fingers severely (the jaw muscles during a seizure are extraordinarily powerful)

They will not swallow their tongue. That is physically impossible. Leave their mouth alone.

### Step 3 — Do NOT give food, drink, or oral glucose
While the person is seizing or unconscious, **nothing should go in their mouth**. No juice, no glucose tablets, no glucose gel. The risk of choking or aspiration (inhaling liquid into the lungs) is extremely high.

### Step 4 — Administer glucagon
If you have glucagon available, administer it as soon as the seizure begins or as soon as you can safely do so:

- **Injectable glucagon:** Mix according to the kit instructions (if it requires reconstitution) and inject into the outer thigh or upper arm. It can be injected through clothing if necessary.
- **Nasal glucagon (e.g., Baqsimi):** Insert the device into one nostril and press the plunger. The person does not need to be conscious or to inhale. It is absorbed through the nasal membrane.

Glucagon stimulates the liver to release stored glucose into the bloodstream. It typically begins to raise blood glucose within 10–15 minutes. The person may vomit as they regain consciousness — this is a common side effect of glucagon and is expected.

### Step 5 — Place them in the recovery position
Once the active seizure has stopped (the jerking has ceased), gently roll the person onto their side — the **recovery position**:
- Tilt the head slightly back to open the airway.
- Bend the upper knee forward for stability.
- Place the lower arm in front of the body for support.

This position prevents choking if they vomit (which is likely after glucagon) and keeps the airway clear.

### Step 6 — Call an ambulance

**Call emergency services (ambulance) IMMEDIATELY if:**

- You **do not have glucagon** available
- The seizure lasts **longer than 5 minutes**
- A **second seizure** occurs
- The person **does not regain consciousness within 15 minutes** of administering glucagon
- The person **vomits repeatedly** after glucagon and you cannot maintain their glucose
- **Breathing does not return to normal** after the seizure stops
- This is the person's **first ever seizure**
- You are **unsure of the cause** — if you do not know whether this is a hypo-related seizure or another medical event
- **You are frightened and need help** — this is always a valid reason to call

**Do not hesitate to call an ambulance.** Emergency services would always rather respond and find the person recovering than arrive too late.

### Step 7 — After they regain consciousness
- Speak calmly and reassuringly. They will be confused and frightened.
- Once they can swallow safely, offer small sips of a sugary drink, then a carbohydrate snack.
- Do not leave them alone for several hours. Monitor blood glucose frequently.
- They may fall into a deep sleep — this is normal post-seizure, but continue to monitor BG and check on them regularly.

## Preparing for this moment before it happens

- **Ensure glucagon is always accessible** — at home, at school, at grandparents' houses, in the travel bag. Check expiry dates monthly.
- **Train multiple people** to administer glucagon. Practise with an expired kit or a trainer device. When the moment comes, you do not want to be reading instructions for the first time.
- **Create an emergency action plan** (see Topic 080) and share it with everyone who cares for the person with T1D — including school staff, babysitters, sports coaches, and extended family.
- **Discuss seizure response** explicitly. Say the words out loud: "If she has a seizure, this is what you do." People who have heard the plan in advance are far more likely to act when it matters.

## After the event — the days that follow

A seizure from severe hypoglycaemia is a significant medical event. In the days after:

- **Contact your diabetes care team** as soon as possible. They will want to review insulin doses, identify the likely cause, and adjust the management plan to reduce the risk of recurrence.
- **Emotional recovery** takes time. The person who had the seizure — and the people who witnessed it — may experience anxiety, fear, and a loss of confidence. This is entirely normal. Some families benefit from counselling or peer support. Please do not underestimate the emotional impact.
- **Review your emergency supplies** — replace used glucagon immediately.
- If seizures recur, your diabetes team may reassess glucose targets, CGM alarm settings, and whether additional supports (such as a diabetes alert dog or overnight monitoring systems) are appropriate.

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **A seizure during severe hypoglycaemia is a medical emergency** — the brain is critically low on fuel and needs glucose immediately.
2. **Do NOT put anything in the mouth of a seizing person** — no food, no drink, no objects between the teeth; the choking risk is severe.
3. **Administer glucagon** (injection or nasal) as soon as possible, then place the person in the **recovery position** on their side to protect their airway.
4. **Call an ambulance** if you have no glucagon, if the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, if a second seizure occurs, or if the person does not regain consciousness within 15 minutes.
5. **Prepare before the emergency** — train multiple people in glucagon use, keep glucagon accessible and in-date, and ensure an emergency action plan is shared with everyone who cares for the person.
