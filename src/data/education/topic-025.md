# Sleepovers — How to Prepare the Host Family

**Topic ID:** 025
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. Sleepovers are a childhood milestone — staying up late, giggling under blankets, eating popcorn in the dark. For families living with Type 1 diabetes, the idea of their child spending the night in someone else's home can feel daunting. But with good preparation, a clear briefing for the host family, and a well-packed overnight bag, sleepovers are absolutely possible and absolutely worth it.

Let's walk through how to prepare the host family so that everyone — your child, the host parents, and you — feels confident and calm.

## Why Sleepovers Matter

It is tempting to avoid sleepovers altogether. The overnight period is when delayed hypos can occur, when routines are disrupted, and when you are not there to check the CGM at 2 a.m. But avoiding sleepovers altogether sends your child a message that their diabetes makes them different in a way that limits their life. ISPAD guidelines emphasise the importance of psychosocial wellbeing and normal social participation for children with T1D. A sleepover is a normal part of growing up, and your child deserves to be part of it.

The goal is not to eliminate all risk — it is to reduce risk to a manageable level and to equip the host family with enough knowledge to act if needed.

## The Host Family Briefing Sheet

The single most effective tool is a one-page briefing sheet that you give to the host parent in person, walk through together, and leave with them for the night. Keep it simple, warm, and non-medical. Here is a template:

---

**[Your child's name] — Overnight Diabetes Info**

**What is Type 1 diabetes?**
[Child's name]'s pancreas does not make insulin, so they wear a pump / take injections to manage their blood sugar. They also wear a sensor on their arm/tummy that monitors their sugar levels continuously.

**What you need to know:**

1. **Before bed:** [Child's name] needs to check their blood sugar (they can show you their sensor/reader). If the number is below [X — as advised by your diabetes team], please give them [specific snack — e.g., a glass of milk and a biscuit] and text me.

2. **If [child's name] feels shaky, sweaty, or says they feel "funny":** This may be a low blood sugar. Give them a juice box from their kit immediately. You do not need to check anything first — just give the juice. Then text or call me.

3. **If [child's name] will not wake up or seems very confused:** Call an ambulance immediately (999 / 911 / 10177). Tell them your guest has Type 1 diabetes and may be having a severe low blood sugar. Then call me.

4. **Snacks and meals:** [Child's name] can eat whatever the other children eat. They may need to check their sensor before eating. If you are not sure about anything, just text me — I am happy to help at any time.

**My phone:** [your number]
**Partner's phone:** [partner's number]
**Diabetes team emergency line:** [number]

---

Print this, hand it over, and talk through each point. Emphasise: "You will almost certainly not need any of this. But if you do, it is here."

## What to Pack for the Sleepover

Think of the overnight bag as having two layers: normal sleepover stuff, and a diabetes kit tucked inside.

### Diabetes Overnight Kit

- Blood glucose meter and test strips (even if your child wears a CGM)
- Fast-acting glucose: at least 4 juice boxes or glucose tablet rolls
- Follow-up snacks: crackers, a muesli bar, or a small sandwich
- Glucagon emergency kit (nasal or injectable) — show the host parent where it is and that instructions are printed on the box
- Spare CGM sensor (if you have one — sensors occasionally fail at the worst moments)
- Spare pump supplies if applicable: infusion set, reservoir, batteries
- Insulin pen and needles if on MDI (in case the pump fails)
- A clearly labelled bag or pouch for all diabetes supplies
- Ketone testing strips
- The briefing sheet (described above)

### Normal Sleepover Stuff

- Pyjamas, toothbrush, change of clothes
- Sleeping bag or pillow
- Whatever your child normally brings to a sleepover — a favourite toy, a book, a torch

Pack the diabetes supplies in a separate, brightly coloured bag inside the overnight bag so your child can easily find it and show the host parent.

## Emergency Contacts

Make sure the host parent has:

1. **Your mobile number** — and confirm you will keep your phone on, volume up, all night
2. **Your partner's or another family member's number** — as a backup
3. **Your child's diabetes team emergency number** — for clinical questions you cannot answer over the phone
4. **The address of the nearest hospital with a paediatric department** — just in case

Write all four on the briefing sheet so the host parent has everything in one place.

## Preparing Your Child

Before the sleepover, talk to your child about what to do:

- **"Show your friend's mum or dad your CGM reader before bed."** Normalise it. It is not a secret.
- **"If you feel low, tell an adult straight away, even if it is the middle of the night."** Practise this at home. Role-play waking up and telling a grown-up.
- **"Eat what everyone else eats. Just check your sensor first."** Keep the message simple and positive.
- **"I will have my phone right next to my bed all night. You can call me any time."**

For younger children (5–7), you may want to offer to be on standby nearby — perhaps at a coffee shop or parked outside for the first hour — until everyone is settled. For older children (8–10), a phone call at bedtime may be enough.

## Preparing Yourself

Let's be honest: the first sleepover is often harder on the parent than the child. You might lie awake watching the CGM share app on your phone, watching every dip and rise. That is completely normal.

Some practical tips for your own peace of mind:

- **Set up CGM sharing** if your system supports it (Dexcom Share, LibreLink, etc.), so you can see your child's glucose on your phone in real time.
- **Set alarms** on the CGM for low glucose (e.g., below 4.0 mmol/L / 72 mg/dL) and high glucose (e.g., above 14 mmol/L / 250 mg/dL).
- **Agree a communication plan with the host parent:** "If the sensor alarms and I can see it on my phone, I may text you. If I text, here is what I might ask you to do — give a juice box, or give a snack."
- **Accept imperfect numbers.** One night of slightly higher or slightly lower glucose is not going to affect your child's long-term health. The goal is safety, not perfection.

## When the Host Parent Says "I'm Nervous"

Some host parents will be anxious. That is fair — they are being asked to take on a responsibility they have never had before. Reassure them:

- "You will not need to give insulin or make any medical decisions."
- "The most likely thing you might need to do is hand them a juice box."
- "I will be awake and available on my phone all night."
- "You are doing something wonderful for my child by having them over."

Most host parents, once they have the briefing sheet and have talked it through, feel reassured and even a little proud to be trusted.

## After the Sleepover

When your child comes home, check blood glucose and ketones if levels have been high. Expect a tired, happy child with possibly imperfect numbers. Celebrate the sleepover, not the glucose graph.

And next time, it will be easier.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Sleepovers are an important part of childhood** — ISPAD emphasises that children with T1D should participate fully in normal social activities, including overnight stays.
2. **A one-page briefing sheet for the host parent** is the single most effective preparation tool — keep it simple, warm, and focused on what to do, not on medical theory.
3. **Pack a clearly labelled diabetes overnight kit** inside the sleepover bag with fast-acting glucose, a meter, glucagon, and spare supplies.
4. **Set up CGM sharing and keep your phone on all night** so you can monitor remotely and text the host parent if needed.
5. **Reassure the host parent** that they will not need to make medical decisions — the most likely action is handing over a juice box.
