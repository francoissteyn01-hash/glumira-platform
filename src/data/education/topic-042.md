# Alcohol and Type 1 — The Honest Truth Teens Need to Hear

**Topic ID:** 42
**Age Range:** 16–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Independence & Lifestyle
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17

---

## Why This Topic Exists

Let us skip the part where we pretend teenagers do not drink. Some of you will. Some of you already have. And telling you "just don't" is not education — it is wishful thinking. The reality is that alcohol interacts with Type 1 Diabetes in specific, physiologically dangerous ways, and you deserve to understand exactly what happens in your body so you can make informed decisions.

This is not a lecture. It is biology. What you do with this information is your choice — but you should have the information first.

## What Alcohol Actually Does to Your Blood Glucose

Here is the part most people get wrong: alcohol does not simply raise or lower your blood glucose. It does both, at different times, through different mechanisms. And that unpredictability is what makes it dangerous.

**The initial rise:** Many alcoholic drinks contain carbohydrates — beer, cider, cocktails with mixers, alcopops. These can cause your blood glucose to spike initially, just like eating carbs would. This is the part that might make you think everything is fine.

**The delayed drop — and this is the dangerous part:** Your liver has a critical job in blood glucose regulation. When your BG starts to fall (between meals, overnight, during exercise), your liver releases stored glucose (glycogen) to stop you from going dangerously low. This is one of your body's main safety nets against hypoglycaemia.

Alcohol blocks this process. When your liver is busy metabolising alcohol, it cannot release glucose effectively. This means your usual safety net is disabled — for hours. Not minutes. Hours. Depending on how much you drink, this effect can last 12 to 24 hours.

So here is the scenario that actually hurts people: you drink on a Saturday night, your BG might be fine or even high while you are out. You go to bed. Sometime between 2am and 8am, your blood glucose drops, your liver cannot compensate because it is still processing alcohol, and you have a severe hypo in your sleep.

This is not a scare tactic. This is the mechanism that leads to real hospitalisations and, in rare but devastating cases, worse outcomes.

## Why Hypos and Alcohol Look the Same from the Outside

Here is another problem. The signs of being drunk — slurred speech, confusion, stumbling, loss of coordination, passing out — look almost identical to severe hypoglycaemia. If you are at a party and you go low, the people around you might assume you have just had too much to drink. They might put you in the "recovery position" and leave you to "sleep it off."

If you are actually having a severe hypo, sleeping it off can be fatal.

This is why the people you are with need to know you have T1D. Not everyone at the party. But the people you are actually with — your close friends, the person you came with — they need to know the difference and they need to know what to do.

## The Non-Negotiable Rules

If you choose to drink, these are not optional:

**1. Never drink on an empty stomach.**
Eating before and during drinking slows alcohol absorption and provides a glucose buffer. This is important for everyone, but for you it is a safety measure. Substantial food — not crisps. Something with carbohydrate and fat and protein.

**2. Eat before bed. Seriously.**
After a night out, eat a proper meal or a substantial snack before you go to sleep. Your liver is going to be compromised for hours. You need carbohydrates on board to get you through the night.

**3. Check your blood glucose. Often.**
Before you start drinking. During the night. Before bed. When you wake up. If you use a CGM, make sure your alarms are on and loud enough to wake you (or someone near you). If you use a meter, set phone alarms to check overnight.

**4. Tell at least one person.**
One friend who is with you needs to know: you have T1D, what a hypo looks like, where your glucose is, and that "sleeping it off" is not acceptable if you are unresponsive. Give them permission to call an ambulance if they are unsure. Better an unnecessary ambulance call than a dead friend.

**5. Wear medical ID.**
A bracelet, a necklace, a tag on your phone case — something that tells a paramedic or a stranger that you have Type 1 Diabetes. If you are found unresponsive, this can literally save your life.

**6. Do not bolus aggressively for high readings while drinking.**
Your BG might be high from the carbs in drinks. The temptation is to correct. But with alcohol suppressing your liver's glucose output, a large correction dose can push you into a severe hypo hours later. This is a conversation to have with your diabetes team before you are in the situation — not during it.

## What About Different Types of Alcohol?

Different drinks have different carb loads, but the liver-suppression effect is from the alcohol itself, not the carbs. So:

- **Beer and cider:** Higher in carbs. May cause an initial BG rise. The delayed low still applies.
- **Spirits with diet mixers:** Lower in carbs, so less of an initial spike, but the delayed hypo risk is the same.
- **Wine:** Moderate carbs. Same delayed risk.
- **Cocktails and premixed drinks:** Often very high in sugar. Can cause dramatic spikes followed by dramatic drops.

There is no "safe" type of alcohol for T1D. The risk is in the alcohol itself.

## The Morning After

Hangovers are rough for everyone. For you, they are also medically relevant. You may wake up with low blood glucose. You may be dehydrated, which can affect your BG readings and your insulin absorption. You may feel too rough to eat properly, which makes managing your levels harder.

Check your BG first thing. Eat something, even if you do not feel like it. Stay hydrated. Keep checking throughout the day. The liver-suppression effect can persist well into the next day, especially after heavier drinking.

And be aware: nausea and vomiting from a hangover can look and feel a lot like diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). If you are vomiting, have high blood glucose, and feel genuinely unwell, do not assume it is just the hangover. Check for ketones. If in doubt, seek medical help.

## The Social Pressure Reality

You might feel pressure to drink to fit in, or pressure to drink the same amount as your friends. Here is the thing: you do not have to drink at all. Plenty of people do not, for all sorts of reasons, and it is increasingly normal. But if you do drink, drinking less than your friends is not a weakness — it is the smartest person in the room reading the situation accurately.

You can hold a drink without finishing it. You can alternate with water or soft drinks. You can say "I'm good" and mean it. Nobody who matters will care.

## A Word About Drugs

While this topic is about alcohol, it would be irresponsible not to mention that recreational drugs also affect blood glucose in unpredictable ways. Stimulants can raise it. Some substances suppress appetite, leading to missed meals and hypos. Cannabis can increase appetite and affect your ability to accurately assess how you feel. The interaction between recreational drugs and T1D is poorly studied precisely because it is hard to study, which means the risks are less understood, not lower.

If you are using any substance, your diabetes team needs to know — not to judge you, but to help you stay safe.

## The Bottom Line

Alcohol and T1D is manageable, but it demands respect. The biology is real: alcohol disables one of your body's most important defences against hypoglycaemia, and it does so for hours. People who understand this and plan accordingly generally do fine. People who do not understand it, or who ignore it, end up in emergency departments.

You are old enough to make your own choices about drinking. Make sure you are also old enough to make them informed.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Alcohol blocks your liver from releasing glucose**, removing a key safety net against hypoglycaemia for up to 12–24 hours after drinking.
2. **Never drink on an empty stomach**, and always eat a substantial snack or meal before bed after drinking — your overnight hypo risk is significantly increased.
3. **Hypo symptoms and drunkenness look identical** — at least one person with you must know you have T1D and what to do if you become unresponsive.
4. **Check your blood glucose before, during, and after drinking**, and keep CGM alarms active overnight. Set phone alarms if using a meter.
5. **Wear medical ID every time you go out** — if you cannot speak for yourself, it speaks for you.
