# Night-time Hypos — Prevention and Response

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 078
**Related Topics:** [topic-019](/education/topic-019), [topic-057](/education/topic-057), [topic-071](/education/topic-071)

---

## Hello from Mira

If there is one thing that keeps parents of children with T1D awake at night — sometimes literally — it is the fear of a hypo happening while their child sleeps. And if you are an adult living with T1D, you may know that uneasy feeling of lying down and wondering whether your blood glucose will hold steady until morning.

Night-time hypoglycaemia is a real risk. It is also one of the most manageable risks in T1D, once you understand why it happens and what you can do to prevent it. I am not going to pretend there is a way to eliminate the worry entirely — but I can help you reduce the risk significantly and give you a clear plan for when it does happen.

---

## Why overnight hypos happen

Blood glucose does not pause while you sleep. Insulin continues to work. The liver continues to release glucose. Hormones shift. And the body's sensitivity to insulin changes through the night in patterns that can catch you off guard.

Several factors make overnight hypoglycaemia more likely:

### 1. The lag effect of daytime activity
Exercise and physical activity increase insulin sensitivity — not just during the activity, but for hours afterwards. A child who played sport in the afternoon, or an adult who went for an evening run, may experience a significant drop in blood glucose 6–12 hours later, often in the middle of the night. This delayed effect is one of the most common causes of overnight hypos.

### 2. Too much active insulin at bedtime
If a correction dose or meal bolus is given too close to bedtime, there may be too much insulin on board (IOB) during the early hours of sleep. The glucose from dinner has been absorbed, but the insulin is still working.

### 3. Basal insulin timing and peaks
Long-acting insulin analogues are designed to provide steady background coverage, but they are not perfectly flat. Some formulations have subtle peaks, and individual responses vary. If the peak of basal action coincides with a time when BG is already trending down, a hypo can result.

### 4. Alcohol (in adults and older adolescents)
Alcohol inhibits the liver's ability to release glucose (gluconeogenesis). This effect can last for many hours after drinking. Overnight hypos following evening alcohol consumption are well-documented and can be severe, because the liver — the body's safety net for falling glucose — is impaired.

### 5. Missed or insufficient bedtime snack
For some people, particularly children, a bedtime snack provides the slow-release carbohydrate that sustains glucose levels through the early hours. Skipping it, or having a snack that is digested too quickly, can leave a gap in glucose supply overnight.

### 6. Growth hormone cycles in children
In children and adolescents, growth hormone release follows complex overnight patterns. The "dawn phenomenon" — a rise in BG in the early morning hours due to growth hormone — is well known. But before that rise, there can be a dip in the preceding hours that, combined with active insulin, pushes glucose too low.

## The danger of overnight hypos

During sleep, the body's warning system for hypoglycaemia is blunted. The sweating, shaking, and hunger that signal a daytime hypo may not wake the person — or may be missed entirely. This is especially true for:

- Young children who cannot recognise or articulate symptoms
- People with impaired hypoglycaemia awareness (reduced symptom perception, more common after years of T1D)
- Anyone who is deeply fatigued or has consumed alcohol

ISPAD and ADA guidelines note that prolonged nocturnal hypoglycaemia can occur without the person waking. In rare but serious cases, this can lead to seizures during sleep. This is why prevention and monitoring are so critical.

## Prevention strategies — reducing the risk

### Check blood glucose at bedtime
A bedtime BG reading is essential. Discuss target ranges with your diabetes team, but generally:
- If BG is below 7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) at bedtime, a snack with slow-release carbohydrate and some protein or fat may be warranted.
- If BG is trending downward (on CGM), even if the absolute number looks acceptable, consider a snack or reduced basal insulin (on medical advice).

### Use a CGM with low-glucose alarms
Continuous glucose monitoring with properly configured alarms is one of the most significant advances in overnight safety. Set low-glucose alerts at a level that gives you time to act — many families and clinicians set alerts at 4.5–5.0 mmol/L (80–90 mg/dL) to provide a buffer before BG reaches the hypo threshold.

**Critical:** Alarms only work if they are heard. Ensure the volume is adequate. Some families place the CGM receiver or phone on a hard surface to amplify the vibration. For children, parents may use a follow app that sends alerts to their own device.

If the CGM has a **predictive low alert** feature (alerting you when BG is predicted to go low within 20–30 minutes), enable it. This provides even earlier warning.

For those using **hybrid closed-loop** or **automated insulin delivery** systems, the system can suspend or reduce insulin delivery automatically when glucose is predicted to drop. These systems have been shown to reduce nocturnal hypoglycaemia significantly. Discuss options with your diabetes team.

### Bedtime snacks — what works
Not all snacks are equal for overnight BG stability. The goal is slow, sustained glucose release, not a spike followed by a crash.

Effective bedtime snack approaches include:
- Complex carbohydrate combined with protein or fat — for example, a slice of wholegrain bread with peanut butter, cheese and crackers, or yoghurt with a handful of nuts.
- The fat and protein slow digestion and provide a more gradual glucose release over several hours.
- Avoid high-sugar, low-fibre snacks at bedtime — they spike BG quickly but do not sustain it.

Your diabetes team can advise on appropriate snack choices and whether a routine bedtime snack is needed or only on specific occasions (after exercise, when BG is trending low, etc.).

### After-exercise nights
On days with significant physical activity, be especially vigilant at bedtime:
- Check BG more frequently in the evening.
- Consider a bedtime snack even if BG appears adequate — the delayed effect of exercise may cause a drop later.
- If using a pump, discuss temporary basal rate reductions with your diabetes team for post-exercise nights.
- Set CGM low alerts slightly higher than usual on these nights.

### Alcohol-related precautions (adults)
- Never go to bed after drinking alcohol without checking BG and having a snack.
- Set a CGM low alert, and ensure someone is aware you have been drinking.
- Alcohol-related hypos can be severe because the liver cannot compensate. Take this seriously.

## Responding to an overnight hypo

### If the person wakes (or is woken by an alarm) and is conscious
1. Check BG with a finger-prick to confirm (CGM readings can lag during rapid changes).
2. Give fast-acting glucose — glucose tablets, glucose gel, or a small glass of juice.
3. Wait 10–15 minutes and recheck BG.
4. Once BG is above 4.0 mmol/L (72 mg/dL), give a slow-release snack to prevent recurrence.
5. Continue monitoring — set an alarm to recheck in 30–60 minutes if not using a CGM.

### If the person is difficult to rouse, confused, or unresponsive
This is a severe hypo. Follow the emergency steps in Topic 071:
1. **Do NOT put food or drink in their mouth.**
2. Administer **glucagon** if available.
3. Place them in the **recovery position** (on their side).
4. **Call an ambulance** if no glucagon is available, if they do not respond within 15 minutes, or if they have a seizure.

### After a night-time hypo
- Record the event — time, BG level, suspected cause, treatment given.
- Contact your diabetes team to discuss whether adjustments to insulin, snacks, or monitoring are needed.
- The person may feel exhausted, nauseous, or headachy the next day. This is normal after a hypo and should be treated with rest and careful monitoring.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Overnight hypos are common but preventable** — delayed effects of exercise, excess insulin on board, and missed bedtime snacks are the most frequent causes.
2. **A CGM with properly configured low-glucose alarms is the most effective safety tool** for overnight monitoring — ensure alarms are set, audible, and tested.
3. **A bedtime snack with slow-release carbohydrate and protein** can sustain glucose levels through the night — talk to your diabetes team about whether and when this is appropriate.
4. **After days with significant physical activity, increase bedtime vigilance** — the insulin-sensitising effect of exercise can cause glucose to drop hours later during sleep.
5. **If someone cannot be roused during a night-time hypo, administer glucagon and call an ambulance** — never put food or drink in the mouth of someone who is not fully conscious.
