## Hello from Mira

In the age of the internet, anyone can find a research paper about Type 1 Diabetes within seconds. Headlines shout about breakthroughs. Social media shares study findings as if they are settled fact. And in the middle of it all, you — a person with T1D, a parent, a carer, or a clinician — are trying to work out what is actually meaningful and what is noise.

The ability to critically evaluate a research paper is one of the most empowering skills you can develop. You do not need a science degree to do it. You need a framework, some key concepts, and a healthy dose of scepticism. Let me walk you through it.

## The anatomy of a research paper

Most published research papers follow a standard structure, sometimes abbreviated as **IMRAD**:

- **Introduction** — Why was this study done? What question were the researchers trying to answer? What is already known?
- **Methods** — How was the study designed? Who participated? What was measured? How was data analysed?
- **Results** — What did they find? The data is presented here, often in tables and figures.
- **Discussion** — What do the results mean? How do they compare to previous research? What are the limitations?

There is also an **Abstract** at the beginning — a brief summary of the entire paper — and a **Conclusion** at the end.

A critical reading tip: **never form your opinion from the abstract alone.** Abstracts are summaries and can sometimes present findings more favourably than the full data supports. The methods and results sections are where the truth lives.

## Study design — the hierarchy of evidence

Not all studies are created equal. The design of a study determines how much confidence you can place in its findings. Here is a simplified hierarchy, from strongest to weakest:

### Systematic reviews and meta-analyses
These combine data from multiple studies to reach a pooled conclusion. They sit at the top of the evidence hierarchy because they synthesise the broadest evidence base. A well-conducted meta-analysis is generally the most reliable source of evidence.

### Randomised controlled trials (RCTs)
Participants are randomly assigned to either the intervention group or a control group (which may receive a placebo, standard care, or a different treatment). Randomisation reduces bias and helps ensure that differences in outcomes are due to the intervention rather than other factors. RCTs are the gold standard for testing whether a treatment works.

### Cohort studies
Researchers follow a group of people over time, observing outcomes based on exposure. These can be prospective (following forward in time) or retrospective (looking back at historical data). They can identify associations but cannot prove causation as strongly as RCTs.

### Case-control studies
Researchers compare people who have an outcome (cases) with people who do not (controls), looking backward to identify potential causes or risk factors.

### Case reports and case series
Descriptions of outcomes in individual patients or small groups. Useful for generating hypotheses but not for drawing conclusions.

### Expert opinion
Based on the experience of authorities in the field, without systematic evidence. Important but the weakest level of evidence.

When you read a study, one of your first questions should be: **what type of study is this?** An RCT carries more weight than a case report. A meta-analysis of multiple RCTs carries more weight than a single RCT.

## Sample size — does the number matter?

Yes. The number of participants in a study — the **sample size** — matters enormously.

A study involving 15 people might produce interesting preliminary findings, but those findings may not apply to the broader T1D population. Small studies are more susceptible to chance findings and less able to detect real but modest effects.

Larger studies — particularly large RCTs with hundreds or thousands of participants — provide more reliable and generalisable results. When evaluating a study, look at the sample size and consider whether it is large enough to support the conclusions being drawn.

That said, some research questions — particularly those involving rare conditions or novel interventions — legitimately start with small studies. Early-phase clinical trials (Phase 1 and 2) are expected to be small. The key is not to over-interpret their findings.

## P-values and statistical significance

You will frequently encounter the term **p-value** in research papers. The p-value answers a specific question: **if the intervention had no real effect, what is the probability that we would see results this extreme (or more extreme) purely by chance?**

By convention, a p-value of less than 0.05 (written as p < 0.05) is considered **statistically significant**. This means there is less than a 5% probability that the observed result occurred by chance alone.

However, statistical significance has important limitations:

- **Statistically significant does not mean clinically meaningful.** A study might find a statistically significant reduction in HbA1c of 0.1% — but is that difference large enough to matter to a real person in real life? Often not.
- **P-values do not tell you the size of the effect.** Look for **effect sizes** and **confidence intervals** alongside p-values. A confidence interval tells you the range within which the true effect is likely to fall.
- **Multiple comparisons inflate false positives.** If a study tests many different outcomes, some will inevitably reach p < 0.05 by chance. Honest papers adjust for this; not all do.
- **A non-significant result does not mean "no effect."** It may mean the study was too small to detect an effect that actually exists.

## Peer review — what it means and what it doesn't

Before a paper is published in a reputable journal, it typically undergoes **peer review** — independent experts in the field evaluate the study's methodology, analysis, and conclusions and decide whether it meets the journal's standards.

Peer review is an important quality filter. It catches errors, challenges assumptions, and generally improves the quality of published work. **However**, it is not infallible:

- Peer reviewers are volunteers and may have limited time
- They may have their own biases or conflicts of interest
- Flawed or fraudulent papers do occasionally pass through peer review
- Pre-print servers (where papers are posted before peer review) have become common — findings on these platforms have not yet been independently scrutinised

When you encounter a study, check whether it has been published in a **peer-reviewed journal**. Papers in journals like *The Lancet*, *Diabetes Care*, *Diabetologia*, *The BMJ*, and *The New England Journal of Medicine* have undergone rigorous review. Papers on pre-print servers or in predatory journals deserve much more caution.

## Media hype vs actual findings

This is perhaps the most important section.

When a study is published, it is often picked up by the media. And the media operates by different rules than science. Headlines are designed to attract clicks. Nuance does not generate engagement. The result is that a study showing modest improvement in a specific subgroup under specific conditions becomes "CURE FOR DIABETES FOUND" on social media.

Here is how to protect yourself:

- **Go to the source.** If a headline interests you, find the actual paper and read at least the abstract, methods, and discussion.
- **Look for the word "cure" with extreme scepticism.** As of now, there is no cure for T1D. Any headline claiming otherwise is, at best, dramatically overstating the findings.
- **Check who funded the study.** Industry-funded studies are not automatically invalid, but funding sources should be disclosed and considered as a potential source of bias.
- **Look at what was actually measured.** Did the study measure a hard clinical endpoint (like HbA1c, severe hypoglycaemia events, or complications) or a surrogate marker (like C-peptide levels or antibody titres)? Surrogate markers are useful but less directly meaningful.
- **Notice the species.** Promising results in mice do not automatically translate to humans. Many interventions that work in animal models fail in human trials.

## Applying this to your own care

Understanding research does not mean you should try to manage your T1D based on the latest study. It means you can engage in more informed conversations with your care team, ask better questions, and resist being swayed by sensational but unsupported claims.

Your care team interprets research in the context of established guidelines — from ISPAD, the ADA, NICE, and other bodies — that synthesise the evidence base across many studies, not just one. Individual studies contribute to the body of evidence; they rarely, on their own, change practice.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Study design matters.** Randomised controlled trials and meta-analyses sit at the top of the evidence hierarchy. Case reports and expert opinion are the weakest. Always ask what type of study you are looking at.
2. **Sample size affects reliability.** Larger studies generally produce more trustworthy and generalisable results. Be cautious about drawing strong conclusions from studies with very few participants.
3. **Statistical significance (p < 0.05) does not equal clinical importance.** Always look for the size of the effect, confidence intervals, and whether the difference matters in real-world terms.
4. **Peer review is a quality filter, not a guarantee.** Published, peer-reviewed papers are more reliable than pre-prints or non-reviewed sources, but no system is perfect.
5. **Media headlines routinely overstate research findings.** Always go to the original paper, check the methods and sample size, and discuss findings with your care team before changing anything about your management.
