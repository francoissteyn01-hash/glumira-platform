# Travel Emergencies — What to Do When You're Far from Home

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 076
**Related Topics:** [topic-075](/education/topic-075), [topic-080](/education/topic-080), [topic-029](/education/topic-029)

---

## Hello from Mira

Travelling with Type 1 Diabetes is absolutely possible — and you should never let T1D stop you from exploring the world, visiting family, or going on adventures. People with T1D climb mountains, cross continents, and live full, adventurous lives every day.

But travel does add layers of complexity to diabetes management. Time zones shift. Routines break. Access to familiar healthcare disappears. The food is different. The climate is different. And if something goes wrong — a severe hypo, a pump failure, lost supplies — you need to know how to handle it, even when you are thousands of kilometres from your usual care team.

This topic is about being prepared so that travel remains a joy, not a source of fear.

## Before you leave — the travel preparation that matters most

### Build your travel diabetes kit

Your travel kit should be treated as the most important luggage you carry. Pack it in your **hand luggage** (carry-on) — never in checked bags, which can be lost, delayed, or exposed to extreme temperatures in cargo holds.

Your travel kit should include:

- **Insulin** — at least double what you expect to need for the trip. Insulin is your lifeline; running out abroad is a serious emergency.
- **Insulin delivery supplies** — syringes or pen needles, pump infusion sets, pump reservoirs, and batteries or chargers. Bring spares for everything.
- **Blood glucose meter, test strips, and lancets** — even if you use a CGM, always carry a manual backup meter.
- **CGM sensors and transmitters** — enough for the trip plus extras in case of sensor failures.
- **Ketone testing supplies** — blood ketone meter and strips.
- **Fast-acting glucose** — glucose tablets, glucose gel, or similar. Carry these on your person at all times, not just in your main bag.
- **Glucagon** — check the expiry date before you leave. Ensure your travel companion knows how to use it.
- **A letter from your diabetes team** — on hospital or clinic letterhead, stating that you have T1D and listing your insulin types, delivery devices, and the medical necessity of carrying needles, syringes, and liquid medications through airport security. This is essential for international travel.
- **Prescriptions** — carry copies of your prescriptions with generic (international) drug names, not just brand names, as brand names vary between countries.
- **Medical identification** — a medical ID bracelet, necklace, or card that clearly states Type 1 Diabetes and lists emergency contacts.

### Arrange travel insurance

Travel insurance that explicitly covers pre-existing conditions including Type 1 Diabetes is non-negotiable. Read the policy carefully. Ensure it covers emergency medical treatment, hospital admission, medical evacuation, and repatriation. Some policies exclude diabetes-related emergencies unless specifically declared — check this before you travel.

### Research your destination

Before you leave, know:
- Where the nearest hospital or emergency department is at your destination
- Whether insulin is available over the counter in that country (it varies)
- The local emergency services number (not everywhere uses 999 or 911)
- Whether your insulin brand is available locally, or what the equivalent is
- Any extreme climate considerations — insulin degrades in heat above 30 degrees Celsius and can freeze in very cold conditions

## Time zone changes and insulin adjustments

Crossing time zones is one of the trickiest aspects of travel with T1D, particularly for people on long-acting basal insulin or insulin pumps.

**The core issue:** Your insulin regimen is timed to your body's 24-hour cycle. When you cross multiple time zones, the day becomes artificially shorter (travelling east) or longer (travelling west). This means the gap between basal insulin doses may shrink or stretch.

**For pump users:** The simplest approach is to change the pump clock to local time on arrival. The pump will continue to deliver basal rates as programmed, now aligned to the new time zone. Discuss this with your diabetes team before travel.

**For people on long-acting injections:** Adjustments to timing and sometimes dose may be needed. Your diabetes team should provide a specific plan based on your itinerary and the number of time zones crossed. As a general principle:
- Travelling **east** (shorter day): the gap between doses shortens; there may be a risk of insulin stacking.
- Travelling **west** (longer day): the gap between doses lengthens; there may be a period with less basal coverage.

GluMira does not provide specific dosing instructions — please discuss your travel plan with your diabetes care team well before departure.

## Common travel emergencies and what to do

### Lost or damaged insulin

If your insulin is lost, damaged, or exposed to extreme temperatures:
- **Do not use insulin that has been frozen or exposed to temperatures above 30 degrees Celsius for extended periods** — it may have lost effectiveness.
- Go to a local pharmacy or hospital. In many countries, insulin can be obtained without a prescription in an emergency. Your doctor's letter and prescriptions with generic drug names will help.
- Contact your travel insurance provider — they may be able to arrange emergency supply or direct you to a suitable facility.
- In the meantime, ration any remaining insulin carefully and monitor blood glucose and ketones closely.

### Pump failure

- **Always carry a backup insulin pen or syringes** when travelling with a pump. This is not optional — it is essential.
- If your pump fails, switch to manual injections using the plan your diabetes team prepared before travel.
- Contact your pump manufacturer's emergency support line — most have 24-hour international helplines.

### Severe hypoglycaemia abroad

- Ensure your travel companion knows how to administer glucagon and when to call emergency services.
- Carry fast-acting glucose on your person at all times — not in a separate bag.
- Know the local emergency number. Programme it into your phone before you arrive.
- If you need emergency medical care, show your medical ID and doctor's letter. If there is a language barrier, a translation card or app can help communicate "I have Type 1 Diabetes" and "I need sugar" in the local language.

### Gastroenteritis while travelling

- This is the SUBJ-NAM-001 Scenario (Topic 073) in an unfamiliar setting. Follow the same principles: small frequent sips, monitor ketones relentlessly, do not stop insulin.
- Traveller's diarrhoea is common in many destinations. Carry oral rehydration sachets in your travel kit.
- If vomiting persists for more than 2 hours with rising ketones, seek local medical attention immediately. Do not wait for it to pass.

### Illness or infection abroad

- Fever and infection increase insulin needs (see Topic 074). Follow your sick-day plan.
- If you need antibiotics or other medications abroad, inform the prescribing doctor that you have T1D, as some medications can affect blood glucose.

## During travel — practical tips

- **On flights:** Keep insulin and supplies in your carry-on. Cabin temperatures are safe for insulin; cargo holds are not. Drink water regularly — flying is dehydrating, and dehydration affects BG.
- **In hot climates:** Use an insulated cooling case for insulin. Never leave insulin in a hot car, on a sunny windowsill, or in direct sunlight.
- **In cold climates:** Keep insulin close to your body to prevent freezing. A pocket under your jacket works well.
- **Activity changes:** Holidays often involve more physical activity than usual (walking, swimming, hiking) or less (long car journeys, lounging). Both affect BG. Monitor more frequently when your routine changes.
- **Food:** Unfamiliar foods make carb counting harder. When unsure, check BG more frequently and be conservative. Enjoy local cuisine — just stay attentive.

## Emergency contacts — have them ready

Before you travel, prepare a card or phone note with:
- Your diabetes team's contact details (including international dialling code)
- Your travel insurance emergency helpline
- The local emergency services number at your destination
- Your insulin types and doses (using generic drug names)
- Your blood type (if known)
- Emergency contacts at home

Share this information with your travel companion and keep a copy in your luggage as well as on your person.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Pack double the supplies you need, all in carry-on luggage** — lost checked bags or extreme cargo temperatures can destroy insulin and supplies.
2. **Carry a doctor's letter with generic drug names** — this is essential for airport security, for obtaining insulin abroad, and for emergency medical care in another language.
3. **Discuss time zone adjustments with your diabetes team before travel** — crossing time zones affects insulin timing, and a plan should be made in advance.
4. **Always carry a backup injection method if using a pump** — pump failures abroad without a backup pen or syringe is a medical emergency.
5. **Know the local emergency number and nearest hospital at every destination** — programme them into your phone before arrival.
