# Explaining T1D to Classmates — Age-Appropriate Scripts

**Topic ID:** 030
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Child (with parent support)
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira — and this one is for you, the person who actually lives with Type 1 diabetes every single day. Whether you were diagnosed recently or have had T1D for a while, there will be times when classmates, friends, or even teachers ask you questions about it. That is completely normal. People are curious, and curiosity is not a bad thing — it just means they want to understand.

The tricky part is knowing what to say. So let's practise together. I have put together some scripts — things you can actually say out loud — that explain your diabetes in a way that is honest, simple, and makes you feel good about yourself. Because here is the truth: having T1D is not something to be embarrassed about. It is just one part of who you are, like having brown eyes or being good at drawing.

## The Big Idea: My Pancreas Stopped Working

Every explanation starts with the same basic idea. Your pancreas — a small organ inside your body — stopped making something called insulin. Insulin is like a key that unlocks your cells so they can use sugar from food for energy. Without insulin, the sugar stays in your blood and your cells get hungry.

That is not your fault. It is not because of anything you ate, or anything your parents did, or anything anyone did. It just happened. Your immune system — the part of your body that fights germs — made a mistake and attacked the insulin-making cells in your pancreas. Doctors do not know exactly why this happens, but they do know it is not caused by eating too many sweets.

Now, instead of your pancreas making insulin, you get insulin from the outside — through a pump on your body or through injections. You also wear a sensor that tells you (and your mum, dad, or carer) how much sugar is in your blood, so you can keep things balanced.

That is it. That is the whole story. Everything else is detail.

## Scripts for Ages 5–6

At this age, keep it very short. Your classmates have short attention spans, and a simple sentence is usually enough.

**When someone asks "What is that on your arm/tummy?"**
> "It is a sensor. It checks my blood sugar. It does not hurt — it just sticks on like a plaster."

**When someone asks "Why do you have to eat a snack in class?"**
> "My body needs a snack sometimes to keep my energy right. The teacher knows about it."

**When someone asks "What is wrong with you?"**
> "Nothing is wrong with me! I have something called diabetes. It means my body needs a bit of extra help with sugar, so I wear this sensor and sometimes I have a special snack."

**When someone says "You can't eat that, you have diabetes"**
> "Actually, I can eat the same things as you. I just have to count the carbs first."

**Parent tip:** At this age, you may want to speak to the teacher about doing a short "show and tell" about diabetes. Many infant classrooms do this well — the child shows their pump or CGM, explains it simply, and the mystery is gone. Children at this age accept differences easily once they understand what they are seeing.

## Scripts for Ages 7–8

By this age, children can handle a bit more detail and may ask more specific questions.

**The basic explanation:**
> "I have Type 1 diabetes. My pancreas — that is an organ inside my body — stopped making insulin. Insulin helps my body use energy from food. So I get my insulin from this pump [or: from injections], and I wear this sensor to check my blood sugar levels. It is not contagious — you can't catch it. And it was not caused by eating sugar."

**When someone asks "Does it hurt?"**
> "The sensor goes on every 10–14 days, and it is a tiny pinch. After that, I don't even feel it. The pump site changes every few days. It is not fun, but it is not terrible either. I am used to it."

**When someone asks "Can you still play/do sports/come to the party?"**
> "Yes! I can do everything you can do. I just check my blood sugar first and sometimes have a snack before I run around. No big deal."

**When someone asks "Will you have it forever?"**
> "Right now, yes. But scientists are working on ways to fix it, and there might be a cure one day. For now, I manage it really well."

**When someone says something unkind or teasing:**
> "That is not very kind. Diabetes is a real medical condition, and I did not choose it. I would rather you ask me a question than make a joke about it."

**Parent tip:** At this age, some children prefer to explain T1D privately to their close friends rather than to the whole class. Respect your child's preference. The goal is for them to feel in control of their own story.

## Scripts for Ages 9–10

Older primary school children can be wonderfully empathetic and curious. They can also be more aware of social dynamics, which means your child may feel more self-conscious. Equip them with confident, knowledgeable responses.

**The detailed explanation (for a class presentation or a curious friend):**
> "I have Type 1 diabetes, which is an autoimmune condition. That means my immune system — the thing that fights off colds and germs — accidentally destroyed the cells in my pancreas that make insulin. Insulin is a hormone that lets your body turn food into energy. Without it, sugar builds up in my blood, which is dangerous. So I get insulin through this pump [or: through injections I give myself], and this sensor on my arm reads my blood sugar every few minutes and sends it to my phone [or: reader]. I count the carbohydrates in my food so I know how much insulin I need. It is a lot to manage, but I have been doing it for [X years] and I am good at it."

**When someone confuses Type 1 and Type 2:**
> "There are different types of diabetes. Mine is Type 1, which is autoimmune — my body attacked itself. It has nothing to do with diet or weight or lifestyle. Type 2 is different — that is more about how the body uses insulin and is more common in adults. They are both real, but they are not the same thing."

**When someone asks "Can you eat that?"**
> "I can eat anything. Seriously, anything. I just need to know how many grams of carbohydrate are in it so I can take the right amount of insulin. It is like doing maths before every meal."

**When someone is being a good friend and wants to help:**
> "Thank you for asking! The most helpful thing is to know that if I ever say I feel low or shaky, I need a juice box or glucose tablets really fast. They are in my bag. You do not need to do anything else — just help me get to my supplies or tell a teacher."

**When your child does not want to talk about it:**
> "I have a medical condition that I manage. I would rather not go into the details right now, but thanks for asking."

That is a perfectly valid response too. Your child does not owe anyone an explanation. They get to choose when, how, and whether to share.

## Dealing with Persistent Questions

Sometimes classmates ask the same questions repeatedly, or the questions get personal in a way that feels uncomfortable. Here are some strategies:

- **The redirect:** "I have told you about it before — it is just diabetes. Want to play?"
- **The boundary:** "I do not really want to talk about it right now. Can we talk about something else?"
- **The humour (for older children who are comfortable with it):** "My pancreas went on permanent holiday. I am doing its job now. It is fine."
- **The ally request:** "If other people ask, you can tell them for me — I have Type 1 diabetes, it is not contagious, and I can do everything they can do."

Having a trusted friend who can answer questions on your behalf is incredibly helpful. It takes the pressure off and means you are not the only one explaining all the time.

## For Parents: Supporting Your Child's Voice

The most important thing you can do is let your child lead. Ask them: "Do you want to tell your class? Or would you rather I talk to the teacher?" "What words feel right to you?" "Is there anything you do not want people to know?"

Some children are proud diabetes advocates by age 7. Others prefer to be as private as possible. Both are healthy responses. Your role is to equip them with the words and the confidence, and then let them decide how to use them.

If your child is experiencing persistent teasing or bullying related to their diabetes, involve the school. Diabetes-related bullying is a safeguarding and wellbeing issue, not just a playground spat. Schools have a duty to address it.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The core explanation is simple:** "My pancreas stopped making insulin, so I get it from a pump or injections, and I wear a sensor to check my blood sugar."
2. **Tailor the detail to the age group** — a one-sentence answer works for 5-year-olds, while 9–10-year-olds can explain the autoimmune mechanism with pride.
3. **Correct common myths confidently:** T1D is not caused by eating sugar, it is not contagious, and children with T1D can eat anything and do everything their peers can.
4. **Your child gets to choose when and how to share** — they do not owe anyone an explanation, and "I would rather not talk about it" is always a valid response.
5. **Equip a trusted friend** who can answer questions on your child's behalf, reducing the pressure of being the sole explainer.
