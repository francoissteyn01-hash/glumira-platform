# Exercise Physiology and T1D — Why Muscles Eat Glucose

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, and care teams

## Hello from Mira

Exercise is one of the most beneficial things anyone can do for their health — and that includes people living with Type 1 Diabetes. But if you have T1D, you have probably noticed that exercise and glucose levels have a complicated relationship. Sometimes activity sends glucose plummeting. Other times it spikes. And sometimes it does both, one after the other.

This is not random. There is real physiology behind it, and once you understand the mechanisms, the patterns start to make a lot more sense. So let me take you inside the working muscle and explain what is actually happening.

---

## The basics: muscles need fuel

When you move your body — whether you are walking, swimming, sprinting, or lifting weights — your muscles need energy. That energy comes primarily from two sources:

1. **Glucose** — either from the bloodstream or from glycogen (stored glucose) within the muscle itself
2. **Fat** — broken down into fatty acids and used for energy, especially during longer, lower-intensity activity

The balance between these fuel sources depends on the **type, intensity, and duration** of the exercise. This is where things get interesting for people with T1D.

---

## GLUT4 transporters — the exercise shortcut

Normally, glucose needs insulin to enter most cells. Insulin acts like a key, unlocking special channels called **glucose transporters** — specifically **GLUT4** — on the surface of muscle cells. Without insulin, glucose stays locked out in the bloodstream.

But exercise has a remarkable trick. When muscles contract during physical activity, they can **move GLUT4 transporters to the cell surface without needing insulin**. This is an insulin-independent pathway. It means that exercising muscles actively pull glucose out of the bloodstream through their own mechanism.

This is why exercise tends to **lower blood glucose** — your muscles are consuming glucose at an increased rate, and they are doing it through a pathway that does not require insulin. If you have insulin on board at the same time (from a recent injection or pump delivery), the effect is compounded: insulin is opening some doors, and exercise is opening others. The result can be a rapid drop in blood glucose.

This GLUT4 effect does not stop when you stop moving, either. **Muscle glucose uptake can remain elevated for hours after exercise** as the muscles replenish their glycogen stores. This is one reason post-exercise hypoglycaemia — sometimes occurring many hours later, including overnight — is a well-recognised phenomenon in T1D.

---

## Aerobic exercise — the steady glucose consumer

Aerobic exercise includes activities like walking, jogging, cycling, swimming at a moderate pace, and dancing. These activities are sustained, rhythmic, and rely heavily on oxygen to generate energy.

During aerobic exercise:

- Muscles primarily use a mix of glucose and fat for fuel
- As intensity increases, the proportion of glucose used increases
- Blood glucose typically **falls** during and after the activity
- The longer the session, the greater the cumulative glucose consumption
- Glycogen stores in both muscles and the liver become progressively depleted

For people with T1D, aerobic exercise is most commonly associated with a **drop in blood glucose** during and after the session. The risk of hypoglycaemia is highest during prolonged moderate-intensity activity and in the hours following exercise.

---

## Anaerobic exercise — the unexpected spiker

Anaerobic exercise includes activities like sprinting, heavy weightlifting, high-intensity interval training (HIIT), and competitive sports that involve short bursts of maximal effort.

During high-intensity anaerobic activity, something different happens:

- The body perceives intense exertion as a form of physical stress
- The adrenal glands release **adrenaline (epinephrine)** and the liver releases **cortisol**
- These counter-regulatory hormones signal the liver to **dump stored glucose** (glycogen) into the bloodstream
- The glucose release from the liver can **exceed** what the muscles are consuming
- The result: blood glucose **rises** during or immediately after the activity

This can be confusing. You just exercised intensely, and your glucose went up instead of down. But it makes physiological sense — the body is mounting a stress response and flooding the bloodstream with fuel for what it perceives as a "fight or flight" situation.

The rise is usually temporary. In the hours following anaerobic exercise, glucose often drops as the stress hormones clear and the muscles replenish their glycogen using the GLUT4 pathway. This means anaerobic exercise can cause a **delayed hypoglycaemia risk**, just like aerobic exercise — it just takes a more circuitous route to get there.

---

## Mixed activity — the real-world picture

Most real-world exercise is not purely aerobic or purely anaerobic. A football match involves jogging (aerobic) punctuated by sprints (anaerobic). A hike might be steady walking with steep climbs. A gym session might combine resistance training with cardio intervals.

Mixed activity produces **mixed glucose responses** — and this is partly why exercise can feel so unpredictable with T1D. Understanding the aerobic-anaerobic spectrum helps you anticipate the general direction, even if the exact magnitude is hard to predict.

Some general patterns observed in research and clinical practice:

- **Steady moderate activity** (cycling, swimming laps, brisk walking): glucose tends to fall
- **High-intensity intervals or competitive sport**: glucose may rise initially, then fall later
- **Resistance training** (weightlifting): often causes a transient rise during the session, followed by improved insulin sensitivity for hours afterward
- **Morning exercise** may produce different glucose responses than evening exercise, partly due to cortisol levels and circadian insulin sensitivity

---

## Post-exercise hypoglycaemia — the delayed risk

One of the most important concepts for anyone with T1D to understand is that the glucose-lowering effect of exercise does not end when the activity stops. As muscles replenish glycogen stores, they continue to draw glucose from the bloodstream for **up to 24 hours or more** after significant activity.

This is why **nocturnal hypoglycaemia** following an active day is a well-documented risk. Studies referenced by ISPAD highlight this as a particular concern in children and adolescents, who may be active during the day and experience low glucose during sleep.

CGM technology (explored in [topic-093](/education/topic-093)) has been invaluable in revealing these post-exercise patterns that finger-prick testing alone might miss.

## Why understanding the physiology matters

Knowing what is happening inside your muscles during exercise does not give you a formula for perfect glucose management — the variables are simply too numerous. But it gives you a **framework for understanding**. When you see glucose drop during a long bike ride, you know why. When it spikes during a sprint session, you know why. When it dips overnight after a tournament, you know why.

That understanding reduces the anxiety and frustration that unpredictable glucose responses can cause. It also helps you have more productive conversations with your care team about exercise-related strategies.

Exercise is profoundly beneficial for cardiovascular health, mental wellbeing, bone density, and overall quality of life. T1D should never be a reason to avoid it — it simply requires more thought and preparation.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **During exercise, muscles can absorb glucose without insulin** through GLUT4 transporters activated by muscle contraction — an insulin-independent pathway that can cause rapid blood glucose drops.
2. **Aerobic exercise (moderate, sustained) typically lowers blood glucose** during and after activity, as muscles steadily consume glucose and deplete glycogen stores.
3. **Anaerobic exercise (high-intensity, short bursts) can initially raise blood glucose** due to adrenaline and cortisol triggering liver glucose release — but often leads to delayed drops as stress hormones clear.
4. **Post-exercise hypoglycaemia can occur up to 24 hours after activity** as muscles replenish glycogen, making overnight lows a particular concern after active days.
5. **Different sports produce different glucose responses**, and real-world activity is usually a mix of aerobic and anaerobic work. Understanding the spectrum helps you anticipate patterns, even when exact predictions are difficult.
