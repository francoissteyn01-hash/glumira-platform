# Contraception and T1D — What Every Teen Girl Needs to Know

**Topic ID:** 48
**Age Range:** 15–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Reproductive Health
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17 / NICE CG30 / FSRH Guidelines

---

## A Conversation That Should Happen Sooner

Contraception is a topic that affects most teenagers at some point, whether they are sexually active now or thinking about it in the future. For teen girls with Type 1 Diabetes, there are specific considerations that go beyond what your friends without diabetes need to think about. These are not barriers — they are information.

The reality is that unplanned pregnancy in someone with T1D carries genuine medical risks that are significantly higher than in the general population. This is not about judgement. It is about biology. Understanding your contraceptive options and how they interact with your diabetes puts you in control of your own health decisions.

## Why This Matters More for You

Here is the clinical reality: pregnancy in T1D requires extremely tight blood glucose control — ideally starting several months before conception. An unplanned pregnancy, where BG control may not be optimised, carries increased risks for both the mother and the baby. These include higher rates of congenital abnormalities, pre-eclampsia, macrosomia (large birth weight), and pregnancy loss.

This does not mean you cannot have children. Many people with T1D have healthy pregnancies and healthy babies. But the outcomes are significantly better when pregnancy is planned, when HbA1c is optimised before conception, and when a specialist team is involved from the start.

The logical conclusion: reliable contraception is genuinely more important for you than for your peers without diabetes. Not more morally important — more medically important. An accidental pregnancy at 16 is complicated for anyone. With T1D, it is also medically high-risk.

## How Different Contraceptive Methods Affect Blood Glucose

Not all contraceptive methods are equal when it comes to T1D. Here is what the evidence says:

**Combined hormonal methods (combined pill, patch, vaginal ring):** These contain oestrogen and progestogen. Oestrogen can affect insulin sensitivity, and some people notice changes in their blood glucose patterns when starting or stopping combined methods. The effect varies — some people see slightly higher readings, some see no change. The bigger concern with combined methods in T1D is the cardiovascular risk: oestrogen-containing contraception slightly increases the risk of blood clots, and T1D already carries elevated cardiovascular risk. For most young, healthy teens with T1D who do not smoke and do not have complications, the combined pill is generally considered safe by guidelines. But it needs to be discussed with your care team, who can assess your individual risk profile.

**Progestogen-only methods:** These include the progestogen-only pill (mini-pill), the contraceptive implant, the injection (Depo-Provera), and the hormonal IUS (such as the Mirena). Progestogen-only methods do not carry the same blood clot risk as combined methods, which is an advantage for people with T1D.

- The **progestogen-only pill** has minimal effect on blood glucose for most people but must be taken at very consistent times (depending on the type), which can be a challenge if your routine is variable.
- The **contraceptive implant** is a small rod inserted in your upper arm that works for three years. It has a very low failure rate because it does not depend on you remembering to take anything. Some people report BG fluctuations when it is first inserted, which usually settle.
- The **Depo-Provera injection** (given every 12–13 weeks) is effective but has been associated with some weight gain in some users, which can affect insulin sensitivity. It also has a slower return to fertility after stopping.
- The **hormonal IUS** (Mirena or similar) releases a low dose of progestogen directly into the uterus. It has minimal systemic hormonal effects and very little impact on blood glucose. It is considered one of the best options for long-term contraception in people with T1D by many guidelines.

**Copper IUD (non-hormonal):** The copper IUD contains no hormones and therefore has no direct effect on blood glucose or insulin sensitivity. It is highly effective, lasts 5–10 years, and is completely reversible. The main downsides are heavier or more painful periods in some users. For BG management, it is neutral, which is a significant advantage.

**Barrier methods (condoms, diaphragm):** No hormonal effects, no BG impact. However, their effectiveness depends entirely on correct and consistent use, and failure rates are higher than hormonal or intrauterine methods. Condoms also provide protection against sexually transmitted infections — no other method on this list does. For STI protection, condoms should be used regardless of what other contraception you are on.

**Emergency contraception:** Available as a pill (levonorgestrel or ulipristal acetate) or as a copper IUD fitted within five days. Emergency contraception is safe for people with T1D. It is not a substitute for regular contraception, but you should know it exists and how to access it if you need it.

## The Long-Acting Methods — Why They Are Worth Knowing About

Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) — the implant, IUS, and IUD — are recommended by most clinical guidelines as first-line options for teenagers, with or without T1D. The reasons are practical: they have the lowest failure rates because they do not depend on daily adherence, and they are reversible when you want to stop.

For teens with T1D specifically, the fact that you already have a demanding daily medical routine makes "set and forget" methods particularly practical. One less thing to remember every day is not trivial when you are already managing insulin, monitoring, and carb counting.

This is not to pressure you into any particular method. Your body, your choice, always. But understanding which options are available and how they interact with your diabetes lets you make that choice from a position of knowledge.

## Discussing Contraception with Your Care Team

This is a conversation that should happen with your diabetes team or your GP, ideally before you need it rather than after. Some teens find this awkward. That is completely understandable. But doctors and nurses discuss contraception regularly — it is a normal part of healthcare, and yours have heard it all before.

Points to raise:

- Your current insulin regimen and how well your BG is controlled
- Any diabetes-related complications (though these are rare in teenagers)
- Whether you smoke (this affects which methods are safe)
- Your preferences — daily pill, long-acting method, non-hormonal, etc.
- Any concerns about weight, periods, or side effects

Your diabetes team and your sexual health provider should ideally communicate with each other. If they are not the same people, make sure each knows what the other has recommended.

In many countries, teens can access contraception confidentially from a specific age (in the UK, there is no minimum age for confidential contraceptive advice; in the US, it varies by state). You do not necessarily need parental consent, though having a trusted adult to talk to can be helpful.

## Periods, Hormones, and Blood Glucose

Even without contraception, your menstrual cycle affects your blood glucose. Many people with T1D notice patterns: BG tends to be higher in the days before a period (the luteal phase) due to progesterone's effect on insulin sensitivity, and drops when the period starts. Tracking your cycle alongside your BG data can reveal patterns that help you anticipate and manage these fluctuations.

Hormonal contraception can alter or eliminate these cyclical patterns, which for some people actually makes BG management easier and more predictable. For others, it introduces new patterns that take time to learn. Neither outcome is wrong — it is about finding what works for your body.

## The Bottom Line

Contraception is a health decision. For you, it is a health decision that intersects directly with your diabetes management. The most important things are: understand your options, talk to a healthcare professional who knows your diabetes, choose a method that works for your life and your body, and use it consistently.

And the single most important message: unplanned pregnancy in T1D carries real medical risks. Planning ahead is not pessimistic — it is exactly the kind of self-advocacy that good diabetes management is built on.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Unplanned pregnancy in T1D carries significantly higher medical risks** — reliable contraception is medically important, not just a lifestyle choice.
2. **Different contraceptive methods interact with diabetes differently** — hormonal methods can affect insulin sensitivity, while non-hormonal options (copper IUD, condoms) have no BG impact.
3. **Long-acting reversible methods (implant, IUS, IUD) have the lowest failure rates** and reduce the daily management burden, making them particularly practical for teens already managing T1D.
4. **Discuss contraception with your diabetes team or GP** — they can assess which options are safest based on your individual health profile, cardiovascular risk, and preferences.
5. **Condoms are the only method that protects against STIs** — use them alongside other contraception for complete protection.
