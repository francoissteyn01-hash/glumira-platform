# Autoimmune Cluster — Thyroid, Coeliac, T1D Connections

## Hello from Mira

When you or your child was diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes, you learned that it is an autoimmune condition — the immune system attacked the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreas. What you may not have been told, at least not right away, is that having one autoimmune condition **increases the likelihood of developing others**.

This is not meant to frighten you. Most people with T1D will not develop another autoimmune condition. But understanding the connection — and knowing what to watch for — means you and your care team can catch things early, when they are most manageable. Knowledge, in this case, truly is power.

---

## Why autoimmune conditions cluster

The immune system is extraordinarily complex. In autoimmune conditions, it makes errors — mistaking the body's own healthy cells for foreign invaders and attacking them. In T1D, the target is the pancreatic beta cells. But the underlying tendency toward immune dysregulation is often not limited to one organ.

This clustering is partly **genetic**. Specific genes in the **HLA (human leukocyte antigen) region** on chromosome 6 are strongly associated with T1D. Many of these same genes — particularly HLA-DR3 and HLA-DR4 — are also associated with other autoimmune conditions. If you carry the genetic variants that predisposed you to T1D, you may also carry variants that increase susceptibility to thyroid autoimmunity, coeliac disease, and other conditions.

It is not just genetics, though. Environmental factors, immune regulation pathways, and even gut microbiome composition are all being researched as contributors to autoimmune clustering. The field is evolving rapidly, but the clinical reality is clear: **people with T1D are screened for certain other autoimmune conditions because the association is well established.**

The formal term for having multiple autoimmune conditions is **autoimmune polyendocrine syndrome (APS)**. There are several types, but the most relevant to T1D is **APS type 2** (also called Schmidt syndrome in its classic form), which commonly involves T1D, autoimmune thyroid disease, and sometimes adrenal insufficiency.

## Autoimmune thyroid disease

The **thyroid gland** is a butterfly-shaped gland in the front of your neck. It produces hormones (T3 and T4) that regulate metabolism, energy, heart rate, body temperature, and growth.

Autoimmune thyroid disease is the **most common autoimmune condition associated with T1D**. Studies referenced by ISPAD indicate that thyroid autoantibodies are found in approximately **15-30% of people with T1D**, and clinical thyroid disease develops in a significant proportion of those.

There are two main forms:

### Hashimoto's thyroiditis (hypothyroidism)

The immune system attacks the thyroid, gradually reducing its ability to produce hormones. This leads to an **underactive thyroid** (hypothyroidism). Symptoms can be subtle and develop slowly:

- Fatigue and low energy
- Weight gain or difficulty losing weight
- Feeling cold
- Dry skin and hair
- Constipation
- In children: slowed growth velocity

Hypothyroidism can also affect glucose management. Low thyroid hormone levels can alter insulin sensitivity, and untreated hypothyroidism may make glucose patterns more erratic.

### Graves' disease (hyperthyroidism)

Less common than Hashimoto's, Graves' disease causes the thyroid to become **overactive**, producing excess hormones. Symptoms include:

- Weight loss despite normal or increased appetite
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Anxiety and irritability
- Tremor
- Heat intolerance
- In children: accelerated growth, restlessness

Hyperthyroidism can significantly increase insulin resistance and cause glucose levels to run higher than usual.

### Screening

ISPAD recommends **thyroid function screening at the time of T1D diagnosis and regularly thereafter** — typically every one to two years, or sooner if symptoms develop. Screening usually involves measuring TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) and thyroid autoantibodies (anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin). Thyroid conditions are very treatable once identified.

## Coeliac disease

Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system reacts to **gluten** — a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye. This reaction damages the lining of the small intestine, specifically the tiny finger-like projections called **villi** that are responsible for nutrient absorption.

The association with T1D is significant. Research indicates that **approximately 5-10% of people with T1D also have coeliac disease**, compared to roughly 1% of the general population. The shared genetic link is strong — both conditions are associated with HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8.

### Symptoms of coeliac disease

Coeliac disease is sometimes called a "great mimicker" because its symptoms are highly variable:

- Abdominal pain, bloating, and diarrhoea
- Unexplained weight loss or poor weight gain in children
- Iron deficiency anaemia
- Fatigue
- In children: delayed puberty, short stature, irritability

Crucially, some people with coeliac disease have **no obvious gastrointestinal symptoms** — the condition is detected only through screening. This is called **silent coeliac disease**, and it is one reason routine screening is so important.

Undiagnosed coeliac disease can affect glucose management by altering nutrient absorption. If the gut is not absorbing carbohydrates predictably, glucose responses to food become less predictable.

### Screening

ISPAD recommends **screening for coeliac disease at the time of T1D diagnosis and periodically thereafter** — typically every two to three years in the early years after diagnosis, with ongoing screening if symptoms develop. The standard screening test measures **tissue transglutaminase (tTG) IgA antibodies**. If positive, a confirmatory intestinal biopsy is usually performed, though evolving guidelines in some regions now allow diagnosis without biopsy under specific conditions.

If coeliac disease is confirmed, the treatment is a **strict lifelong gluten-free diet**. This can be challenging on top of T1D management — particularly carbohydrate counting — but with support from a dietitian experienced in both conditions, it is entirely manageable.

## Other associated autoimmune conditions

While thyroid disease and coeliac disease are the most common autoimmune associations with T1D, others include:

- **Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency)** — rare but serious; the immune system attacks the adrenal glands, reducing cortisol and aldosterone production. Symptoms include fatigue, low blood pressure, salt cravings, and unexplained hypoglycaemia.
- **Vitiligo** — loss of skin pigmentation in patches due to destruction of melanocytes. It is generally not medically dangerous but can be cosmetically and psychologically significant.
- **Pernicious anaemia** — autoimmune destruction of cells in the stomach that produce intrinsic factor, leading to vitamin B12 deficiency.
- **Alopecia areata** — autoimmune hair loss, which can range from small patches to more extensive involvement.

These conditions are less common than thyroid disease and coeliac disease, but awareness of them is worthwhile.

## What to watch for

You do not need to become hypervigilant about every symptom. But being aware of certain patterns can help you and your care team intervene early:

- Unexplained changes in energy levels, weight, or growth velocity
- New digestive symptoms — bloating, pain, changes in bowel habits
- Skin changes — including patches of depigmentation or new rashes
- Unexplained changes in glucose patterns that persist despite no changes in diet, activity, or insulin
- Unusual or recurrent fatigue beyond what is expected

If you notice any of these, mention them at your next clinic visit — or sooner if they are significant. Screening blood tests are simple and can provide answers quickly.

## Five key takeaways

1. **People with T1D have an increased risk of developing other autoimmune conditions**, particularly autoimmune thyroid disease and coeliac disease, due to shared genetic susceptibility in the HLA gene region.
2. **Autoimmune thyroid disease affects 15-30% of people with T1D** by antibody prevalence. Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism can affect glucose management and overall wellbeing.
3. **Coeliac disease occurs in approximately 5-10% of people with T1D** — up to ten times the general population rate — and can be present without obvious gastrointestinal symptoms (silent coeliac disease).
4. **Regular screening is recommended by ISPAD and the ADA** — thyroid function every one to two years and coeliac antibodies periodically, especially in the early years after T1D diagnosis.
5. **Early detection leads to effective treatment.** Thyroid conditions are managed with medication, coeliac disease with a gluten-free diet. Both are very treatable when identified, and treatment often improves glucose management as well.
