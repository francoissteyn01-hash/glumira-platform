# Surgery Preparation — Fasting Protocols with Active Insulin

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 075

## Hello from Mira

Surgery is stressful for anyone. When you or your child also lives with Type 1 Diabetes, there is an extra layer of complexity that must be planned for carefully. The good news is that with proper preparation and communication between your diabetes team and the surgical team, surgery can be managed safely and smoothly.

The key word is **preparation**. Surgery with T1D should never be a last-minute scramble. The more planning that happens in advance, the safer the experience will be. This topic will help you understand what needs to happen, what to ask, and how to work with your medical teams to keep blood glucose stable through the perioperative period.

---

## Why surgery is complicated with T1D

Surgery introduces several challenges to diabetes management simultaneously:

**Fasting requirements.** Most surgical procedures require a period of fasting beforehand — typically no food for 6 hours and no clear fluids for 2 hours before anaesthesia (though this varies by hospital and procedure). Fasting while insulin is active in the body creates a risk of hypoglycaemia, similar to the dynamics described in The Gastroenteritis Crisis Protocol (Topic 073).

**Stress response.** Surgery itself triggers a significant counter-regulatory hormone response — cortisol, adrenaline, glucagon, and growth hormone all rise. This drives blood glucose up, often dramatically, even without food intake. The physiological stress of surgery can increase insulin requirements substantially.

**Anaesthesia.** General anaesthesia masks the symptoms of hypoglycaemia. The patient cannot feel or report shakiness, sweating, or confusion while under anaesthesia. This means blood glucose must be monitored by the medical team throughout the procedure.

**Recovery.** Post-surgical nausea, pain, and reduced appetite can make it difficult to eat normally, while the stress response and inflammation continue to push glucose up. Pain medications can also affect blood glucose readings unpredictably.

## The conversation with your diabetes team — start early

The moment surgery is planned — whether it is elective or semi-urgent — contact your diabetes care team. Do not wait for the surgical team to raise the topic of diabetes. In many cases, the surgical and anaesthetic teams will have general protocols for diabetes, but your diabetes team knows **your** specific insulin regimen, sensitivity factors, and patterns.

Key points to discuss with your diabetes team:

- **When to adjust insulin** in the days and hours leading up to surgery
- **Basal insulin management** — whether to reduce, maintain, or adjust long-acting insulin the night before; or how to manage pump basal rates
- **What to do about bolus insulin** — when the last meal bolus should be given relative to the fasting window
- **Target blood glucose range** during the perioperative period — ISPAD and ADA recommend maintaining glucose between 6–10 mmol/L (108–180 mg/dL) during surgery, though your team may have specific targets
- **A written plan** that can be handed to the surgical and anaesthetic team

## The conversation with the anaesthetist — do not skip this

Before any procedure requiring anaesthesia, you should have a direct conversation with the anaesthetist. This is not optional — it is essential. The anaesthetist is responsible for monitoring your vital signs during surgery, and they need to know:

- That you have Type 1 Diabetes (ensure this is clearly documented and not confused with Type 2)
- Your current insulin regimen — injections or pump, insulin types, typical doses
- Whether you use a CGM, and whether it can remain in place during surgery (some can, depending on the surgical site and equipment used)
- Your recent blood glucose patterns and any episodes of severe hypoglycaemia or DKA
- Any other relevant conditions

**Ask the anaesthetist:**
- How blood glucose will be monitored during the procedure
- Whether an intravenous insulin and glucose infusion (sometimes called a "sliding scale" or variable-rate insulin infusion) will be used
- What the plan is if blood glucose goes too low or too high during surgery
- Whether you can keep your insulin pump running during the procedure, or whether it needs to be removed (in which case, an alternative insulin delivery plan must be in place)
- When you will be able to eat and drink after the procedure, and how insulin will be managed in the recovery period

## Fasting with active insulin — the core risk

The fundamental tension of surgical fasting with T1D is this: **long-acting (basal) insulin continues to work during the fast, and its glucose-lowering effect does not pause just because food intake has stopped.**

For people on multiple daily injections (MDI), the long-acting insulin taken the night before (or that morning, depending on the regimen) will still be active. If the dose is not adjusted appropriately, hypoglycaemia can occur during the fasting period.

For people using insulin pumps, the pump continues to deliver basal insulin. While pump basal rates can be adjusted more precisely than long-acting injections, the pump must not simply be disconnected and forgotten — without any insulin delivery, ketones will begin to rise within hours.

Your diabetes team will advise on the specific adjustments needed. GluMira does not provide dosing instructions, but the principle is this: insulin must continue in some form to prevent DKA, but it must be balanced against the lack of food intake to prevent hypoglycaemia.

## Practical preparation checklist

In the days before surgery:

- **Confirm the plan in writing** with both your diabetes team and the surgical team. Ensure both teams have communicated with each other.
- **Ask to be scheduled first on the operating list** if possible. This minimises the duration of fasting and reduces the window during which blood glucose may become unstable.
- **Pack your diabetes supplies** for the hospital stay — glucose meter, test strips, ketone meter, ketone strips, fast-acting glucose (tablets, gel), a list of your current insulin regimen and doses, your diabetes team's contact details, and your insulin pump supplies if applicable.
- **Label everything.** In a hospital setting, your personal diabetes equipment should be clearly identified as yours.
- **Bring your emergency action plan** (see Topic 080) — a concise document that summarises your diabetes management needs.

On the day of surgery:

- Check blood glucose on waking and before leaving for the hospital.
- Follow the specific insulin adjustment plan provided by your diabetes team.
- Bring fast-acting glucose to the hospital in case of hypoglycaemia during the waiting period.
- Inform the nursing staff on arrival that you have T1D and remind them of the agreed management plan.
- If wearing a CGM, confirm with the surgical team whether it can remain in place.
- If using an insulin pump, confirm whether it stays on or is switched to an IV insulin protocol.

## After surgery — the recovery period

Post-operative diabetes management is just as important as pre-operative preparation.

- Blood glucose should be monitored frequently — every 1–2 hours in the immediate post-operative period.
- Nausea from anaesthesia can prevent eating, creating the same risks as the fasting period. Small sips of sugary fluids may be needed if BG drops.
- Pain and surgical stress may keep BG elevated for 24–72 hours after the procedure.
- Communicate with nursing staff clearly about your insulin needs. In some hospitals, patients with T1D are allowed to self-manage their insulin with the team's agreement — discuss this before the procedure.
- Resume your normal insulin regimen only when you are eating and drinking normally and your diabetes team has confirmed it is appropriate.

---

## When to raise concerns

**Before surgery:** If you feel the surgical team does not have a clear plan for your diabetes management, speak up. Ask to see the anaesthetist. Ask your diabetes team to contact the hospital directly. Do not proceed if you are not confident that blood glucose will be managed safely.

**After surgery:** Contact your diabetes team if BG remains persistently above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) or below 4.0 mmol/L (72 mg/dL) in the post-operative period, if ketones are rising, or if you cannot eat or drink for an extended period.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Contact your diabetes team as soon as surgery is planned** — preparation is the single most important factor in safe surgical outcomes with T1D.
2. **Have a direct conversation with the anaesthetist** — ensure they understand your insulin regimen, your monitoring needs, and have a plan for managing glucose during the procedure.
3. **Insulin must continue during fasting** in some form — stopping insulin entirely leads to ketone buildup and DKA risk; your diabetes team will advise on appropriate adjustments.
4. **Request first-on-the-list scheduling** to minimise fasting duration — and bring your own diabetes supplies and fast-acting glucose to the hospital.
5. **Post-operative monitoring is critical** — pain, stress, nausea, and reduced appetite all affect glucose; stay vigilant for 48–72 hours after surgery.
