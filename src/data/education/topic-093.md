# CGM Basics — What It Tells You and What It Doesn't

**Age Range:** All ages

## Hello from Mira

Continuous glucose monitors — CGMs — have changed the landscape of Type 1 Diabetes management in ways that would have seemed almost magical a generation ago. A small sensor under the skin, a reading every few minutes, trend arrows showing which direction glucose is heading. For many people, it has transformed daily life.

But like any technology, a CGM is most useful when you understand what it is actually measuring, how it works, and where its limitations lie. So let me walk you through the essentials — the things I wish everyone was told on the day they started wearing one.

---

## How a CGM works

A CGM consists of three main components:

1. **A sensor** — a tiny, flexible filament inserted just beneath the skin, typically on the arm, abdomen, or buttock depending on the device and the person's age and preference.
2. **A transmitter** — a small device that sits on top of the sensor and sends data wirelessly.
3. **A receiver or display** — this could be a dedicated reader, a smartphone app, or in some systems, an insulin pump.

The sensor sits in the **interstitial fluid** — the fluid that surrounds the cells just beneath the skin. This is a critical distinction. A CGM does not measure blood glucose directly. It measures **glucose in the interstitial fluid**, and from that, it estimates what the blood glucose is.

Most modern CGMs take a reading approximately every one to five minutes. Over a 24-hour period, this produces a dense, near-continuous stream of data — hundreds of readings — which is where the name "continuous glucose monitor" comes from.

---

## Interstitial fluid vs blood glucose — the lag

Because the CGM measures glucose in interstitial fluid rather than directly in the blood, there is an inherent **physiological lag** between what is happening in your blood and what the sensor detects. Glucose moves from the bloodstream into the interstitial fluid, and this takes time.

The lag is typically estimated at **5 to 15 minutes**, though it can vary depending on the individual, the sensor site, hydration, blood flow, and how rapidly glucose is changing.

What does this mean in practice?

- When glucose is **rising quickly** (for example, after a meal), the CGM reading may be **lower** than the actual blood glucose, because the interstitial fluid has not yet caught up.
- When glucose is **falling quickly** (for example, during a hypo), the CGM reading may be **higher** than the actual blood glucose for the same reason.

This lag is most significant during **rapid changes**. When glucose is relatively stable, the interstitial fluid and blood glucose are closely aligned, and the CGM reading is typically very accurate.

This is why guidelines — including those from ISPAD and the ADA — generally recommend that if a CGM reading does not match how you feel, or if you are making a critical treatment decision during a rapid change, a **confirmatory finger-prick blood glucose test** may be appropriate. Discuss this with your care team.

## Trend arrows — your early warning system

One of the most powerful features of a CGM is something a single finger-prick can never provide: **trend arrows**. These small arrows or indicators show the direction and speed at which glucose is changing.

Common arrow conventions (though they vary by manufacturer) include:

- **Steady** (flat arrow) — glucose is relatively stable
- **Rising slowly** — glucose is increasing at a moderate rate
- **Rising quickly** — glucose is increasing rapidly
- **Falling slowly** — glucose is decreasing at a moderate rate
- **Falling quickly** — glucose is decreasing rapidly

Trend arrows give you **predictive information**. A glucose reading of 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL) with a flat arrow tells a very different story from 5.5 mmol/L with a rapidly falling arrow. In the first case, you might feel reassured. In the second, you might recognise that without action, hypoglycaemia could be approaching.

Learning to read and respond to trend arrows — in partnership with your care team — is one of the most valuable skills in CGM use.

## Calibration — factory vs finger-prick

Older CGM systems required regular **calibration** — you needed to enter finger-prick blood glucose values so the sensor could adjust its readings. This was important because raw sensor signals can drift over time, and calibration helped keep the readings accurate.

Many modern CGMs are **factory-calibrated**, meaning they are pre-calibrated during manufacturing and do not require routine finger-prick calibration. This has been a significant convenience improvement.

However, even with factory-calibrated sensors, there are situations where accuracy may be reduced:

- **The first 12 to 24 hours after insertion** — sensors often need time to "settle in" as the body responds to the insertion, and readings during this period may be less reliable.
- **Compression lows** — if you lie on the sensor during sleep, pressure can restrict blood flow to the area and produce falsely low readings. These "compression lows" typically resolve when you change position.
- **Extreme temperatures** — very hot or very cold environments can affect sensor performance.
- **Certain medications** — some substances, such as high-dose paracetamol (acetaminophen) in older sensor models, could interfere with readings. Newer sensors have largely addressed this, but it is worth checking the manufacturer's guidance for your specific device.

## Sensor accuracy — MARD explained

You may encounter the term **MARD** — Mean Absolute Relative Difference. This is the standard measure of CGM accuracy. It compares the CGM reading to a reference blood glucose value and expresses the average difference as a percentage.

A lower MARD means greater accuracy. Current-generation CGMs typically have a MARD of approximately **9 to 11%**, which means the CGM reading is, on average, within about 9-11% of the true blood glucose. This is remarkably good — but it is not perfect. A blood glucose of 100 mg/dL could produce a CGM reading anywhere from roughly 89 to 111 mg/dL and still be within the expected accuracy range.

Understanding MARD helps set realistic expectations. A CGM is an extraordinarily useful tool, but it is an estimate, not an absolute measurement.

## What a CGM does brilliantly

- Provides a **near-continuous data stream** — hundreds of readings per day compared to four to ten finger-pricks
- Reveals **patterns and trends** that isolated finger-pricks cannot capture
- Offers **trend arrows** that give early warning of rising or falling glucose
- Enables the calculation of **Time in Range**, glycaemic variability, and other modern metrics
- Supports **alarms and alerts** for high and low glucose thresholds, including predictive alerts that warn before glucose reaches a critical level
- Feeds data into **automated insulin delivery systems** when paired with compatible pumps

---

## What a CGM does not do

- It does not measure blood glucose directly — it measures interstitial glucose and estimates blood glucose
- It does not eliminate the need for clinical oversight and regular check-ins with your care team
- It does not make management decisions for you (though automated systems use its data to make some adjustments)
- It cannot account for every variable — sensor accuracy varies, and readings should always be interpreted in context
- It is not a replacement for understanding your own body — how you feel remains important data

---

## A note on information overload

Having hundreds of glucose readings per day is powerful, but it can also be overwhelming — especially in the early days of CGM use. It is common to feel anxious watching the numbers, to react to every alert, or to feel frustrated when the line does not stay flat.

A flat CGM line is not a realistic goal. Glucose fluctuates in everyone — including people without diabetes. The aim is to spend as much time as possible within range, not to achieve perfection. Be kind to yourself as you learn to use this technology, and discuss alert settings and data interpretation strategies with your care team.

## Five key takeaways

1. **CGMs measure glucose in the interstitial fluid, not directly in the blood.** This means there is a physiological lag of approximately 5-15 minutes, which is most noticeable during rapid glucose changes.
2. **Trend arrows are one of the most valuable CGM features**, providing directional information that a single finger-prick reading cannot. Learning to interpret them is a key skill.
3. **Modern factory-calibrated sensors do not require routine finger-prick calibration**, but accuracy can be reduced in the first 24 hours after insertion, during compression events, or in extreme conditions.
4. **CGM accuracy (MARD) is typically within 9-11% of true blood glucose** — excellent but not perfect. If a reading does not match how you feel, a confirmatory finger-prick may be warranted.
5. **A CGM is a tool, not a judge.** It provides data to inform decisions, but interpreting that data — in the context of your life, your body, and your goals — is a skill you develop over time with your care team.
