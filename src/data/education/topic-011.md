# Insulin Dosing for Infants and Toddlers (0.25U Precision)

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 11
**Category:** Insulin management — early years

---

Small children need small doses. That sounds obvious, but when you're standing in your kitchen at 2am trying to draw up a quarter of a unit of insulin for a baby who weighs less than your cat, "small" takes on a whole new meaning.

If you're feeling overwhelmed by the precision required, you're not alone. This is one of the hardest parts of managing Type 1 diabetes in very young children, and the fact that you're here reading about it says everything about how seriously you're taking your child's care.

You're doing great. Let's talk about why these tiny doses matter so much.

## Why Precision Matters More for Little Bodies

An adult with Type 1 might take 5-10 units of rapid-acting insulin with a meal and barely notice if they're half a unit off. For an infant or toddler, half a unit can be the difference between a stable blood sugar and a frightening low.

Here's why:

- **Body weight matters.** Insulin sensitivity is closely tied to body mass. A 10kg toddler is roughly 7-8 times more sensitive to insulin per unit than a 75kg adult.
- **Smaller glycogen stores.** Young children have less stored glucose in their liver to call on if blood sugar drops. This means lows can happen faster and be harder to correct.
- **Unpredictable eating.** Toddlers are famously unreliable eaters. The dose you calculated for a full bowl of porridge becomes a problem when they eat three spoonfuls and launch the rest at the wall.
- **Rapid metabolism.** Small bodies process food and insulin quickly, which means blood sugar can swing more dramatically in shorter windows.

The clinical guidelines from ISPAD (International Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Diabetes) emphasise that insulin dosing in young children requires careful individualisation and that the smallest available dose increments should be used.

## The Tools: Half-Unit Pens and Syringes

Standard insulin pens dose in whole units. For an adult, that's fine. For a baby who might need 0.25 or 0.5 units, it's not nearly precise enough.

**Half-unit insulin pens** dose in 0.5-unit increments. Several manufacturers make these, and they can be genuinely life-changing for families managing T1D in small children. If your diabetes team hasn't mentioned these, it's worth asking.

**Insulin syringes** can offer even finer precision. Some families and clinicians prefer syringes for very small doses because:

- They allow doses as small as 0.25 units with practice
- They can be more accurate at the very low end of dosing
- They give visual confirmation of the dose in the barrel

**Diluted insulin** is another option your care team may discuss. This involves mixing insulin with a special diluent to create a weaker concentration, making it easier to measure very small doses accurately. This should only ever be done under the direct guidance of your diabetes team — never attempt to dilute insulin at home without their instruction.

## The Emotional Weight of Tiny Doses

Let's be honest about something. Drawing up insulin for a baby is terrifying, especially in the early days. The numbers are so small that any error feels catastrophic.

Some things that might help:

- **It's okay to double-check.** Count the dose twice. Have your partner verify it. There is no prize for speed.
- **Mistakes happen.** If you think you've given too much or too little, contact your diabetes team. They've heard it all before and they won't judge you.
- **Write it down.** When you're sleep-deprived (and you will be), a simple log of doses given can prevent the "did I already give that?" panic.
- **Practice with saline.** Some teams will let you practise drawing up tiny doses using saline before you do it with insulin. If this would help you feel more confident, ask.

## Dosing After Meals vs Before Meals

For toddlers and infants, many diabetes teams recommend giving insulin **after** a meal rather than before. The reason is simple: you can't dose accurately for food that might end up on the floor.

This approach means:

- You wait to see what your child actually eats
- You count the carbs they consumed (not what you served)
- You give the insulin based on what went in, not what was offered

This is a common and well-supported strategy for this age group. It doesn't work as neatly for older children and adults, but for little ones whose eating is unpredictable, it can reduce the risk of hypoglycaemia significantly.

Your diabetes team will guide you on whether pre- or post-meal dosing is right for your child. Both approaches have their place.

## When Doses Change (And They Will)

Insulin needs in young children are not static. They change with:

- **Growth spurts** — more growth hormone means more insulin resistance
- **Illness** — sick days can push insulin needs up or down unpredictably
- **Activity levels** — a toddler who discovers running will burn through glucose faster
- **Developmental leaps** — stress hormones associated with developmental changes can affect blood sugar
- **Seasons** — some families notice patterns with weather and activity changes

If you feel like you've just figured out the right dose and then everything changes again, that's not you doing something wrong. That's Type 1 diabetes in a growing child. It's a moving target, and you're meant to keep adjusting.

## Working With Your Diabetes Team

Your paediatric diabetes team is your most important resource. They should be:

- Reviewing doses regularly (not just at quarterly clinic visits)
- Available for advice between appointments when patterns shift
- Willing to explain the reasoning behind dose changes
- Open to discussing different delivery methods (pens, syringes, pumps)

If you're not getting this level of support, it's okay to advocate for it. Your child's needs are complex, and you deserve a team that treats them that way.

**Insulin pumps** are worth mentioning here. For very young children, insulin pump therapy can offer the fine-tuned dosing precision that's so difficult with injections. Pumps can deliver insulin in increments as small as 0.025 units. Many families find that pumps reduce the daily stress of managing tiny doses. This is a conversation to have with your team when you're ready.

## A Word About Guilt

If you've ever given a dose and then spent the next two hours watching the CGM with your heart in your throat, you're in very large company. The weight of being responsible for these calculations — for a child who can't tell you how they feel — is enormous.

You are not a pancreas. You are a parent doing a pancreas's job with far less information and far more love than any organ ever had. Some days the numbers will be beautiful. Some days they won't. Neither of those days defines you.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Young children are significantly more insulin-sensitive than adults**, making precise dosing critical for safety and stable blood sugars.
2. **Half-unit pens, insulin syringes, and diluted insulin** are tools that can improve dosing accuracy — ask your care team what's right for your child.
3. **Post-meal dosing** is a common strategy for unpredictable eaters, allowing you to dose for what was actually consumed.
4. **Insulin needs change frequently** in growing children — regular review with your diabetes team is essential, not a sign of failure.
5. **You don't have to be perfect.** Precision matters, but so does your wellbeing. Double-check, write things down, and ask for help when you need it.
