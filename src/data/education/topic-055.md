# Rapid-Acting vs Short-Acting — Fiasp, NovoRapid, Actrapid Compared

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with diabetes, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 055
**Related Topics:** [topic-056](/education/topic-056), [topic-052](/education/topic-052), [topic-060](/education/topic-060)

---

## Hello from Mira

Not all insulins are created equal. Even among the insulins that work relatively quickly — the ones you might use at mealtimes or for corrections — there are meaningful differences in how fast they start working, when they hit their peak, and how long they continue to act. These differences are not just pharmacological trivia. They directly affect how your body responds after a meal, how IOB is calculated, and how you and your care team make decisions throughout the day.

Let me walk you through the key rapid-acting and short-acting insulins, explain how they differ, and show you why those differences matter more than you might think.

---

## The sprinter, the runner, and the jogger

Think of three athletes lining up for a race. They all run the same course, but they run it differently.

**Fiasp (faster-acting insulin aspart)** is the sprinter. It gets off the starting blocks incredibly quickly, reaches its top speed early, and finishes the race a little sooner. It is the fastest of the three.

**NovoRapid (insulin aspart)** — also known as Novolog in some regions — is the runner. Fast, reliable, well-understood. It starts a little after the sprinter but maintains a strong, predictable pace. It has been the workhorse of mealtime insulin therapy for over two decades.

**Actrapid (soluble human insulin)** is the jogger. It starts more slowly, takes longer to peak, and keeps going for longer. It was the standard mealtime insulin before the rapid-acting analogues arrived, and it is still used in certain clinical contexts today.

Each athlete finishes the race, but their timing is different — and in insulin therapy, timing is everything.

---

## Onset — how quickly they start working

**Onset** is the time from injection to the point where the insulin begins to measurably lower blood glucose.

- **Fiasp:** Approximately **2.5 to 5 minutes** for initial appearance in the blood, with glucose-lowering activity beginning within about **15 to 20 minutes**. Fiasp achieves this speed through the addition of niacinamide (vitamin B3) and L-arginine, which accelerate initial absorption.
- **NovoRapid:** Onset typically within **10 to 20 minutes**. This was revolutionary when it was introduced, compared to the older human insulins.
- **Actrapid:** Onset typically within **30 to 60 minutes**. This slower start is why Actrapid is classified as **short-acting** rather than rapid-acting, and why it is often recommended to be taken 20-30 minutes before meals rather than at the time of eating.

Why does onset matter? Because the faster insulin starts working, the better it can match the glucose rise from a meal. If insulin arrives late to the party, blood glucose spikes first and is brought down later — creating that frustrating post-meal mountain on your CGM graph.

---

## Peak — when they work hardest

**Peak** is the time when insulin activity is at its maximum — the point where it is lowering blood glucose most aggressively.

- **Fiasp:** Peak activity approximately **1 to 1.5 hours** after injection. Because Fiasp peaks earlier, it is better positioned to intercept the glucose rise from rapidly absorbed carbohydrates.
- **NovoRapid:** Peak activity approximately **1 to 3 hours** after injection, with most individuals experiencing peak effect around **60 to 90 minutes**.
- **Actrapid:** Peak activity approximately **2 to 4 hours** after injection. This later peak means the strongest glucose-lowering effect arrives well after the meal glucose surge has begun.

Understanding peak timing helps you and your care team think about pre-bolusing (taking insulin before you eat) and about which foods are more or less well matched to your insulin's peak. A fast-digesting meal paired with a slow-peaking insulin creates a mismatch — glucose arrives before insulin is ready.

---

## Duration — how long they keep working

**Duration of action** is the total time from injection until the insulin is no longer having a meaningful glucose-lowering effect. This is the number that directly feeds into your **IOB (Insulin On Board)** calculations.

- **Fiasp:** Duration approximately **3 to 5 hours**, with some studies suggesting slightly shorter overall duration compared to NovoRapid.
- **NovoRapid:** Duration approximately **3 to 5 hours**. This is the most commonly used Duration of Insulin Action (DIA) setting in IOB calculations, typically set between 3.5 and 5 hours depending on individual physiology.
- **Actrapid:** Duration approximately **6 to 8 hours**. This significantly longer tail means Actrapid contributes to IOB for much longer, increasing the window during which stacking is a concern.

The duration is where the biggest practical difference lies. A longer-acting insulin means a longer tail (explored in [topic-057](/education/topic-057)), a wider danger window for stacking (explored in [topic-054](/education/topic-054)), and a need for more patience when considering corrections.

## Why this matters for IOB calculations

IOB Hunter in GluMira calculates your active insulin based on the **activity profile** of the insulin you are using. If your profile is set for NovoRapid with a DIA of 4 hours, the curve will look different than if it is set for Fiasp with a DIA of 3.5 hours, or Actrapid with a DIA of 6 hours.

Getting this setting right matters. If your DIA is set too short, IOB Hunter will underestimate the insulin still active in your body — which could lead you to stack. If it is set too long, it will overestimate IOB, which could lead you to under-correct. Your care team will help you determine the right DIA setting for your specific insulin and your individual physiology.

This is one of the reasons GluMira asks you to specify which insulin you use. It is not just a label — it changes the mathematics behind the IOB calculation.

## A quick comparison table

| Property | Fiasp | NovoRapid | Actrapid |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classification** | Ultra-rapid-acting analogue | Rapid-acting analogue | Short-acting human insulin |
| **Onset** | ~15-20 min | ~10-20 min | ~30-60 min |
| **Peak** | ~1-1.5 hours | ~1-3 hours | ~2-4 hours |
| **Duration** | ~3-5 hours | ~3-5 hours | ~6-8 hours |
| **Pre-bolus timing** | Often at or just before meals | Often 10-15 min before meals | Often 20-30 min before meals |

*Note: All values are approximate ranges. Individual responses vary based on dose size, injection site, temperature, activity level, and personal physiology. Always rely on your care team's guidance for your individual parameters.*

## Which one is "best"?

There is no universal best. Each insulin has its role. Fiasp may suit someone who struggles with post-meal spikes and wants the fastest possible onset. NovoRapid remains the most widely used rapid-acting insulin worldwide, with decades of clinical data supporting its efficacy and safety. Actrapid may be appropriate in specific clinical settings or for individuals who need a longer, gentler activity profile.

The choice of insulin is a clinical decision made by your care team, based on your individual needs, your lifestyle, your glucose patterns, and your preferences. What matters from an education standpoint is that you **understand the profile of the insulin you use** — because that profile shapes every IOB calculation, every correction decision, and every post-meal experience.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Rapid-acting and short-acting insulins differ in onset, peak, and duration.** Fiasp is the fastest, NovoRapid is the established standard, and Actrapid is slower but longer-lasting.
2. **Onset affects post-meal glucose spikes.** Faster-onset insulins are better positioned to match the speed of glucose absorption from food.
3. **Duration of action directly determines how long insulin contributes to IOB.** Actrapid's longer duration means a wider stacking window compared to NovoRapid or Fiasp.
4. **Your DIA (Duration of Insulin Action) setting in IOB Hunter must match the insulin you use.** An incorrect setting leads to inaccurate IOB calculations.
5. **There is no universally "best" mealtime insulin.** The right choice depends on your individual needs and is a clinical decision made with your care team.
