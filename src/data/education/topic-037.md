# Growth Spurt Identification — When to Adjust Doses

**Topic ID:** 037
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Category:** Growth & Development
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## Growing Bodies, Changing Needs

One week, everything is in range. The next, your child's blood glucose seems to have a mind of its own — running high despite no changes in diet, activity, or routine. Before you start questioning everything you're doing, consider this: your child might simply be growing.

Growth spurts are a defining feature of the pre-teen years, and for children with Type 1 diabetes, they bring a predictable but often surprising shift in insulin needs. Understanding the relationship between growth and insulin requirements can save your family a great deal of frustration and help you respond to changing numbers with confidence rather than confusion.

## The Biology Behind the Numbers

During a growth spurt, the body increases its production of growth hormone. Growth hormone is essential — it drives the increase in height, the development of bone density, and the maturation of organs and tissues. It's exactly what a growing body is supposed to produce.

However, growth hormone also opposes the action of insulin. It makes muscle and fat cells less responsive to insulin — a state called insulin resistance. For a child without T1D, the pancreas compensates by producing more insulin. For a child with T1D, the external insulin supply needs to be adjusted to match the body's increased demands.

This means that during active growth periods, your child may need significantly more insulin than usual — not because anything is going wrong, but because everything is going right. Their body is growing, and insulin needs are keeping pace.

## Recognising a Growth Spurt

Growth spurts in pre-teens can be dramatic or subtle, rapid or gradual. Here are the signs that one may be underway:

**Increased appetite.** If your child is suddenly ravenous — eating more at meals, snacking more frequently, or asking for food at unusual times — their body may be fuelling a growth period. This increased intake, combined with growth-hormone-driven insulin resistance, can push blood glucose higher.

**Persistent high blood glucose readings.** This is often the first diabetes-specific sign. If you're seeing elevated readings — especially fasting highs and overnight highs — that persist for several days without an obvious cause (illness, dietary changes, missed doses), growth is a likely culprit.

**Rapid height gain.** This one seems obvious, but it's easy to miss in real time. Pre-teens can grow several centimetres in a matter of weeks. If clothes are suddenly shorter, shoes are suddenly tighter, or the pencil marks on the door frame are climbing fast, a growth spurt is in progress.

**Increased sleep.** Growth hormone is released predominantly during deep sleep, and growing bodies often demand more of it. If your child is sleeping longer, napping more, or harder to wake in the morning, growth may be a factor.

**Growing pains.** Some children experience aching in their legs, particularly at night, during periods of rapid growth. While not universally experienced, these pains can be another indicator.

**Mood and energy fluctuations.** The hormonal shifts that accompany growth can affect mood and energy levels. Combined with the blood glucose variability that comes with increased insulin resistance, your child may seem more irritable, tired, or emotionally sensitive than usual.

## Tracking the Patterns

The key to managing growth-related insulin changes is pattern recognition. Individual blood glucose readings tell you what's happening right now. Patterns tell you what's changing over time.

**Look at trends over 3–7 days.** A single high reading might be explained by a dozen things. Three to five days of consistently elevated readings at similar times of day suggest a systemic change — and growth is one of the most common causes in this age group.

**Pay special attention to overnight and fasting numbers.** Growth hormone release peaks during sleep, so overnight and morning readings are often the first to be affected. If fasting glucose is creeping up despite consistent evening routines, growth hormone is a prime suspect.

**Note the basal vs. bolus pattern.** In many cases, growth spurts affect basal insulin needs more than bolus needs (though both may increase). If your child's between-meal numbers are rising but post-meal numbers are relatively stable, a basal adjustment may be the primary need.

**Keep a simple log.** You don't need a complex spreadsheet. A quick daily note — height if you're measuring, appetite observations, sleep patterns, and any notable BG trends — gives your diabetes team valuable information for making informed adjustments.

## Working With Your Diabetes Team

When you suspect a growth spurt is affecting insulin needs, your diabetes team is your most important partner.

**Don't wait for the scheduled appointment.** Growth spurts don't follow clinical calendars. If you're seeing persistent highs that you believe are growth-related, contact your team. Most clinics welcome interim check-ins — a phone call, a message through their portal, or a quick review of uploaded CGM data.

**Share your observations.** "We've been running high for five days, especially overnight. Appetite is way up. They've grown two centimetres this month." This kind of context helps your team distinguish growth-related patterns from other causes and respond with appropriate guidance.

**Expect gradual adjustments.** Dose changes during growth spurts are typically made in small increments, with monitoring between each change. Your team will guide the specific approach based on your child's individual needs, insulin regimen, and the data available.

**Understand that this is iterative.** Growth isn't linear. A spurt may last two weeks, plateau for a month, then resume. Insulin needs may increase, stabilise, and increase again. This isn't a one-time fix — it's an ongoing conversation between you, your child, and your clinical team.

## What Happens When the Spurt Ends

Growth spurts don't last forever, and when they end, insulin needs often decrease. The same vigilance that helped you identify rising needs should be applied in reverse — if your child starts having more frequent lows after a period of higher doses, the spurt may be winding down.

This is another reason to stay in close contact with your diabetes team during active growth periods. They can help you adjust in both directions — up when growth demands it, and back down when it eases.

## Reassurance for Parents

Watching your child's numbers climb despite your best efforts can feel deeply unsettling. It's natural to wonder if you're missing something, if you've made a mistake, or if something is wrong.

Most of the time, what's "wrong" is that your child is growing — exactly as they should be. The insulin adjustments that follow are a normal, expected part of managing T1D through the pre-teen years. You're not failing. You're parenting a growing child with a condition that requires ongoing recalibration.

Trust the patterns. Trust your team. And trust yourself — you've been doing this longer than you think.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Growth hormone directly causes insulin resistance**, meaning growth spurts predictably increase insulin needs in children with T1D.
2. **Recognise growth spurt signs early** — increased appetite, persistent high BG, rapid height gain, more sleep, and growing pains are all indicators.
3. **Track 3–7 day patterns rather than reacting to single readings** — focus especially on overnight and fasting numbers, which growth hormone affects first.
4. **Contact your diabetes team proactively** when you see growth-related patterns — don't wait for scheduled appointments.
5. **Insulin needs will decrease again** when the growth spurt ends — stay vigilant for increasing lows as a signal that doses may need to come back down.
