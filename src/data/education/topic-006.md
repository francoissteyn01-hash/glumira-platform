## Hello from Mira

Whether you are a parent checking your toddler's levels at 2am, a ten-year-old wondering why the number on your meter keeps changing, or a teenager frustrated that your readings make no sense — this one is for you. Blood glucose numbers are going to be a big part of your life with Type 1 Diabetes, and I want to help you understand what they mean, why they move, and most importantly, why they are **not** a judgement of how well you are doing.

---

## What is blood glucose?

Blood glucose (sometimes called blood sugar) is the amount of glucose — a type of sugar — circulating in your bloodstream at any given moment. Glucose comes primarily from the carbohydrates in the food you eat. It is your body's main source of energy. Every cell in your body needs glucose to function.

In someone without Type 1 Diabetes, the pancreas constantly monitors blood glucose levels and releases insulin to keep them within a narrow range — typically between about 4.0 and 7.0 mmol/L (or 70-126 mg/dL). The body does this automatically, thousands of times a day, without the person ever thinking about it.

In Type 1 Diabetes, that automatic system is no longer functioning. The beta cells that produced insulin have been destroyed by the immune system. So now, you and your care team take on the role that the pancreas used to play — monitoring glucose levels and providing insulin from outside the body.

## What do the numbers mean?

When you check your blood glucose (via a finger prick, a continuous glucose monitor, or a flash glucose monitor), you get a number. That number tells you the concentration of glucose in your blood at that moment.

Here is a general framework, based on guidance from ISPAD, ADA, and NICE. **Please note:** your care team will give you target ranges specific to your age, situation, and individual needs. These are general educational ranges, not personal targets.

**Generally speaking:**
- **Below 4.0 mmol/L (70 mg/dL):** This is considered low (hypoglycaemia, or "hypo"). Your body does not have enough glucose available. Action is needed.
- **4.0 - 7.0 mmol/L (70-126 mg/dL) before meals:** This is often considered a good range for many people, though individual targets vary.
- **5.0 - 10.0 mmol/L (90-180 mg/dL) after meals:** Blood glucose naturally rises after eating. A moderate rise that comes back down is expected.
- **Above 10.0 mmol/L (180 mg/dL) for extended periods:** This is considered high (hyperglycaemia, or "hyper"). It may need attention depending on the circumstances.
- **Above 14.0 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) with symptoms:** This may require checking for ketones and contacting your care team, especially if you feel unwell.

These numbers are not absolute rules. A brief spike after a meal is different from a sustained high over many hours. A reading of 4.2 is very different from 3.1, even though both are "low-ish." Context matters enormously.

## Why do the numbers keep changing?

This is one of the most frustrating aspects of Type 1 Diabetes, especially early on. You might do the exact same thing two days in a row and get completely different numbers. That is not your fault — it is the nature of blood glucose.

Here are just some of the factors that influence blood glucose levels:

**Food-related:**
- The type of carbohydrates you eat (fast-acting vs. slow-release)
- The amount of carbohydrates
- Fat and protein content (these can delay glucose absorption)
- The timing of meals and snacks

**Activity-related:**
- Physical exercise (can lower glucose during and after activity)
- The type of exercise (intense anaerobic activity can sometimes raise glucose temporarily)
- How long since you last ate before exercising

**Insulin-related:**
- The timing of insulin relative to food
- The type of insulin (rapid-acting, long-acting, etc.)
- Injection site and absorption variability
- Insulin on board — the amount still active from previous doses. GluMira's **IOB Hunter** feature helps you explore this concept visually. Always discuss insulin adjustments with your care team.

**Body-related:**
- Hormones (growth hormones, stress hormones, puberty hormones — the "dawn phenomenon" where glucose rises in the early morning is a well-known hormonal effect)
- Illness and infection (being sick almost always raises blood glucose)
- Stress and emotional state
- Sleep quality and quantity
- Menstrual cycle (for those who menstruate, hormonal changes can significantly affect glucose levels)

**Environmental:**
- Temperature and weather
- Altitude
- Travel and time zone changes

With this many variables, it is honestly remarkable that blood glucose levels are ever in range. When they are, that is great. When they are not, it does not mean you failed — it means one of dozens of factors pushed things in an unexpected direction.

## Your numbers are not a report card

This is perhaps the most important thing I will say in this entire article.

**A blood glucose reading is a piece of data. It is not a grade. It is not a measure of your worth, your effort, or your character.**

A high reading does not mean you were "bad." A low reading does not mean you were "careless." An in-range reading does not make you a better person than an out-of-range one. The numbers tell you what is happening right now so you can respond — nothing more.

Research supported by ISPAD and the ADA consistently shows that attaching moral judgements to blood glucose numbers is harmful. It increases diabetes distress, reduces motivation, and can lead to avoidance of checking altogether (because who wants to see a number that makes them feel guilty?).

If you are a parent, be mindful of how you react to your child's numbers. Saying "your number is too high" in a disappointed tone teaches a child that high numbers equal parental disapproval. Try instead: "Your number is 15. Let's figure out what happened and what we can do." Neutral language. Problem-solving, not blaming.

If you are a teen, be gentle with yourself. Some days the numbers will cooperate. Some days they absolutely will not. Both kinds of days happen to every single person living with Type 1 Diabetes — even those who have had it for decades.

## Time in range — a more useful way to think about it

Rather than fixating on individual readings, many care teams now focus on **Time in Range (TIR)** — the percentage of time your blood glucose spends within your target range over a period of days or weeks. This is a more balanced and accurate way to assess glucose management than any single number.

ISPAD and ADA guidelines suggest that a TIR of around 70% (about 17 hours per day in range) is a reasonable target for many people with Type 1 Diabetes. That means even with excellent management, it is expected that blood glucose will be out of range for roughly 7 hours per day. Let that sink in — **out of range for 7 hours a day is considered good management.**

Perfection is not the goal. Patterns are what matter.

---

## When to call your care team

Reach out to your care team if:
- You are regularly seeing readings below 4.0 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) or above 14.0 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) and are unsure why
- You notice recurring patterns (always high in the morning, always low after sport) and want to understand them
- You are feeling overwhelmed or distressed by your numbers
- You are avoiding checking your blood glucose because of how the numbers make you feel
- Ketone levels are elevated alongside high blood glucose readings

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Blood glucose numbers tell you the concentration of sugar in your blood at a specific moment.** They fluctuate constantly and are influenced by dozens of factors.
2. **Your numbers are not a report card.** A high or low reading is data to respond to, not a judgement on you as a person.
3. **Dozens of factors affect blood glucose** — food, activity, insulin, hormones, stress, illness, sleep, and more. Perfect control is not a realistic or healthy goal.
4. **Time in Range is a more useful measure than individual readings.** Even with excellent management, being out of range for several hours a day is normal and expected.
5. **If the numbers are causing you distress, discuss it with your care team.** Emotional wellbeing is a critical part of diabetes management.
