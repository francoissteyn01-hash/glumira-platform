# Understanding Your HbA1c — What It Measures and Its Limits

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, and care teams

## Hello from Mira

If you have been living with Type 1 Diabetes for any length of time, you have almost certainly heard the term HbA1c. It might arrive in a letter from your clinic, flash up on an app, or be the first number your endocrinologist mentions at a review appointment. It is arguably the most widely used metric in diabetes care — and yet it is also one of the most misunderstood.

I want to help you understand what HbA1c actually measures, why it matters, and — just as importantly — where its limitations lie. Because a single number, no matter how useful, never tells the whole story.

---

## What HbA1c actually measures

HbA1c stands for **glycated haemoglobin**. Let me break that down.

Haemoglobin is a protein found inside your red blood cells. Its primary job is to carry oxygen from your lungs to every tissue in your body. As red blood cells travel through your bloodstream, the haemoglobin inside them is constantly exposed to glucose — the sugar circulating in your blood.

When glucose attaches to haemoglobin, the haemoglobin becomes **glycated**. This process is natural and happens in everyone, whether they have diabetes or not. The more glucose present in the blood over time, the more haemoglobin becomes glycated.

Here is the key insight: red blood cells have a **lifespan of approximately 90 to 120 days**. When a blood sample is taken and the percentage of glycated haemoglobin is measured, the result reflects the average blood glucose level over roughly the previous two to three months. It is not a snapshot of one moment. It is more like a long-exposure photograph — capturing a blurred average of everything that has happened over that window.

The result is reported as either a percentage (for example, 7.0%) or in mmol/mol (for example, 53 mmol/mol). Both express the same thing in different units.

---

## Why it matters

HbA1c has been the cornerstone of diabetes management for decades, and for good reason. Large-scale studies — including the landmark Diabetes Control and Complications Trial (DCCT) — demonstrated a clear relationship between lower HbA1c values and a reduced risk of long-term diabetes complications, including retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy.

This is why clinical guidelines from ISPAD, the ADA, and NICE use HbA1c as a benchmark when discussing diabetes management goals.

**General target ranges referenced in guidelines include:**

- **Children and adolescents (ISPAD 2022):** An HbA1c below 53 mmol/mol (7.0%) is recommended for most young people with T1D, individualised to the person and with the important caveat that this should be achieved without excessive hypoglycaemia.
- **Adults (ADA Standards of Care):** A target of below 53 mmol/mol (7.0%) is suggested for many adults, though targets may be relaxed for individuals with significant hypoglycaemia unawareness, limited life expectancy, or other individual factors.
- **NICE (UK):** Recommends a target of 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) or lower for most adults, while acknowledging that individual targets should be agreed upon with the care team.

It is worth noting that targets are **individualised**. Your care team may recommend a different target based on your unique circumstances. The right target for you is one that balances long-term health with day-to-day safety and quality of life.

## The three-month average — a strength and a weakness

The fact that HbA1c reflects a long window of time is both its greatest strength and its most significant limitation.

**The strength:** It smooths out day-to-day fluctuations. A single finger-prick reading can be influenced by what you ate an hour ago, whether you exercised, or whether you are stressed. HbA1c is not swayed by any one day. It gives your care team a broad view of glucose management over time.

**The weakness:** It hides the detail. Two people can have an identical HbA1c of 53 mmol/mol (7.0%), yet their daily glucose patterns could look completely different. One person might have relatively stable glucose levels throughout the day. The other might experience dramatic swings — soaring highs followed by crashing lows — that happen to average out to the same number.

This is sometimes called the **"HbA1c paradox"** — a reassuring average can mask a turbulent reality. And that turbulence matters. Glycaemic variability — the degree to which glucose levels swing up and down — is increasingly recognised as a factor in both short-term wellbeing and long-term outcomes.

## Other factors that can affect HbA1c accuracy

Because HbA1c depends on red blood cells, anything that changes red blood cell turnover or haemoglobin structure can affect the result — sometimes making it misleadingly high or low.

Conditions that may influence HbA1c include:

- **Iron deficiency anaemia** — can falsely elevate HbA1c
- **Haemolytic anaemia or significant blood loss** — can falsely lower HbA1c because red blood cells are replaced more quickly
- **Haemoglobin variants** (such as sickle cell trait) — may interfere with certain assay methods
- **Chronic kidney disease** — can affect red blood cell lifespan
- **Pregnancy** — changes in blood volume and red cell turnover can alter results
- **Recent blood transfusion** — introduces donor red blood cells with a different glycation history

If your HbA1c result does not seem to match your day-to-day glucose readings, it is worth discussing these possibilities with your care team. There is no need to panic — but it is important to interpret the number in context.

---

## HbA1c is weighted toward the recent

Although HbA1c is described as a "three-month average," it is not equally weighted across those three months. Research suggests that approximately **50% of the HbA1c value is determined by the most recent four weeks**, with progressively less influence from the weeks before that. This means changes in glucose management will begin to show in HbA1c relatively quickly — but the full picture takes time to develop.

---

## A useful tool, not the only tool

HbA1c remains an important metric. It is well-validated, widely available, and strongly linked to long-term outcomes. But the diabetes community — including ISPAD and the ADA — increasingly recognises that it should be used **alongside** other measures, not in isolation.

Metrics like **Time in Range** (the percentage of time glucose stays within a target window), **time below range** (hypoglycaemia), and **glycaemic variability** provide a richer, more nuanced picture of daily life with T1D. We explore Time in Range in detail in [topic-092](/education/topic-092).

The best approach is to view HbA1c as one piece of a larger puzzle. It tells you where you have been on average, but it does not tell you how the journey felt along the way.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **HbA1c measures glycated haemoglobin** — the percentage of haemoglobin that has glucose attached — and reflects average blood glucose over approximately two to three months, linked to the lifespan of red blood cells.
2. **Lower HbA1c values are associated with reduced risk of long-term complications**, but targets should be individualised in discussion with your care team, taking into account hypoglycaemia risk and quality of life.
3. **Two identical HbA1c results can hide very different daily glucose patterns.** A stable day and a rollercoaster day can produce the same average — this is a fundamental limitation of the metric.
4. **Medical conditions affecting red blood cells** — such as anaemia, haemoglobin variants, or kidney disease — can make HbA1c results misleading. Always interpret the number in clinical context.
5. **HbA1c is most powerful when used alongside other metrics**, including Time in Range and measures of glycaemic variability, to build a complete picture of diabetes management.
