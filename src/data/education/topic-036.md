# Transition to Secondary School — New Routines, New Risks

**Topic ID:** 036
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** All (children, parents, and caregivers)
**Category:** School & Social Life
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## Everything Changes at Once

Starting secondary school is one of the biggest transitions in a young person's life. New building, new teachers, new classmates, new timetable, new expectations. For a child with Type 1 diabetes, it also means new routines, new risks, and new conversations about a condition that most of their peers don't fully understand.

This is a lot. And it's OK to feel a mix of excitement and anxiety about it — whether you're the child walking through those doors or the parent watching them go.

The good news: with the right preparation, secondary school can be a place where your child thrives — academically, socially, and in their diabetes management. Let's talk about how to set that up.

## Setting Up the New School

The groundwork for a successful transition happens before the first day. Here's what needs to be in place:

**Meet with the school early.** Contact the school's special educational needs coordinator (SENCO), school nurse, or pastoral lead well before term starts. Request a meeting to discuss your child's diabetes management plan. Don't assume information will transfer automatically from primary school — it rarely does.

**Create or update a written care plan.** This document should clearly outline your child's daily management needs: when and where they check blood glucose, where supplies are stored, what to do in a hypo, who to contact in an emergency, and any accommodations they need (such as access to snacks, water, and the toilet at any time). Your diabetes team can help you draft or review this.

**Identify key staff.** Your child needs to know which adults in the school are aware of their T1D and can help. This typically includes their form tutor, the school nurse, the front office staff, and ideally at least one teacher they see daily. Having multiple informed adults means there's always someone available.

**Establish supply storage.** Where will hypo treatments be kept? Where is the spare insulin stored? Where is the glucagon kit? These items should be accessible quickly — not locked in a cabinet on the other side of the building. Work with the school to find a practical, safe arrangement.

**Discuss exam and test accommodations.** Depending on your region's policies, your child may be entitled to accommodations during exams — such as additional time, the ability to check BG during tests, or access to food and drink. Getting these in place early avoids stress later.

## More Independence, More Responsibility

Secondary school typically means less adult supervision than primary school. Your child may move between classrooms, manage their own timetable, and eat lunch without a teacher watching. This increased independence is healthy and appropriate — but it does mean they need to take on more of their own diabetes management.

**Lunchtime management.** In primary school, a teaching assistant may have reminded your child to check before eating. In secondary school, they're probably on their own. Talk through the lunchtime routine: check BG, count carbs, take insulin (if injecting), eat. Practise this at home until it's second nature.

**Carrying supplies.** Your child should carry essential items with them — glucose tablets or another fast-acting carb, their meter or CGM receiver, and their phone if they use it for diabetes management. A small, discreet pouch or pencil case works well and avoids drawing unwanted attention.

**Recognising and treating lows independently.** At this age, your child should be able to recognise their own hypo symptoms, treat promptly, and inform a teacher if needed. Review this skill regularly, especially in the early weeks when routines are new and stress may lower their threshold for lows.

**Speaking up in class.** If your child needs to check their BG, eat a snack, or leave the room during a lesson, they need the confidence to do so — and the knowledge that they are allowed to. Some children find it helpful to have a pre-agreed signal with their teacher (like placing a card on their desk) rather than having to explain publicly.

## Peer Pressure and Social Navigation

Secondary school introduces a more complex social landscape. Your child is making new friends, establishing their identity, and — like every pre-teen — desperately wanting to fit in.

**Diabetes can feel like a spotlight.** A CGM alarm going off in a quiet classroom. Having to inject before lunch. Turning down a sweet at a birthday celebration. These moments can feel enormous to a pre-teen who just wants to be like everyone else.

**Normalise without minimising.** Help your child develop a simple, confident way to explain their diabetes if they choose to. "I have Type 1 diabetes — my body doesn't make insulin, so I take it instead. It's no big deal." Rehearsing this at home reduces the anxiety of saying it at school.

**Peer reactions are mostly positive.** Most children are curious, not cruel. Many pre-teens respond to a classmate's T1D with genuine interest and supportiveness. Remind your child that most people react better than they expect.

**But some won't understand — and that's where self-advocacy matters.** If a child is teased about their diabetes, or pressured to skip management steps to fit in, they need to know they can tell a trusted adult. Self-advocacy is a skill that develops over time, and secondary school is where many young people start building it.

## The First Few Weeks

The transition period is the highest-risk time. Routines haven't solidified. Stress levels are elevated. Your child may be too distracted or too anxious to manage diabetes as carefully as they normally would.

**Expect some wobble.** Blood glucose may be less stable in the first few weeks. Stress hormones affect glucose levels. New activity patterns, different lunch timing, and emotional intensity all contribute. This is temporary.

**Check in without interrogating.** Ask "How was your day?" before "What was your blood sugar?" Your child is adjusting to an enormous amount of change. Lead with connection, not clinical review.

**Stay in touch with the school.** A quick email to the school nurse or form tutor after the first week — "How's it going from your perspective?" — can flag issues your child might not mention.

**Celebrate the wins.** Every day your child manages their T1D in a new, unfamiliar environment is an achievement. Tell them so. The confidence they're building now will carry them through every transition that follows.

## Looking Further Ahead

Secondary school isn't just a challenge to survive — it's a place where your child will grow, learn, make friends, discover passions, and develop into the person they're becoming. Diabetes is part of that journey, but it doesn't define it.

With the right setup, the right support, and a healthy dose of patience from everyone involved, this transition can be one of the most empowering experiences of your child's T1D life so far.

They're ready. And so are you.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Early contact with the school and a written care plan are essential** — don't assume diabetes information will transfer from primary school automatically.
2. **Secondary school requires greater self-management** — practise lunchtime routines, independent hypo treatment, and speaking up in class before the first day.
3. **Equip your child with a simple, confident way to explain their T1D** — rehearsed at home, delivered with ease at school.
4. **Expect glucose variability in the first few weeks** — stress, new routines, and emotional intensity all affect blood glucose, and this settles with time.
5. **Self-advocacy is a critical skill that secondary school helps build** — support your child in knowing their rights and speaking up for their needs.
