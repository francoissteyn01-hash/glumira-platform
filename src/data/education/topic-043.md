# Dating and Disclosure — When and How to Tell Someone

**Topic ID:** 43
**Age Range:** 13–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Relationships & Social Life
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17

---

## The Question Everyone with T1D Asks

At some point, if you are dating or thinking about it, this question will come up: when do I tell them I have Type 1 Diabetes? And right behind it: how? And will they care?

Let us start with the most important thing: **there is no single right answer to when.** There is no rulebook, no perfect moment, no script that works for everyone. What matters is that you feel comfortable, that you are safe, and that the person you are telling gets accurate information — not a dramatic medical confession.

T1D is a part of your life. It is not a guilty secret, and it is not the most interesting thing about you.

## You Choose When — But Here Are Some Things to Think About

Some people with T1D bring it up on the first date. Some wait until things feel more serious. Some mention it casually before they even go out — a quick text, a passing comment. All of these are valid.

Here are some practical factors that might shape your timing:

**Safety first.** If you are going to be alone with someone — on a date, at their house, wherever — they need to know enough to help you if you have a hypo. That does not mean you need to deliver a full diabetes education session. It means they need to know that if you say "I need sugar," it is not a joke, and if you seem confused or unresponsive, they should call for help. This is a baseline safety consideration, and it applies regardless of how serious the relationship is.

**Visibility might decide for you.** If you wear an insulin pump, a CGM sensor, or medical ID, the person you are dating may notice these before you bring it up. That is fine. It can actually make the conversation easier — "Oh, that thing on your arm? That is my glucose sensor. I have Type 1 Diabetes." Done. No big buildup needed.

**The longer you wait, the bigger it can feel.** If you treat T1D disclosure like a dramatic reveal, it becomes one. If you mention it early and casually, it signals that this is just a normal part of your life — because it is. Waiting weeks or months can make the other person wonder why you hid it, which can create unnecessary tension.

**There is a difference between first-date honesty and medical oversharing.** You do not owe anyone your full medical history on a first date. Saying "I have Type 1 Diabetes, so I might need to check my blood sugar or eat something sometimes" is plenty. The details — how your pump works, what your HbA1c is, the time you had DKA — those come later, if and when you are comfortable.

## How to Actually Say It

There is no perfect script, but here are some approaches that real teens with T1D have found work:

**The casual mention:** "Just so you know, I have Type 1 Diabetes. It's an autoimmune thing — my pancreas doesn't make insulin, so I manage it with a pump/injections. You might see me check my levels or eat something random. It's totally routine."

**The practical heads-up:** "Before we go out, I should mention I'm Type 1 Diabetic. If I ever seem spacey or say I'm going low, I just need some sugar — juice, sweets, whatever. It passes quickly."

**The device conversation:** "This [pointing to pump/sensor] is for my diabetes. It monitors my blood sugar. Pretty cool tech, actually."

Notice what all of these have in common: they are matter-of-fact. They are not apologetic. They are not asking for permission or pity. They are giving information.

## What If They React Badly?

Most people, when told "I have Type 1 Diabetes," will respond with curiosity, concern, or a fairly neutral "okay." The vast majority of people are not going to be put off by a medical condition.

But occasionally, someone might:
- Confuse T1D with Type 2 and make ignorant comments about diet or lifestyle
- Act uncomfortable or awkward
- Decide it is "too much" to deal with

If someone confuses T1D with Type 2, you can correct them calmly. It is an autoimmune condition. You did not cause it. It is not going away. A simple correction usually clears things up.

If someone is genuinely put off by the fact that you have a chronic health condition, that tells you something important about them — and it is better to know early. A person who cannot handle you checking your blood sugar is not going to handle much else either. This is not a loss. It is a filter.

You do not need to convince anyone that you are worth dating despite having T1D. You are not "despite" anything.

## Practical Tips for Dates

**Carry your supplies.** Glucose tablets, your meter (even if you have a CGM), your phone. A small bag or pocket supply kit. Do not leave your safety net at home because you want to look "normal."

**Wear medical ID.** A bracelet, a necklace, a tag — something. If something goes wrong and your date does not know what to do, medical ID guides first responders. It does not have to be ugly or medical-looking — there are plenty of options now that look like regular jewellery.

**Food-based dates are your friend.** Dinner, coffee, a picnic — these all make it easy to eat when you need to and to manage your BG without it being a big deal. If you are doing something active (hiking, ice skating, a theme park), plan for the extra glucose impact.

**Have a plan for hypos.** Know where your fast-acting glucose is. If you start to feel low, say so. Do not try to hide it or push through. A quick "hang on, I need to grab some sugar, give me a minute" is far less disruptive than collapsing because you were too embarrassed to say something.

**Check your BG as needed.** Do not skip checks because you are on a date. If you need to scan your sensor or prick your finger, do it. Most people will not even notice, and if they do, a brief explanation is all that is needed.

## What About Physical Intimacy?

As relationships progress, there are practical considerations. Pump sites and CGM sensors are on your body. They might be in the way. They might get knocked or pulled during physical contact. This is normal and manageable.

If you wear a pump, you can disconnect it temporarily for short periods — but this is something to discuss with your diabetes team, not to figure out in the moment. Know your plan in advance.

Being physically close with someone means they will see your devices, your injection sites, your bruises from sensors. Some people feel self-conscious about this. That is understandable. But most partners, once they understand what they are looking at, are completely fine with it. Many find the technology genuinely interesting.

If someone makes you feel ashamed of your body because of T1D-related marks or devices, that is their problem, not yours.

## Longer-Term Relationships

As a relationship becomes more serious, your partner will naturally learn more about your T1D. They may start noticing when you are going low before you do. They may learn how your pump works. They may keep glucose tablets in their bag.

This is not codependency — it is partnership. Let them in. Teach them what matters. Accept help when it is offered genuinely.

But also: your diabetes is your responsibility, not theirs. A partner who supports you is wonderful. A partner who manages your diabetes for you is taking on a role that is not theirs, and it can breed resentment on both sides. Keep the ownership clear.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **You choose when to disclose** — there is no "right" moment, but earlier and more casual tends to feel easier than a delayed, dramatic reveal.
2. **Frame it as information, not confession** — T1D is an autoimmune condition, not a flaw. State it factually and move on.
3. **Safety is non-negotiable** — anyone you are alone with should know enough to help if you have a hypo, even on a first date.
4. **Always carry supplies and wear medical ID** on dates — do not sacrifice safety for appearance.
5. **How someone reacts to your T1D tells you about them** — if they cannot handle it, that is information you needed, and it is better to know early.
