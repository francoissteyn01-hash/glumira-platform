# Fast Food Survival Guide — The Actual Carb Counts

**Audience:** All people living with Type 1 Diabetes and their support networks

## No Judgement — Just Facts

Let Mira be clear from the start: this topic is not here to tell you that fast food is bad, or that you should never eat it, or that "real" diabetes management means only eating steamed chicken and broccoli. That is not how life works, and it is not how Mira works either.

Fast food is part of modern life. It is convenient, it is affordable, it is sometimes the only option, and sometimes it is simply what you want. The goal here is to give you the actual carbohydrate counts for common fast food items so you can make informed decisions and manage your glucose effectively — no guilt, no lectures.

## Why Fast Food Is Tricky for T1D

Fast food presents a specific challenge because it combines three factors:

1. **High carbohydrate content** — large buns, thick-cut chips, sugary sauces, and regular soft drinks
2. **High fat content** — deep frying, cheese, mayo, and fatty meats
3. **Rapid eating pace** — fast food is designed to be eaten quickly

This combination means you get a large carb load delivered alongside a large fat load. As discussed in Topic 62, fat delays carbohydrate absorption. The result is often a two-phase glucose response: an initial moderate rise from the carbs, followed by a prolonged, stubborn elevation from the fat-delayed absorption.

Understanding this pattern is half the battle. The other half is knowing the numbers.

## Actual Carb Counts — Common Fast Food Items

The values below are approximate and based on standard serving sizes from major international chains. Portions vary by country and outlet, but these give you a reliable working range.

### Burgers

| Item | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Single beef patty burger (standard) | 30–35g |
| Double beef patty burger (standard) | 35–40g |
| Chicken burger / fillet burger | 40–50g |
| Fish burger | 40–45g |
| Large specialty burger (with extras) | 50–65g |
| Burger bun only | 25–35g |
| Burger without the bun | 3–8g |

**Mira's note:** The bun is the main carb source. Removing the bun — or eating half — is the single most effective carb reduction in any burger meal. Not a recommendation, just a fact.

### Chips / Fries

| Size | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Small fries | 25–30g |
| Medium fries | 40–50g |
| Large fries | 55–70g |

Chips are high in both carbohydrate and fat. They produce a delayed and extended glucose response. Many people find chips harder to manage than the burger itself.

### Chicken

| Item | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Fried chicken piece (original, with skin/coating) | 8–15g per piece |
| Fried chicken strips/tenders (3 pieces) | 15–25g |
| Chicken nuggets (6 pieces) | 15–20g |
| Chicken nuggets (10 pieces) | 25–35g |
| Grilled chicken piece (no coating) | 0–2g |

The coating and crumbing are the carb sources in fried chicken. Grilled options are significantly lower in carbohydrate.

### Pizza

| Item | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Thin crust slice (large pizza) | 20–30g |
| Standard crust slice (large pizza) | 30–40g |
| Thick / deep-dish slice (large pizza) | 35–50g |
| Stuffed crust slice | 40–55g |

**For a typical 2-slice serving:**
- Thin crust: 40–60g carb
- Standard crust: 60–80g carb
- Deep-dish: 70–100g carb

Pizza is the classic fat-protein-carb triple combination. Expect a prolonged glucose response lasting 4–6 hours (the pizza effect — see Topic 62).

### Sides and Extras

| Item | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Corn on the cob | 18–25g |
| Coleslaw (small) | 12–18g |
| Side salad (no dressing) | 3–5g |
| Mashed potato and gravy | 20–30g |
| Onion rings (small) | 25–35g |
| Hash brown (1 piece) | 12–15g |

### Sauces and Dips

| Sauce (1 sachet/portion) | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Tomato sauce / ketchup | 5–7g |
| BBQ sauce | 8–12g |
| Sweet chilli sauce | 8–10g |
| Mayo / aioli | 0–1g |
| Mustard | 0–1g |
| Ranch dressing | 1–2g |

Sauces are the silent carb adders. Three sachets of tomato sauce add 15–20g of carbohydrate that most people never count.

### Drinks

| Drink | Approximate Carbs |
|---|---|
| Regular soft drink (500ml) | 50–55g |
| Regular soft drink (330ml) | 35g |
| Diet / zero soft drink | 0g |
| Orange juice (300ml) | 30–35g |
| Milkshake (medium) | 60–80g |
| Milkshake (large) | 80–110g |
| Water | 0g |
| Iced tea (sweetened, 500ml) | 30–40g |

**Mira's note:** A large milkshake can contain more carbohydrate than the entire burger meal. Drinks are the single biggest source of unexpected carbs in fast food.

## Best and Worst Choices — By the Numbers

### Lower-Carb Options (under 40g total)

- Grilled chicken burger without the bun + side salad: ~10g
- Single burger (no fries, diet drink): ~30–35g
- Fried chicken pieces (2) with coleslaw: ~25–35g
- Bunless burger with cheese and salad: ~5–10g

### Moderate-Carb Options (40–70g total)

- Standard burger + small fries + diet drink: ~55–65g
- Chicken wrap (flour tortilla): ~45–55g
- 2 slices thin-crust pizza + side salad: ~45–65g

### Higher-Carb Options (70g+ total)

- Burger + large fries + regular soft drink: ~120–160g
- 3 slices standard pizza + regular soft drink: ~125–155g
- Chicken burger + medium fries + milkshake: ~140–180g

These are not "good" and "bad" categories. They are simply the numbers. Sometimes you want the large meal and that is perfectly fine — you just need to know what you are working with.

## Strategies That Work

### 1. Check Nutrition Information in Advance

Most major chains publish detailed nutritional information on their websites or apps. Many have in-store displays. Checking before you order removes the guesswork entirely.

### 2. Build Your Own Meal

Many fast food outlets now allow customisation. Swap the large fries for a side salad. Choose a grilled option instead of crumbed. Select water or a diet drink instead of a regular one. Each swap reduces the carb load without eliminating the meal.

### 3. Choose Your Splurge

If you want the large fries, perhaps skip the bun. If you want the milkshake, have a smaller main. This is not restriction — it is strategic enjoyment.

### 4. Account for the Fat Delay

Fast food is almost always high in fat. Expect a delayed and prolonged glucose response. Check your glucose at 3–4 hours, not just 1–2 hours, after a fast food meal.

### 5. Keep a Personal Fast Food Log

Your favourite order at your favourite chain has a consistent carb count. Once you have figured it out (by checking your glucose afterwards a few times), write it down. Next time, you already know.

## Fast Food During Ramadan and Religious Observance

During Ramadan, Iftar may sometimes include fast food — particularly for younger members of the family or in social settings. The same carb counts apply. Be mindful that breaking a fast with a high-carb, high-fat fast food meal will produce a particularly significant glucose response after hours of fasting.

Halal-certified fast food outlets use the same buns, fries, and sauces as other outlets — the carb content is identical. Kosher fast food follows the same principle. The certification relates to preparation and ingredients, not to macronutrient content.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Fast food is manageable with T1D when you know the actual carb counts** — the goal is information, not avoidance.
2. **Drinks and sauces are the biggest hidden carb sources** — a large milkshake can exceed the carbs in the entire main meal.
3. **The bun, the fries, and the drink are the three main carb contributors** — adjusting any one of these significantly changes the total.
4. **Fast food is high in fat, so expect a delayed and prolonged glucose response** — check glucose at 3–4 hours, not just 1–2.
5. **Most chains publish nutritional information** — checking before you order is the simplest and most effective strategy.
