# Weaning Onto Solids — Carb Counting for Baby Food

**Age range:** 0-2 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 13
**Category:** Infant nutrition and diabetes management

---

Starting solids is exciting. Starting solids when your baby has Type 1 diabetes is exciting and terrifying in roughly equal measure.

Every parent worries about weaning — is the food the right texture, will they choke, are they getting enough nutrients? You have all of those worries plus: how many carbs are in three licks of sweet potato? And do I need to dose for the portion that ended up in their hair?

You're doing great. This is a learnable skill, and it doesn't need to be perfect from day one.

## When to Start Solids

The general guidance (from the NHS, WHO, and ADA) is to introduce solid foods **around 6 months of age**, when your baby shows signs of readiness:

- They can sit up and hold their head steady
- They can coordinate eyes, hands, and mouth to look at food, pick it up, and bring it to their mouth
- They can swallow food rather than pushing it back out

Having Type 1 diabetes does not change when your baby should start solids. The timeline is the same. What changes is that you now have an additional variable to track alongside the usual weaning journey.

Let your diabetes team and paediatric dietitian know when you're planning to start, so they can review insulin strategies in advance.

## First Foods and Their Carb Content

In the early days of weaning, your baby will eat very small amounts. We're talking teaspoons, not tablespoons. The carb impact of these early tastes is often negligible, but it's helpful to start building your knowledge now.

Here's a rough guide to common first foods:

**Very low carb (less than 2g per tablespoon of puree):**
- Avocado
- Courgette (zucchini)
- Green beans
- Broccoli
- Cauliflower
- Meat and fish purees

**Low to moderate carb (2-5g per tablespoon of puree):**
- Carrot
- Butternut squash
- Peas
- Apple
- Pear

**Moderate to higher carb (5-8g per tablespoon of puree):**
- Sweet potato
- Banana
- Mango
- Baby rice/porridge
- Potato

These are approximate values and will vary based on how the food is prepared and how thick the puree is. Your paediatric dietitian can provide more specific guidance tailored to the foods your family uses.

## The Real Challenge: How Much Actually Gets Eaten?

This is the crux of the problem. You can puree and weigh and calculate to your heart's content, but babies are spectacularly inefficient eaters.

In the early weeks of weaning:

- Most food goes on the face, bib, highchair, floor, and dog
- The amount actually swallowed may be a fraction of what you served
- There is no reliable way to measure what was consumed versus what was redistributed around the kitchen

**Practical strategies:**

- **Weigh before and after.** Some parents weigh the bowl before and after feeding, accounting for what was dropped. It's not perfect, but it gives you a ballpark.
- **Start with low-carb foods.** Beginning with vegetables and proteins rather than fruits and starches gives you room to learn the weaning process before carb counting becomes critical.
- **Accept approximation.** In the early stages, the carb quantities are so small that precise counting matters less than it will later. Your diabetes team understands this.
- **Post-meal dosing.** For the same reasons as with breastfeeding, many teams recommend dosing insulin after the meal based on what was actually consumed. This is particularly important during weaning when intake is so unpredictable.

## Baby-Led Weaning vs Traditional Weaning

Both approaches work with Type 1 diabetes, and the choice is yours.

**Traditional weaning (spoon-fed purees):**
- You control what goes on the spoon
- Slightly easier to estimate amounts consumed
- Progression from smooth to lumpy to chopped is gradual
- Can feel more manageable when you're also managing insulin

**Baby-led weaning (finger foods from the start):**
- Baby controls what and how much they eat
- Harder to estimate exact intake
- Encourages self-regulation and independence
- Can feel more daunting when carb counting matters

Many families use a **combination approach** — offering some purees alongside finger foods. There is no evidence that one method is better than the other for children with T1D. Choose what suits your family and discuss with your dietitian.

## Commercial Baby Food — Reading the Labels

Pouches and jars of baby food have one significant advantage: **nutrition labels.** The carb content per 100g (or per pouch) is printed right there.

Tips for using commercial baby food:

- **Read the label every time.** Different flavours from the same brand can vary enormously in carb content. A vegetable pouch might have 4g of carbs while a fruit one has 14g.
- **Watch for added ingredients.** Some savoury baby foods contain fruit juice or concentrated apple puree as sweeteners, pushing the carb content up unexpectedly.
- **Note the portion size.** A pouch might be 100g or 120g or 150g. The "per 100g" and "per pouch" figures may be very different.
- **Keep a list.** Once you've found products your baby likes, note down the carb content so you're not re-reading labels every mealtime.

## Homemade Baby Food — Building Your Own Reference

If you're making food at home (which is wonderful and also more work — see a theme here?), you'll need to build your own carb reference over time.

A kitchen scale is your best investment. Weigh ingredients before cooking, note the carb content from packaging or a reliable database, and calculate the carb content of the finished batch.

**Example:**
- 200g sweet potato (raw) = approximately 40g carbs
- Cooked, mashed, and divided into 8 portions
- Each portion = approximately 5g carbs

This isn't laboratory-grade precision, but it's workable. Over time, you'll develop an intuitive sense of the carb content of your baby's usual foods.

## Allergies, Intolerances, and the Extra Layer

Current guidance recommends introducing common allergens (peanut, egg, milk, wheat, etc.) from around 6 months, one at a time. Having T1D doesn't change this recommendation.

However, there is a well-documented association between Type 1 diabetes and **coeliac disease**. Your diabetes team should be screening for this regularly. If your child does develop coeliac disease, the dietary adjustments will affect carb counting. Cross that bridge if and when you come to it — and know that your dietitian can help.

## The Mess Is Normal

Weaning is messy for every baby. The food-in-the-hair, puree-on-the-ceiling, banana-smeared-across-the-dog reality of it is universal. Your baby happens to have diabetes, but they're still a baby learning to eat, and the mess is part of the process.

Some days you'll nail the carb count and the blood sugar will be beautiful. Some days you'll carefully prepare a balanced meal and your child will eat three peas and a cracker. Both of those days are normal.

## Building Toward Independence

The skills you're building now — reading labels, estimating portions, understanding which foods affect blood sugar and how — are skills your child will eventually learn too. You're laying the groundwork for a lifetime of food confidence.

That's worth celebrating, even on the days when dinner ends up on the floor.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start solids around 6 months** based on developmental readiness — Type 1 diabetes does not change the recommended timeline.
2. **Early weaning portions are tiny**, and the carb impact is often small. Begin with low-carb vegetables to give yourself room to learn.
3. **Post-meal insulin dosing** is often recommended during weaning, since actual intake is impossible to predict in advance.
4. **Commercial baby food labels** are helpful — read them carefully and note that carb content varies widely between products and flavours.
5. **Perfection is not the goal.** Approximation, flexibility, and regular contact with your diabetes team and dietitian will get you through.
