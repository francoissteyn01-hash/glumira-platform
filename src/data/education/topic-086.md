# Building a Support Network — Who to Call at 3am

**Topic ID:** 086
**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people living with T1D, parents, caregivers, and family members
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## You don't have to do this alone. But it can feel like you do.

It's 3am. The alarm has gone off. The glucose is 2.8 and dropping. You're on the floor of your child's bedroom with juice and glucose tabs, hands shaking, heart pounding. You know what to do — you've done it before. You'll do it again. But in this moment, what you want more than anything is someone who understands. Someone you could text who wouldn't reply with "What's a hypo?" Someone who would just say, "I've been there. You're doing great. It's going to be okay."

That person exists. You just might not have found them yet.

Building a support network when you're living with T1D — or caring for someone who is — is not a luxury. It is as essential as insulin, as necessary as a working pump. Because this condition doesn't just challenge your body. It challenges your mind, your relationships, your sense of self. And human beings are not built to carry that alone.

## Why support networks matter — the evidence

This isn't just common sense. Research consistently demonstrates that social support is one of the strongest predictors of psychological wellbeing and diabetes management outcomes. People with T1D who have strong support networks show:

- Lower rates of diabetes burnout and distress
- Better glycaemic outcomes (though this is not the only measure that matters)
- Reduced anxiety and depression
- Greater resilience during difficult periods — diagnosis, transitions, complications
- Improved quality of life across all age groups

For caregivers, the effect is equally significant. Parents with access to peer support report lower levels of caregiver fatigue, better sleep quality (even when the overnight demands are the same), and greater confidence in managing their child's condition.

Support doesn't change the reality. But it changes what the reality does to you.

## The layers of a support network

Not every person in your network serves the same function. Think of support in layers:

**Layer 1: Your inner circle.**
These are the people who know everything. Who have seen you cry, who understand the terminology, who you can call at 3am without apology. This might be a partner, a parent, a best friend, or another T1D family. You may have zero people in this layer right now. That's okay. The goal is to build towards having at least one or two.

**Layer 2: Your informed network.**
These are people who understand the basics — a colleague who knows you might need to step out for a low, a neighbour who can collect your other child if there's an emergency, a teacher who's been properly briefed. They don't carry the emotional weight, but they carry practical weight. They make the logistical machinery of your life function.

**Layer 3: Your community.**
These are the people who share the experience. Other T1D families. Adults living with T1D. Online groups. Local meetups. They may not know your name, but they know your 3am. They know the feeling of a failed sensor, a stubborn high, a birthday party negotiation. This layer provides something the other layers often cannot: **normalisation.** The powerful, healing experience of being unremarkable. Of not having to explain.

**Layer 4: Professional support.**
Your diabetes team — endocrinologist, diabetes nurse educator, dietitian. And beyond them: psychologists, counsellors, social workers who specialise in chronic illness. These relationships are not personal friendships, but they are vital components of your support infrastructure.

## Where to find your people

**Online communities.**
The T1D online space is vast, active, and often extraordinarily generous. Platforms include:

- Facebook groups dedicated to T1D parenting, adult T1D, specific pump and CGM users, and country-specific communities
- Reddit communities (r/diabetes_t1, r/parents, and others) where honest conversations happen daily
- Instagram and TikTok accounts run by people living with T1D who share real, unfiltered experiences
- Dedicated forums and apps like Beyond Type 1, Diabetes UK community, and JDRF Connect

A word of caution: online communities can also be sources of misinformation, comparison, and judgement. Choose spaces that feel supportive rather than competitive. If a group makes you feel worse, leave it. Your wellbeing matters more than your membership.

**Local and national organisations.**
- **JDRF (Breakthrough T1D):** Offers family events, mentoring programmes, and advocacy resources in multiple countries.
- **Diabetes UK:** Provides helplines, local groups, and online support across the United Kingdom.
- **Diabetes South Africa (DSA):** Offers resources, camps, and community connections.
- **Beyond Type 1:** Global resources, community features, and educational content.
- **Local hospital-based support groups:** Many diabetes centres run regular meet-ups for families. Ask your team.

**T1D camps and events.**
For children and teenagers, T1D camps are among the most powerful support experiences available. For the first time, they are in a room where everyone checks their glucose. Where no one stares at the pump. Where they are not the odd one out. The impact of this experience — of being normal for a week — cannot be overstated.

For adults, T1D retreats and conferences serve a similar function. Look for events run by T1D organisations in your region.

**Parent-to-parent networks.**
Some diabetes centres operate formal parent mentoring programmes, pairing newly diagnosed families with experienced ones. If yours doesn't, ask if they can connect you informally. Sometimes one conversation with a family who is two years ahead of you changes everything.

## Professional support — when and why

Building a support network is not a substitute for professional help when it's needed. Consider seeking professional support if:

- You are experiencing persistent anxiety, depression, or grief
- Your relationships are being significantly affected by the demands of T1D
- You or your child are showing signs of burnout or disordered eating related to diabetes management
- You need mediation or guidance around shared caregiving responsibilities
- You simply need a space to talk where the focus is entirely on you

Psychologists, counsellors, and social workers who understand chronic illness can offer evidence-based strategies for managing distress. Some specialise specifically in diabetes psychology — a growing and important field.

Your diabetes team can usually refer you, or you can search through professional directories for therapists with chronic illness experience.

## How to ask for help when you've forgotten how

If you've been doing this alone for a long time, asking for help can feel impossible. You've become the strong one, the capable one, the one who manages. Admitting that you're struggling feels like a failure.

It isn't. It is the most rational thing you can do.

Start small:
- Text one person: "I'm having a hard day."
- Join one online group and read without posting for a while. When you're ready, say hello.
- Tell your diabetes nurse: "I'm struggling emotionally. Can you point me towards support?"
- Accept one offer of help — even if you have to fight the urge to say "I'm fine."

You don't have to build your entire network in a week. You just have to take one step away from isolation.

## Your people are out there

Right now, somewhere, another parent is sitting on a child's bedroom floor at 3am, juice box in hand, wondering if anyone else in the world understands. A teenager is staring at a CGM reading in a school bathroom, feeling completely alone. An adult is standing in a pharmacy queue, fighting back tears over a prescription that costs more than they can afford.

They exist. You exist. And when you find each other — in a Facebook group, at a clinic waiting room, at a camp, through a friend of a friend — something shifts. The weight doesn't disappear. But it distributes. And distributed weight is survivable weight.

You were never meant to carry this alone.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Social support is one of the strongest predictors of wellbeing in T1D** — it is not a luxury but a clinical necessity for both people with T1D and their caregivers.
2. **An effective support network has multiple layers** — from an inner circle who knows everything, to informed helpers, to peer communities, to professional support.
3. **Online T1D communities, national organisations, and camps are powerful resources** — they provide normalisation, practical advice, and connection with people who understand.
4. **Professional psychological support is available and important** — especially for persistent anxiety, depression, grief, or burnout related to T1D.
5. **Asking for help is not failure — it is the most rational response to an irrational load** — start small, and know that your people are out there waiting to be found.
