# Teething, Sleep Regression, and Blood Sugar Chaos

**Age range:** 0-3 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 18
**Category:** Developmental milestones and blood sugar

---

It's 3am. Your baby is screaming, drooling, chewing their fist, and refusing to be comforted. Their CGM is alarming because blood sugar is climbing for no apparent reason. You haven't slept properly in four days.

Is it a tooth? Is it the diabetes? Is it both? Is it possible to lose your mind from teething-related sleep deprivation? (Asking for a friend.)

If you're reading this through bleary, exhausted eyes — you're doing great. Truly. This is a brutal phase, and it does end.

Let's talk about what's happening in that angry little mouth and why it's wrecking your glucose graphs.

## Teething and the Stress Response

Teething hurts. We know this because babies tell us very clearly with their behaviour, even if they can't use words. The pain and inflammation of teeth cutting through gum tissue triggers the body's stress response.

When the body is stressed — from pain, discomfort, poor sleep, or all three — it releases **cortisol** and other stress hormones. These hormones have a direct impact on blood sugar:

- **Cortisol increases insulin resistance.** The body's cells respond less effectively to insulin, causing blood sugar to rise.
- **Adrenaline triggers glucose release from the liver.** The body dumps stored glucose into the bloodstream as part of the fight-or-flight response.
- **Disrupted sleep amplifies the hormonal effect.** Sleep deprivation independently increases cortisol levels, creating a compounding effect.

So when your teething baby has unexplained high blood sugars, this is the mechanism. It's not something you're doing wrong. It's biochemistry responding to pain and stress.

## The Sleep Regression Layer

Sleep regressions are periods when a baby who was sleeping reasonably well suddenly starts waking frequently, fighting sleep, or sleeping for shorter stretches. They're a normal part of development and typically occur around:

- 4 months
- 8-10 months
- 12 months
- 18 months
- 2 years

These regressions often coincide with teething, which means you get the double impact: developmental sleep disruption plus teething pain.

For a baby with Type 1 diabetes, disrupted sleep creates a cascade of problems:

- **Stress hormones stay elevated** when sleep is fragmented, keeping blood sugar higher than usual.
- **Feeding patterns change.** A baby who's teething and sleeping badly often eats less during the day and feeds more at night. This shifts the carbohydrate distribution and can make insulin timing harder.
- **Overnight monitoring becomes harder.** You're waking for teething comfort and glucose checks and maybe a feed, and after a while all the wakings blur together.
- **Parent fatigue impairs decision-making.** Research consistently shows that sleep deprivation affects cognitive function, which is unhelpful when you're making medical calculations at 2am.

## What the Blood Sugar Pattern Might Look Like

During teething episodes, you may see:

- **Higher than usual blood sugars**, particularly overnight and in the early morning
- **More variability** — swings between higher highs and lower lows as the stress response waxes and wanes
- **Resistance to usual correction doses** — the insulin seems to be working less effectively
- **Unexplained lows** once the worst of the teething pain passes and cortisol drops back down

Not every tooth causes the same disruption. Some teeth (particularly molars) cause more pain and therefore more stress hormone release than others. You may find that some teething episodes barely affect blood sugar while others create days of chaos.

## Managing Blood Sugar During Teething

There is no magic solution here, but there are strategies that can help:

**Monitor more frequently.** During active teething, check blood sugar more often than usual. If your child has a CGM, pay close attention to trends and set tighter alarm thresholds if that's helpful for you.

**Expect higher readings and don't chase every spike.** A few days of slightly elevated blood sugars during teething, while not ideal, are a known phenomenon. Aggressively correcting every high can lead to lows once the teething pain subsides.

**Manage the pain.** Effective pain relief reduces the stress response, which in turn reduces the hormonal impact on blood sugar. Age-appropriate pain relief (paracetamol or ibuprofen as recommended by your pharmacist or doctor) can help with both comfort and glucose stability.

- Note: liquid medications often contain sugar. Check the label and factor the carbs in if relevant.
- Teething gels, cold teething rings, and chilled foods (for babies on solids) are non-medical options that can complement pain relief.

**Keep your diabetes team informed.** If you're seeing a pattern of persistently elevated blood sugars coinciding with teething, let your team know. They can advise on whether temporary adjustments are appropriate.

**Lower your expectations.** This is temporary. The goal during teething is safe management, not perfect numbers. If your child's blood sugar is staying out of the dangerous ranges (no severe lows, no DKA), you are succeeding.

## The Cortisol Connection Beyond Teething

Understanding the cortisol-blood sugar link during teething helps you recognise the same pattern in other stressful situations:

- Vaccinations (pain and immune response)
- Ear infections and other minor illnesses
- Developmental leaps (frustration, separation anxiety)
- Starting childcare (emotional stress)
- Any situation causing sustained discomfort or distress

The mechanism is the same: stress triggers cortisol, cortisol drives insulin resistance, blood sugar rises. Recognising this pattern means you'll be better prepared for it each time.

## Night-Time Survival Strategies

The combination of teething wake-ups and diabetes monitoring can push parents to breaking point. Some strategies for getting through:

- **Tag-team if possible.** If you have a partner, split the night. One person handles teething comfort (rocking, medication, cuddles) while the other handles glucose management (checking, treating lows or highs). Swap on alternate nights.
- **Pre-prepare hypo treatments.** Have juice boxes, glucose tablets, or whatever your team recommends ready on the bedside table so you're not stumbling to the kitchen in the dark.
- **Use CGM data to guide wake-ups.** If your child's CGM shows a stable, in-range trend, you may be able to skip a scheduled finger prick check and get an extra hour of sleep. Discuss with your team what thresholds warrant a wake-up versus what can wait.
- **Accept help.** If a family member or friend offers to take a shift, let them. Brief them on what to watch for and give yourself permission to sleep.
- **Nap when you can.** The housework can wait. The laundry can wait. Sleep cannot.

## When Teething Ends (For Each Tooth)

Once a tooth breaks through the gum, the pain typically subsides within a few days. When this happens, watch for:

- Blood sugars dropping as cortisol levels normalise
- Increased sensitivity to insulin (readings that were fine during teething may suddenly be too low)
- Appetite returning to normal (which changes carb intake)
- Sleep improving (which further normalises hormones)

This settling period is another time to monitor closely. The transition from stress to non-stress can cause lows if insulin doses were increased during the teething phase.

## The Timeline

Most children have all 20 primary teeth by age 2.5-3 years. That's 20 potential episodes of teething-related blood sugar disruption. Some will be barely noticeable. Some will be memorable for all the wrong reasons.

After the last tooth comes through, you get a break — until the 6-year molars arrive. But by then, your child will be older, better able to communicate their pain, and the management tools will be different.

## It's Temporary

This is worth repeating because at 3am it doesn't feel true: **this is temporary.** The tooth will come through. The sleep will improve. The blood sugars will settle. You will sleep again.

You are navigating one of the hardest intersections of normal parenting and chronic disease management. The fact that you're doing it at all — let alone reading about it to do it better — is remarkable.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Teething triggers cortisol and stress hormones** that increase insulin resistance and cause blood sugars to rise — this is a physiological response, not a management error.
2. **Sleep regressions compound the problem** by further elevating cortisol and disrupting feeding and monitoring routines.
3. **Managing teething pain effectively** (with age-appropriate pain relief) can help reduce the hormonal impact on blood sugar.
4. **Expect temporary blood sugar disruption** during active teething and watch for lows once the tooth breaks through and stress hormones settle.
5. **Prioritise survival over perfection.** Safe blood sugar ranges during teething episodes are the goal. Perfect numbers are not required and not realistic.
