# First Time Managing T1D Alone — Home Alone Protocols

**Topic ID:** 033
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** All (children, parents, and caregivers)
**Category:** Independence & Safety
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## A Big Step — And You're Ready to Think About It

There comes a moment in every T1D family's life when the question surfaces: "Can they manage on their own for a bit?"

Maybe it's an hour after school before you get home from work. Maybe it's a Saturday morning while you run errands. Maybe it's the first time they'll be truly home alone, responsible for their own blood glucose checks, their own snacks, their own decisions.

This is a big step. It's also a normal, healthy, necessary step. Independence doesn't arrive all at once — it's built through preparation, practice, and trust. This topic is about helping your family get ready, together.

## Before the First Time: Are They Ready?

Readiness for managing T1D independently — even for short periods — involves several skills working together:

**They can check their own blood glucose reliably.** Whether using a finger-prick meter or a continuous glucose monitor (CGM), your child should be able to obtain an accurate reading and understand what the number means in broad terms: in range, too low, too high.

**They know what to do for a low.** This is the single most important safety skill. Your child should be able to recognise the symptoms of hypoglycaemia in their own body, know where fast-acting carbohydrates are kept, know how much to take, and know to recheck after 15 minutes.

**They can reach you.** A charged phone, a memorised number, or a reliable way to contact you (or another trusted adult) at any time is non-negotiable.

**They understand when to call for help.** Not every situation requires a parent's intervention — but some absolutely do. Your child needs to know the difference.

## The Home Alone Checklist

Before your child manages independently for the first time, work through this checklist together. Make it a conversation, not a lecture. Let them tell you the answers — that's how you'll know they truly understand.

**Phone and contacts.** Their phone is charged and within reach. Your number is accessible. A backup adult's number is saved (a neighbour, relative, or family friend who knows about their T1D). Emergency services number is known.

**Glucose supplies are accessible.** Fast-acting carbohydrates (glucose tablets, juice boxes, or whatever your family uses) are in an agreed, easy-to-find location. Your child knows exactly where they are and can get to them quickly.

**CGM or meter is working.** If they use a CGM, the sensor is active and the receiver or phone app is functioning. If they use a meter, test strips are available and the meter has battery. A backup plan exists if technology fails.

**Snacks and meals are planned.** If your child will need to eat while alone, the food is prepared or clearly identified. Carbohydrate counts are noted if relevant. This isn't the time for guesswork — keep it simple and familiar.

**They know the house rules.** This includes diabetes-specific rules (check BG at agreed times, don't ignore a CGM alarm) and general safety rules (don't answer the door to strangers, don't use the stove unsupervised — whatever applies in your household).

**A glucagon kit is accessible and they know its location.** Even if they cannot administer it themselves, knowing where it is means anyone who comes to help can find it quickly.

## When to Call a Parent

Help your child understand these clear triggers for calling you:

- **Blood glucose below [the threshold your diabetes team has set] that doesn't come up after treatment.** One round of fast-acting carbs, wait 15 minutes, recheck — if still low, call.
- **Blood glucose very high with symptoms** — nausea, vomiting, stomach pain, fruity-smelling breath. These could indicate diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and need adult attention.
- **They feel unwell and aren't sure why.** Uncertainty is always a reason to call. Always. Tell your child this clearly: "If you're not sure, call me. I will never be annoyed that you called."
- **Technology failure.** CGM stops reading, pump malfunctions, insulin pen is damaged. These are problems that need an adult's input.
- **They feel scared or overwhelmed.** This is just as valid a reason to call as any medical situation. Being alone with a chronic condition can feel heavy sometimes, and reaching out is a sign of strength, not weakness.

## Practice Runs Build Real Confidence

Don't make the first solo session the real thing. Do practice runs.

**Round one: You're in the next room.** Your child manages a BG check, a snack, and a simulated "what would you do if..." scenario while you're nearby but not intervening. Debrief together afterwards.

**Round two: You're outside or in the garden.** A small increase in distance. They can still reach you in seconds, but they're making decisions independently.

**Round three: A short errand.** You leave for 20–30 minutes. Phone lines are open. When you return, talk through what happened — not with interrogation, but with genuine interest. "How did it go? Anything come up?"

**Round four: A longer stretch.** Gradually extend the time as confidence builds. There's no rush.

## A Note for Young People Reading This

If you're the one who's going to be home alone — Mira wants you to know something. Being trusted to manage your T1D by yourself is a genuinely impressive thing. Not every kid your age could do it. It takes knowledge, responsibility, and courage.

But here's the other thing: being independent doesn't mean being alone with everything. The strongest, most capable adults in the world still ask for help when they need it. You calling your parent, your diabetes nurse, or a trusted adult is not a step backward. It's the smartest thing you can do when something feels off.

You've got this. And you've got people behind you.

## A Note for Parents

Letting go — even a little — is hard. You've spent years being the frontline manager of your child's T1D, and stepping back can feel deeply uncomfortable. You might hover by your phone. You might drive home faster than necessary. You might ask seventeen questions when you walk through the door.

That's all normal. Give yourself grace, too.

The goal isn't to stop worrying. The goal is to prepare your child so thoroughly that your worry is manageable — and their confidence is genuine. Every successful solo session is a brick in the foundation of the independent, capable person they're becoming.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Readiness for independent management requires specific skills** — BG checking, hypo treatment, communication ability, and knowing when to call for help.
2. **Create a clear, simple checklist together** covering phone access, glucose supplies, food plans, and emergency protocols.
3. **Define explicit "call me" triggers** so your child never has to guess whether a situation warrants reaching out.
4. **Use graduated practice runs** — starting with you nearby and slowly increasing distance and duration.
5. **Independence is built in layers** — there is no rush, and asking for help is always the right call when something feels wrong.
