# Glycaemic Index for T1D — Which Carbs Hit Fastest

## Not All Carbs Are Created Equal

You have counted your carbs carefully. You know that your plate of rice has about 45g of carbohydrate and your bowl of lentil soup has about 40g. Nearly the same number. But when you check your glucose an hour later, the rice has sent you soaring while the lentil soup has produced a gentle, manageable rise. What happened?

The answer is the Glycaemic Index — and understanding it will change how you think about every carbohydrate you eat.

## What Is the Glycaemic Index?

The Glycaemic Index (GI) is a ranking system that measures how quickly a carbohydrate-containing food raises blood glucose after eating. It uses a scale from 0 to 100, where pure glucose is the reference point at 100.

- **Low GI (55 or less):** Slow, gradual glucose rise
- **Medium GI (56–69):** Moderate glucose rise
- **High GI (70 or above):** Fast, sharp glucose rise

The GI tells you about the *speed* of glucose release, not the *amount*. That is an important distinction. A food can be low in total carbs but high in GI, or high in total carbs but low in GI. For effective T1D management, you need both pieces of information — how much carbohydrate (carb counting, Topic 61) and how fast it hits (GI).

## Why GI Matters for Type 1 Diabetes

In a body without Type 1 Diabetes, the pancreas releases insulin in real time, perfectly matching the speed of glucose entering the bloodstream. In T1D, injected or pumped insulin cannot respond with that same precision. There is always a timing gap.

This is where GI becomes practical:

- **High-GI foods** release glucose faster than most rapid-acting insulin can work. This often creates a sharp spike followed by a correction low — the glucose rollercoaster.
- **Low-GI foods** release glucose more slowly, giving insulin a better chance to match the pace. This tends to produce flatter, more stable glucose curves.
- **Medium-GI foods** fall somewhere in between and are often the easiest to manage day-to-day.

Understanding GI helps you predict not just *how much* your glucose will rise, but *when* and *how sharply* — and that changes your entire approach to meal planning and timing.

## How to Use GI in Practice

### Pair High-GI with Low-GI

You do not need to eliminate high-GI foods. Instead, combine them with low-GI foods, protein, fat, or fibre to lower the overall GI of the meal.

**Example:** White rice (high GI) served with lentil dal (low GI) and vegetables produces a much more moderate glucose curve than white rice eaten alone. The lentils and fibre slow down the overall absorption.

**Example:** A banana (medium GI) eaten with peanut butter (very low GI, high fat and protein) will produce a gentler glucose rise than the banana eaten on its own.

### Choose Lower-GI Swaps

Sometimes a simple swap makes a meaningful difference:

- White rice → basmati rice or brown rice
- White bread → sourdough or heavy rye bread
- Instant oats → traditional rolled oats
- Cornflakes → bran cereal or muesli
- Baked potato → sweet potato or boiled new potatoes
- Jasmine rice → long-grain or parboiled rice

These swaps do not change the carb count dramatically, but they change the speed of glucose delivery — and speed is everything when you are trying to match insulin timing.

### Consider GI During Ramadan

During Ramadan, the Suhoor (pre-dawn) meal benefits enormously from low-GI food choices. Low-GI foods release glucose slowly, which helps sustain energy and maintain more stable glucose levels through the fasting hours. Traditional rolled oats, lentils, yoghurt, and whole grains are excellent Suhoor choices.

At Iftar, many traditional foods — dates, white rice, fried items — are higher GI. Knowing this helps you plan your approach and discuss timing strategies with your healthcare team.

### GI and the Bernstein Approach

Those following the Bernstein low-carb approach are already limiting total carbohydrate intake significantly. Within that smaller carb allowance, GI still matters. A small amount of a high-GI food can produce a sharper spike than the same carb amount from a low-GI source, even at low total quantities. Choosing low-GI vegetables, nuts, and dairy within the Bernstein framework helps maintain the tight glucose control that approach aims for.

## Factors That Change GI

The GI of a food is not fixed. Several factors can raise or lower it:

1. **Cooking time and method.** Al dente pasta (GI ~46) has a lower GI than well-cooked, soft pasta (GI ~55+). Boiled potatoes have a lower GI than baked potatoes.

2. **Ripeness.** A green banana has a lower GI than a ripe, spotted banana. The starches convert to sugars as fruit ripens.

3. **Processing.** Whole rolled oats have a lower GI than instant oats. The more processed a food is, the faster your body can break it down.

4. **What you eat alongside it.** Adding fat, protein, or fibre to a high-GI food lowers the overall meal GI. This is the practical principle behind balanced meals (see Topic 62 on protein and fat effects, and Topic 68 on fibre).

5. **Cooling and reheating.** Cooled and reheated starchy foods — cold potato salad, reheated rice — develop "resistant starch" that slightly lowers GI.

## GI Is a Guide, Not a Rule

Mira always reminds: the GI is a helpful tool, but it is not the only factor. A food with a low GI might still have a very high total carb content. Pasta, for instance, has a moderate-to-low GI, but a large serving still delivers substantial carbohydrate that requires careful management.

The most effective approach combines carb counting (Topic 61) with GI awareness. Know how much carbohydrate you are eating *and* how fast it is likely to arrive.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The Glycaemic Index measures how quickly a carbohydrate raises blood glucose** — not how much carbohydrate a food contains.
2. **High-GI foods (70+) produce rapid glucose spikes** that are harder to match with insulin timing, while low-GI foods (55 or less) produce slower, more manageable rises.
3. **Simple swaps — basmati for jasmine rice, rolled oats for instant, sourdough for white bread — can meaningfully improve glucose curves** without changing what you enjoy eating.
4. **Combining high-GI foods with low-GI foods, protein, fat, or fibre lowers the overall meal GI** — balanced plates are naturally easier to manage.
5. **Cooking method, ripeness, and processing all affect GI** — the same food can behave differently depending on how it is prepared.
