# Puberty and Insulin Resistance — The Hormone Rollercoaster

**Topic ID:** 031
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** All (children, parents, and caregivers)
**Category:** Growth & Development
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## A Body That's Changing in Every Way

Hey there. If you're reading this, there's a good chance someone in your house is growing — fast. Shoes that fit last month are suddenly too small, moods are shifting like weather, and blood glucose numbers that used to behave are now doing their own thing.

Welcome to puberty with Type 1 diabetes.

First, let's take a breath together. Puberty is completely normal and completely healthy. Every human body goes through it. But when you're also managing T1D, puberty adds an extra layer of complexity that can feel frustrating, confusing, and sometimes even scary. That's OK. You're not doing anything wrong. Your body is just doing a lot at once.

## What Happens During Puberty — And Why Insulin Gets Involved

During puberty, the body releases a surge of hormones — primarily growth hormone, oestrogen, and testosterone. These hormones are essential. They drive the growth spurts, the changes in body shape, the development of secondary sexual characteristics, and all the other milestones that come with growing up.

Here's the thing: growth hormone, in particular, has a direct effect on how insulin works in the body. Growth hormone makes cells less responsive to insulin. In medical terms, this is called **insulin resistance**. It means the same amount of insulin that worked perfectly well six months ago may no longer be enough to keep blood glucose in range.

This isn't a failure. It's biology.

For young people without T1D, the pancreas simply produces more insulin to compensate. For young people with T1D, the body can't do that — so the insulin doses from injections or a pump need to be adjusted to match what the body now requires.

## The Dawn Phenomenon Gets Louder

You may have already heard of the **dawn phenomenon** — that early morning rise in blood glucose caused by hormones released in the hours before waking. During puberty, this effect often becomes more pronounced.

Growth hormone is released in pulses during deep sleep, and puberty amplifies those pulses. The result? Morning blood glucose readings that seem to climb for no reason, even when everything was stable at bedtime.

If you're seeing higher fasting numbers or waking up out of range more often, puberty hormones are very likely a contributing factor. This is one of the most common patterns diabetes teams see in this age group, and it can be addressed with guidance from your healthcare team.

## Dose Increases Are Normal — Really Normal

Here's a number that sometimes surprises families: during puberty, it is entirely typical for total daily insulin doses to increase by **20 to 50 percent**. Some young people need even more than that during peak growth periods.

Let that sink in for a moment. If your child's insulin needs have jumped significantly, it does not mean their diabetes is "getting worse." It does not mean they've been eating too much or making poor choices. It means their body is growing, hormones are surging, and insulin needs are rising in response.

Parents, this is a moment where your reassurance matters enormously. Pre-teens who see their doses climbing can feel anxious or even guilty — as though they've done something to cause it. They haven't. Remind them of that, clearly and often.

And young people — if you're reading this yourself — hear this from Mira: **your body is supposed to grow. The insulin is just keeping up with you.**

## The Emotional Side of the Rollercoaster

Puberty is emotionally intense for everyone. Add T1D management on top of hormonal mood swings, and you've got a recipe for some really tough days.

Blood glucose levels can directly affect mood. High glucose can cause irritability, fatigue, and difficulty concentrating. Low glucose can cause anxiety, shakiness, and emotional sensitivity. When those effects layer on top of the natural emotional intensity of puberty, it can be genuinely hard to tell what's causing what.

This is important for both parents and young people to understand. Sometimes a meltdown is puberty. Sometimes it's a blood glucose swing. Sometimes it's both. And sometimes it's just a hard day — because being 11 or 12 with a chronic condition is allowed to be hard.

Be patient with each other. If emotions are running high, checking blood glucose before having a serious conversation is a small step that can make a big difference.

## What You Can Do Together

Managing puberty and T1D is a team effort. Here are some practical approaches that can help:

**Track the patterns.** Puberty doesn't hit all at once — it comes in waves. Growth spurts may last weeks, then plateau. Keep an eye on trends over days and weeks rather than reacting to every individual reading. Logging can help your diabetes team spot the patterns and make smarter adjustments.

**Communicate with your diabetes team.** This is a phase where more frequent check-ins with your endocrinologist or diabetes educator can be genuinely helpful. Don't wait for the scheduled appointment if numbers have shifted significantly. A quick phone call or message can often lead to a dose adjustment that makes the next few weeks much smoother.

**Expect variability.** Some days will be in range. Some won't. Puberty introduces a level of unpredictability that even the most diligent management can't fully control. Aiming for perfection during this phase is a recipe for burnout. Aim for "good enough most of the time" and be kind to yourselves on the rest.

**Talk about it.** Young people, if your numbers are frustrating you, say so. Parents, if you're worried, name it gently. Silence around diabetes struggles tends to make everyone feel more alone. You're not alone — and this phase, while challenging, is temporary.

## Looking Ahead

Puberty does end. The hormonal surges stabilise, insulin resistance often eases (though it may not return to pre-puberty levels), and the wild swings in glucose tend to settle. Many families look back on the puberty years as the hardest stretch of T1D management — and they get through it.

You will, too.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Puberty hormones — especially growth hormone — cause insulin resistance**, making blood glucose harder to manage even when nothing else has changed.
2. **The dawn phenomenon often worsens during puberty** due to amplified growth hormone release during sleep.
3. **A 20–50% increase in total daily insulin needs is entirely normal** during puberty and does not mean diabetes is worsening.
4. **Emotional intensity is compounded** by the overlap of puberty mood changes and blood glucose fluctuations — patience and communication are essential.
5. **Frequent contact with your diabetes healthcare team** during this phase allows timely dose adjustments and reduces frustration.
