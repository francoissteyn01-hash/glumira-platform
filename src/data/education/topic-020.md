# Emergency Glucagon for Small Children — Practice Before You Need It

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 20
**Category:** Emergency preparedness

---

Nobody wants to think about emergencies. Especially not emergencies involving your small child, a needle (or a nasal device), and the words "severe hypoglycaemia."

But here's the thing about glucagon: the time to learn how to use it is not when you need it. The time to learn is now, while you're calm, while the lights are on, and while your child is perfectly fine and probably trying to eat a crayon.

You're doing great. Reading this topic is an act of preparation, not panic. Let's make sure you're ready for something that will most likely never happen — and if it does, you'll know exactly what to do.

## What Is Glucagon and Why Does It Matter?

Glucagon is a hormone that your body naturally produces. Its job is the opposite of insulin: while insulin lowers blood sugar, glucagon raises it. It does this by signalling the liver to release stored glucose (glycogen) into the bloodstream.

In a severe hypoglycaemic event — when blood sugar drops so low that a child becomes unconscious, has a seizure, or is unable to eat or drink safely — **glucagon is the emergency treatment.** It's what you use when oral glucose isn't an option because the child can't swallow.

Every family managing Type 1 diabetes in a young child should have glucagon available at all times. This means at home, in the changing bag, and anywhere your child spends regular time (grandparents' house, daycare).

## Types of Glucagon Available

Glucagon has traditionally come in one form: a powder-and-liquid kit that needs to be mixed before injection. In recent years, newer, easier-to-use formulations have become available.

**Traditional glucagon injection kit:**
- Contains a vial of glucagon powder and a syringe pre-filled with diluent
- Requires mixing: inject the diluent into the vial, swirl to dissolve, draw the solution back into the syringe, then inject
- Effective and well-established
- The mixing process can be stressful under pressure, particularly with shaking hands

**Nasal glucagon:**
- A dry powder delivered via a nasal device — no injection, no mixing
- Insert into one nostril and press the plunger
- Does not require the child to inhale — the powder is absorbed through the nasal lining
- Simpler to administer in an emergency
- Particularly helpful for caregivers who are uncomfortable with needles

**Pre-mixed glucagon auto-injector:**
- A ready-to-use injection — no mixing required
- Similar concept to an adrenaline auto-injector (EpiPen)
- Simpler than the traditional kit under pressure

Availability varies by country and healthcare system. Your diabetes team will advise on which formulation is available to you and appropriate for your child.

## Weight-Based Considerations

Glucagon dosing for young children is based on body weight. This is a critical point and one of the reasons you must discuss the correct dose with your diabetes team in advance.

**General guidance (always confirm with your team):**

- Children under approximately 25kg (or under about 6-8 years of age) typically receive a reduced dose
- The specific dose depends on your child's weight and the formulation being used
- Your diabetes team will tell you the exact dose for your child and it should be documented in your child's care plan

**Nasal glucagon** is typically supplied as a single fixed dose. Your team will advise on whether this is appropriate for your child's size and weight.

It is essential that the correct dose for your child is written on the glucagon packaging (or on a label attached to it) and included in their emergency care plan. In an emergency, you or another caregiver should not have to calculate anything — the information should be immediately visible.

## When to Use Glucagon

Glucagon is for severe hypoglycaemia. This means:

- **The child is unconscious or unresponsive**
- **The child is having a seizure**
- **The child is unable to swallow safely** (choking risk if you tried to give oral glucose)
- **The child is not responding to initial hypo treatment** and is deteriorating

Glucagon is **not** for:

- Mild lows where the child is awake, alert, and able to eat or drink
- Routine blood sugar management
- "Just in case" situations where blood sugar is a bit low but the child is functioning normally

If your child is conscious and able to swallow, fast-acting oral glucose (juice, glucose tablets, glucose gel) remains the first-line treatment for hypoglycaemia.

## How to Practise

This is the most important section of this topic. Please read it twice.

**You need to practise using glucagon before you ever need it for real.** In an emergency, fine motor skills deteriorate, hands shake, and the step-by-step instructions on the packaging become impossible to read through tears.

**How to practise with the traditional injection kit:**

Your diabetes team or diabetes educator can provide expired or training kits for practice. Some teams run dedicated glucagon training sessions. If yours doesn't, ask for one.

Practice should include:
- Opening the kit under pressure (fumbling with packaging is real)
- Mixing the powder and diluent (the vial is small, and drawing up liquid accurately while panicked is difficult)
- Drawing up the correct dose for your child's weight
- Administering the injection (your team will show you where and how)
- Placing the child in the recovery position after injection

**How to practise with nasal glucagon:**

Nasal glucagon devices are simpler, but still practise:
- Opening the packaging quickly
- Holding the device correctly
- Inserting it into the nostril and pressing the plunger
- Understanding that you don't need the child to inhale

**How to practise with auto-injector glucagon:**

Training devices (without medication) are often available:
- Practise removing the safety cap
- Pressing the device against the skin in the correct location
- Holding it in place for the required duration

**Who else should practise:**

Everyone who regularly cares for your child should know how to administer glucagon:
- Your partner
- Grandparents
- Babysitters
- Daycare staff
- Anyone who might be alone with your child

A glucagon kit that nobody knows how to use is not much better than no glucagon kit at all.

## After Glucagon Is Given

Knowing what happens after administration is part of being prepared:

- **Glucagon typically takes 10-15 minutes to work.** Blood sugar should begin to rise within this time.
- **Call an ambulance.** Even if your child starts to recover after glucagon, call emergency services. A child who needed glucagon needs medical assessment.
- **Place the child in the recovery position** (on their side) in case they vomit. Vomiting after glucagon is common.
- **Once the child is conscious and able to swallow,** offer oral carbohydrates to help maintain the blood sugar rise. Small, frequent sips of juice or bites of a snack.
- **Do not give anything by mouth while the child is unconscious.** This is a choking risk.
- **Note the time glucagon was given** and the dose. Emergency services will need this information.

## Storage and Expiry

Glucagon is useless if it's expired or improperly stored:

- **Check expiry dates regularly.** Set a reminder in your phone every 3 months to check.
- **Store according to manufacturer instructions.** Most formulations should be stored at room temperature (below 25-30 degrees C) and protected from light. Some require refrigeration — check the specific product.
- **Replace before it expires.** Order the replacement in advance so there's no gap in coverage.
- **Keep it accessible.** Glucagon locked in a medicine cabinet on the top shelf is not helpful in an emergency. It should be in a known location that any caregiver can reach quickly.

## The Emotional Reality

Let's address the fear directly. The idea of your child being unconscious from a low blood sugar is terrifying. Many parents describe keeping glucagon as both a comfort (it's there if I need it) and a source of anxiety (what if I need it?).

Some things that might help:

- **Practising reduces fear.** The more familiar you are with the device, the less frightening the hypothetical emergency becomes.
- **Severe hypos in well-managed T1D are uncommon.** Most families never need to use glucagon. Having it is a precaution, not a prediction.
- **You are not expected to be a paramedic.** You are expected to give glucagon and call for help. That's it. The professionals take it from there.
- **It's okay to feel scared.** Fear in this context is rational. It doesn't make you weak or anxious — it makes you a parent who understands the stakes.

## Building Your Emergency Plan

Glucagon is one part of a broader emergency preparedness plan. Make sure you also have:

- Fast-acting glucose treatment accessible in every room of your house and in every bag you take out
- Emergency contact numbers saved in your phone and displayed at home
- A written emergency plan that any caregiver can follow
- Medical identification for your child (bracelet, shoe tag, or similar)
- A clear understanding of when to call an ambulance

Review this plan every 6 months. Update it when doses change, when new caregivers are added, or when your child starts at a new setting.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Glucagon is the emergency treatment for severe hypoglycaemia** — when a child is unconscious, seizing, or unable to swallow safely.
2. **Multiple formulations exist** (injection kit, nasal device, auto-injector) — your diabetes team will prescribe what's appropriate and advise on the correct weight-based dose for your child.
3. **Practise before you need it.** Every caregiver who is regularly alone with your child should have hands-on practice with the specific glucagon device you carry.
4. **After giving glucagon, call an ambulance**, place the child in the recovery position, and note the time and dose given.
5. **Check expiry dates regularly and keep glucagon accessible** — not locked away, not expired, and not at the bottom of a bag you haven't opened in months.
