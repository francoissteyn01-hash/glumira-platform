## Hello from Mira

If you have ever been mystified by a blood glucose reading that seemed to come from nowhere — high glucose with no obvious cause, no extra carbohydrates, no missed insulin — there is a very good chance that hormones were involved. Not insulin itself, but the other hormones your body produces that work in the opposite direction.

Understanding these **counter-regulatory hormones** is one of the most illuminating things you can learn about life with Type 1 Diabetes. Once you see the connections, many of those "unexplained" glucose spikes suddenly have an explanation.

---

## The counter-regulatory hormone system

In a body without diabetes, blood glucose is maintained within a narrow range by a carefully orchestrated hormonal balancing act. **Insulin** brings glucose down. A group of other hormones — collectively known as **counter-regulatory hormones** — push glucose up.

In a body without T1D, this system is self-correcting. When glucose drops, counter-regulatory hormones kick in to raise it. When glucose rises, the pancreas releases more insulin to bring it down. It is an elegant, automatic feedback loop.

In T1D, half of this system is missing. Your body can still produce counter-regulatory hormones that raise glucose, but it can no longer produce the insulin needed to bring it back down. This is why counter-regulatory hormone surges can cause such dramatic and persistent hyperglycaemia in T1D.

The four main counter-regulatory hormones are:

1. **Cortisol**
2. **Adrenaline (epinephrine)**
3. **Growth hormone**
4. **Glucagon**

Let me walk through each one.

---

## Cortisol — the stress and rhythm hormone

Cortisol is produced by the **adrenal glands**, which sit on top of your kidneys. It is often called the "stress hormone," but that label is incomplete. Cortisol does much more than respond to stress — it follows a **circadian rhythm**, rising naturally in the early morning hours and falling through the day.

### What cortisol does to glucose

Cortisol raises blood glucose through several mechanisms:

- It stimulates the liver to produce glucose through a process called **gluconeogenesis** (literally "making new glucose")
- It reduces the sensitivity of muscle and fat cells to insulin — making existing insulin less effective (increasing **insulin resistance**)
- It promotes the breakdown of glycogen stores in the liver, releasing glucose into the bloodstream

### When cortisol matters most in T1D

- **The dawn phenomenon.** The early-morning rise in cortisol (typically between 4:00 and 8:00 AM) can cause blood glucose to rise before breakfast — even if you have not eaten anything. This is the single most common hormonal glucose challenge in T1D, and it is driven in large part by cortisol working alongside growth hormone.
- **Physical illness.** When the body is fighting infection, cortisol rises as part of the immune and stress response. This is a major reason why blood glucose often runs persistently high during illness — sometimes dramatically so.
- **Psychological stress.** Exams, arguments, job interviews, grief, anxiety — all of these can trigger cortisol release. The glucose rise from psychological stress is real, physiological, and not "in your head."

---

## Adrenaline — the fight-or-flight fuel dump

Adrenaline (also called epinephrine) is produced by the **adrenal medulla** — the inner part of the adrenal glands. It is released rapidly in response to perceived threats, excitement, pain, or intense physical activity.

### What adrenaline does to glucose

Adrenaline's primary job is to make fuel available — fast. It does this by:

- Triggering the liver to rapidly convert glycogen into glucose and release it into the bloodstream (**glycogenolysis**)
- Suppressing insulin secretion (in a body that makes insulin — in T1D this mechanism is already absent)
- Increasing the breakdown of fat for additional energy

The result is a sharp, rapid rise in blood glucose.

### When adrenaline matters most in T1D

- **Competitive sports and intense exercise.** The excitement and intensity of competition triggers adrenaline, which is why a football match may cause glucose to spike even as muscles are consuming glucose. The adrenaline-driven liver glucose release can temporarily outpace muscle uptake.
- **Fear and acute stress.** A near-miss in traffic, an argument, a scary film — these can all trigger an adrenaline surge and a corresponding glucose spike.
- **Hypoglycaemia itself.** When blood glucose drops low, adrenaline is one of the first hormones released as part of the body's defence. The symptoms you associate with a hypo — trembling, sweating, rapid heartbeat, anxiety — are largely adrenaline symptoms. The adrenaline also stimulates the liver to release glucose, which is the body's attempt to self-correct the low.

In people with long-standing T1D, the adrenaline response to hypoglycaemia can become blunted — a condition called **hypoglycaemia unawareness**. This is clinically significant and something your care team monitors.

## Growth hormone — the overnight builder

Growth hormone is produced by the **pituitary gland** at the base of the brain. It is released in pulses, with the largest pulses occurring during **deep sleep** and during **puberty**.

### What growth hormone does to glucose

Growth hormone raises blood glucose by:

- Reducing the sensitivity of cells to insulin (increasing insulin resistance)
- Stimulating the liver to produce glucose
- Promoting the use of fat for energy, which spares glucose and keeps blood levels elevated

### When growth hormone matters most in T1D

- **Puberty.** This is perhaps the most challenging hormonal period in T1D management. Growth hormone secretion surges during puberty, dramatically increasing insulin resistance. It is common for insulin requirements to increase by 50% or more during adolescence — and for glucose management to become markedly more difficult. ISPAD guidelines specifically address the impact of puberty on insulin requirements. This is not a failure of management — it is biology.
- **Overnight and early morning.** Growth hormone pulses during sleep contribute, alongside cortisol, to the dawn phenomenon. They also contribute to elevated fasting glucose in adolescents.
- **Growth spurts in children.** Even before puberty, growth hormone pulses can cause temporary periods of increased insulin resistance and less predictable glucose patterns.

---

## Glucagon — the direct glucose raiser

Glucagon is produced by the **alpha cells** of the pancreas — right next door to the beta cells that produce insulin. In a body without diabetes, glucagon and insulin work as a tightly paired system: insulin lowers glucose, glucagon raises it.

Glucagon raises blood glucose by stimulating the liver to convert stored glycogen into glucose and release it into the blood. It is the body's primary defence against hypoglycaemia.

In T1D, glucagon regulation can become **dysregulated** over time. Some people with long-standing T1D have an impaired glucagon response to hypoglycaemia, which — combined with reduced adrenaline response — can make hypoglycaemia more dangerous. This is another reason why hypoglycaemia awareness and prevention are so important.

Glucagon is also used therapeutically as an **emergency treatment for severe hypoglycaemia**, available in injection and nasal spray forms.

## Putting it all together

When you see glucose rise during an exam, an illness, an argument, or in the early morning, counter-regulatory hormones are usually at work. Understanding this does not eliminate the frustration — but it does provide an explanation, and it helps you and your care team think about patterns and strategies.

It also means that some glucose variability is genuinely beyond your direct control. Hormones do not ask for permission. They respond to stress, illness, sleep, growth, and circadian rhythms. Accepting this is not resignation — it is accuracy. And it is a reason to be kinder to yourself on the days when glucose simply will not cooperate.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Counter-regulatory hormones — cortisol, adrenaline, growth hormone, and glucagon — all raise blood glucose** through mechanisms including liver glucose release and increased insulin resistance.
2. **Cortisol follows a circadian rhythm** and rises during illness and stress, contributing significantly to the dawn phenomenon and sick-day hyperglycaemia.
3. **Adrenaline causes rapid glucose spikes** during acute stress, intense exercise, and competition — and is also part of the body's defence against hypoglycaemia.
4. **Growth hormone surges during puberty and deep sleep**, dramatically increasing insulin resistance in adolescents and contributing to overnight and fasting glucose challenges.
5. **Many "unexplained" glucose spikes have a hormonal explanation.** Understanding counter-regulatory hormones helps reduce frustration and supports more productive conversations with your care team.
