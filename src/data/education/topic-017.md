# Growth Spurts and Insulin — Why Doses Change Without Warning

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 17
**Category:** Growth, development, and insulin needs

---

You finally had it figured out. The doses were working, the numbers were stable, and you'd strung together three good days in a row. You were starting to feel like you actually understood this diabetes thing.

Then, overnight, everything fell apart. Blood sugars started running high for no apparent reason. The doses that worked yesterday aren't working today. Nothing has changed — same food, same routine, same everything — and yet the numbers are climbing like your child is eating invisible biscuits.

Welcome to the growth spurt.

You're doing great. And what's happening isn't your fault, isn't a mystery, and — most importantly — is temporary.

## What Growth Hormone Does to Insulin

Growth hormone (GH) is produced primarily during sleep and is released in pulses, particularly during deep sleep phases. In young children, these pulses can be frequent and significant, especially during periods of rapid growth.

Here's the key point: **growth hormone directly opposes the action of insulin.** It makes the body's cells more resistant to insulin, which means more insulin is needed to achieve the same blood glucose levels.

During a growth spurt, your child's body ramps up growth hormone production. This isn't a subtle shift — growth hormone levels can increase substantially, and the impact on insulin sensitivity can be dramatic and seemingly sudden.

This is why your child's blood sugars can go from beautifully in-range to persistently high over the course of a day or two, without any change in diet or activity.

## How Growth Happens in Young Children

Growth in the under-5 age group is not steady and linear. It happens in bursts:

- **Infants (0-12 months)** grow an average of 25cm in their first year. This is the fastest growth period in a human life. Growth hormone activity is intense.
- **Toddlers (1-3 years)** grow approximately 7-12cm per year, but this comes in spurts rather than a steady centimetre per month.
- **Preschoolers (3-5 years)** grow roughly 5-7cm per year, again in intermittent bursts.

During a spurt, a child might grow 1-2cm in a matter of weeks, then plateau for several weeks before the next spurt. Each of these growth phases can affect insulin needs.

## Signs That a Growth Spurt Is Happening

Growth spurts don't announce themselves with a banner, but there are often clues:

- **Increased appetite.** Your child suddenly wants to eat everything. (This also increases carb intake, adding to the blood sugar challenge.)
- **Increased sleep.** Growth hormone is released during sleep, so children in a growth spurt often sleep longer or nap more.
- **Growing pains.** Aching legs, particularly at night, are a classic sign in toddlers and preschoolers.
- **Clothes suddenly don't fit.** The trousers that fitted last week are now showing ankle. Shoes are suddenly tight.
- **Mood changes.** Some children become more irritable or clingy during spurts — possibly related to discomfort, disrupted sleep, or the hormonal changes themselves.
- **Persistently elevated blood sugars** that don't respond to the usual doses.

Not every child shows all of these signs, and blood sugar changes may be the first (or only) clue.

## The Pattern You'll See in the Numbers

Growth-spurt-related insulin resistance typically looks like this:

- Blood sugars gradually (or suddenly) trending higher than usual
- Particularly elevated readings overnight and in the early morning (when growth hormone activity peaks)
- Usual correction doses not bringing levels down as expected
- Post-meal spikes that are higher and longer than normal
- A sense that the insulin "isn't working" — which, in a sense, it isn't, because the body is actively resisting it

This pattern can last anywhere from a few days to several weeks, depending on the duration of the growth spurt.

## What to Do (And What Not to Do)

**What to do:**

- **Contact your diabetes team.** When you notice a persistent pattern of elevated blood sugars that doesn't have an obvious explanation (illness, dietary changes, pump site issues), let your team know. They can review the data and advise on adjustments.
- **Monitor more frequently.** During periods of insulin resistance, more data helps you and your team respond appropriately.
- **Be patient.** Growth spurts end. The insulin resistance associated with them is temporary, and doses will need to come back down once the spurt passes.
- **Keep a log.** Noting when these episodes happen, how long they last, and what adjustments were made creates a useful reference for future spurts.

**What not to do:**

- **Don't panic.** A few days of elevated blood sugars during a growth spurt, while not ideal, are a known and manageable phenomenon.
- **Don't keep increasing doses indefinitely without team input.** The spurt will end, and if you've been escalating doses aggressively, you risk significant lows when growth hormone levels return to baseline.
- **Don't assume every high reading is a growth spurt.** Rule out other causes first — illness, pump site issues (if applicable), dietary changes, stress, or missed doses.

## The Other Direction: After the Spurt

When the growth spurt ends, growth hormone production drops back to baseline, and insulin sensitivity increases again. The doses that were necessary during the spurt can suddenly become too much.

This is the moment to watch for unexplained lows. If your child has been on higher doses and you start seeing blood sugars dropping lower than expected — particularly overnight — the spurt may be over and doses need to come back down.

Contact your diabetes team when you suspect the spurt has passed, just as you did when it started. This is a two-part conversation: up and then back down.

## Growth Spurts and Pump Therapy

If your child uses an insulin pump, growth spurts typically require adjustments to:

- Basal rates (particularly overnight)
- Insulin-to-carb ratios
- Correction factors (insulin sensitivity factors)

Many families find that temporary basal rate increases are a useful tool during growth spurts, as they can be easily reversed once the spurt passes. Your diabetes team can guide you on how to use temporary rates effectively.

For children using automated insulin delivery (hybrid closed-loop) systems, the algorithm may handle some of the increased insulin demand automatically. However, significant growth spurts can exceed the system's adaptive range, and manual adjustments to settings may still be needed.

## The Bigger Picture: Growth and A1c

It's worth knowing that growth spurts can affect your child's HbA1c (the 3-month average blood sugar measure). A significant spurt in the weeks leading up to an A1c test can push the result higher than it might otherwise be.

If your child's A1c has risen and you know there's been a recent growth phase, mention this to your team. It provides context for the number and may mean the situation is less concerning than it first appears.

## A Note on Weight Gain

Healthy weight gain is a normal part of growth, and adequate insulin dosing supports it. If your child is growing well and gaining weight appropriately, that's a positive sign that their diabetes is being managed effectively.

Conversely, poor weight gain or weight loss in a child with T1D can be a sign that insulin dosing is insufficient. If you have concerns about your child's growth trajectory, raise them with your diabetes team.

## It Gets Easier (Sort of)

As your child gets older, growth slows down and the hormonal surges become less frequent (until puberty, at which point the whole thing starts again with added drama — but that's a topic for another day).

In the meantime, every growth spurt you navigate teaches you something. You'll get better at spotting the signs, quicker at contacting your team, and more confident in knowing that this phase will pass.

Your child is growing. That's a wonderful thing. The blood sugar chaos that comes with it is just the price of admission.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Growth hormone directly opposes insulin**, causing increased insulin resistance during growth spurts — this is a normal physiological process, not a management failure.
2. **Signs of a growth spurt** include increased appetite, more sleep, growing pains, clothes outgrown suddenly, and persistently elevated blood sugars.
3. **Contact your diabetes team** when you notice unexplained persistent highs so they can advise on appropriate dose adjustments.
4. **Watch for lows after the spurt ends**, as insulin sensitivity returns to baseline and the higher doses may become too much.
5. **Keep a log of growth-related insulin changes** — it creates a useful reference for future spurts and helps your team see patterns over time.
