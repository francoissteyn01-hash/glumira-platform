# Sick Days for Under-5s — When to Panic, When to Watch, When to Go to Hospital

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 16
**Category:** Sick day management — early years

---

Your child is sick. They're hot, miserable, and refusing to eat. If they didn't have Type 1 diabetes, you'd do what every parent does — cuddles, fluids, Calpol, and wait it out.

But they do have T1D, and now you're watching a CGM graph that looks like a mountain range while simultaneously trying to remember what "sick day rules" are and whether you should be calling someone.

Take a breath. This is manageable. Sick days are stressful, but most of them are navigable at home with the right knowledge and a low threshold for calling your diabetes team.

You're doing great. Let's walk through this step by step.

## Why Illness Affects Blood Sugar

When the body fights infection, it releases stress hormones — cortisol, adrenaline, and growth hormone among them. These hormones make the body more resistant to insulin, which means blood sugar tends to rise during illness, even if your child is eating less than usual.

This is confusing because your instinct says: they're eating nothing, so surely blood sugar should be low? But the hormonal response to illness often overrides the reduced food intake, pushing glucose up.

However — and this is important — **some illnesses cause blood sugar to drop.** Gastroenteritis (vomiting and diarrhoea) can cause lows because carbohydrates aren't being absorbed. The pattern depends on the illness, and it can change hour by hour.

This is why monitoring is so critical on sick days.

## The Non-Negotiable Sick Day Rules

These principles apply to every sick day, regardless of the specific illness:

**1. Never stop insulin.**
Even if your child isn't eating, they still need insulin. The body needs insulin to function — it's not just for food. Stopping insulin is the fastest route to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), which is a medical emergency.

Your diabetes team will have specific guidance on how to adjust insulin during illness, and this is the time to use it.

**2. Monitor blood glucose frequently.**
Every 2-3 hours at minimum, including overnight. If your child has a CGM, watch the trends closely. If you're using finger pricks, set alarms so you don't miss a check.

**3. Check ketones.**
This is critical for under-5s. Blood ketone testing is more reliable than urine testing in young children. Check ketones:

- Every 2-4 hours during illness
- Any time blood glucose is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL)
- If your child is vomiting
- If they seem unusually unwell

**Ketone thresholds to know:**

| Blood ketone level | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Below 0.6 mmol/L | Normal | Continue monitoring |
| 0.6 - 1.4 mmol/L | Elevated | Contact your diabetes team for advice |
| 1.5 - 2.9 mmol/L | High | Contact your diabetes team urgently. Follow their sick day protocol. |
| 3.0 mmol/L or above | Very high — DKA risk | **Go to hospital or call an ambulance.** |

These thresholds are based on ISPAD and NICE guidelines. Your team may have slightly different cutoffs — follow their specific advice.

**4. Push fluids.**
Dehydration makes everything worse. Small, frequent sips are more effective than trying to get a sick child to drink a full cup.

- If blood sugar is **high**: offer sugar-free fluids (water, sugar-free squash, sugar-free ice lollies)
- If blood sugar is **low or normal**: offer fluids that contain sugar (flat lemonade, diluted juice, regular ice lollies) to help maintain glucose levels
- If your child is vomiting, tiny sips every few minutes. A medicine syringe can help.

**5. Keep a written log.**
When you're tired and scared, your memory is unreliable. Write down every blood glucose check, every ketone reading, every fluid intake, every dose of medication. This log is invaluable if you need to call your team or go to hospital.

## The Fluid Guide for Small Children

Keeping a sick toddler hydrated is a challenge in itself. Here's a rough guide to minimum fluid targets:

- **Under 10kg:** At least 100ml per kg per day (so a 9kg baby needs at least 900ml)
- **10-20kg:** 1000ml plus 50ml for each kg over 10 (so a 15kg toddler needs at least 1250ml)

These are baseline requirements and may increase with fever, vomiting, or diarrhoea. Your diabetes team can advise on specific targets for your child.

**Fluid strategies that work for tiny children:**

- Ice lollies (frozen diluted juice or sugar-free squash)
- Medicine syringe — 5ml at a time, every few minutes
- Jelly (sugar-free or regular depending on blood sugar)
- Wet flannels to suck on
- Breastfeeds (if still breastfeeding — this counts as fluid)

## When to Call Your Diabetes Team

Call your diabetes team if:

- Blood glucose is persistently above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) despite following your sick day plan
- Ketones are elevated (above 0.6 mmol/L) and not coming down
- Your child is vomiting and unable to keep fluids down for more than 2 hours
- You're unsure about insulin adjustments
- Your child seems unusually lethargic, confused, or unresponsive
- You feel out of your depth (this is a completely valid reason to call)

Most paediatric diabetes teams have out-of-hours advice lines. Programme this number into your phone now, before you need it at 2am.

## When to Go to Hospital

**Go to your nearest emergency department or call an ambulance if:**

- Ketones are 3.0 mmol/L or above
- Your child is vomiting and cannot keep any fluids down
- Your child has rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul breathing — a sign of DKA)
- Your child is increasingly drowsy, difficult to rouse, or unresponsive
- You feel something is seriously wrong, even if the numbers don't look alarming yet

Trust your instincts. You know your child better than any guideline. If something feels wrong, seek help. No emergency department will criticise you for bringing a sick child with Type 1 diabetes in for assessment.

## The Vomiting Question

Vomiting is the sick day scenario that frightens parents most, and for good reason. When a child is vomiting:

- They can't keep food down, so blood sugar may drop
- They can't keep fluids down, so dehydration accelerates
- They may not be absorbing insulin from subcutaneous injections as effectively
- Ketones can rise rapidly

If your child is vomiting and has T1D, contact your diabetes team early. Don't wait to see if it settles. For under-5s, the margin for dehydration is smaller and the progression to DKA can be faster.

## Medications and Diabetes

Common children's medications to be aware of:

- **Paracetamol (acetaminophen):** Generally fine. Sugar-free versions are available. Note: some CGM systems can give falsely elevated readings with paracetamol — check your device guidance.
- **Ibuprofen:** Generally fine for children over 3 months. Avoid if dehydrated.
- **Cough syrups and cold remedies:** Often contain significant amounts of sugar. Read labels and factor in the carbs.
- **Antibiotics:** Some liquid antibiotics contain sugar. Check with your pharmacist.
- **Steroids:** Can dramatically increase blood sugar. If your child is prescribed steroids (for croup, asthma, etc.), alert your diabetes team immediately.

## After the Illness

Once your child is recovering, blood sugar may take a few days to settle back to normal patterns. Insulin needs can remain elevated or may drop as the stress response resolves. Continue monitoring more frequently than usual for 24-48 hours after the illness resolves.

Your diabetes team will help you readjust insulin back to usual levels.

## Preparing Before the Next Sick Day

Because there will be a next sick day. Children get sick — a lot. Being prepared in advance makes the experience less frightening.

**Your sick day kit should include:**
- Blood ketone meter and test strips (check expiry dates regularly)
- Extra blood glucose test strips
- Sugar-free and sugar-containing fluids
- Your written sick day protocol from your diabetes team
- Diabetes team contact numbers (daytime and out-of-hours)
- A simple log sheet or notebook

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Never stop insulin during illness**, even if your child isn't eating. The body needs insulin to prevent DKA.
2. **Monitor blood glucose every 2-3 hours and check blood ketones every 2-4 hours** during any illness. Keep a written log.
3. **Push fluids relentlessly** — sugar-free if glucose is high, sugar-containing if glucose is low or normal. Small frequent sips work best.
4. **Call your diabetes team early** if ketones are rising, vomiting persists, or you're unsure. Go to hospital if ketones reach 3.0 mmol/L or above, or if your child becomes drowsy or has rapid breathing.
5. **Prepare a sick day kit in advance** so that when illness strikes at midnight, you're not searching for ketone strips in the dark.
