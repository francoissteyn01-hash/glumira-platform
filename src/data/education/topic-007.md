# Hypo vs Hyper — Knowing the Difference and What to Do

## Hello from Mira

Two words you are going to hear a lot in your Type 1 Diabetes journey: **hypo** and **hyper**. They sound similar, but they mean very different things, and knowing the difference — quickly and confidently — is one of the most important skills you will develop. I am going to break both down clearly, so you always know what is happening and what to do.

---

## The basics

**Hypo** is short for **hypoglycaemia** — it means your blood glucose level has dropped too low. Generally, this means below **4.0 mmol/L (70 mg/dL)**, though your care team may define the threshold slightly differently for your situation.

**Hyper** is short for **hyperglycaemia** — it means your blood glucose level has risen too high. There is no single universal cutoff, but many care teams consider readings consistently above **10.0 mmol/L (180 mg/dL)** to be in the hyperglycaemic range.

Both hypos and hypers are a normal part of life with Type 1 Diabetes. They are not emergencies by default — but they do require attention and action.

---

## Hypoglycaemia (Hypo) — too low

### What causes it?

- Too much insulin relative to the amount of glucose in your blood
- Skipping or delaying a meal or snack
- More physical activity than usual
- Alcohol (in older teens/adults)
- Hot weather
- The insulin on board from a recent dose still being active — GluMira's **IOB Hunter** feature helps you visualise this concept. Always discuss insulin-related decisions with your care team.

### What does it feel like?

Symptoms can vary from person to person, and they can change over time. Common signs include:

**Early/mild symptoms:**
- Feeling shaky or trembling
- Sweating (especially cold sweats)
- Feeling hungry — suddenly and intensely
- Heart racing or pounding
- Feeling anxious or irritable
- Tingling lips or fingers
- Pale skin

**Moderate symptoms:**
- Difficulty concentrating or thinking clearly
- Confusion or brain fog
- Feeling dizzy or lightheaded
- Blurred vision
- Feeling weak or tired
- Slurred speech
- Mood changes — tearfulness, irritability, aggression (especially in children)

**Severe symptoms (medical emergency):**
- Unable to treat yourself
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizure/convulsion

**In young children** who cannot describe how they feel, parents may notice: unusual irritability, crying, clinginess, pallor, unsteadiness, or seeming "spaced out."

### What to do

The general approach recommended by ISPAD, ADA, and NICE follows a principle often summarised as **"treat, wait, recheck"**:

1. **Treat with fast-acting glucose** — examples include glucose tablets, fruit juice, or sugary sweets (such as jelly babies). Your care team will have advised you on the specific amount appropriate for your child's age and weight.
2. **Wait 10-15 minutes** and recheck blood glucose.
3. **If still below target**, repeat the treatment.
4. **Once blood glucose has recovered**, follow up with a longer-acting snack if your next meal is not soon — to prevent the level from dropping again.

**For severe hypos** where the person is unconscious or unable to swallow safely: **do not put anything in their mouth.** Place them in the recovery position and call emergency services. If you have been prescribed a glucagon kit, use it as instructed by your care team.

## Hyperglycaemia (Hyper) — too high

### What causes it?

- Not enough insulin
- Eating more carbohydrates than the insulin can cover
- Illness or infection (the body releases stress hormones that raise blood glucose)
- Stress — emotional or physical
- Hormonal changes (puberty, menstrual cycle, dawn phenomenon)
- A missed or delayed insulin dose
- Insulin that has degraded (expired, exposed to heat or extreme cold)
- Injection site issues (injecting into lumpy skin reduces absorption)

### What does it feel like?

Hyperglycaemia symptoms often develop more gradually than hypo symptoms:

**Common symptoms:**
- Feeling very thirsty
- Needing to urinate frequently
- Feeling tired or lethargic
- Blurred vision
- Headache
- Difficulty concentrating
- Dry mouth
- Feeling generally unwell or "off"

**If blood glucose stays high for a prolonged period, or rises very high, watch for signs of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA):**
- Nausea or vomiting
- Abdominal (tummy) pain
- Fruity or acetone smell on the breath
- Rapid or deep breathing
- Drowsiness or confusion
- **DKA is a medical emergency. Call your care team or emergency services immediately.**

### What to do

The response to hyperglycaemia depends on the situation, the level, and your individual management plan. General principles include:

1. **Check for ketones** if blood glucose is above the threshold your care team has set (often 14.0 mmol/L / 250 mg/dL or as advised). Ketone testing may be done via blood or urine, depending on what your care team recommends.
2. **Drink water** — staying hydrated helps the body manage high glucose levels.
3. **Follow your care team's guidance** on whether a correction is needed and how to manage it. Do not make insulin adjustment decisions without clear guidance from your care team.
4. **Identify potential causes** — was a dose missed? Is the insulin in date? Are you unwell? Is there a pattern?
5. **If ketones are present and/or you feel unwell**, contact your care team urgently or seek emergency care.

## Hypo vs Hyper — a comparison

**Blood glucose level:**
- Hypo: Below 4.0 mmol/L (70 mg/dL)
- Hyper: Consistently above 10.0 mmol/L (180 mg/dL)

**Speed of onset:**
- Hypo: Usually rapid (minutes)
- Hyper: Usually gradual (hours)

**Common early feelings:**
- Hypo: Shaky, sweaty, hungry, anxious
- Hyper: Thirsty, tired, needing to urinate often

**Immediate risk:**
- Hypo: Can become dangerous quickly if untreated (confusion, loss of consciousness, seizure)
- Hyper: Usually less immediately dangerous, but sustained highs can lead to DKA

**Immediate action:**
- Hypo: Fast-acting glucose NOW, then recheck
- Hyper: Water, check ketones, follow care team guidance

**Key danger sign:**
- Hypo: Inability to self-treat, loss of consciousness
- Hyper: Vomiting, fruity breath, rapid breathing (signs of DKA)

## Important notes for parents and caregivers

- **Teach everyone who cares for your child** to recognise both hypo and hyper symptoms — family members, teachers, coaches, babysitters.
- **Hypos need to be treated immediately.** Do not wait to finish a lesson, a game, or a car journey.
- **Carry fast-acting glucose at all times.** In the car, in your bag, in your child's school bag, by the bed — everywhere.
- **Night-time hypos** can happen while your child is asleep. Discuss overnight monitoring with your care team.
- **Hypers happen.** They are not a sign of failure. Identify the cause if you can, respond appropriately, and move on.

---

## When to call your care team

Contact your care team if:
- Hypos are happening frequently (more than two to three per week, or as defined by your care team)
- Hypos are severe (requiring help from another person)
- Blood glucose is consistently high and you are unsure why
- Ketones are present
- You or your child shows any signs of DKA
- You are unsure whether symptoms are hypo or hyper — when in doubt, check and call

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Hypo means blood glucose is too low; hyper means it is too high.** Both are a normal part of Type 1 Diabetes and require attention, not panic.
2. **Hypos come on fast** — shaky, sweaty, hungry — and need immediate treatment with fast-acting glucose. Recheck after 10-15 minutes.
3. **Hypers develop gradually** — thirst, tiredness, frequent urination — and require hydration, possible ketone checking, and following your care team's guidance.
4. **DKA is a medical emergency.** If you see vomiting, fruity breath, rapid breathing, or drowsiness alongside high blood glucose, seek urgent medical care.
5. **Carry fast-acting glucose everywhere**, and make sure everyone who cares for the person with diabetes can recognise the signs of both hypo and hyper.
