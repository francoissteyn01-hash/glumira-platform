## Hello from Mira

If blood glucose monitoring is your daily compass, ketone testing is your storm warning system. Most days, you will not need it. But on the days you do — during illness, when blood glucose is stubbornly high, when a pump may have failed — ketone testing can be the difference between catching a problem early and ending up in the emergency department.

Too many families tell me they were never really taught what ketones are, when to test, or what the numbers mean. That changes now. Let us go through it clearly, so that you feel confident picking up that ketone meter and knowing exactly what the result is telling you.

---

## What are ketones?

Ketones are chemicals produced when the body breaks down fat for energy. In a healthy metabolism, cells primarily use glucose for fuel. Insulin is the hormone that allows glucose to enter cells. When there is not enough insulin — or when the body cannot use the insulin effectively — glucose stays locked out of the cells, and the body turns to its backup fuel source: fat.

When fat is broken down rapidly, the liver produces three types of ketone bodies: **acetoacetate**, **beta-hydroxybutyrate**, and **acetone**. In small amounts, ketones are normal and harmless — the body produces trace amounts during overnight fasting or prolonged exercise, for example.

But in Type 1 Diabetes, when insulin is severely lacking, ketone production can accelerate far beyond what the body can safely process. Ketones are acidic. When they accumulate in the blood, they lower the blood's pH, leading to a condition called **ketoacidosis** — specifically, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). This is a medical emergency (see Topic 072 for a detailed discussion of DKA).

Ketone testing tells you whether this dangerous process has begun, and how far it has progressed.

## Blood ketones vs urine ketones — which should you use?

There are two ways to test for ketones: blood testing and urine testing. They are not equivalent.

### Blood ketone testing (recommended)

Blood ketone meters measure **beta-hydroxybutyrate** — the predominant ketone body during DKA. The test requires a small finger-prick blood sample (similar to a glucose test) and a specific ketone test strip.

**Advantages:**
- Gives a **real-time, quantitative result** — you know exactly what the ketone level is right now.
- Results appear in seconds.
- More accurate and clinically reliable than urine testing.
- ISPAD, ADA, and NICE all recommend blood ketone testing as the preferred method, particularly in children.

**Disadvantages:**
- Ketone test strips can be expensive and are not always covered by insurance or national health systems.
- Strips have a limited shelf life — check expiry dates regularly.

### Urine ketone testing

Urine ketone strips measure **acetoacetate**. You dip the strip in urine and compare the colour change to a chart.

**Advantages:**
- Strips are inexpensive and widely available.
- Non-invasive — no finger prick required.

**Disadvantages:**
- Urine ketones reflect what was happening in the blood **hours ago**, not right now. There is a significant time lag.
- Results are semi-quantitative — "negative," "trace," "small," "moderate," "large" — rather than a precise number.
- During treatment for DKA, urine ketones can paradoxically appear to worsen even as blood ketones improve, because the body converts beta-hydroxybutyrate back to acetoacetate as it recovers.
- Dehydration concentrates urine, potentially giving a falsely high reading. Over-hydration can dilute it, giving a falsely low reading.

**Mira's recommendation:** If you can access blood ketone testing, use it. It is faster, more accurate, and gives you the information you need to make decisions. If urine testing is your only option, it is still valuable — but interpret the results with awareness of its limitations.

## When to test for ketones

You should test for ketones in the following situations:

### 1. Blood glucose above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) for more than 2–3 hours
Persistent hyperglycaemia that does not respond to correction may indicate that insulin is not being delivered effectively. Ketones will tell you whether the body has started breaking down fat due to insulin deficiency.

### 2. During any illness
Illness increases insulin demand. Even mild infections can tip the balance. Test ketones at the start of any illness and continue testing every 2–4 hours throughout (see Topic 074).

### 3. When vomiting or experiencing diarrhoea
Gastrointestinal illness is one of the highest-risk situations for DKA. Test ketones immediately and frequently (see Topic 073 — The Gastroenteritis Crisis Protocol).

### 4. When an insulin pump may have failed
If you suspect a pump malfunction — unexpected high BG, a kinked or dislodged cannula, an occlusion alarm — test ketones immediately. Pump users have no long-acting insulin reserve; ketones can develop within 2–4 hours of interrupted insulin delivery.

### 5. When feeling unusually unwell
Nausea, abdominal pain, excessive thirst, rapid breathing, or the smell of acetone on the breath (fruity or nail-polish-like) — all of these warrant immediate ketone testing.

### 6. Before and during exercise (in some cases)
If BG is above 14 mmol/L before exercise, check ketones. Exercising with elevated ketones can worsen the situation, as exercise further increases counter-regulatory hormones. If ketones are above 1.5 mmol/L, do not exercise until they have cleared.

## What the numbers mean — blood ketone thresholds

This table reflects the thresholds recommended by ISPAD and supported by ADA and NICE guidelines:

| Blood ketone level | Category | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Below 0.6 mmol/L** | Normal | No significant ketone production. | No ketone-related action needed. Continue routine diabetes management. |
| **0.6–0.9 mmol/L** | Mildly elevated | The body is producing ketones at a higher-than-normal rate. This may be an early sign that insulin delivery is insufficient. | Drink extra fluids (sugar-free if BG is high). Recheck ketones in 1–2 hours. Ensure insulin is being delivered correctly. If on a pump, check the infusion site. |
| **1.0–1.4 mmol/L** | Moderately elevated | Ketone production is increasing. There is a meaningful risk of progression to DKA if the trend continues. | Contact your diabetes care team. Follow your sick-day plan. Increase fluid intake. Recheck ketones every 1–2 hours. Do NOT wait to see if it resolves on its own. |
| **1.5–2.9 mmol/L** | Significantly elevated — high risk | The body is in a state of significant ketosis. DKA may be developing. | Contact your diabetes care team urgently. If you cannot reach them, consider going to the emergency department. Do not exercise. Ensure insulin is being delivered. Drink fluids. Monitor continuously. |
| **3.0 mmol/L or above** | DKA range | Diabetic ketoacidosis is likely occurring. This is a medical emergency. | **Go to the emergency department immediately.** Do not attempt to manage this at home. IV fluids and insulin are needed under medical supervision. |

## Interpreting urine ketone results

If you are using urine test strips:

| Urine ketone result | Approximate blood equivalent | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Negative or trace | Below 0.6 mmol/L | Continue monitoring |
| Small | 0.6–1.0 mmol/L | Increase fluids, recheck in 2 hours |
| Moderate | 1.0–3.0 mmol/L | Contact diabetes team, follow sick-day plan |
| Large | Likely above 3.0 mmol/L | **Seek emergency medical care immediately** |

Remember: urine results lag behind blood levels. If urine ketones are "moderate" or "large," the situation may already be more advanced than the strip suggests.

## Practical tips for ketone testing

- **Keep ketone strips in stock at all times.** Do not discover they are expired during an emergency.
- **Check expiry dates regularly.** Expired strips give unreliable results.
- **Store strips correctly** — in their original container, sealed, at room temperature. Do not leave them in humid bathrooms.
- **Teach everyone** who cares for the person with T1D how to test ketones and what the results mean — parents, grandparents, school staff, babysitters.
- **Record results** with the time, and share them with your diabetes team during sick-day calls. Trends matter as much as individual numbers.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Ketones are produced when the body breaks down fat due to insufficient insulin** — in T1D, this process can escalate rapidly into the life-threatening emergency of DKA.
2. **Blood ketone testing is more accurate and timely than urine testing** — it gives a real-time, precise number rather than a delayed, semi-quantitative estimate.
3. **Test ketones whenever BG is above 14 mmol/L for more than 2–3 hours, during illness, when vomiting, or when a pump may have failed** — do not wait for symptoms to appear.
4. **Blood ketones of 1.5 mmol/L or above require urgent contact with your diabetes team**; ketones of 3.0 mmol/L or above mean go to the emergency department immediately.
5. **Keep ketone strips in stock, check expiry dates, and ensure multiple people know how to test** — ketone testing is only useful if you can do it when you need it.
