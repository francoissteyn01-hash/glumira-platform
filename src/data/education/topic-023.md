# PE and Sports Day — Pre-Exercise, During, and Post-Exercise Management

**Topic ID:** 023
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 6 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. Let's talk about one of the most wonderful — and most nerve-wracking — parts of your child's school week: physical education and sports day. Exercise is brilliant for children with Type 1 diabetes. It supports cardiovascular health, helps with insulin sensitivity, builds confidence, and is simply fun. But it does change how glucose behaves, and understanding those changes is the key to your child participating fully and safely.

## How Exercise Affects Blood Glucose

When muscles work, they use glucose for fuel. During moderate aerobic exercise — running, swimming, cycling, playing tag — muscles pull glucose from the bloodstream much more efficiently than they do at rest. This means blood glucose levels tend to drop during and after exercise.

However, intense anaerobic exercise — sprinting, competitive races, the adrenaline of sports day — can temporarily raise blood glucose because stress hormones like adrenaline tell the liver to release stored glucose. So you might see a spike during the 100-metre dash, followed by a drop an hour later.

ISPAD guidelines describe this dual response clearly: the direction and magnitude of glucose change depend on the type, intensity, duration, and timing of exercise relative to meals and insulin.

## Before PE or Sports Day

Preparation starts before your child even puts on their trainers.

### Check Blood Glucose

The ideal pre-exercise blood glucose range is generally between 7–10 mmol/L (126–180 mg/dL), though your child's diabetes team may set an individualised target. If your child wears a CGM, check the trend arrow as well as the number:

- **Glucose in range with a stable or rising arrow:** Good to go.
- **Glucose in range but dropping:** A small carb snack before exercise may be wise.
- **Glucose below 5 mmol/L (90 mg/dL):** Treat the low first, wait until glucose has recovered and is stable, then allow activity.
- **Glucose above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) with ketones:** Exercise should be delayed until ketones have cleared. Exercise with high ketones can be dangerous.

### The 15-Gram Carb Rule

This is one of the most practical rules in paediatric diabetes and exercise management. If your child's blood glucose is between 5–7 mmol/L (90–126 mg/dL) before exercise, offer approximately 15 grams of carbohydrate without insulin coverage to provide fuel for the activity. Good options include:

- A small juice box (150–200 ml)
- 3–4 glucose tablets
- A small banana
- A small handful of dried fruit (about 2 tablespoons)

This is a starting point. Some children need more, some need less, depending on the intensity and duration of the activity. Over time, you will learn your child's pattern.

### Timing Matters

If PE is scheduled right after lunch, the active meal insulin may cause a sharper drop during exercise. Many families and diabetes teams discuss reducing the lunchtime insulin dose on PE days — this is something to work out with your child's healthcare provider, not something to guess at.

If PE is mid-morning or mid-afternoon (well after the last meal), the risk of a hypo is still present but may be more predictable.

## During PE or Sports Day

### Keep Supplies Accessible

Your child's teacher or PE teacher should have quick access to:

- Fast-acting glucose (juice, glucose tablets, or glucose gel)
- The blood glucose meter or CGM reader
- A water bottle

These should be pitchside, on the bench, or wherever the class gathers — not back in the classroom.

### Watch for Hypo Signs

Young children may not recognise or communicate low blood glucose symptoms well during the excitement of sport. Teachers and coaches should watch for:

- Sudden tiredness or sitting down unexpectedly
- Pale or clammy skin
- Shakiness or wobbliness
- Confusion or unusual behaviour
- Complaints of tummy ache or headache

If in doubt, check blood glucose. It is always safer to check than to assume.

### Hydration

Children with T1D should drink water regularly during exercise, just like their peers. Dehydration can make blood glucose harder to manage. Water is the best choice — sports drinks contain carbohydrate and should be reserved for when additional carbs are actually needed.

## After PE — The Delayed Hypo

This is the part that catches many families off guard. After exercise, muscles continue to replenish their glycogen stores, pulling glucose from the blood for hours afterwards. This means a hypo can occur 4–8 hours after exercise, sometimes even overnight after an active sports day.

ISPAD and ADA literature both highlight the risk of delayed post-exercise hypoglycaemia, particularly overnight.

### What You Can Do

- **Check blood glucose after PE and again 2–3 hours later.** If your child wears a CGM, watch the trend through the afternoon and evening.
- **Offer a balanced snack after exercise** that includes both carbohydrate and protein — for example, a cheese sandwich or crackers with peanut butter. The protein and fat help sustain blood glucose levels.
- **On sports day or after unusually intense activity**, consider checking blood glucose at bedtime and again during the night (or set CGM alarms at a slightly higher threshold for that night).
- **Talk to your diabetes team** about whether any adjustments to evening insulin are appropriate after very active days. Do not make changes without their guidance.

## Sports Day — Extra Considerations

Sports day is a special case because it combines intense exercise with excitement, heat (if outdoors in summer), and disrupted routine. Here are additional tips:

- **Pack extra hypo treatment.** Double what you would normally send. Adrenaline and repeated events burn through glucose stores.
- **Send a clear written note to the class teacher** reminding them about pre-exercise checks, hypo signs, and the location of supplies.
- **Sunscreen and shade.** Heat increases blood flow to the skin and can speed up insulin absorption from injection or pump sites, increasing hypo risk.
- **Let your child participate in everything.** With preparation, there is no event they cannot join. T1D is not a reason to sit on the sideline.

## Building Your Child's Awareness

Even at age 5–7, children can begin learning to recognise how exercise makes them feel. Use simple language:

- "Before we run, we check your number."
- "If you feel wobbly or funny, tell your teacher straight away."
- "After running, you have a snack — that's your body refuelling, just like a car."

By age 8–10, many children can begin to check their own CGM and ask for a snack when they see a dropping trend. Encourage this independence while making sure adult backup is always in place.

## Communicating with School Staff

Give the PE teacher a one-page summary — separate from the full care plan — that covers:

1. Check glucose before exercise (or ask the child to show their CGM reading)
2. If below 5 mmol/L (90 mg/dL), give fast-acting glucose and wait
3. Keep hypo treatment pitchside
4. Watch for signs of low blood glucose
5. Allow the child to stop and check glucose at any time without penalty

Keep it simple, friendly, and non-medical. Teachers respond best to clear, practical instructions.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Check blood glucose before exercise** — the ideal starting range is generally 7–10 mmol/L (126–180 mg/dL), and use the 15 g carb rule if glucose is between 5–7 mmol/L.
2. **Keep fast-acting glucose pitchside** during PE so it is immediately accessible, not locked in a classroom.
3. **Watch for delayed hypoglycaemia** 4–8 hours after exercise, including overnight — this is one of the most common post-exercise complications in children with T1D.
4. **Sports day requires extra preparation** including double hypo supplies, written staff reminders, and attention to heat and hydration.
5. **Every child with T1D can and should participate fully in PE and sports day** — preparation and awareness are all it takes.
