# Snacking Strategies — Planned vs Panic Snacking

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All people living with Type 1 Diabetes and their support networks

## Two Kinds of Snacking — And They Are Completely Different

In Type 1 Diabetes, snacking is not just snacking. There are two fundamentally different types, and confusing them is one of the most common sources of glucose chaos.

**Planned snacking** is a deliberate choice to eat something between meals — for energy, for enjoyment, to bridge a gap, or to fuel activity. It is proactive.

**Panic snacking** is a reactive response to hypoglycaemia — the urgent need to eat something, right now, because your glucose is dropping and your body is screaming for fuel. It is survival.

Both are valid. Both are part of life with T1D. But they require completely different approaches, different foods, and different mindsets. Understanding the distinction — and having strategies for each — will make your daily management smoother and calmer.

## Planned Snacking — The Bridge Between Meals

Planned snacks serve several purposes:

- **Bridging long gaps between meals** (more than 4–5 hours)
- **Fuelling before, during, or after physical activity** (see Topic 39)
- **Managing afternoon or evening energy dips**
- **Social eating** — when everyone around you is snacking and you want to join in
- **Pre-sleep snacking** to prevent overnight lows (discuss with your healthcare team)

The key to planned snacking is choosing foods that provide sustained energy without causing dramatic glucose spikes. This means favouring snacks that combine carbohydrate with protein, fat, or fibre — slowing the glucose release and providing longer-lasting satiety.

### Smart Planned Snack Options

**Low-carb snacks (under 10g carb) — often need no additional insulin coverage:**
- A small handful of nuts (almonds, cashews, walnuts): 3–5g carb
- Cheese cubes or cheese sticks: 0–1g carb
- Biltong or jerky: 1–3g carb
- Hard-boiled egg: 0–1g carb
- Celery sticks with cream cheese: 2–3g carb
- Cucumber slices with hummus (2 tablespoons): 5–7g carb

**Moderate-carb snacks (10–20g carb) — balanced and sustaining:**
- Apple slices with peanut butter (1 tablespoon): 15–18g carb
- Small tub of plain yoghurt with a few berries: 12–18g carb
- Two oatcakes with cheese: 15–18g carb
- A small banana: 20g carb
- Whole wheat crackers (4–5) with hummus: 15–20g carb
- A small handful of trail mix: 15–20g carb

**Higher-carb snacks (20–35g carb) — for bigger gaps or activity fuel:**
- Peanut butter sandwich on whole wheat bread: 25–30g carb
- A granola bar: 20–30g carb
- A small bowl of popcorn (3 cups popped): 15–20g carb
- Fruit smoothie (small, homemade): 25–35g carb
- A medium muffin: 30–40g carb

### Snacking with Specific Dietary Frameworks

If you eat within Kosher dietary guidelines, planned snacks might include cheese and crackers (dairy), or biltong and nuts (meat or pareve). The carb-counting principle is the same — identify the carbohydrate component and estimate accordingly.

Within Halal dietary practice, snack options are very similar to general guidance. Halal-certified jerky, nuts, fruit, and dairy products are all excellent planned snack choices.

For those following the Bernstein low-carb approach, planned snacks tend to be very low carbohydrate — nuts, cheese, eggs, and vegetables. The lower the carb content, the less glucose impact, which aligns with the tight control goals of that approach.

During Ramadan, planned snacking takes on a different dimension. Between Iftar and Suhoor, well-chosen snacks can help maintain stable glucose through the night. Low-GI, moderate-carb options like yoghurt with nuts or a small serving of fruit with cheese are effective choices for the late evening.

## Panic Snacking — When You Need Glucose Now

Panic snacking is your response to hypoglycaemia — a blood glucose level below 4.0 mmol/L (or your healthcare team's defined threshold), or the symptoms of a low: shakiness, sweating, confusion, rapid heartbeat, weakness.

This is not the time for thoughtful food selection. This is the time for fast-acting glucose. The goal is simple: raise your blood glucose as quickly as possible.

### The 15-15 Rule

The standard approach to treating hypoglycaemia is the 15-15 rule:

1. **Consume 15g of fast-acting carbohydrate**
2. **Wait 15 minutes**
3. **Recheck your blood glucose**
4. **If still below target, consume another 15g and wait another 15 minutes**
5. **Once glucose is above 4.0 mmol/L, have a small snack with protein or fat to sustain the recovery** (if a meal is not imminent)

For children, the amount may be adjusted — typically 10g for young children. Discuss the right amount for your child with your healthcare team (see Topic 26 for full hypoglycaemia management guidance).

### Fast-Acting Hypo Treatments (approximately 15g fast-acting carb)

- **Glucose tablets** (3–4 tablets, depending on brand): 12–16g carb — the most precise option
- **Juice box** (125–150ml): 15–18g carb — convenient and portable
- **Regular soft drink** (150ml — about half a can): 15–17g carb
- **Jelly sweets** (4–5 small sweets): 15–20g carb
- **Honey or sugar** (1 tablespoon): 15–17g carb
- **Dried fruit** (2 tablespoons raisins): 15g carb — slightly slower than pure glucose

### What NOT to Use for Hypo Treatment

- **Chocolate** — the fat slows glucose absorption significantly. By the time the glucose arrives, you have waited too long and often overeaten.
- **Ice cream** — same problem. High fat, slow absorption.
- **Peanut butter sandwiches** — too much fat and protein slowing things down when you need speed.
- **Diet drinks** — zero carbohydrate, zero help during a hypo.

These foods are perfectly fine as the follow-up snack once your glucose has recovered, but they are poor choices as the initial treatment.

### The Overtreating Trap

This is where panic snacking earns its name. When your glucose is 2.8 mmol/L and you feel terrible, every instinct screams at you to eat everything in sight. Your body is in survival mode. The adrenaline is flowing. The fridge door is open and nothing is off limits.

The result is often a dramatic overtreatment — consuming 40, 60, even 80g of carbohydrate when 15g would have been sufficient. This leads to a glucose rebound that sends you soaring from 2.8 to 16 mmol/L within an hour. The rollercoaster continues.

**Strategies to prevent overtreating:**

1. **Pre-portion your hypo treatments.** Keep glucose tablets, juice boxes, or small bags of jelly sweets specifically designated for lows. When you reach for them, you eat the pre-measured amount and stop.

2. **Treat, then leave the kitchen.** Consume your 15g, set a 15-minute timer, and physically move away from the food. Sit down. Wait. Recheck.

3. **Talk yourself through it.** "I have eaten 15g of glucose. It will take 10–15 minutes to work. I will feel better soon. I do not need to eat more right now."

4. **Remember that glucose tablets are your friend precisely because they are boring.** Nobody binge-eats glucose tablets the way they binge-eat biscuits during a hypo.

## Building Your Snack Kit

Every person with T1D benefits from having a snack kit — a small, portable collection of items that covers both planned and panic snacking.

**Your snack kit should contain:**

- **Fast-acting hypo treatment:** Glucose tablets or a juice box (for panic snacking)
- **A planned snack option:** A small packet of nuts, a muesli bar, or crackers (for planned snacking or post-hypo follow-up)
- **Water:** Dehydration makes everything harder (see Topic 69)

Keep a kit in your bag, your car, your desk at work or school, and your bedside table. Replace items before they expire. Check it regularly.

## Snacking and Guilt

Mira needs to address this directly: many people with T1D carry guilt about snacking. They feel they "should not need to" snack, or they feel they "failed" if they overtreated a low, or they feel judged for eating between meals.

Snacking in T1D is not a moral issue. Planned snacking is a legitimate management strategy. Panic snacking is a medical necessity. Neither requires justification or apology.

If you find that guilt around food is affecting your relationship with eating or your diabetes management, please speak with your healthcare team. Disordered eating is more common in T1D than in the general population, and support is available.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Planned snacking and panic snacking are fundamentally different** — they require different foods, different amounts, and different approaches.
2. **The best planned snacks combine carbohydrate with protein, fat, or fibre** — this slows glucose release and provides sustained energy.
3. **Hypo treatment requires fast-acting glucose — 15g, wait 15 minutes, recheck** — chocolate and fatty foods are too slow for initial treatment.
4. **Overtreating hypoglycaemia is one of the most common causes of glucose rollercoasters** — pre-portioning hypo treatments and leaving the kitchen after treating are practical prevention strategies.
5. **A portable snack kit with both fast-acting hypo treatment and a planned snack option is essential** — keep one in your bag, car, desk, and bedside table.
