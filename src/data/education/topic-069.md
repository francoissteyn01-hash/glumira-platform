## The Simplest Thing You Can Do — And the Most Overlooked

Mira has a question for you: how much water have you had today? If you need to think about it for more than a few seconds, the answer is probably "not enough."

Hydration is the most basic, most accessible, and most underappreciated aspect of Type 1 Diabetes management. It requires no prescription, no technology, no special knowledge, and no money. And yet, when it goes wrong — when dehydration sets in — it makes every other part of diabetes management harder.

This topic explains why hydration matters specifically for people with T1D, how dehydration directly affects blood glucose, and what practical steps keep you ahead of the curve.

## How Dehydration Affects Blood Glucose

The relationship between hydration and glucose is straightforward, and understanding it changes how you think about that glass of water.

### Concentration Effect

Your blood is a liquid. Glucose is dissolved in that liquid. When you are well-hydrated, your blood volume is normal, and your glucose readings reflect the actual concentration of glucose in a normal volume of blood.

When you are dehydrated, your blood volume decreases. The same amount of glucose is now dissolved in a smaller volume of liquid. The result: your blood glucose reading goes up — not because there is more glucose, but because the glucose is more concentrated.

This is not a trivial effect. Mild dehydration can elevate blood glucose readings by 1–2 mmol/L. Significant dehydration can push readings even higher. This means that some of the "unexplained highs" people experience may not be caused by food, insulin, or stress — they may simply reflect inadequate hydration.

### Kidney Function

Your kidneys play an important role in glucose regulation. When blood glucose rises above approximately 10 mmol/L (the renal threshold), your kidneys begin filtering excess glucose into your urine. This is actually a protective mechanism — your body is trying to lower blood glucose by excreting it.

But this process requires water. Glucose pulls water with it into the urine through osmosis. This is why high blood glucose causes frequent urination — and why frequent urination leads to further dehydration. It becomes a cycle:

**High glucose → increased urination → dehydration → more concentrated blood → even higher glucose readings → more urination → more dehydration**

Breaking this cycle requires two things: addressing the glucose level (with your healthcare team's guidance) and drinking water.

### Insulin Absorption

Dehydration can also affect how well insulin absorbs from injection or infusion sites. Adequate hydration supports healthy blood flow to subcutaneous tissue, which is where injected or pumped insulin is delivered. Reduced blood flow from dehydration may slow insulin absorption, potentially reducing its effectiveness — another mechanism by which dehydration worsens glucose control.

## How Much Should You Drink?

There is no single universal answer, but practical guidelines exist:

### General Daily Intake

- **Adults:** Approximately 2–3 litres (8–12 glasses) of fluid per day
- **Teenagers:** Approximately 1.5–2.5 litres per day
- **Children (4–8 years):** Approximately 1–1.5 litres per day
- **Children (9–13 years):** Approximately 1.5–2 litres per day

These are baseline amounts for normal conditions. You need more in several situations:

### When You Need to Drink More

- **Hot weather:** Heat increases fluid loss through sweat. Add 500ml–1 litre on hot days.
- **Physical activity:** Exercise increases fluid needs significantly. Drink before, during, and after activity (see Topic 39).
- **When blood glucose is elevated:** If your glucose is above 10 mmol/L, actively increase your water intake. Your body is losing extra fluid through glucose-driven urination.
- **During illness:** Fever, vomiting, and diarrhoea all cause rapid fluid loss. Staying hydrated during illness is critical for T1D management and for preventing DKA (see Topic 73 and Topic 74).
- **During Ramadan:** Between Iftar and Suhoor, deliberate hydration is essential to prepare for the fasting hours. Drink water consistently through the evening — not just large amounts at Suhoor. Sipping throughout the non-fasting hours is more effective than drinking large volumes at once. Discuss hydration strategies for fasting days with your healthcare team.
- **In heated or air-conditioned environments:** Indoor heating in winter and air conditioning in summer both reduce ambient humidity and increase insensible fluid loss.

## What Counts as Hydration?

### Best Choices

- **Water** — the gold standard. No carbs, no calories, no complications.
- **Sparkling water / soda water** — equally hydrating, zero carbs.
- **Herbal teas** (unsweetened) — rooibos, peppermint, chamomile — all hydrating and zero carb.
- **Sugar-free flavoured water** — if plain water is not appealing, flavoured options are fine.

### Good Choices

- **Tea and coffee** (unsweetened or with a small amount of milk) — despite the old myth, moderate caffeine intake does not cause meaningful dehydration. A cup of tea or coffee provides net hydration.
- **Milk** — hydrating and nutritious, though it does contain carbohydrate (approximately 12g per 250ml for full-cream milk).
- **Diet or zero-sugar soft drinks** — they hydrate, though water is preferable as a primary fluid source.

### Poor Choices for Hydration

- **Regular soft drinks** — they provide fluid but also deliver 35–55g of fast-acting carbohydrate per 500ml, making glucose management harder.
- **Fruit juice** — contains significant carbohydrate (25–30g per 250ml). It is appropriate as a hypo treatment (see Topic 67) but not as a hydration strategy.
- **Energy drinks** — high in sugar (unless sugar-free versions), high in caffeine, and often contain other stimulants. Not recommended as a hydration source.
- **Alcohol** — acts as a diuretic and can worsen dehydration. Alcohol also has complex effects on blood glucose in T1D.

## Signs of Dehydration

Learning to recognise dehydration early is important. By the time you feel thirsty, you are already mildly dehydrated.

### Mild Dehydration

- Thirst
- Darker yellow urine (aim for pale straw colour)
- Dry lips and mouth
- Mild headache
- Slight fatigue or difficulty concentrating
- Reduced urine output

### Moderate Dehydration

- Strong thirst
- Dark yellow or amber urine
- Dry skin
- Dizziness when standing up
- Increased heart rate
- Notable fatigue
- Muscle cramps

### Severe Dehydration (Seek Medical Attention)

- Very dark urine or no urine output
- Extreme thirst
- Confusion or irritability
- Sunken eyes
- Rapid breathing and heartbeat
- Fainting or feeling faint

In the context of T1D, severe dehydration alongside high blood glucose and ketones may indicate diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) — a medical emergency. See Topic 73 for DKA awareness and Topic 74 for sick day management.

## Practical Hydration Strategies

### Make It Automatic

1. **Start each morning with a glass of water.** Before coffee, before breakfast, before checking your phone. Make it the first thing you do.

2. **Keep a water bottle with you at all times.** Visibility drives behaviour. If the bottle is on your desk, in your bag, or on the kitchen counter, you will drink from it.

3. **Set reminders.** Use your phone or smartwatch to prompt you every 1–2 hours. This is especially helpful for people who get absorbed in work or school and forget to drink.

4. **Drink a glass of water with every meal and snack.** This creates a natural rhythm — three meals and two snacks means five glasses of water without thinking about it.

5. **Track your intake for one week.** Many health apps allow fluid tracking. Knowing your actual intake often reveals a gap between what you think you drink and what you actually drink.

### Make It Appealing

6. **Add natural flavour.** Slices of lemon, lime, cucumber, or fresh mint make water more interesting without adding significant carbohydrate.

7. **Try different temperatures.** Some people prefer ice-cold water. Others prefer room temperature. Find what you enjoy and you will drink more of it.

8. **Invest in a bottle you like.** This sounds trivial, but a good water bottle that you enjoy using genuinely increases intake. Choose one with volume markings so you can track how much you drink.

### For Children and Teens

9. **Make it visible and accessible.** Keep water bottles at child height. Have a bottle packed in the school bag every day.

10. **Use a reward system for younger children.** A simple sticker chart for meeting daily water goals can be effective.

11. **Let them choose their own bottle.** Ownership increases use — a bottle they picked out themselves gets carried more often.

## Hydration During Illness

When you are sick — particularly with vomiting, diarrhoea, or fever — dehydration happens fast. This is one of the most dangerous times for hydration in T1D, because dehydration accelerates the progression toward DKA.

During illness:
- Sip water or clear fluids frequently, even if you cannot eat
- If vomiting prevents fluid intake, contact your healthcare team or seek medical care
- Monitor glucose and ketones closely (see Topic 74 for full sick day guidance)
- Oral rehydration solutions can replace lost electrolytes

This is not optional guidance. Hydration during T1D illness is a safety-critical priority.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Dehydration concentrates blood glucose** — even mild dehydration can elevate glucose readings by 1–2 mmol/L without any change in food or insulin.
2. **High blood glucose causes increased urination, which causes further dehydration** — this cycle must be actively broken with deliberate water intake.
3. **Most people need 2–3 litres of fluid daily, with more needed during heat, exercise, illness, and elevated glucose** — aim for pale straw-coloured urine as your hydration indicator.
4. **Water is the best hydration choice** — tea, coffee, and sugar-free drinks also contribute, while regular soft drinks and juice add carbohydrate load.
5. **During illness, hydration becomes a safety-critical priority** — dehydration accelerates DKA risk, and fluid intake must be maintained even when eating is difficult.
