## Hello from Mira

If you are living with Type 1 Diabetes today, you are managing a condition that looks profoundly different from what it looked like even ten years ago. Continuous glucose monitors, automated insulin delivery systems, faster-acting insulins — the tools available now would have seemed like science fiction a generation ago.

But what is coming next? What does the research pipeline hold? And — perhaps most importantly — how do you hold space for genuine hope without being swept up in hype?

I want to walk you through the most significant areas of active research and development, honestly and clearly. Some of these are close to reaching everyday practice. Others are further away. All of them are real science being pursued by serious researchers.

---

## Closed-loop systems — the next generation

Automated insulin delivery (AID) systems — sometimes called closed-loop or "artificial pancreas" systems — represent the most immediately impactful area of progress. These systems combine a CGM, an insulin pump, and a control algorithm that automatically adjusts insulin delivery based on real-time glucose data.

### Where we are now

Current commercially available systems are **hybrid closed-loop** — they automate basal insulin delivery and can deliver automatic correction boluses, but they still require the user to announce meals and enter carbohydrate estimates. Systems like the Medtronic 780G, Tandem Control-IQ, CamAPS FX, Omnipod 5, and several others are in clinical use or regulatory review across different regions.

The evidence is strong. Clinical trials and real-world data published in journals including *The Lancet*, *Diabetes Care*, and *The New England Journal of Medicine* have shown that hybrid closed-loop systems improve Time in Range, reduce HbA1c, decrease hypoglycaemia, and improve quality of life compared to standard pump therapy or MDI.

### What is coming

The next frontier is **fully closed-loop** systems — sometimes called "hands-free" systems — that manage both basal and meal-related insulin delivery without requiring meal announcements. Several research groups and companies are working on this, with promising trial results emerging.

Challenges remain. Meals cause rapid glucose spikes that are difficult for algorithms to manage without advance warning. Faster-acting insulins (discussed below) and dual-hormone systems may help bridge this gap. But the trajectory is clear: systems are becoming progressively more automated, more accurate, and less burdensome to use.

The integration of **machine learning and artificial intelligence** into control algorithms is also an active area. Algorithms that learn from individual patterns and adapt over time could further personalise and improve automated delivery.

## Faster-acting and smarter insulins

The insulin available today is dramatically better than what was available even two decades ago. But current rapid-acting insulins still take approximately 15-20 minutes to begin working and peak at 60-90 minutes — a profile that does not perfectly match the speed at which food raises blood glucose.

### Ultra-rapid insulins

Ultra-rapid insulin formulations — designed to begin working faster and peak sooner — are in clinical use and continued development. These insulins better match the post-meal glucose curve and may improve post-meal glucose control, particularly in closed-loop systems where speed of insulin action is critical.

### Glucose-responsive ("smart") insulin

Perhaps the most exciting insulin research frontier is **glucose-responsive insulin** — sometimes called "smart insulin." The concept is an insulin that is always present in the body but only becomes active when glucose levels rise. When glucose is normal or low, the insulin remains inert. When glucose rises, the insulin activates.

If achieved, this would fundamentally change T1D management. It would dramatically reduce the risk of hypoglycaemia and could potentially be delivered as a once-daily injection or even a weekly injection, removing much of the daily burden of management.

Several research groups and pharmaceutical companies are pursuing this concept. Some approaches use glucose-sensing molecular switches built into the insulin molecule itself. Others use encapsulation technologies that release insulin in response to glucose concentration.

**Where does it stand?** As of now, glucose-responsive insulin is in **early-stage human trials**. It has shown proof of concept in animal models and early Phase 1 studies. It is not yet close to clinical availability, and significant scientific hurdles remain — particularly achieving the right sensitivity and speed of response. But it is real science, not fantasy, and it is being actively pursued by credible research teams.

## Immunotherapy — stopping the attack

Type 1 Diabetes is caused by the immune system destroying beta cells. What if we could stop that destruction — or even prevent it from starting?

### Teplizumab and disease modification

Teplizumab — an anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody — made headlines as the first FDA-approved therapy to **delay the onset of clinical T1D** in people at high risk. The TrialNet TN-10 study showed that a single course of teplizumab delayed the onset of clinical T1D by a median of approximately two years in at-risk individuals.

This is a genuine milestone. It does not cure T1D, and it does not prevent it permanently, but it demonstrates that the autoimmune process can be **modified** — slowed or interrupted — with targeted immunotherapy.

### Broader immunotherapy research

Numerous other immunotherapy approaches are under investigation:

- **Antigen-specific therapies** that aim to retrain the immune system to tolerate beta cells without broadly suppressing immunity
- **Regulatory T-cell therapies** that boost the immune system's own suppressive mechanisms
- **Combination approaches** that pair immunotherapy with beta cell preservation or regeneration strategies

The challenge with immunotherapy is balancing efficacy against safety. Broadly suppressing the immune system carries risks — including increased susceptibility to infections and other conditions. The goal is precision: targeting the specific immune pathways involved in beta cell destruction while leaving the rest of the immune system intact.

## Beta cell replacement and regeneration

If the immune attack destroys beta cells, could we replace them?

### Islet transplantation

Transplanting insulin-producing islet cells from donor pancreases has been performed for decades, but it is limited by the need for lifelong immunosuppression (to prevent rejection) and by a severe shortage of donor organs.

### Stem cell-derived beta cells

This is one of the most actively pursued areas. Researchers have developed methods to grow **insulin-producing cells from stem cells** in the laboratory. Several companies have advanced to human clinical trials, with early results showing that transplanted stem cell-derived cells can produce insulin in recipients.

The remaining challenges are significant:

- **Immune protection** — the transplanted cells must be protected from the same immune attack that destroyed the original beta cells. Encapsulation devices (physical barriers that allow nutrients and insulin to pass but block immune cells) and gene-editing approaches to make cells "immune-invisible" are both being tested.
- **Scalability** — producing enough cells for widespread clinical use at consistent quality is a manufacturing challenge.
- **Long-term survival** — ensuring the transplanted cells continue to function for years, not just months.

### Beta cell regeneration

Rather than transplanting new cells, some research aims to stimulate the body to **regenerate its own beta cells** — either by coaxing existing pancreatic cells to convert into beta cells or by activating dormant regenerative pathways. This remains at an early research stage, but the concept is compelling.

## Realistic hope vs hype

Every person living with T1D has heard the phrase "a cure is five years away" — often multiple times across many years. It is understandable to feel cynicism alongside hope.

Here is my honest assessment:

- **Closed-loop technology is real and available now**, with meaningful improvements expected in the next few years. This is the area most likely to change your day-to-day experience in the near term.
- **Immunotherapy has achieved a genuine proof of concept** with teplizumab, and the field is progressing. Disease modification — not cure, but meaningful delay or reduction — is becoming realistic.
- **Stem cell-derived beta cells have reached human trials**, but widespread clinical availability is likely years away, and the immune protection problem is not yet fully solved.
- **Glucose-responsive insulin is scientifically promising** but still in early development. It is not a near-term reality.

None of these is a cure. All of them represent genuine, measurable progress. The future of T1D management is not a single breakthrough — it is the **accumulation of incremental advances** across technology, immunology, and cell biology that collectively make the condition progressively more manageable.

Hold hope. But hold it alongside the understanding that progress is measured in steps, not leaps, and that the tools available to you right now are genuinely powerful.

## Five key takeaways

1. **Next-generation closed-loop systems are moving toward fully automated insulin delivery**, reducing the need for meal announcements and leveraging machine learning for increasingly personalised glucose management.
2. **Glucose-responsive ("smart") insulin — which activates only when glucose rises — is in early human trials**, representing a potentially transformative advance, though clinical availability is still years away.
3. **Immunotherapy has achieved proof of concept**, with teplizumab demonstrating the ability to delay clinical T1D onset. Broader immunotherapy approaches are in active development.
4. **Stem cell-derived beta cells have reached human clinical trials**, but challenges in immune protection, scalability, and long-term cell survival remain significant hurdles before widespread availability.
5. **Progress in T1D is real and accelerating, but it is incremental.** The future is not a single cure announcement — it is the steady accumulation of better tools, deeper understanding, and more effective therapies that collectively transform the experience of living with T1D.
