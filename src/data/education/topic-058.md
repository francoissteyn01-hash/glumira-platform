## Hello from Mira

One of the most powerful things about understanding insulin is the ability to think ahead — to ask "what if?" before you act, rather than discovering the consequences after the fact. What if I eat now? What if I wait an hour? What if I correct this high reading? What if I exercise instead?

These are not idle questions. They are the daily calculations that every person managing insulin-treated diabetes makes, often multiple times a day. And for most of the history of diabetes management, those calculations have been done in the dark — relying on experience, intuition, and sometimes guesswork.

GluMira's **IOB Hunter** was built to bring light into that darkness. Not to make decisions for you, but to help you **explore scenarios with information**, so that learning replaces guessing.

---

## The flight simulator analogy

Think of a flight simulator. Trainee pilots do not learn to fly by reading a manual and then climbing into a cockpit at 30,000 feet. They sit in a simulator and practise. They ask: "What happens if I bank too steeply? What happens if the engine fails? What happens if I come in too fast?" They learn by exploring scenarios in a safe environment, where mistakes are lessons rather than emergencies.

IOB Hunter works on the same principle. It is not a cockpit — it is a simulator. It lets you explore what your insulin is doing and ask questions about what might happen next, using the data you have already logged. It helps you build intuition through information, not trial and error.

This is an important distinction. IOB Hunter is not telling you what to do. It is not prescribing a dose or recommending an action. It is showing you the landscape — your current IOB, the activity curve, the time remaining — and letting you think through the possibilities with your care team's guidance.

## Scenario 1 — "What if I eat now?"

You took your lunch bolus two hours ago. You are feeling peckish and considering an afternoon snack. Before you reach for food, you glance at IOB Hunter.

It shows you still have 1.2 units of active insulin from your lunch dose. The activity curve is declining but still meaningful. What does this tell you?

It tells you that there is insulin in your system that is still working to lower your blood glucose. If you eat carbohydrates now, some of those carbohydrates may be covered — at least partially — by the remaining IOB from lunch. Depending on the size and type of snack, you might not need an additional bolus at all, or you might need a smaller one than you would otherwise calculate.

This is not a decision IOB Hunter makes for you. It is information that helps you and your care team think about the right approach. The key insight is this: without knowing your IOB, you might bolus for the full carbohydrate content of the snack and end up with more insulin than needed — a stacking scenario.

## Scenario 2 — "What if I correct this high?"

Your blood glucose is 13 mmol/L (234 mg/dL) and you want to bring it down. Your correction factor says one unit should lower you by about 3 mmol/L. Simple maths says you need roughly 2 units to get to your target of 7 mmol/L.

But you check IOB Hunter first. It shows 0.8 units of active insulin from your meal bolus taken 2.5 hours ago. That 0.8 units is still working, still lowering your blood glucose. If you add a full 2-unit correction on top, the total effective insulin will be closer to 2.8 units of glucose-lowering power — and that may bring you well below your target.

The what-if here is educational: "If I had corrected without checking IOB, what might have happened?" IOB Hunter lets you see the overlap, the compounding effect, the potential for a hypo later in the afternoon. It does not prevent you from correcting — but it ensures you correct with full awareness of what is already in play.

## Scenario 3 — "What if I exercise right now?"

You are thinking about going for a walk or a run. Exercise increases your muscles' sensitivity to insulin and accelerates glucose uptake — which is generally beneficial, but can be problematic if there is still significant IOB.

You check IOB Hunter. It shows 1.5 units of active insulin, with the curve suggesting most of the activity is in the tail phase. You now have information: exercise will amplify the effect of that remaining insulin. The combination of the tail and increased insulin sensitivity from exercise could lower your blood glucose more than either factor alone.

Armed with this knowledge, you might decide to have a small carbohydrate snack before exercising, or to wait until IOB is lower, or to check your blood glucose more frequently during the activity. These are decisions you make with your care team's guidance — but the information from IOB Hunter gives you a starting point for the conversation.

---

## Scenario 4 — "What if I wait?"

Sometimes the most powerful what-if is the simplest: what if I just wait?

Your blood glucose is high, and every instinct tells you to act. But IOB Hunter shows you still have significant active insulin. The activity curve is declining, which means the insulin is still working. What if you wait 30 minutes and recheck?

This is perhaps the most underrated scenario. Waiting is not the same as doing nothing. Waiting is **giving the previous dose time to work**. It is allowing the pharmacokinetics to play out before layering on more. And it is one of the most effective ways to avoid insulin stacking.

IOB Hunter makes waiting easier because it shows you why waiting makes sense. You are not waiting blindly — you are watching the curve decline, watching the IOB number drop, and watching the insulin do its job. It transforms an anxious wait into an informed one.

## Learning patterns over time

The what-if mindset is not just useful in the moment — it builds knowledge over time. When you regularly check IOB Hunter before making decisions, you start to notice patterns:

- "Every time I correct within two hours of my lunch bolus, I go low by 4 pm."
- "When I exercise with more than 1 unit of IOB, I need a snack. When IOB is below 0.5, I am usually fine."
- "My insulin seems to last longer than the 4-hour DIA I have set — I might need to talk to my care team about adjusting it."

These observations are gold. They represent personalised learning that no textbook can provide, because they come from your data, your insulin, your body. IOB Hunter provides the lens; you provide the experience. Together, they build a picture that gets sharper over time.

## The educational philosophy

Let me be clear about what IOB Hunter is and what it is not. It is an **educational awareness tool**. It shows you what your insulin is doing based on the data you have logged and the parameters you and your care team have set. It helps you explore scenarios, build understanding, and approach daily decisions with more information.

It is **not** a dosing calculator. It does not tell you how much insulin to take. It does not replace clinical guidance. It does not make medical decisions. The what-if scenarios I have described above are educational thought exercises — ways of thinking about insulin that help you become a more informed partner in your own care.

Every decision about insulin — how much, when, and whether to take it — should be made in consultation with your care team. IOB Hunter gives you the information to have richer, more specific conversations with them. "I noticed that when I correct with 1.5 units of IOB, I tend to go low" is a far more useful observation than "I sometimes go low in the afternoon." It is the difference between a vague concern and actionable data.

## The power of informed curiosity

Diabetes management is lifelong. The people who manage it best are often those who stay curious — who keep asking "what if?" not out of anxiety, but out of a genuine desire to understand. IOB Hunter is built for that curiosity. It is a safe space to explore, to learn, and to grow in confidence.

So the next time you reach for your insulin, pause for a moment. Open IOB Hunter. Ask a what-if question. See what the curve tells you. And then — with that information in hand — talk to your care team about the best next step.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **IOB Hunter lets you explore what-if scenarios with real data,** helping you understand the implications of eating, correcting, exercising, or waiting before you act.
2. **Checking IOB before a correction reveals potential stacking.** The overlap between a previous dose and a new one becomes visible, not hidden.
3. **Exercise amplifies the effect of active insulin.** Knowing your IOB before physical activity helps you and your care team plan for safety.
4. **Waiting is not doing nothing.** Sometimes the most powerful action is allowing the previous dose to finish its job, and IOB Hunter shows you why.
5. **IOB Hunter is an educational tool, not a dosing calculator.** It provides information to support conversations with your care team, not to replace clinical guidance.
