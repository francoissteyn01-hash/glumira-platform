# Long-Acting Insulin Profiles — Tresiba vs Levemir vs Lantus

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with diabetes, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 056
**Related Topics:** [topic-055](/education/topic-055), [topic-052](/education/topic-052), [topic-059](/education/topic-059)

---

## Hello from Mira

If rapid-acting insulin is the sprinter — fast, intense, and done within a few hours — then long-acting insulin is the marathon runner. It does not rush. It does not peak dramatically. It just keeps going, quietly, steadily, hour after hour, through the night and into the next day.

Long-acting insulin provides your **basal** coverage — the background insulin your body needs around the clock, even when you are not eating. As we explored in [topic-052](/education/topic-052), basal insulin keeps blood glucose stable between meals and overnight by counteracting the glucose your liver continuously releases.

But not all long-acting insulins run the marathon the same way. Tresiba, Levemir, and Lantus each have distinct profiles, and understanding those differences helps you see why your regimen works the way it does.

---

## The three marathon runners

**Tresiba (insulin degludec)** is the ultra-marathon runner. Once injected, it forms a depot under the skin that releases insulin slowly and steadily for more than 42 hours. It has the longest duration of any basal insulin currently available and the flattest profile — meaning it works at a remarkably consistent rate without a significant peak.

**Lantus (insulin glargine U100)** is the steady marathon runner. It provides approximately 20 to 24 hours of coverage with a relatively flat profile. It was the first "peakless" basal insulin to reach the market and transformed diabetes management when it arrived. In practice, some individuals do experience a mild peak around 6 to 8 hours, but it is far less pronounced than older basal insulins.

**Levemir (insulin detemir)** is the reliable half-marathon runner. Its duration is typically **12 to 20 hours**, which is shorter than Lantus or Tresiba. Because of this, many people on Levemir take it **twice daily** — splitting the dose into a morning and evening injection to ensure continuous basal coverage. Levemir also has a mild peak, typically around 6 to 8 hours after injection.

---

## Duration — the most important difference

The single most important distinction between these three insulins is **how long they work**.

| Insulin | Duration | Typical Dosing |
|---|---|---|
| **Tresiba** | >42 hours | Once daily (flexible timing) |
| **Lantus** | ~20-24 hours | Once daily (consistent timing preferred) |
| **Levemir** | ~12-20 hours | Once or twice daily |

Tresiba's ultra-long duration gives it a unique property: **dose timing flexibility**. Because its effect overlaps significantly from one day to the next, the exact time you inject matters less. Clinical studies have shown that Tresiba can be injected at varying times day to day without significant impact on glucose control — though consistency is always preferred when practical.

Lantus, with its 20-24 hour duration, is more sensitive to injection timing. If you inject at 10 pm one night and 6 am the next morning, there may be a gap in coverage. Most care teams recommend taking Lantus at the same time each day.

Levemir's shorter duration means that for many people, a single daily dose does not provide full 24-hour coverage. This is why split dosing — typically morning and evening — is common. While this means an extra injection, it also gives care teams more flexibility to adjust the daytime and nighttime basal rates independently.

---

## Peak — or peakless?

In the world of basal insulin, "peakless" is the gold standard. The ideal basal insulin would work at a perfectly constant rate, like a dripping tap that never speeds up or slows down. In reality, nothing is perfectly flat, but some insulins come closer than others.

**Tresiba** has the flattest profile of the three. Its slow, steady release from the subcutaneous depot means that insulin activity is remarkably consistent over 24 hours and beyond. This flat profile translates to more predictable blood glucose levels and, in clinical studies, a lower risk of nocturnal hypoglycaemia.

**Lantus** was marketed as "peakless" and is close to it for most people, though some individuals do notice a mild increase in activity around 6 to 8 hours post-injection. This is generally not clinically significant, but it is worth being aware of.

**Levemir** has a slightly more defined peak, typically around 6 to 8 hours, and then activity declines. This peak-and-decline pattern is one of the reasons Levemir is often split into two doses — to prevent a dip in basal coverage as one dose wears off.

---

## The reservoir analogy

Think of each injection as filling a reservoir. The reservoir has a small, steady outflow that represents insulin being released into your bloodstream.

With **Tresiba**, the reservoir is enormous. Each injection fills it generously, and the outflow is remarkably constant. Even if you are a few hours late with your next injection, the reservoir has not run dry. This provides a buffer of safety and consistency.

With **Lantus**, the reservoir is large but not quite as vast. The outflow is steady, but the reservoir can run low if the next injection is significantly delayed. Consistent timing keeps the outflow — and therefore your basal coverage — smooth.

With **Levemir**, the reservoir is smaller. It fills well but drains faster. To keep the outflow constant throughout 24 hours, you may need to fill it twice — morning and evening — ensuring it never runs low.

## How basal insulin affects total IOB

Here is a question people sometimes ask: "If I am on long-acting insulin, does that count as Insulin On Board?"

Technically, yes — basal insulin is active insulin in your body. But in practice, basal insulin is handled **differently** from bolus insulin in IOB calculations. Because basal insulin works at a low, steady rate, it does not create the peaks and valleys that drive short-term glucose changes. It is like background noise — always there, but not the signal that causes sudden shifts.

**IOB Hunter** in GluMira primarily focuses on **bolus IOB** — the insulin from your mealtime and correction doses that creates defined activity curves. However, understanding your basal insulin is essential context, because:

- **If your basal is too high**, you may see blood glucose dropping between meals even without bolus IOB. This looks like a hypo risk that IOB Hunter alone would not explain.
- **If your basal is too low**, blood glucose may rise between meals, tempting you to give correction boluses that then compound with later meal boluses.
- **If your basal timing is off**, you may see patterns at specific times of day — such as rising blood glucose before breakfast (the dawn phenomenon, explored in [topic-059](/education/topic-059)) — that point to a basal, not bolus, issue.

Your total insulin picture includes both basal and bolus. GluMira helps you see the bolus side in detail; your care team helps you optimise the basal foundation beneath it.

## Split dosing — why some people take basal twice

For people on Levemir, or in some cases Lantus, split dosing is a strategy where the total basal dose is divided into two injections — typically morning and evening. This can provide smoother 24-hour coverage than a single dose that may taper off before the next injection is due.

Split dosing also gives care teams the ability to adjust daytime and nighttime basal rates independently. For example, if someone experiences the dawn phenomenon (rising blood glucose in the early morning hours), their care team might increase the evening basal dose while keeping the morning dose the same.

The decision to use single or split dosing is individualised and should always be made in consultation with your care team.

---

## A word about newer options

The landscape of basal insulin continues to evolve. Newer formulations, such as insulin glargine U300 (Toujeo), offer even longer durations and flatter profiles than their predecessors. Your care team will be aware of the options available in your region and can discuss whether a different basal insulin might suit your needs better.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Tresiba, Lantus, and Levemir are the three most commonly used long-acting basal insulins,** each with a distinct duration, peak profile, and dosing schedule.
2. **Tresiba has the longest duration (>42 hours) and the flattest profile,** offering the most flexibility in injection timing and the most consistent basal coverage.
3. **Lantus provides approximately 20-24 hours of relatively flat coverage** and works best when taken at a consistent time each day.
4. **Levemir has a shorter duration (12-20 hours) and is often split into two daily doses** to maintain continuous basal coverage.
5. **Basal insulin provides the foundation beneath your bolus doses.** While IOB Hunter focuses on bolus activity, understanding your basal profile is essential for interpreting your overall glucose patterns.
