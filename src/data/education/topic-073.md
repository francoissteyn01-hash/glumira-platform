Gastroenteritis — the common stomach bug that causes vomiting and diarrhoea — is unpleasant for anyone. For a person with Type 1 Diabetes, it can become a medical emergency within hours. Understanding why, and knowing exactly what to do, can prevent that.

---

## Why this scenario is so dangerous

Here is the core problem, stated as simply as I can:

**Insulin is still active in the body. But the person cannot eat or drink to match it.**

When someone with T1D takes insulin — whether by injection or pump — that insulin is going to work regardless of whether food follows. If a stomach bug means food cannot be kept down, glucose levels can plummet dangerously (hypoglycaemia). At the same time, if insulin delivery is reduced too much or stopped in an attempt to prevent lows, ketones begin to build up and DKA becomes the threat instead.

This creates a cruel paradox:
- **Too much active insulin + no food absorption = severe hypoglycaemia**
- **Too little insulin + illness stress hormones = rising ketones and DKA**

The person is caught between two emergencies. That is why this scenario demands careful, informed, step-by-step management — and why you must know when to call for help.

---

## What happens in the body during gastroenteritis with T1D

When the stomach and intestines are inflamed (gastroenteritis), several things happen simultaneously:

1. **Food and fluids are vomited or pass through too quickly to be absorbed.** Carbohydrates eaten before vomiting may not have been fully absorbed, meaning the insulin calculated for that food is now "unmatched."

2. **The body mounts a stress response.** Illness triggers the release of counter-regulatory hormones — cortisol, adrenaline, glucagon, and growth hormone. These hormones raise blood glucose and increase the body's need for insulin. Even without eating, BG may rise.

3. **Dehydration accelerates.** Vomiting and diarrhoea cause rapid fluid loss. Dehydration makes blood glucose readings higher (because the blood is more concentrated) and makes the body less responsive to insulin.

4. **Ketone production increases.** If cells are not getting enough glucose (because of vomiting) or enough insulin (because doses were skipped), the body turns to fat for fuel and produces ketones. Add dehydration to this, and the situation escalates fast.

In children, this cascade can progress from "a bit of a tummy bug" to a medical emergency in as little as **two to four hours**.

## Step-by-step protocol — The Gastroenteritis Crisis Protocol

This protocol is for guidance only. Always follow the sick-day plan agreed with your diabetes care team. Contact your team early — do not wait until things are out of control.

### Step 1 — Begin monitoring immediately

The moment vomiting starts in someone with T1D, shift into active management mode.

- Check **blood glucose** every 30–60 minutes. If using a CGM, watch the trend arrows closely but confirm with a finger-prick if readings seem unreliable (dehydration can affect sensor accuracy).
- Check **blood ketones** immediately, then every 1–2 hours.
- Record everything: time, BG, ketones, vomiting episodes, fluid intake.

### Step 2 — Maintain hydration with small, frequent sips

The person may not be able to drink large amounts. That is okay. Small sips, very frequently, are the goal.

- Offer **small sips every 5–10 minutes** — even 5 mL (a teaspoon) at a time counts.
- Use **oral rehydration solution** (such as Dioralyte or Pedialyte) if available. This replaces salts and electrolytes lost through vomiting.
- If BG is dropping or low, use **sugary fluids** — flat lemonade, diluted juice, or dissolved glucose tablets in water. The sugar serves a double purpose: hydration and glucose replacement.
- If BG is high, use **sugar-free fluids** — water, sugar-free oral rehydration solution.
- **Glucose gel** (such as GlucoGel or Dextrogel) applied to the inside of the cheek can be absorbed even when swallowing is difficult. This can be a lifeline when the person cannot keep fluids down.

### Step 3 — Do NOT stop insulin entirely

This is critical. The instinct when someone is vomiting and not eating is to stop giving insulin. **This is dangerous.** Without insulin, ketones will rise and DKA will follow.

The body still needs **basal (background) insulin** even when not eating. Contact your diabetes team for guidance on adjusting doses during illness. GluMira does not provide dosing instructions — your team will advise on the right approach for your specific situation.

### Step 4 — Watch the ketones relentlessly

Ketones are your early warning system. They tell you whether the body is coping or tipping toward crisis.

- **Below 0.6 mmol/L** — Keep monitoring. Continue hydration.
- **0.6–1.4 mmol/L** — Elevated. Contact your diabetes team. Increase fluids. Recheck in one hour.
- **1.5–2.9 mmol/L** — Significantly elevated. Speak to your diabetes team urgently if you have not already.
- **3.0 mmol/L or above** — **Go to the emergency department now.**

### Step 5 — Know the emergency triggers

**Call an ambulance or go to the emergency department immediately if ANY of these occur:**

- **Vomiting continues for more than 2 hours** and the person cannot keep any fluids down at all
- **Ketones are rising despite treatment** and are above 1.5 mmol/L
- **Blood glucose drops below 3.5 mmol/L** and you cannot raise it because the person is vomiting
- **Blood glucose is persistently above 14 mmol/L** with ketones above 1.5 mmol/L
- The person becomes **drowsy, confused, or difficult to rouse**
- **Breathing becomes rapid or laboured**
- The person is a **baby or toddler** and you cannot maintain hydration
- **You feel out of your depth** — this alone is a valid reason to seek emergency help

## The moment it becomes an emergency

When vomiting and rising ketones happen together, you are looking at a situation that can become DKA. This is the exact scenario where lives are lost when families try to manage at home for too long.

The combination of **persistent vomiting + rising ketones = EMERGENCY**. Do not wait for the person to "look really sick." By the time they look really sick, DKA may already be advanced.

In SUBJ-NAM-001's case, it was the speed of deterioration that caught her family off guard. A child who seemed to have an ordinary stomach bug moved toward a dangerous metabolic crisis faster than anyone expected. That experience — and the determination that no other family should face it unprepared — is woven into every line of this topic.

## After the vomiting stops — recovery

Once the person can keep fluids down consistently:
- Continue monitoring BG and ketones frequently for 24–48 hours.
- Reintroduce bland, easily digestible foods slowly — plain crackers, toast, rice, banana.
- Ensure insulin delivery returns to normal patterns as eating resumes.
- Replenish fluids — dehydration can persist even after vomiting stops.
- Debrief with your diabetes care team. Discuss what worked, what did not, and update your sick-day plan if needed.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Gastroenteritis with T1D is dangerous** because insulin keeps working even when food cannot be kept down — creating a simultaneous risk of hypos and DKA.
2. **Never stop insulin entirely during vomiting** — the body still needs basal insulin; stopping it leads to ketone buildup and DKA. Contact your diabetes team for dose guidance.
3. **Small, frequent sips are critical** — even a teaspoon at a time; use glucose gel on the inner cheek if the person cannot swallow fluids.
4. **Check ketones every 1–2 hours** during any vomiting episode — ketones are your early warning system for DKA.
5. **Persistent vomiting + rising ketones = EMERGENCY** — go to hospital immediately; do not try to ride it out at home.
