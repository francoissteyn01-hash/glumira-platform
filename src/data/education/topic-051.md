# What is Insulin On Board and Why Does It Matter?

## Hello from Mira

Have you ever poured water into a tank and wondered exactly how much was left an hour later? Two hours later? That is, in a simple way, the question at the heart of one of the most important concepts in insulin therapy — **Insulin On Board**, often shortened to **IOB**.

Understanding IOB is one of the most powerful things you can do for your diabetes management. It is also one of the most commonly misunderstood. So let me walk you through it gently, with no jargon left unexplained, and by the end of this topic I hope you will feel genuinely confident about what IOB means and why it matters so much.

---

## The water tank analogy

Imagine you have a tall, clear water tank sitting on your kitchen counter. Every time you take a dose of rapid-acting insulin, it is like pouring a jug of water into the top of that tank. The water does not disappear instantly — it drains out slowly through a small tap at the bottom.

Right after you pour, the tank is full. An hour later, some water has drained. Two hours later, more has gone. After four or five hours, the tank is nearly empty. The water still in the tank at any given moment? That is your **Insulin On Board**.

IOB is the amount of insulin from previous doses that is still active in your body and still working to lower your blood glucose. It has been injected, it has been absorbed, and it is still doing its job — even though you may have taken that dose hours ago.

This matters because insulin does not work like a light switch. You cannot flip it on and then flip it off. Once it is in your body, it follows a curve — rising to a peak of activity, then slowly tapering off. The tail end of that curve is still lowering your blood glucose, even if you have forgotten about the dose entirely.

---

## Why IOB is so important

Here is where it gets practical. Let us say you took a dose of rapid-acting insulin at lunch. Two hours later, your blood glucose reads higher than you would like. Your instinct might be to take another correction dose. But if you do not account for the insulin still active from lunch — the water still draining from the tank — you may end up with far more active insulin than you intended.

This is called **insulin stacking**, and it is one of the most common causes of unexpected hypoglycaemia. We cover stacking in detail in [topic-053](/education/topic-053), but the principle starts here: if you do not know how much insulin is still on board, you cannot make a safe, informed decision about whether more is needed.

Think of it this way. If someone asked you to pour more water into the tank, your first question would be: "How much is already in there?" That is exactly the question IOB answers for insulin.

---

## How long does insulin stay on board?

The duration depends on the type of insulin. For most rapid-acting insulins (such as NovoRapid, Humalog, or Fiasp), the generally accepted duration of action is between **3 and 5 hours**, though this varies between individuals. Some people metabolise insulin faster; others slower. Factors like injection site, temperature, physical activity, and individual physiology all play a role.

What is consistent is the shape of the curve. After injection, insulin activity rises to a peak (usually between 60 and 90 minutes for rapid-acting), and then it gradually declines. The later portion of that decline — the **tail** — is where many people get caught out. The insulin is quieter, less dramatic in its effect, but it is still there, still working. We explore this tail in detail in [topic-057](/education/topic-057).

Your care team will help you understand your personal insulin duration. This is sometimes called your **Duration of Insulin Action (DIA)** setting, and it is a critical part of how IOB is calculated.

## IOB is not just about corrections

One common misconception is that IOB only matters when you are considering a correction dose. In reality, IOB is relevant any time you are making a decision that involves insulin or food:

- **Before eating:** If you still have insulin on board, the carbohydrates you eat may be partially covered already. Understanding this helps avoid over-bolusing.
- **Before exercise:** Active insulin combined with physical activity can cause blood glucose to drop faster than expected. Knowing your IOB before you move is essential.
- **Before sleep:** Going to bed with significant IOB means insulin is still working while you are not awake to monitor. This is one of the scenarios your care team will discuss with you carefully.
- **Before driving:** Hypoglycaemia while driving is dangerous. Knowing your IOB adds a layer of awareness before you get behind the wheel.

In each of these situations, the question is the same: how much water is still in the tank?

## A note on different insulin types

IOB calculations primarily focus on **rapid-acting** or **short-acting** insulin, because these are the insulins with a defined, relatively short activity curve. Long-acting (basal) insulins like Tresiba, Lantus, or Levemir work differently — they provide a slow, steady background level of insulin over many hours. While they technically contribute to total active insulin, they are usually handled separately in IOB calculations. We explore the difference between basal and bolus insulin in [topic-052](/education/topic-052).

## Five key takeaways

1. **Insulin On Board (IOB) is the amount of previously injected insulin still active in your body.** It does not disappear the moment you inject — it follows a curve over several hours.
2. **Think of it like water in a tank.** Each dose fills the tank, and it drains slowly. Knowing how much is left helps you avoid overfilling.
3. **IOB is essential for preventing insulin stacking** — taking additional doses without accounting for what is already working, which can lead to unexpected hypoglycaemia.
4. **IOB Hunter in GluMira visualises your active insulin in real time,** helping you see the curve and understand where you are in the insulin activity window.
5. **IOB matters in every decision involving insulin, food, exercise, sleep, and driving** — not just correction doses.
