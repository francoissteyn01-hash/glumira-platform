# What is Type 1 Diabetes? (Age 6-10 Child Version)

**Age Range:** 6-10 years
**Audience:** Children
**Topic ID:** 002

## Hi there! I'm Mira

Hello, friend! I am Mira the owl, and I am here to help you understand something important about your body. You have something called **Type 1 Diabetes**. That might sound like a really big, scary word — but I promise you, by the time we finish reading this together, it will make a lot more sense. Ready? Let's go!

---

## The story of your body's security guards

Inside your body, you have an amazing team of tiny helpers called your **immune system**. Think of them like security guards at a castle. Their job is to spot bad guys — like germs and viruses — and chase them away so you stay healthy. Most of the time, they do a brilliant job!

But sometimes, those security guards make a mistake. They get confused and think that something good inside your body is actually a bad guy. In your case, your security guards looked at some very important cells in a small organ called your **pancreas** and thought, "Hmm, those look suspicious!" So they attacked them.

Here is the thing: those cells were not bad guys at all. They were the **insulin-making cells**. And your body really, really needs insulin.

---

## The pancreas factory

Your pancreas is like a little factory hidden behind your stomach. Its most important job is to make **insulin**. Insulin is like a special key.

Imagine every cell in your body is a tiny room with a locked door. When you eat food — like an apple, or pasta, or a sandwich — your body breaks that food down into a type of sugar called **glucose**. Glucose is fuel. It is what gives you energy to run, play, think, and grow.

But glucose cannot just walk into your cells on its own. It needs the insulin key to unlock the door. Without the key, glucose gets stuck in your blood, floating around with nowhere to go. Your blood sugar level goes up and up, but your cells are hungry because they cannot get the fuel they need.

That is what happened in your body. Your security guards damaged the key-making part of the factory. So now, your pancreas cannot make enough insulin keys — or maybe it cannot make any at all.

---

## So what do we do about it?

Here is the really good news: we can give your body insulin from the outside! That is what your injections or your insulin pump are for. They deliver the insulin your pancreas can no longer make. Think of it like getting a spare set of keys delivered to your door every single day.

You also need to check your **blood glucose levels** — that means finding out how much sugar is floating around in your blood at different times. This helps you, your family, and your care team figure out if you need more keys, fewer keys, or if everything is just right.

In GluMira, when we talk about how insulin works in your body over time, you might see something called the **IOB Hunter**. It is a way to see how much insulin is still working. It is like tracking how many keys are still out there unlocking doors. Pretty cool, right?

## Things that were NOT your fault

Let me be really, really clear about something:

- You did **not** get Type 1 Diabetes because you ate too many sweets.
- You did **not** get it because you did something wrong.
- You **cannot** catch it from someone else, like a cold.
- Nobody can give it to you, and you cannot give it to anyone.

Your security guards just made a mistake. It happens. Scientists all around the world — guided by groups like ISPAD and the ADA — are studying why this happens, and they are working hard to find even better ways to help.

---

## You can still do ALL the things

Having Type 1 Diabetes does not mean you have to stop doing the things you love. You can still:

- **Play sports** and run around at break time
- **Go to birthday parties** and enjoy food with your friends
- **Go on sleepovers** (with a little planning!)
- **Travel** and go on adventures
- **Dream big** — there are athletes, astronauts, musicians, and scientists who have Type 1 Diabetes

You just need to do a few extra things that other kids do not have to do — like checking your blood sugar and taking insulin. It is a bit like wearing glasses. Some kids need glasses to see clearly. You need insulin to use your fuel properly. It is just part of how your body works, and it is nothing to be embarrassed about.

## The feelings part

It is totally okay to feel all sorts of feelings about this. You might feel:

- **Annoyed** — because finger pricks and injections are not fun
- **Confused** — because it is a lot of new information
- **Sad** — because you wish things were different
- **Worried** — about what your friends will think

All of those feelings are normal. Even grown-ups feel those things when something big changes. If you are feeling upset, talk to someone you trust — your mum, dad, carer, teacher, or someone on your care team. They want to hear how you are feeling.

---

## When to ask a grown-up for help right away

If you ever feel:
- **Really shaky, dizzy, or sweaty** — your blood sugar might be too low (that is called a "hypo"), and you need some fast sugar like juice or glucose tablets straight away
- **Really thirsty, needing to wee a lot, or feeling sick** — your blood sugar might be too high
- **Unwell in any way and you are not sure what to do**

Tell a grown-up immediately. You are never being a bother by asking for help. That is what your care team, your family, and your teachers are there for.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Your immune system (your body's security guards) made a mistake** and attacked the insulin-making cells in your pancreas. It is nobody's fault.
2. **Insulin is like a key** that lets sugar from food into your cells to give you energy. Your body needs insulin from the outside now.
3. **You did not get diabetes from eating sugar.** You cannot catch it, and you cannot give it to anyone else.
4. **You can still do everything you love** — sports, parties, sleepovers, adventures. You just have some extra steps in your day.
5. **It is okay to have big feelings** about this. Talk to someone you trust whenever you need to.
