# Gaming Sessions and Glucose — Sedentary Days Need Different Plans

**Topic ID:** 038
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** Teens and pre-teens
**Category:** Lifestyle & Activity
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## Let's Talk About Those Long Sessions

So you've got a day off, your friends are online, and the plan is to game for a solid stretch. Maybe it's a tournament. Maybe it's a new release. Maybe it's just a Saturday with nothing else going on and you want to zone out with a controller in your hands.

Cool. Mira's not here to tell you not to game. Gaming is fun, it's social, and it's a totally legitimate way to spend your downtime. But here's the thing — when you're planted on the couch or at your desk for hours at a time, your body handles blood glucose differently than it does on a more active day. And if you've got T1D, those differences matter.

This isn't about making gaming stressful. It's about understanding what happens to your body during long sedentary periods so you can stay in range, feel good, and keep playing.

## What Happens When You Sit Still for Hours

When you're physically active — walking, running, playing sport — your muscles pull glucose out of your bloodstream for energy. This is why exercise often lowers blood glucose. Your body becomes more sensitive to insulin, and glucose gets used up faster.

When you're sedentary, the opposite happens. Your muscles aren't demanding as much glucose, so blood sugar can drift higher. Insulin sensitivity decreases slightly. If you're snacking while you play (and let's be honest, who doesn't?), those carbohydrates hit your bloodstream without much physical activity to help clear them.

The result? A lazy day of gaming can sometimes produce higher blood glucose readings than you'd see on a more active day — even if your food intake is similar.

This isn't a moral judgement. It's just physiology. And once you understand it, you can work with it.

## The Snack Trap

Here's where things get real. Gaming and snacking go together like headsets and Discord. But the type and timing of snacks during a long session can have a big impact on your glucose levels.

**The problem with grazing.** When you continuously snack on carb-heavy foods — crisps, biscuits, sweets, sugary drinks — your blood glucose can stay elevated for hours because you're constantly adding fuel without much activity to burn it. It's not one snack that causes trouble; it's the steady stream.

**Better snack strategies.** You don't have to give up snacking. Just think about what you're reaching for:

- **Lower-carb options** like cheese, nuts, sliced vegetables with hummus, or meat sticks can satisfy the urge to munch without sending your glucose on a rollercoaster.
- **Portioned snacks** work better than open bags. Put a reasonable amount in a bowl before the session starts. When the bowl is empty, you're done until a planned break.
- **Water over sugary drinks.** Keep a water bottle at your setup. If you want something flavoured, sugar-free options exist that won't affect your BG. Save the juice boxes for treating lows — that's their job.

**If you do eat carbs, count them.** This is the same principle as any meal — if you're going to eat something with carbohydrates, count them and dose accordingly. The temptation during gaming is to eat without thinking and deal with it later. Your future self will thank you for dealing with it now.

## Screen Breaks Are BG Checks

Here's a practical tip that works brilliantly: **tie your blood glucose checks to your screen breaks.**

Every hour or two, when you get up to use the bathroom, grab a drink, or stretch, check your BG (or glance at your CGM). Make it part of the break routine, not an extra chore. Stand up, stretch, check, adjust if needed, sit back down.

This does two important things:

1. **It keeps you aware of your numbers** during a period when time tends to blur. Three hours can feel like thirty minutes when you're deep in a game. Regular check-ins prevent surprises.

2. **It gets you moving, even briefly.** Even a few minutes of standing, stretching, or walking to the kitchen helps your body process glucose more effectively. You don't need to do a workout — just break the long sit.

If you use a CGM with alarms, make sure the volume is up or vibration is on. It's easy to miss an alert when you're wearing a headset and focused on gameplay. Some people find it helpful to set the alarm thresholds slightly tighter on gaming days so they catch trends before they become problems.

## Weekends and Holidays — The Pattern Shifts

Long gaming sessions tend to happen on days off — weekends, school holidays, sick days. These are also days when your entire routine may shift. You might sleep later, eat at different times, and be less active overall.

These routine changes can stack. Later wake-up plus different meal times plus less activity plus more snacking equals a very different glucose profile from a school day. That's not a disaster — it's just something to be aware of.

If you notice that weekends are consistently harder to manage than school days, that's useful information for you and your diabetes team. It might mean your weekend and weekday routines need slightly different approaches. There's nothing wrong with that — it's just smart management.

## The Stress Effect

Here's something you might not expect: gaming can actually raise your blood glucose through stress.

Not the relaxing kind of gaming — the competitive, heart-pounding, adrenaline-pumping kind. When you're in a tense match, your body releases stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones trigger glucose release from the liver, pushing blood sugar up — even without eating anything.

If you notice your numbers spike during intense online matches but stay stable during casual gameplay, stress hormones may be the reason. This is completely normal, and it doesn't mean you should stop playing competitively. It just means those post-match BG checks are especially worth doing.

## Building Your Gaming Day Plan

Here's a simple framework for managing T1D during long gaming sessions:

**Before you sit down:** Check your BG. Make sure your supplies are within reach — meter or CGM receiver, fast-acting carbs for lows, water. Set out any snacks in reasonable portions.

**Every 1–2 hours:** Take a screen break. Stand up, stretch, walk around for a few minutes. Check your BG or review your CGM trend. Adjust if needed.

**When you eat:** Count your carbs. Dose accordingly. Don't skip this step just because you're mid-game — pause if you need to.

**When you're done:** Check your BG one more time. Go do something active, even if it's just a walk around the block or some stretches. Your body has been still for a while — it'll appreciate the movement.

## You're Not Doing It Wrong

If you love gaming, keep gaming. Having T1D doesn't mean you have to give up the things you enjoy. It just means you think about them a little differently — and honestly, the thinking becomes automatic once you build the habits.

You've already mastered carb counting, injection routines, and a hundred other things most people your age never have to think about. Adding a few gaming-day strategies to your toolkit? You've got this.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Long sedentary periods reduce insulin sensitivity**, meaning blood glucose tends to run higher on gaming days compared to active days.
2. **Continuous snacking on high-carb foods is the biggest glucose trap** — portion your snacks, choose lower-carb options, and count carbs when you do eat.
3. **Tie BG checks to screen breaks** every 1–2 hours to stay aware of your numbers and break up long periods of sitting.
4. **Competitive gaming can raise blood glucose through stress hormones** — adrenaline and cortisol trigger liver glucose release even without food intake.
5. **Build a simple gaming-day routine** — check before, break hourly, count when you eat, check when you're done — and it becomes second nature.
