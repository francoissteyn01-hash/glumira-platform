# Managing T1D at Daycare/Creche — What Staff Need to Know

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 14
**Category:** Childcare settings and diabetes management

---

Leaving your child with Type 1 diabetes in someone else's care for the first time is one of the hardest things you'll do. Harder than the diagnosis day, some parents say, because at least on diagnosis day you were in the room.

Whether it's a daycare centre, a creche, a childminder, or a nursery school — the thought of handing over the management of your child's condition to people who didn't know what a bolus was three weeks ago is genuinely frightening.

You're doing great. And the good news is: with the right preparation, training, and communication, childcare settings can and do manage Type 1 diabetes safely every single day.

## Start With a Written Care Plan

A written diabetes care plan (sometimes called an Individual Healthcare Plan or IHP) is non-negotiable. This is a document that details everything the childcare setting needs to know about your child's diabetes management.

Your diabetes team should help you create this. It should include:

- **Your child's name, date of birth, and emergency contacts**
- **Type of diabetes and date of diagnosis**
- **Blood glucose monitoring:** how to check, how often, target ranges
- **CGM information:** what the device looks like, what the alarms mean, what to do when it alarms
- **Signs and symptoms of hypoglycaemia** and the step-by-step treatment protocol
- **Signs and symptoms of hyperglycaemia** and what action to take
- **Meal and snack management:** how carbs are counted, whether insulin is given at the setting or only at home
- **Emergency procedures:** when to give glucagon, when to call an ambulance, when to call you
- **Activity considerations:** how exercise affects blood sugar, what to do before and after active play
- **Contact details for the diabetes team**

This plan should be reviewed and updated at least every term, or whenever your child's management changes.

Keep a copy at the setting, a copy at home, and a copy with your diabetes team. Make sure the setting keeps theirs somewhere accessible — not locked in a filing cabinet that nobody can find in an emergency.

## Training the Staff

A written plan is essential, but paper alone isn't enough. Staff need hands-on training, and they need it before your child starts attending.

**Who should provide the training:**

Your paediatric diabetes specialist nurse (PDSN) or diabetes educator should offer to visit the setting and train staff directly. In many countries, this is part of their role. If your team hasn't offered, ask. If they can't attend in person, video training or telephone support should be available.

**What the training should cover:**

- How to use a blood glucose meter (or interpret CGM readings)
- How to recognise and treat a hypo — including what to give, how much, and how long to wait before rechecking
- How to administer emergency glucagon (if this has been agreed as part of the care plan)
- When to call you, when to call the diabetes team, and when to call an ambulance
- Basic carb awareness for snacks and meals provided at the setting
- What to do on sick days or if the child vomits

**How many staff should be trained:**

At least two, ideally more. Staff turnover in childcare is high, and illness or annual leave shouldn't mean there's nobody who knows what they're doing. NICE guidelines recommend that all relevant staff receive training, not just a designated individual.

## Your Legal Rights

In many countries, children with Type 1 diabetes are protected by disability discrimination or equality legislation. The specifics vary by jurisdiction, but the broad principles are:

- **A childcare setting cannot refuse your child** solely because they have diabetes.
- **Reasonable adjustments** must be made to accommodate your child's medical needs.
- **Staff can be expected to carry out medical procedures** (such as blood glucose checks or administering glucagon) as part of their duty of care, provided they receive appropriate training.

In the UK, the Equality Act 2010 and the Children and Families Act 2014 provide clear protections. In the US, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act apply. In South Africa, the Employment Equity Act and the Constitution provide protections against unfair discrimination.

If a setting is reluctant to accept your child or to provide appropriate care, your diabetes team, patient advocacy organisations, and legal advice services can help you understand and assert your rights.

## The Handover Routine

Establishing a consistent daily handover routine makes everything smoother:

**Drop-off:**
- Share current blood glucose level (or CGM trend)
- Mention anything relevant from overnight (lows, highs, illness, poor sleep)
- Confirm meal and snack plans for the day
- Check that supplies are stocked (test strips, hypo treatment, glucagon, spare batteries)

**Pick-up:**
- Review the day's blood glucose readings
- Ask about food intake, activity levels, and any incidents
- Check remaining supplies

A simple communication book or app-based log that travels between home and the setting can capture all of this without requiring a 20-minute conversation at the door every day.

## Snacks, Meals, and Parties

Food in childcare settings is a frequent source of anxiety. Strategies that help:

- **Provide a list of your child's usual snacks** with carb counts, so staff aren't guessing.
- **Label everything.** If your child brings food from home, label it with the carb content.
- **Plan for special occasions.** Birthday parties, holiday celebrations, and cooking activities all involve unexpected food. Ask to be informed in advance so you can plan.
- **Don't ban treats entirely.** Your child has diabetes, not a food allergy. With appropriate planning (and communication with you or the diabetes team), they can participate in food-based activities like everyone else.

## Emergency Preparedness

The setting should have:

- **A hypo treatment box** in a known, accessible location. Contents: fast-acting glucose (juice boxes, glucose tablets), longer-acting carbohydrates (biscuits, crackers), and a copy of the treatment protocol.
- **Glucagon** stored according to the manufacturer's instructions, with clear written instructions for use. Staff who might need to administer it should have practised with a trainer device.
- **Emergency contact numbers** displayed prominently — yours, your partner's, the diabetes team's, and the ambulance service.
- **A plan for outings.** If the children go on a trip, the hypo box and emergency supplies go too. Always. Non-negotiable.

## Managing Your Own Anxiety

Here's the part nobody talks about enough. Even when the care plan is perfect and the staff are trained and the supplies are stocked, you will still worry. That's normal.

Some things that might help:

- **CGM sharing** (if your child wears one) can let you see their glucose levels in real time from your phone. This is reassuring for some parents and anxiety-inducing for others. Decide what works for you.
- **Agree on communication thresholds.** Ask the setting to contact you if glucose goes above or below certain levels, but not for every routine check. This reduces call volume while keeping you informed of anything concerning.
- **Trust builds over time.** The first week will be the hardest. Give the staff (and yourself) grace.
- **Connect with other parents.** Other T1D families who've navigated childcare can offer practical advice and emotional support that no guideline can match.

Your child deserves to attend childcare, to play with other children, to be messy and noisy and normal. And you deserve the time — whether it's for work, rest, or just sitting in a quiet room drinking tea while it's still hot.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **A written diabetes care plan** is essential and should be created with your diabetes team, kept accessible at the setting, and updated regularly.
2. **Staff training must be hands-on**, delivered by a diabetes professional, and cover daily management as well as emergency procedures.
3. **Multiple staff members should be trained** to ensure coverage during absences, and training should be refreshed at least annually.
4. **You have legal rights.** Childcare settings cannot refuse your child because of diabetes and must make reasonable adjustments.
5. **A consistent handover routine and emergency supplies** in a known, accessible location are the practical foundations of safe care.
