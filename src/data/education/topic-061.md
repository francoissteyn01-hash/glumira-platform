## Why Carb Counting Matters

If there is one skill that genuinely transforms how you manage Type 1 Diabetes, it is carb counting. Not because it needs to be perfect — it does not — but because understanding how carbohydrates affect your blood glucose gives you a powerful sense of control and confidence at every meal.

Mira has seen thousands of people go from feeling overwhelmed at mealtimes to feeling capable and calm. The secret is not perfection. It is practice, patience, and a willingness to learn from every plate of food you eat.

## What Are Carbohydrates?

Carbohydrates are one of three macronutrients in food, alongside protein and fat. They are your body's preferred source of quick energy, and they are the nutrient that has the most direct and immediate impact on blood glucose levels.

Carbohydrates are found in:

- **Grains and cereals** — bread, rice, pasta, oats, couscous, quinoa
- **Starchy vegetables** — potatoes, sweet potatoes, corn, peas, butternut
- **Fruits** — all fruits contain carbohydrates, though amounts vary
- **Dairy** — milk and yoghurt contain lactose, a natural sugar
- **Legumes** — beans, lentils, chickpeas (also high in fibre and protein)
- **Sugars and sweets** — table sugar, honey, sweets, chocolates, cool drinks
- **Processed foods** — biscuits, crisps, cereals, sauces, and many packaged items

Foods that contain very little or no carbohydrate include most meats, fish, eggs, cheese, oils, butter, and non-starchy vegetables like spinach, broccoli, and lettuce.

## How to Count Carbs — The Practical Approach

Carb counting means estimating how many grams of carbohydrate are in a meal or snack. This number is then used — alongside your insulin-to-carb ratio — to determine how your body will respond and how your diabetes management plan accounts for that meal.

There are three main ways to count carbs:

### 1. Reading Food Labels

Every packaged food has a nutrition information panel. Look for **"Total Carbohydrates"** per serving or per 100g. Be careful to check the serving size — a packet of crisps may list carbs "per serving" but contain two or three servings inside.

**Example:** A label says 25g carbohydrate per 30g serving. If you eat 60g of that food, you are eating 50g of carbohydrate.

A quick formula: (amount you eat / serving size) x carbs per serving = total carbs.

### 2. Weighing Food

A kitchen scale is one of the most useful tools for carb counting, especially when you are learning. Weigh the food, then use a reference (app, book, or database) to find the carbs per 100g.

**Example:** You weigh 150g of cooked basmati rice. Cooked basmati rice has approximately 28g of carbohydrate per 100g. So: 150 x 0.28 = 42g of carbohydrate.

Common carb values worth memorising:

| Food (cooked) | Carbs per 100g |
|---|---|
| White rice | 28–32g |
| Pasta | 25–30g |
| Potato (boiled) | 17–20g |
| Sweet potato | 17–20g |
| White bread | 45–50g |
| Banana (medium, ~120g) | ~27g total |
| Apple (medium, ~150g) | ~19g total |
| Milk (full cream, 250ml) | ~12g |

### 3. Estimating by Sight

When you cannot weigh food — at a braai, at a friend's house, at a restaurant — you estimate. This is where practice matters. Over time, you develop a visual sense of portions.

Useful visual guides:

- **A fist** = roughly 1 cup = about 200ml of rice or pasta
- **A cupped hand** = roughly half a cup of cooked starch
- **A thumb tip** = roughly 1 teaspoon
- **A deck of cards** = roughly 100g of meat (low carb, but useful for plate building)

Estimation will never be exact, and that is completely fine. Being within 10–15g of the actual value is realistic and effective for most meals.

## Real Meal Examples

Let us walk through a few everyday meals:

**Breakfast — Two slices of toast with peanut butter and a banana:**
- 2 slices white bread: ~30g carb
- Peanut butter (1 tablespoon): ~2g carb
- 1 medium banana: ~27g carb
- **Total: approximately 59g carbohydrate**

**Lunch — Chicken wrap with salad:**
- 1 large flour tortilla: ~35g carb
- Grilled chicken: ~0g carb
- Salad vegetables: ~3g carb
- Sweet chilli sauce (1 tablespoon): ~8g carb
- **Total: approximately 46g carbohydrate**

**Dinner — Spaghetti bolognese with garlic bread:**
- 200g cooked spaghetti: ~56g carb
- Bolognese sauce (homemade, 150ml): ~10g carb
- 1 slice garlic bread: ~18g carb
- **Total: approximately 84g carbohydrate**

Notice how sauces and extras add up. These are the hidden carbs that catch people off guard.

## Apps and Tools That Help

You do not need to memorise every food's carb content. Several excellent tools exist:

- **MyFitnessPal** — large food database, barcode scanner, free version available
- **Calorie King** — popular in many countries, reliable values
- **Carbs & Cals** — visual food guide with photos showing different portion sizes
- **FatSecret** — free app with a comprehensive database
- **Your national food composition database** — many countries publish official values online

When following specific dietary approaches — such as those observed during Ramadan, or within Kosher or Halal dietary frameworks — these same tools apply. The foods may differ, but the principle of identifying and counting carbohydrate content remains identical. If you follow a lower-carb approach such as the Bernstein method, carb counting becomes even more central, as precision at smaller carb quantities has a proportionally larger impact on blood glucose.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

1. **Forgetting liquid carbs** — juice, regular cool drinks, flavoured milk, and sweetened coffee all contain significant carbohydrate.
2. **Ignoring sauces and condiments** — tomato sauce, BBQ sauce, sweet chilli, and salad dressings can add 5–15g of carb per serving.
3. **Confusing cooked and raw weights** — rice and pasta roughly double or triple in weight when cooked. Always check whether your reference lists cooked or raw values.
4. **Skipping the learning phase** — weighing food at home for a few weeks builds visual estimation skills that last a lifetime.
5. **Aiming for perfection** — carb counting is an estimate. Good enough is genuinely good enough.

## Building Confidence Over Time

Carb counting is a skill, not a talent. Nobody is born knowing that a medium potato has about 30g of carbohydrate. You learn by doing — by weighing, checking, recording, and noticing patterns. After a few months of practice, most people find they can estimate meals within a reasonable range without needing a scale or an app for every bite.

And when you get it wrong — because you will, and everyone does — that is not failure. That is data. Every unexpected glucose reading after a meal is an opportunity to refine your understanding.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Carbohydrates are the macronutrient with the most direct impact on blood glucose** — learning to identify and count them is the foundation of confident meal management.
2. **Three practical methods exist: reading labels, weighing food, and visual estimation** — use all three depending on the situation.
3. **Hidden carbs in sauces, drinks, and condiments are the most common source of counting errors** — always account for these extras.
4. **Apps and food databases are powerful allies** — use them freely, especially in the early learning phase.
5. **Perfection is not the goal — consistency and practice are** — being within 10–15g of the actual value is realistic and effective.
