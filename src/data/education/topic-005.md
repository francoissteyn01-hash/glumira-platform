# Your First Injection — What to Expect (Teen Guide)

## Hey. Mira here.

So you are about to give yourself your first injection. Or maybe you already did, and you are here looking for reassurance that what you felt was normal. Either way — I have got you.

Let's be real: sticking a needle into your own body is a weird thing to do. Every instinct you have says "don't do that." And yet, here you are, about to do one of the bravest things a person can do — something that most adults would find incredibly difficult. I want you to know that being nervous does not make you weak. It makes you human. And doing it anyway? That makes you strong.

---

## It is OK to be scared

First things first: **there is nothing wrong with being afraid of needles.** Needle phobia and injection anxiety are among the most common fears in the world — affecting an estimated 20-30% of young adults. You are not being dramatic. You are not being a baby. You are experiencing a completely normal human response.

Many teens with Type 1 Diabetes say the anticipation is worse than the actual injection. The build-up — thinking about it, imagining it, dreading it — creates far more anxiety than the moment itself. The needle is tiny (usually 4-6mm, shorter than the width of your little finger), and the injection takes only a few seconds.

That does not mean it is nothing. It means you can do it.

---

## What it actually feels like

Everyone's experience is slightly different, but here is what most teens report:

- **The pinch:** You might feel a brief pinch or sting when the needle goes in. Some people say they barely feel anything at all. Others feel a small sharp sensation that fades within a second or two.
- **The push:** As the insulin goes in, you might feel a slight pressure or coolness under the skin. This is normal.
- **After:** Once you remove the needle, you might see a tiny dot of blood or nothing at all. Some sites sting slightly for a minute afterwards. Others do not.

It is not the dramatic, painful experience your brain is building it up to be. Your brain is trying to protect you from something sharp — that is its job. But you are smarter than your brain's panic response, and you have a very good reason to do this.

## Practical tips for your first time

**Before:**
- Make sure you have everything you need within reach — your insulin pen or syringe, alcohol swab if advised by your care team, sharps bin.
- Choose a spot where you feel comfortable and private if that helps. Your bedroom, the bathroom — wherever you feel least pressured.
- Take a few slow breaths. Not because breathing is magic, but because it genuinely helps reduce the physical symptoms of anxiety (shaking hands, racing heart).

**During:**
- Your care team will have taught you the specific technique for your injection device. Follow what they showed you.
- Common injection sites include your thighs, abdomen, and upper arms. Your care team will advise what works best for you and your insulin type.
- Some teens find it helps to count "3, 2, 1, go" and commit to the motion. Hesitating with the needle hovering over your skin actually makes it harder.
- Others find that pinching the skin firmly gives them something to focus on besides the needle.
- Do not rush it, but do not overthink it either. Quick and confident is easier than slow and hesitant.

**After:**
- Wait the recommended number of seconds (as instructed by your care team) before removing the needle to ensure the full amount of insulin is delivered.
- Dispose of the needle in your sharps bin. Never recap and never throw loose needles in the regular bin.
- Take a moment to acknowledge what you just did. Seriously — you just self-injected. That is not nothing.

This is for learning purposes. Always discuss your specific insulin routine with your care team.

## The mental game

Self-injecting for the first time is as much a mental challenge as a physical one. Here are some honest thoughts about the psychological side:

**It is normal if your hands shake.** Adrenaline does that. It does not mean you will mess it up.

**It is normal if you cannot do it on the first try.** Some teens hover the needle, psyche themselves out, and have to reset. That is fine. Try again. There is no timer, and nobody is judging you.

**It is normal to cry.** Not because it hurts, but because the whole situation is overwhelming — the diagnosis, the changes, and now this. Tears do not mean failure.

**It is normal to feel proud afterwards.** You should. You just did something genuinely hard.

**It is NOT normal if the fear is so severe that you are consistently unable to inject, or if you are skipping injections because of anxiety.** That is a sign you need support, and there is absolutely no shame in that. Discuss it with your care team — they have strategies, tools, and sometimes referrals to help with injection anxiety specifically.

## Will it always be this hard?

No. Here is the honest trajectory most teens describe:

- **Injection 1-5:** Nerve-wracking. Shaky. Takes a while to psych yourself up.
- **Injection 5-20:** Still a bit anxious, but getting faster. You are developing muscle memory.
- **Injection 20-50:** Becoming routine. You might still not love it, but you are no longer dreading it.
- **Injection 50+:** It is just... a thing you do. Like brushing your teeth. You barely think about it.

This is not a promise — everyone's timeline is different. But the overwhelming majority of teens with Type 1 Diabetes will tell you that what felt impossible on day one becomes unremarkable within weeks.

## A note on doing this at school or in public

At some point, you will need to inject somewhere other than home. This can feel awkward or exposing. Some tips:

- **You have the right to manage your diabetes at school.** This is protected. You should not be asked to hide in a bathroom to inject.
- **Most people will not notice or care.** Seriously. You might feel like everyone is staring, but most people are focused on their own lives.
- **If someone asks, you decide how much to share.** A simple "I have Type 1 Diabetes, this is my insulin" is enough. You do not owe anyone a detailed explanation.
- **If someone is rude about it, that is a reflection of their ignorance, not your condition.**

## When to call your care team

Reach out to your care team if:
- You are unsure about any aspect of your injection technique
- You are experiencing bruising, lumps, or pain at injection sites that is not improving
- Injection anxiety is so severe that you are avoiding or delaying injections
- You want to discuss alternative delivery methods (such as insulin pumps)
- You have questions about anything — literally anything

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Being scared of your first injection is completely normal.** Needle anxiety affects millions of people, and it does not make you weak.
2. **The anticipation is almost always worse than the reality.** The needle is tiny, the injection is brief, and most teens say it barely hurts.
3. **It gets dramatically easier with practice.** What feels impossible now will feel routine within weeks.
4. **Do not skip injections because of anxiety.** If fear is that severe, discuss it with your care team — they have real strategies to help.
5. **You just did something genuinely brave.** Acknowledge it. You are managing a serious condition, and that takes strength.
