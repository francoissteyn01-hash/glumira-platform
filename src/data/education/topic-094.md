# Insulin Pump vs MDI — The Honest Comparison

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, and care teams
**Topic ID:** 094
**Related Topics:** [topic-052](/education/topic-052), [topic-055](/education/topic-055), [topic-056](/education/topic-056)

---

## Hello from Mira

If you are living with Type 1 Diabetes — or caring for someone who is — there is a good chance you have faced, or will face, a decision about insulin delivery: should you use an **insulin pump** or **multiple daily injections (MDI)**? Perhaps a healthcare professional has suggested switching. Perhaps you have seen other people with T1D using a different method and wondered whether it might suit you better.

This is one of the most personal decisions in T1D management, and I want to be honest with you about it. There is no universally "better" option. There are trade-offs, preferences, and individual circumstances. Let me lay them out clearly so you can have an informed conversation with your care team.

---

## What is MDI?

Multiple daily injections is exactly what the name describes. You take several insulin injections each day, typically using two types of insulin:

- **Basal insulin** — a long-acting insulin given once or twice daily that provides a background level of insulin to cover the glucose your liver releases between meals and overnight.
- **Bolus insulin** — a rapid-acting insulin given before meals (and sometimes as corrections) to cover the glucose from the food you eat.

Most people on MDI use an insulin pen for injections, though some use syringes. The typical routine involves one or two basal injections and a bolus injection before each meal — commonly totalling four to six injections per day.

---

## What is an insulin pump?

An insulin pump is a small, battery-powered device that delivers rapid-acting insulin continuously through a thin tube (cannula) inserted under the skin. The pump replaces the separate basal and bolus injections:

- **Basal delivery** — the pump delivers tiny, pre-programmed pulses of rapid-acting insulin throughout the day and night, mimicking the steady background insulin that a long-acting injection provides. Basal rates can be varied by time of day.
- **Bolus delivery** — when you eat, you instruct the pump to deliver a bolus of insulin. Many pumps include bolus calculators that help compute the amount based on carbohydrate intake and current glucose.

Some pumps use traditional tubing that connects the pump (clipped to clothing or worn in a pocket) to the infusion site. Others are **tubeless patch pumps** that adhere directly to the skin with the pump mechanism built into the pod.

When a pump is paired with a CGM and uses an algorithm to automatically adjust insulin delivery, it becomes part of an **automated insulin delivery (AID)** or hybrid closed-loop system — but that is a topic for another day.

## The honest pros and cons

### Advantages of an insulin pump

- **Flexible basal rates.** You can program different basal rates for different times of day — for example, increasing delivery in the early morning to counter the dawn phenomenon. With MDI, basal insulin provides a relatively fixed profile.
- **Precise bolus delivery.** Pumps can deliver insulin in very small increments (as low as 0.025 units in some models), which is particularly valuable for young children or anyone with high insulin sensitivity.
- **Fewer needle insertions.** The infusion set is typically changed every two to three days, meaning approximately two to three insertions per week instead of four to six per day.
- **Temporary basal adjustments.** You can temporarily increase or decrease basal delivery — for example, reducing it during exercise or increasing it during illness — without needing an additional injection.
- **Bolus calculators.** Built-in calculators can reduce the mental burden of maths at mealtimes.
- **Integration with CGM.** Many modern pumps can receive CGM data and, in AID systems, automatically adjust delivery.

### Disadvantages of an insulin pump

- **You are attached to a device.** The pump is worn on or near your body at all times. Some people find this intrusive, uncomfortable, or a visible marker of their condition.
- **Infusion site issues.** Cannula sites can become irritated, infected, or kinked. A kinked or dislodged cannula can interrupt insulin delivery without immediate warning, potentially leading to rapid hyperglycaemia and ketone development — because there is no long-acting insulin depot in the body.
- **Technical failures.** Pumps are electronic devices. Batteries run out, software can glitch, tubing can occlude. You must always carry backup injection supplies.
- **Learning curve.** Starting a pump requires significant education and training. The initial weeks can feel more complex, not less.
- **Cost and access.** Insulin pumps and their consumables are expensive. Access varies enormously by healthcare system, insurance, and geography. Not everyone who wants a pump can get one.
- **Body image and visibility.** Especially for adolescents, wearing a visible device can affect body image and self-consciousness. This is a real and valid concern.

### Advantages of MDI

- **Simplicity.** Pens are straightforward to use, require minimal training, and have no software to manage.
- **No device attached to your body.** Between injections, there is nothing to wear, hide, or worry about disconnecting.
- **Lower cost and wider availability.** Pens and needles are available almost everywhere and are generally less expensive than pump supplies.
- **Reliability.** Pens do not have batteries, software, or tubing. They rarely malfunction.
- **Long-acting depot.** Because basal insulin is given as a depot injection, there is a buffer if a bolus injection is missed — unlike a pump, where interruption of delivery removes all insulin supply.

### Disadvantages of MDI

- **Less flexible basal delivery.** Long-acting insulin provides a relatively fixed basal profile. You cannot easily adjust it hour by hour.
- **More injections.** Four to six needle insertions per day is the norm, which can be burdensome — particularly for young children.
- **Less precise dosing.** Most pens deliver in half-unit or whole-unit increments, which may be too coarse for those with high insulin sensitivity.
- **No automated integration with CGM.** MDI does not connect to CGM-driven algorithms (though smart pens with connected technology are emerging).

## What the evidence says

Large studies and systematic reviews — including those referenced by ISPAD and the ADA — have generally found that insulin pump therapy is associated with modest improvements in HbA1c and reductions in severe hypoglycaemia compared to MDI, particularly in paediatric populations. However, the magnitude of the difference varies across studies, and many well-managed people on MDI achieve excellent outcomes.

The NICE guidelines note that insulin pump therapy should be considered when MDI is not achieving targets or when hypoglycaemia is a significant problem, while also acknowledging that individual preference is an important factor.

Critically, **the quality of diabetes management depends more on education, engagement, and support than on the delivery device.** A motivated, well-educated person on MDI can achieve outcomes comparable to — or better than — someone on a pump who has not received adequate training.

## Who might benefit most from a pump?

While the decision is always individual, certain situations may tip the balance toward considering pump therapy:

- Very young children who require very small, precise insulin amounts
- People with significant dawn phenomenon that is difficult to manage with long-acting insulin
- Those experiencing frequent or unpredictable hypoglycaemia
- People with highly variable schedules (shift workers, for example)
- Those who wish to use or are already using an automated insulin delivery system

Equally, there are many people who thrive on MDI and have no desire to change. That is entirely valid.

---

## It is personal — and it can change

The most important thing I can tell you is this: the "right" choice is the one that works for **your** life, **your** body, and **your** preferences — discussed and agreed with your care team. And it is not a permanent decision. Many people switch between pump and MDI at different life stages. Some use a pump during the school year and switch to MDI for summer holidays. Some try a pump and return to pens. There is no failure in changing your mind.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Insulin pumps deliver continuous rapid-acting insulin through an infusion set**, offering flexible basal rates, precise dosing, and fewer needle insertions — but require wearing a device and come with risks of site issues and technical failures.
2. **MDI uses long-acting basal insulin and rapid-acting bolus injections**, offering simplicity, reliability, and no attached device — but with less dosing flexibility and more daily injections.
3. **Evidence suggests pumps offer modest improvements in HbA1c and hypoglycaemia reduction**, but the quality of education, engagement, and support matters more than the delivery method.
4. **The decision is deeply personal** and should be made in partnership with your care team, considering your lifestyle, preferences, clinical needs, and access.
5. **Switching between methods is always an option.** Choosing one does not lock you in. Your needs may change over time, and your delivery method can change with them.
