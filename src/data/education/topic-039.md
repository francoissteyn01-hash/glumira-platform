# Pocket Carbs — Teaching Self-Rescue

**Topic ID:** 039
**Age Range:** 10–13 years
**Audience:** Children
**Category:** Safety & Self-Care
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## Your Secret Superpower

Hey. Mira here. Let's talk about something that every person with T1D needs to master — and once you do, it's going to make you feel more confident, more independent, and more in control of your own life.

We're talking about pocket carbs. The fast-acting carbohydrates you carry with you everywhere — in your pocket, your bag, your locker, your coat — so that if your blood glucose drops low, you can treat it yourself, right then and there, without needing anyone else to help.

This is self-rescue. And it's one of the most important skills you'll ever learn.

## Why Pocket Carbs Matter So Much

Here's the deal: hypoglycaemia (a low blood sugar) can happen anywhere. It can happen at school, at a friend's house, on the bus, at the shops, on the sports field, or just walking down the street. It doesn't send a calendar invitation. It doesn't wait for a convenient time.

When your blood glucose drops too low, you need to act fast. The symptoms — shakiness, sweating, feeling dizzy, confused, or suddenly starving — are your body's alarm system saying "I need glucose now." Not in ten minutes when you find a shop. Not after you've walked home. Now.

That's why carrying fast-acting carbs on you at all times is non-negotiable. It's not optional. It's not something to do "most of the time." It's every single time you leave the house. Every time.

Think of it this way: you wouldn't go swimming without knowing where the shallow end is. Pocket carbs are your shallow end — the thing that's always there to catch you.

## What to Carry

The best pocket carbs are fast-acting, portable, durable, and easy to consume even when you're feeling shaky or confused. Here are your best options:

**Glucose tablets.** These are the gold standard. They're designed specifically for treating hypos. They come in tubes or packets that fit easily in a pocket or bag. They don't melt, they don't squash, and they have a precise amount of carbohydrate per tablet so you always know what you're getting. Most glucose tablets contain 3–4 grams of carbohydrate each.

**Small juice boxes.** A 150–200ml juice box typically contains 15–20 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate. They're quick to drink, easy to carry, and most kids find them palatable even when feeling unwell. The downside: they can leak if crushed, so pack them carefully.

**Glucose gel.** These come in small sachets or tubes and can be squeezed directly into your mouth. They're particularly useful if you're feeling too shaky to chew a tablet. They're also compact and easy to stash in small pockets.

**Jelly sweets (like jelly babies or fruit gums).** A handful of jelly babies (roughly 5–6) provides about 15 grams of carbohydrate. They're tasty, cheap, and available everywhere. Just make sure they're regular sweets, not sugar-free — sugar-free versions won't help during a hypo.

**What NOT to rely on:** Chocolate, biscuits, and other foods with fat in them. Fat slows down the absorption of sugar, which means they take longer to raise your blood glucose. When you're low, you need speed. Save the chocolate for when you're in range and just want a treat.

## How Much to Carry

Your diabetes team will have given you specific guidance on how much carbohydrate to use for a hypo treatment — typically around 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate as a starting point, though this may vary based on your age, weight, and insulin regimen.

As a general rule, carry enough to treat at least **two hypos**. That means if your treatment amount is 15 grams, carry at least 30 grams of fast-acting carbs. Why two? Because sometimes a hypo takes more than one treatment to resolve. And sometimes you might be out longer than expected.

**Restock immediately.** Every time you use your pocket carbs, replace them as soon as possible. Don't tell yourself "I'll do it later." Make restocking a reflex — treat, recover, restock. This habit ensures you're never caught without supplies.

## Where to Stash Them

The key principle: your pocket carbs should be accessible within seconds, not minutes. Here's how to make that happen:

**On your body.** A jacket pocket, trouser pocket, or a small belt pouch. If you're wearing it, your carbs should be with you.

**In your school bag.** Always. In a specific pocket or pouch that you don't move things around in. You should be able to reach in and grab them without looking.

**In your locker.** A backup stash at school is smart. Label them if you want, or just keep a small bag of glucose tablets tucked in a corner.

**In your sports bag or kit.** If you play sports, a separate stash in your sports bag means you're covered even if your school bag is in a different room.

**At a friend's house.** If you spend a lot of time at a particular friend's home, consider leaving a small supply there. Talk to their parent about it — most families are happy to keep a tube of glucose tablets in a kitchen drawer.

**By your bed.** Nighttime lows happen. Keep glucose tablets or a juice box on your bedside table so you can treat without having to walk to the kitchen when you're half asleep.

## When to Use Them

The simple rule:

**If your blood glucose is below the threshold your diabetes team has set** (this is often around 4 mmol/L or 70 mg/dL, but your team will tell you your specific number), **or if you feel hypo symptoms**, treat immediately.

Don't wait to see if it gets worse. Don't finish what you're doing first. Don't tell yourself you'll check in a minute. Treat now.

Here's the process your team has likely taught you:

1. **Eat or drink your fast-acting carbs** — the amount your diabetes team recommends.
2. **Wait 15 minutes.** This is hard. You might still feel awful. But it takes about 15 minutes for the carbohydrate to reach your bloodstream and start raising your glucose.
3. **Recheck your blood glucose.** If it's still below target, treat again with the same amount.
4. **Once you're back in range**, have a small snack that includes some slower-acting carbohydrate and protein (like a piece of fruit and some cheese, or a handful of crackers with peanut butter) to keep your levels stable.

If you ever feel too unwell to treat yourself — too confused, too shaky, unable to swallow safely — that's when you need someone else's help. Tell the nearest person you're having a low. This is why wearing medical identification and making sure the people around you know about your T1D is so important.

## Making It Normal

Carrying pocket carbs might feel awkward at first. You might wonder what people think when they see you popping glucose tablets in the middle of a lesson or pulling a juice box out of your bag on the bus.

Here's the truth: most people don't notice. And those who do? They're usually impressed, not judgemental. You're taking care of yourself. That's not something to be embarrassed about — it's something to be proud of.

Over time, carrying your pocket carbs will become as automatic as carrying your phone or your keys. You won't think about it. You'll just do it. And on the day when a low hits unexpectedly and you calmly reach into your pocket, treat it, and carry on with your day — that's the moment you'll know you've got this.

Self-rescue. It's your superpower. Use it.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Always carry fast-acting carbohydrates on you** — glucose tablets, juice boxes, glucose gel, or jelly sweets — every time you leave the house.
2. **Carry enough for at least two hypo treatments** and restock immediately after every use.
3. **Stash supplies in multiple locations** — your pockets, school bag, locker, sports kit, friend's house, and bedside table.
4. **Treat immediately when you're below target or feel hypo symptoms** — eat fast carbs, wait 15 minutes, recheck, and follow up with a sustaining snack.
5. **Self-rescue is a skill that builds confidence and independence** — the more you practise it, the more natural and empowering it becomes.
