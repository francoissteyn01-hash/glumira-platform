# Swimming and Water Activities — Glucose Drops Faster Than You Think

**Topic ID:** 027
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** All (Parent, Caregiver, and Child)
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. Swimming is one of the best activities for children — it builds strength, coordination, and confidence, and it is just plain fun. But for families managing Type 1 diabetes, water activities come with a few extra considerations that are worth understanding. The headline: swimming drops blood glucose faster and more dramatically than many land-based activities, and the water environment creates unique challenges for monitoring and treatment. With the right preparation, though, your child can swim as often and as enthusiastically as any of their friends.

## Why Swimming Drops Glucose So Quickly

Swimming is a full-body, sustained aerobic exercise. Unlike running or cycling, which primarily use the legs, swimming engages arms, legs, core, and back simultaneously. This means more muscle mass is actively pulling glucose from the blood at the same time.

On top of that, water temperature plays a role. Pool water is typically cooler than body temperature, which means the body works harder to maintain its core temperature — burning additional energy in the process. Cold water also increases blood flow to muscles and skin, which can speed up insulin absorption from injection or pump sites.

ISPAD guidelines note that aquatic exercise tends to produce a more rapid decline in blood glucose compared with equivalent-intensity land-based exercise. Studies in children with T1D have shown glucose drops of 3–5 mmol/L (54–90 mg/dL) during a 30–45 minute swim session, depending on starting glucose, insulin on board, and intensity.

The practical implication: if your child's glucose is borderline before a swim, it can drop into hypo range surprisingly fast once they are in the water.

## Before the Swim — Pre-Swim Preparation

### Check Blood Glucose

Check glucose 15–30 minutes before getting in the water. The target pre-swim range is generally slightly higher than the standard pre-exercise range — many diabetes teams recommend starting a swim session with blood glucose between 7–12 mmol/L (126–216 mg/dL).

- **Glucose 7–12 mmol/L (126–216 mg/dL) with a stable or rising trend:** Good to swim.
- **Glucose 5–7 mmol/L (90–126 mg/dL):** Give 15–30 g of carbohydrate before swimming, depending on the expected duration and intensity.
- **Glucose below 5 mmol/L (90 mg/dL):** Treat the low, wait for recovery and a stable trend, then reassess.
- **Glucose above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL):** Check for ketones. If ketones are present, do not swim until they have cleared. If no ketones and your child feels well, swimming may be appropriate — check with your diabetes team.

### Pre-Swim Carbs

Because glucose drops quickly during swimming, many families offer a pre-swim snack even when blood glucose is in range. Good options include:

- A banana (~20–25 g carbohydrate)
- A muesli bar or granola bar (~15–20 g)
- A small sandwich (~25–30 g)
- Crackers with peanut butter (~15–20 g)

The combination of carbohydrate with some protein or fat helps sustain glucose levels during a longer swim session.

### Insulin Pump Considerations

If your child uses an insulin pump, discuss with your diabetes team whether to disconnect the pump during swimming. Some waterproof pumps can stay on, while others must be disconnected. Key points:

- If disconnecting, do so 30–60 minutes before the swim if possible, to reduce active insulin during the session.
- Reconnect promptly after swimming.
- Never leave the pump in direct sunlight on the poolside — heat degrades insulin.

## During the Swim

### Monitoring in the Water

This is one of the trickiest parts. You cannot easily check blood glucose while your child is in the pool.

- **CGM sensors:** Most modern CGM sensors (Dexcom G6/G7, Libre 2/3) are rated for water exposure and will continue to read while submerged, though the signal may not transmit to a phone or receiver while the sensor is underwater. When your child surfaces or gets out briefly, the reading should update.
- **Set a timer.** For swim sessions longer than 30 minutes, consider asking your child to get out briefly at the 20–30 minute mark for a glucose check and a small snack if needed.
- **Watch for hypo signs in the water.** A child who suddenly seems tired, stops swimming, clings to the pool edge, or looks pale may be going low. Get them out of the water and check immediately.

### Safety Rule

**A child experiencing a hypo should get out of the water immediately.** Low blood glucose in the water is a drowning risk. This is non-negotiable. If there is any doubt, get them out, check, and treat first.

Discuss this rule with your child in age-appropriate language: "If you feel wobbly or strange in the pool, hold onto the side and tell the adult straight away. We get out first, then have a juice box."

## After the Swim — Post-Swim Monitoring

Just like land-based exercise, swimming causes delayed glucose drops as muscles replenish glycogen stores. This effect can last 4–8 hours after the swim.

- **Check glucose immediately after the swim.** Offer a snack with carbohydrate and protein (e.g., a cheese sandwich, yoghurt and fruit, or crackers with hummus).
- **Check again 1–2 hours later.** Watch the CGM trend through the afternoon or evening.
- **If swimming was in the afternoon or evening, check at bedtime** and consider setting a CGM alarm at a slightly higher low threshold for that night.
- **Offer a bedtime snack with sustained carbohydrate** (e.g., milk and a biscuit, or toast with peanut butter) if your diabetes team recommends this after active days.

## The Pool Bag Checklist

Keep a dedicated "pool bag" kit that goes wherever your child swims — swimming lessons, pool parties, beach days, water parks.

### Essentials

- Blood glucose meter and test strips (in a waterproof zip-lock bag)
- Fast-acting glucose: at least 4 juice boxes or glucose gel sachets (juice boxes are ideal poolside — easy to open, easy to drink)
- Follow-up snack: muesli bar, crackers, a sandwich
- A towel for your child to sit on while checking glucose and eating (wet, cold children go low faster)
- Water bottle (swimming is dehydrating even though you are in water)
- Glucagon emergency kit
- Spare CGM sensor and applicator
- Spare pump supplies if applicable
- Medical ID bracelet (waterproof silicone bands work well)
- Your phone with the diabetes team's emergency number saved

### Nice to Have

- Insulated pouch for insulin (if the pump is disconnected, keep the insulin cool)
- Sunscreen (sunburn increases insulin sensitivity and can complicate glucose management)
- A waterproof pouch for the CGM reader or phone
- Spare batteries for the meter

## Waterproof CGM — What to Know

Modern CGM sensors are designed to withstand water exposure, but there are practical points to keep in mind:

- **Adhesion:** Water, chlorine, and sunscreen can loosen sensor adhesive. Use an overlay patch or adhesive tape designed for CGM sensors to keep the sensor secure during swimming. Apply it before the swim, not after it starts to peel.
- **Chlorine and salt water:** Rinse the sensor site with fresh water after swimming in a chlorinated pool or the sea. Chlorine can irritate the skin under the adhesive over time.
- **Signal transmission:** Bluetooth signals do not travel well through water. The CGM may show a "signal lost" message while your child is submerged. This is normal — the reading will resume when they surface.
- **Depth and duration limits:** Check your specific sensor's product information. Most are rated for 1–2.5 metres of depth for up to 30–60 minutes, which covers pool swimming. Deep diving or extended open-water swimming may exceed these limits.

## Open Water, Beach Days, and Water Parks

Open water (sea, lakes, rivers) and water parks add variables: waves, currents, distance from supplies, and difficulty getting out quickly. Extra precautions:

- Keep the pool bag on the shore within sight and easy reach.
- Designate one adult as the "diabetes watcher" — their job is to track time, watch for hypo signs, and have glucose treatment in hand.
- At water parks, check glucose between rides and offer a snack every 30–45 minutes if your child is actively swimming or walking between slides.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Swimming drops blood glucose faster than most land-based exercise** because it engages the full body and cool water increases energy expenditure.
2. **Start a swim session with glucose slightly higher** than your usual pre-exercise target — many diabetes teams recommend 7–12 mmol/L (126–216 mg/dL).
3. **A child experiencing a hypo must get out of the water immediately** — low blood glucose in water is a drowning risk, and this rule is non-negotiable.
4. **Monitor for delayed post-swim hypos** for 4–8 hours after swimming, including overnight if the swim was in the afternoon or evening.
5. **Keep a dedicated pool bag** stocked with a glucose meter, fast-acting glucose, snacks, glucagon, and spare CGM supplies so you are prepared for every water activity.
