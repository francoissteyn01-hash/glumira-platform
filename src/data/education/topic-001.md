## What happened inside your child's body

Deep inside your child's tummy, there is a small organ called the **pancreas**. Think of it like a tiny factory. In a body without Type 1 Diabetes, that factory produces something called **insulin** — a hormone that acts like a key, unlocking the doors of cells so that sugar (glucose) from food can get inside and become energy.

Your child's immune system — the part of the body that normally fights off germs and infections — made a mistake. It identified the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas (called **beta cells**) as invaders, and it attacked them. This is what we call an **autoimmune** response. It is not an infection. It is not contagious. And it is absolutely, categorically **not something you caused**.

Because those beta cells have been damaged, your child's pancreas can no longer make enough insulin — or in many cases, any insulin at all. Without insulin, glucose stays in the blood instead of entering the cells. The blood glucose level rises, and the body starts looking for other sources of energy. That is why your child may have been drinking a lot, weeing more frequently, losing weight, or seeming unusually tired before diagnosis.

---

## This is not Type 2 Diabetes

You will hear people confuse the two. They are very different conditions. Type 1 Diabetes is **not** caused by eating too much sugar, being overweight, or any lifestyle factor. It can occur at any age, but it is one of the most common chronic conditions diagnosed in young children. According to the International Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Diabetes (ISPAD), the incidence in children under five has been rising globally for decades — your child is far from alone.

---

## You did not cause this

I want to say this clearly, because I know the thought is circling in your mind: **you did not do anything wrong.** You did not feed your child the wrong foods. You did not miss a sign too early. You could not have prevented this. The exact triggers for the autoimmune response are still being researched, but current evidence from ISPAD and the American Diabetes Association (ADA) confirms that Type 1 Diabetes is not preventable with any known intervention.

If guilt is weighing on you, please know that every parent in your position has felt the same way. It is a natural response — but it is not the truth. The truth is that your child's immune system made an error, and now you are stepping up to provide what the body no longer can.

---

## What does this mean day to day?

Because your child's body can no longer produce insulin, insulin must be given from outside the body — through injections or an insulin pump. This replaces what the pancreas used to do.

Your child will also need regular **blood glucose monitoring** — checking the level of sugar in their blood throughout the day. This helps you and your care team understand how their body is responding to food, activity, sleep, illness, and insulin.

You will hear a lot of new words: **bolus**, **basal**, **correction factor**, **carb counting**, **hypo**, **hyper**. Please do not feel you need to learn them all today. GluMira is designed to introduce these concepts gently, at your pace.

When insulin is discussed in GluMira, you may see references to the **IOB Hunter** feature, which helps visualise insulin on board — the insulin still active in your child's body. This is an educational tool to help you understand insulin activity patterns. Always discuss insulin-related clinical decisions with your care team.

## The emotional side

A diagnosis in a child this young carries a unique emotional weight. Your baby or toddler cannot understand what is happening to them. They cannot tell you how they feel. You become their voice, their monitor, their protector in a way that feels immense.

It is normal to feel:
- **Grief** — for the life you imagined without this condition
- **Fear** — especially about nighttime, about lows, about the future
- **Anger** — at the unfairness of it all
- **Exhaustion** — both physical and emotional

These feelings do not make you a bad parent. They make you a human one. Many families find that connecting with other parents of children with Type 1 Diabetes provides comfort that no textbook can. Discuss support groups and counselling options with your care team.

---

## When to call your care team

In the early days especially, do not hesitate to reach out to your care team if:
- Your child's blood glucose is consistently outside the target range given to you at discharge
- You are unsure about any aspect of insulin administration
- Your child is unwell with vomiting, fever, or diarrhoea and you are unsure how to manage their diabetes alongside the illness
- You notice signs of **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)**: fruity-smelling breath, rapid breathing, excessive sleepiness, or vomiting
- You simply feel overwhelmed and need reassurance — that is a valid reason to call

## Five key takeaways

1. **Type 1 Diabetes is an autoimmune condition** — the immune system attacked the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. It is not caused by diet, lifestyle, or anything you did.
2. **Insulin must now be provided from outside the body** — through injections or a pump — because the pancreas can no longer make enough on its own.
3. **Blood glucose monitoring is essential** — it helps you and your care team understand how your child's body responds to food, activity, and insulin.
4. **You did not cause this, and you could not have prevented it.** Current research confirms there is no known way to prevent Type 1 Diabetes.
5. **You are not alone.** Thousands of families are navigating this same path, and your care team is there to support you every step of the way.
