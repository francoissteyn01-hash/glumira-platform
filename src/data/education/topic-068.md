## The Unsung Hero of Glucose Management

If carb counting is the headline skill of T1D nutrition (and it is — see Topic 61), then fibre is the quiet background player that makes everything work better. Most people with Type 1 Diabetes know they should "eat more fibre." Fewer people understand exactly *why* fibre matters so much for glucose management, or how to use it strategically.

Mira is here to change that. Fibre is not just a health buzzword. It is a practical, everyday tool that can genuinely smooth out your glucose curves — and once you understand how it works, you will start seeing opportunities to use it at every meal.

## What Is Fibre?

Dietary fibre is the part of plant foods that your body cannot digest or absorb. Unlike other carbohydrates — which are broken down into glucose — fibre passes through your digestive system largely intact. It is found in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds.

There are two main types:

### Soluble Fibre

Soluble fibre dissolves in water to form a gel-like substance in your gut. This gel slows the absorption of nutrients — including glucose. It is the type of fibre most directly relevant to glucose management.

**Good sources of soluble fibre:**
- Oats and oat bran
- Barley
- Legumes (lentils, chickpeas, beans)
- Apples, oranges, pears
- Sweet potatoes
- Flaxseeds and chia seeds
- Psyllium husk

### Insoluble Fibre

Insoluble fibre does not dissolve in water. It adds bulk to stool and helps food move through the digestive system. While it is less directly involved in glucose management, it contributes to overall digestive health and satiety.

**Good sources of insoluble fibre:**
- Whole wheat bread and wheat bran
- Brown rice
- Vegetables (cauliflower, green beans, potatoes with skin)
- Nuts
- Seeds

Most plant foods contain both types, in varying proportions. You do not need to track them separately — eating a variety of plant foods gives you both.

## How Fibre Slows Glucose Absorption

This is the mechanism that matters most for T1D:

When you eat carbohydrates alongside soluble fibre, the fibre forms a viscous gel in your small intestine. This gel physically slows the rate at which glucose molecules can be absorbed through the intestinal wall and into your bloodstream.

The result: the same amount of carbohydrate produces a slower, lower, more extended glucose rise when eaten with fibre compared to when eaten without it.

**Practical example:** 
- 200g of white rice (low fibre): produces a fast, high glucose spike peaking at 30–60 minutes
- 200g of brown rice (higher fibre): produces a slower, lower glucose rise peaking at 60–90 minutes
- 200g of lentils (very high fibre): produces a gentle, gradual glucose rise over 90–120 minutes

The total carbohydrate may be similar, but the glucose delivery profile is dramatically different. This is fibre at work — and it is the same mechanism that underlies the Glycaemic Index differences discussed in Topic 63.

## The Net Carbs Concept

In some countries and dietary approaches — particularly the Bernstein low-carb method and many food labels in the United States — you will encounter the concept of "net carbs."

**Net carbs = Total carbohydrates minus fibre**

The reasoning is simple: since fibre is not digested and does not convert to glucose, it should not be counted as part of your active carbohydrate load.

**Example:**
A food label reads:
- Total carbohydrates: 25g
- Dietary fibre: 8g
- Net carbs: 25 - 8 = 17g

**Important considerations:**

1. **This approach works best for very high-fibre foods.** For foods where fibre makes up a small percentage of total carbs (like white bread with 2g fibre per 30g carb), the difference is minimal.

2. **Not everyone responds identically.** Some people find that subtracting all fibre works well for them. Others find that subtracting only half the fibre is more accurate. Your CGM data will tell you which approach matches your body.

3. **Food labels vary by country.** In Australia, the UK, and South Africa, fibre is generally already excluded from the total carbohydrate figure on the label. In the US and Canada, fibre is included in total carbohydrates. Check which system your country uses — this affects whether you need to subtract fibre or not.

4. **Discuss the net carbs approach with your healthcare team.** They can help you determine whether it improves your post-meal glucose management.

## High-Fibre Foods — A Practical Guide

Here are fibre-rich foods with their approximate fibre content per typical serving:

| Food | Serving | Fibre |
|---|---|---|
| Lentils (cooked) | 1 cup (200g) | 15–16g |
| Chickpeas (cooked) | 1 cup (200g) | 12–13g |
| Black beans (cooked) | 1 cup (200g) | 15g |
| Rolled oats (dry) | 40g | 4g |
| Whole wheat bread | 1 slice | 2–3g |
| Broccoli (cooked) | 1 cup | 5g |
| Sweet potato (medium, with skin) | 1 medium | 4g |
| Apple (with skin) | 1 medium | 4g |
| Pear (with skin) | 1 medium | 5–6g |
| Avocado | Half | 5g |
| Chia seeds | 1 tablespoon | 5g |
| Flaxseeds (ground) | 1 tablespoon | 3g |
| Almonds | Small handful (30g) | 3–4g |
| Popcorn (air-popped) | 3 cups | 3–4g |
| Brown rice (cooked) | 1 cup | 3–4g |
| Raspberries | 1 cup | 8g |

The daily fibre recommendation for most adults is 25–30g. Most people in Western diets eat only 15–18g. Even a modest increase — adding lentils to a stew, choosing whole grain bread, snacking on an apple with skin — can make a meaningful difference.

## Practical Ways to Add Fibre to Meals

### Breakfast
- **Swap instant oats for traditional rolled oats.** Add chia seeds or flaxseeds. Top with berries. This turns a high-GI breakfast into a moderate-GI, high-fibre meal.
- **Add a piece of whole fruit** instead of fruit juice. The fibre in the whole fruit slows sugar absorption — juice has the sugar but not the fibre.

### Lunch
- **Add a cup of lentil or bean soup** as a side. Legumes are the fibre champions.
- **Choose whole wheat or rye bread** over white bread for sandwiches.
- **Include a side salad with vegetables** — even small amounts of fibre from mixed vegetables contribute.

### Dinner
- **Mix legumes into main dishes.** Add chickpeas to a curry. Stir kidney beans into a bolognese. Serve lentil dal alongside rice.
- **Keep potato skins on.** A baked potato with skin has more fibre than peeled, mashed potato.
- **Choose brown rice or barley** when possible. Even mixing half white rice with half brown rice increases the fibre content.

### Snacks
- **Nuts and seeds** — almonds, walnuts, sunflower seeds
- **Fruit with skin** — apples, pears, berries
- **Vegetables with hummus** — carrots, celery, capsicum

### During Ramadan

The Suhoor meal is an ideal opportunity to maximise fibre intake. High-fibre foods at Suhoor — oats, legumes, whole grains, fruits with skin — release glucose slowly through the fasting hours, helping to maintain more stable levels. This is one of the most practical applications of fibre knowledge in T1D management during Ramadan.

## Fibre and Digestive Comfort

One important note: if you currently eat a low-fibre diet, increase your intake gradually over 2–3 weeks. A sudden large increase in fibre can cause bloating, gas, and discomfort. Your digestive system adapts, but it needs time.

Also increase your water intake as you increase fibre. Fibre absorbs water — without adequate hydration (see Topic 69), high-fibre foods can cause constipation rather than preventing it.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Soluble fibre forms a gel in the gut that physically slows glucose absorption** — producing flatter, more manageable post-meal glucose curves.
2. **Legumes are the fibre champions** — lentils, chickpeas, and beans deliver 12–16g of fibre per cup, far more than most other foods.
3. **The net carbs concept (total carbs minus fibre) can improve carb-counting accuracy for high-fibre foods** — but check your country's labelling system and discuss the approach with your healthcare team.
4. **Simple additions — chia seeds in oats, beans in a curry, an apple instead of juice — meaningfully increase fibre intake** without changing what you enjoy eating.
5. **Increase fibre gradually and drink more water** — your digestive system needs time to adapt to higher fibre intake.
