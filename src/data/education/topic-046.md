# Part-Time Jobs — Managing T1D at Work

**Topic ID:** 46
**Age Range:** 15–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Independence & Lifestyle

## Your First Job, with a Pancreas That Doesn't Work

Getting your first part-time job is a huge step. Your own money, your own schedule, a taste of the adult world. For most teens, the biggest concerns are awkward customers and whether the pay is any good. For you, there is an added dimension: how do I manage my diabetes at work?

The good news is straightforward: people with T1D work in virtually every profession and every industry. There is almost no job you cannot do. But a part-time job — especially your first one — requires some practical planning that your colleagues will not have to think about. That is not unfair. It is just the reality.

## Telling Your Employer — Do You Have To?

This is a question that comes up a lot, and the answer has both a legal and a practical side.

**Legally:** In most countries, you are not required to disclose a medical condition on a job application. Anti-discrimination laws (the Equality Act in the UK, the ADA in the US, the Disability Discrimination Act in Australia) protect you from being turned down for a job because of a health condition. An employer cannot legally refuse to hire you because you have T1D.

**Practically:** Once you are hired, it is strongly advisable to tell your manager or supervisor. Not because you are obligated to, but because:

1. You may need to take short breaks to check your BG or treat a hypo. If your manager does not know why, this can look like slacking off.
2. If you have a hypo at work, your colleagues need to know what is happening and how to help. Someone calling an ambulance because they think you are having a seizure when you actually need a juice box is avoidable — but only if they know.
3. You may need access to your phone (for CGM), food or drink during shifts, or a place to store insulin. These are reasonable adjustments that most employers are happy to make, but they need to know why.

You do not need to disclose T1D in your interview or on your application. But once you are on the job, a brief conversation with your direct supervisor is both practical and, in many cases, protective of your rights.

## How to Have the Conversation

Keep it simple and professional. You are giving information, not asking for sympathy.

"I have Type 1 Diabetes — it's an autoimmune condition where my body doesn't produce insulin, so I manage it with injections/a pump. It's well-managed, and it doesn't affect my ability to do this job. I just need to be able to check my blood sugar occasionally, eat or drink if my levels drop, and have access to my phone for my glucose monitor. If my blood sugar ever goes low, I might need to stop and have some sugar — it takes about 10 to 15 minutes to sort out."

That is it. Most managers will nod, ask a couple of questions, and move on. If they ask intrusive or inappropriate questions, you can keep your answers focused on what is relevant to the workplace.

## Your Legal Rights at Work

Depending on where you live, you have specific legal protections:

**United Kingdom:** Under the Equality Act 2010, Type 1 Diabetes is classified as a disability. Your employer has a legal duty to make "reasonable adjustments." This includes allowing breaks for BG checks, access to food and drink during shifts, time off for medical appointments, and a safe place to store insulin. These are not special favours — they are legal requirements.

**United States:** The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and, for younger workers, state labour laws provide similar protections. Employers must provide reasonable accommodations. You have the right to check your BG, treat hypos, eat and drink as needed, and keep diabetes supplies at your workstation.

**Australia:** The Disability Discrimination Act 1992 protects workers with T1D from discrimination and entitles them to reasonable workplace adjustments.

If an employer refuses to make reasonable adjustments, or if you experience discrimination because of your T1D, this is a legal issue. Keep a record of what happened and when, and seek advice from your diabetes team, your parents, or a relevant employment advisory service.

## Break Scheduling and Shift Patterns

This is where the day-to-day management actually happens. Different jobs present different challenges:

**Retail and hospitality:** Often involve long periods standing, irregular break times, and environments where eating at your workstation is not the norm. Talk to your manager about guaranteeing regular breaks — not just the standard breaks everyone gets, but the ability to step away briefly if your BG needs attention. Carry glucose on your person (pocket, apron, belt bag), not in a locker across the building.

**Food service:** Working around food is both a practical advantage (access to carbs) and a potential challenge (temptation, irregular eating). Be aware that the physical nature of these jobs (on your feet, lifting, carrying) can lower your BG, particularly during long or busy shifts.

**Physical jobs:** Warehouse work, farm work, outdoor jobs, sports coaching — anything physically demanding increases glucose uptake and hypo risk. You may need more frequent breaks and more carbohydrate intake during these shifts than during sedentary work.

**Office or desk-based work:** Generally easier to manage, as you can usually keep supplies at your desk and monitor your BG discreetly.

Whatever the job, the principle is the same: you need reliable access to glucose, the ability to check your levels, and the flexibility to pause briefly if your BG needs attention. These are baseline requirements, not luxuries.

## Hypos at Work

A hypo at work is not the end of the world, but it does require a plan. You need fast-acting glucose accessible during every shift — on your body, not in your bag in the break room. Glucose tablets are ideal because they are small, portable, and do not look like you are stopping to eat.

If you go low during a shift:

1. Stop what you are doing. If you are operating machinery, serving a customer, or doing anything that requires coordination or concentration, stop safely.
2. Treat the hypo. Eat your glucose.
3. Tell your supervisor or a colleague if you need a few minutes.
4. Wait for recovery before resuming work. Do not push through.

If a colleague or customer seems confused by what is happening, a brief "I just need a minute — blood sugar thing, I'll be fine" is sufficient. You do not owe customers a medical explanation.

## Managing Supplies at Work

Bring more than you think you need to every shift. A good work kit includes:

- Fast-acting glucose (tablets, gel, juice)
- Longer-acting snacks (cereal bars, crackers)
- Your blood glucose meter and strips
- Spare pump supplies or insulin pen if applicable
- Your phone (charged) for CGM access
- Water

Store insulin safely — it should not be left in a hot car, a sunny windowsill, or a freezing stockroom. If your workplace does not have a suitable storage option, discuss this with your manager as part of reasonable adjustments.

## Night Shifts and Late Finishes

Part-time jobs for teens often involve evening or weekend shifts that disrupt your normal routine. Late finishes mean late meals, disrupted sleep, and changed activity levels — all of which affect BG.

If you work late shifts, plan your meals and insulin timing around your work schedule, not the other way around. Eat before a late shift. Bring food for during the shift. Check your BG before driving home. Eat something before bed, especially if the shift was physically demanding.

## Balancing Work, School, and Diabetes

Adding a job on top of school and diabetes management is a lot. There will be days when it feels like too much. That is normal, and it is okay to adjust — to reduce your hours, to take a break, to choose your health over a shift.

Your employer cannot penalise you for medical absences or appointments. If your diabetes is making it hard to sustain a particular job or schedule, that is a legitimate reason to renegotiate your hours or look for something more compatible.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **You have legal protections at work** — anti-discrimination laws in most countries classify T1D as a disability entitling you to reasonable adjustments including breaks, food access, and supply storage.
2. **Tell your direct supervisor after you are hired** — a brief, factual conversation protects your right to manage your diabetes during shifts and ensures colleagues can help in an emergency.
3. **Keep fast-acting glucose on your person during every shift**, not in a locker or break room. Seconds matter during a hypo.
4. **Plan your meals and monitoring around your shift pattern**, especially for evening, weekend, or physically demanding work.
5. **Your health comes first** — if a job or schedule is unsustainable alongside T1D management, adjusting your hours or role is not failure, it is self-management.
