## Hey. I'm Mira.

I am not going to sugarcoat this (no pun intended). Getting diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes is a big deal. Your life just changed, and you probably have a hundred questions — or maybe you are so overwhelmed that you cannot even form the questions yet. Either way, that is fine. I am Mira, and I am here to give you the real information, straight up, without talking down to you.

Let's start with what actually happened.

---

## The autoimmune process — what went wrong

Your body has an immune system. It is an incredibly complex defence network made up of white blood cells, antibodies, and specialised proteins designed to identify and destroy threats — bacteria, viruses, infected cells. Normally, it is one of the most impressive systems in biology.

But your immune system made a critical error. It identified the **beta cells** in your **pancreas** as foreign invaders and launched an attack against them. Beta cells are the only cells in your body that produce **insulin** — a hormone that regulates how glucose (sugar from food) enters your cells to be used as energy.

This is called an **autoimmune response**. "Auto" means self. Your body attacked itself. The result: your beta cells have been significantly damaged or destroyed, and your pancreas can no longer produce enough insulin to keep your blood glucose levels in a safe range.

This process likely started months — possibly even years — before you had any symptoms. By the time you were diagnosed, it is estimated that around 80-90% of your beta cells were already gone. The symptoms you experienced — excessive thirst, frequent urination, weight loss, fatigue — were your body's signals that it could no longer compensate.

---

## Let's clear something up right now

**You did not eat too much sugar.** Full stop.

Type 1 Diabetes is not caused by diet. It is not caused by being overweight. It is not caused by lack of exercise, or by anything you did or did not do. It is fundamentally different from Type 2 Diabetes, which involves insulin resistance and has different risk factors.

Type 1 Diabetes is an autoimmune condition. The exact trigger is still being researched, but current evidence from ISPAD, the ADA, and NICE suggests it involves a combination of genetic predisposition and environmental factors (possibly viral infections or other immune triggers). There is no known way to prevent it.

So when someone at school inevitably says, "Oh, is that because you ate too many sweets?" — you now have the knowledge to set them straight. And if you do not feel like explaining, that is fine too. You do not owe anyone a medical lecture.

## What insulin actually does

Every cell in your body needs glucose for energy. When you eat carbohydrates — bread, pasta, fruit, rice — your digestive system breaks them down into glucose, which enters your bloodstream. Insulin is the hormone that signals your cells to absorb that glucose.

Without insulin:
- Glucose accumulates in your blood (hyperglycaemia)
- Your cells are starved of their primary energy source
- Your body starts breaking down fat and muscle for fuel, producing **ketones** as a byproduct
- If ketones build up, this leads to **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** — a serious and potentially life-threatening condition

This is why you need to take insulin every day, either through injections or an insulin pump. You are replacing what your body can no longer produce. It is not optional, and it is not a sign of weakness. It is survival, and it is science.

When exploring how insulin works over time in GluMira, you will encounter the **IOB Hunter** feature. IOB stands for Insulin on Board — the amount of insulin still actively working in your body. Understanding IOB helps you see why blood glucose levels change the way they do. Always discuss insulin-related decisions with your care team.

## The numbers game

You are going to be dealing with numbers a lot now. Blood glucose readings, carb counts, insulin amounts, target ranges. It can feel like maths homework that never ends.

Here is something important: **your blood glucose number is not a grade.** A high reading does not mean you failed. A low reading does not mean you did something wrong. These numbers are data — information that helps you and your care team understand what is happening and make adjustments.

Blood glucose levels are influenced by dozens of factors: what you ate, when you ate, physical activity, stress, hormones (puberty makes this extra fun), sleep, illness, weather — even emotions. Some days the numbers will make no sense at all, and that is genuinely normal.

Target ranges vary by age and individual circumstances. Your care team will discuss what ranges are appropriate for you specifically. According to ISPAD and ADA guidelines, the general aim is to keep blood glucose levels within a safe range as much as possible while avoiding severe lows — but perfection is not the goal. Management is.

## The emotional reality

Let's be honest: this can mess with your head. You might feel:

- **Angry** — why you? Why now?
- **Isolated** — like nobody around you gets it
- **Anxious** — about lows, about the future, about being different
- **In denial** — wanting to pretend it is not happening
- **Burned out** — diabetes management is relentless, and burnout is real

These are documented psychological responses. Research published in line with ISPAD guidelines confirms that teens with Type 1 Diabetes have a higher risk of diabetes distress, anxiety, and depression. This is not a personal failing. It is a recognised consequence of living with a demanding chronic condition during an already challenging stage of life.

If you are struggling, say so. Discuss it with your care team. Many diabetes clinics have psychologists or counsellors who specialise in exactly this. You are not being dramatic. You are being smart.

---

## When to call your care team

Reach out to your care team if:
- You are experiencing frequent highs or lows that you cannot explain
- You feel unwell and are unsure how to manage your diabetes alongside illness
- You notice symptoms of DKA: nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity-smelling breath, rapid breathing
- You are struggling emotionally and it is affecting your diabetes management or your daily life
- You have questions — any questions. That is literally what they are there for.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Type 1 Diabetes is an autoimmune condition.** Your immune system attacked the insulin-producing beta cells in your pancreas. It is not caused by sugar, diet, or lifestyle.
2. **Insulin is essential for life.** Without it, glucose cannot enter your cells, and dangerous ketones build up. Taking insulin is replacing what your body can no longer make.
3. **Blood glucose numbers are data, not a report card.** Dozens of factors affect them, and perfection is not the goal.
4. **The emotional impact is real and recognised.** Feeling angry, anxious, or burned out is a normal response. Discuss it with your care team.
5. **You did not cause this, and you could not have prevented it.** The science is clear on this.
