# Telling Friends — Scripts for Kids and Teens at Every Age

**Age Range:** 5-18 years
**Audience:** Children and teens
**Topic ID:** 010
**Related Topics:** [topic-030](/education/topic-030), [topic-040](/education/topic-040), [topic-043](/education/topic-043)

---

## Hi there! I'm Mira.

One of the most common questions I hear from kids and teens with Type 1 Diabetes is: **"What do I actually say?"**

You know you probably need to tell your friends at some point. Maybe you have already been back at school and people noticed you checking your blood sugar or taking an injection. Maybe you have been avoiding it because you do not know how to start the conversation. Maybe a friend has already asked and you froze.

That is all completely normal. Telling people about a medical condition is not something anyone teaches you how to do. So I am going to give you actual words — real scripts you can practise and adapt — for different ages and different situations. You can use them exactly as they are, or change them to sound more like you. The point is: you do not have to figure this out from scratch.

---

## Before we start: some important truths

**You get to choose who you tell.** Type 1 Diabetes is your personal medical information. You are not obligated to tell everyone. You might want to tell your close friends and not the whole class. You might want to tell everyone and get it over with. Both are fine. It is your call.

**You get to choose how much you share.** Some people want the full explanation. Others just want to say the minimum and move on. There is no right amount of detail.

**Most people will be kind.** They might ask awkward questions out of curiosity, but the vast majority of kids and teens respond with interest and support, not cruelty.

**Some people will say something ignorant.** "Did you eat too much sugar?" "Can you catch that?" "My grandma has that." These comments usually come from not knowing, not from meanness. You can correct them, or you can let it go. Your energy, your choice.

**It gets easier every time you tell someone.** The first time is the hardest. By the third or fourth time, you will have it down.

## Scripts for ages 5-7

At this age, short and simple works best. Your friends are unlikely to ask detailed questions — they mostly want to know if you are okay and if they can still play with you.

**Script 1 — The basic explanation:**
"I have something called Type 1 Diabetes. It means my body cannot make something called insulin anymore, so I have to check my blood sugar and take medicine every day. It is not catching — you cannot get it from me. I can still do everything we normally do."

**Script 2 — When a friend sees you checking your blood sugar:**
"This is my blood sugar checker. I have to prick my finger to see if my body has enough sugar. It does not really hurt — it is just a tiny poke. I have to do it a few times every day because of my diabetes."

**Script 3 — When a friend sees you having a snack at an unusual time:**
"I have Type 1 Diabetes, so sometimes I need to eat a snack even if it is not snack time. My body needs it to keep working properly. It is like putting fuel in a car."

**Script 4 — When a friend asks "What is wrong with you?":**
"Nothing is wrong with me! I have Type 1 Diabetes, which means my body needs a bit of extra help. I take insulin, which is like a special medicine, and I check my blood sugar. But I am the same me as always."

**A tip for this age group:** If your parent or teacher offers to explain to the class (with your permission), that can take the pressure off you. Some teachers read a story about diabetes or let you show your kit in a "show and tell" style — which can actually make you feel pretty cool.

## Scripts for ages 8-11

At this age, friends are more curious and may ask more questions. They can handle a slightly more detailed explanation. The key is confidence — if you seem relaxed about it, they will be too.

**Script 1 — Telling a close friend:**
"Hey, I want to tell you something. I have been diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes. Basically, my immune system attacked part of my pancreas by mistake, so my body cannot make insulin anymore. Insulin is what helps your body turn food into energy. So now I have to take insulin through injections [or a pump] and check my blood sugar throughout the day. It is not because of anything I ate — it is an autoimmune thing. I am still the same person, I can still do everything, I just have some extra stuff to deal with."

**Script 2 — When someone asks in front of a group:**
"I have Type 1 Diabetes. It means I need to take insulin every day and check my blood sugar. It is not a big deal — it is just part of my routine now. And no, it is not because I ate too many sweets." (You can say that last part with a smile — humour helps.)

**Script 3 — When someone says "My nan has diabetes":**
"That is probably Type 2, which is different from what I have. Type 1 is when your immune system attacks your pancreas. Type 2 is more about how the body uses insulin. They are actually pretty different, even though they have similar names."

**Script 4 — When someone asks "Does it hurt?":**
"The finger pricks are not great, but you get used to them. The injections are with a really tiny needle, so it is more annoying than painful. Honestly, the worst part is having to think about it all the time, not the actual needles."

**Script 5 — When you want to prepare a friend for sleepovers or outings:**
"Just so you know, I might need to check my blood sugar a couple of times, and I might need to eat a snack at random times. If I ever seem shaky or weird, it might mean my blood sugar is low, and I need some juice or sweets fast. It is not an emergency — just grab me something sugary and I will be fine."

## Scripts for ages 12-15

At this age, you might worry more about being seen as different, and the social stakes feel higher. The good news: most teenagers are far more accepting than you expect. The key here is casual confidence. Treat it as information, not a confession.

**Script 1 — Telling a close friend:**
"So, I have Type 1 Diabetes. My immune system destroyed the cells in my pancreas that make insulin, so I have to inject it. I check my blood sugar throughout the day. It is an autoimmune condition — nothing to do with eating sugar or being unhealthy. I just wanted you to know because you might see me checking levels or injecting, and I did not want it to be a weird surprise."

**Script 2 — The casual mention:**
Sometimes the easiest approach is not to make it a formal announcement. Just mention it naturally when it comes up:
- (Checking blood sugar in front of a friend) "Oh, this is my CGM / blood sugar meter — I have Type 1 Diabetes."
- (Declining a spontaneous plan) "I need to eat first — diabetes stuff."
- (Taking an injection) "Just taking my insulin. Type 1 — my pancreas is basically retired."

**Script 3 — When someone says something ignorant:**
"Actually, Type 1 Diabetes is an autoimmune condition. My immune system attacked my pancreas. It has nothing to do with sugar or diet — that is a really common misconception." (Calm, factual, not angry. You are educating, not defending.)

**Script 4 — When someone asks if you can eat something:**
"I can eat whatever I want — I just need to take insulin for it. Type 1 Diabetes is about managing insulin, not avoiding food."

**Script 5 — When you really do not want to explain:**
"I have a medical condition that I manage with injections. It is not a big deal. I would rather not go into it right now." (You are allowed to say this. You do not owe anyone a medical textbook.)

## Scripts for ages 16-18

By now, you may have had diabetes for years, or you might be newly diagnosed and navigating it alongside the social complexity of older teenage life — relationships, parties, driving, independence.

**Script 1 — Telling a new friend or partner:**
"I have Type 1 Diabetes — it is an autoimmune condition where my body does not produce insulin. I manage it with injections [or a pump] and blood glucose monitoring. It is a pretty big part of my daily life, but it does not stop me from doing anything. I just wanted you to know because you will probably see me checking levels or injecting at some point."

**Script 2 — At a party or social gathering:**
"I am just checking my blood sugar — I have T1D. I can still eat and drink [within the limits your care team has discussed with you regarding alcohol, if applicable], I just need to keep track of what my levels are doing."

**Script 3 — When someone tries to police your food:**
"I appreciate the concern, but I can eat this. I take insulin to cover it. Type 1 is not about avoiding sugar — it is about replacing a hormone my body does not make."

**Script 4 — When you want your friends to know what to do in an emergency:**
"Hey, can I tell you something practical? If I ever seem confused, shaky, or not making sense, my blood sugar might be really low. If that happens, give me some juice or sugary sweets and stay with me. If I pass out, put me on my side and call an ambulance. It probably will not happen, but I would feel better knowing you know what to do."

## What if someone is unkind?

It is rare, but it happens. If someone teases you, mocks your injections, or makes jokes about your diabetes:

- **Remember: their behaviour says everything about them and nothing about you.**
- You can respond with facts: "Diabetes is a serious medical condition. It is not funny."
- You can walk away. You do not need to educate everyone.
- If it continues, tell a trusted adult — a parent, teacher, or school counsellor. Bullying related to a medical condition is not something you should have to tolerate.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **You choose who you tell, when, and how much you share.** There is no obligation to tell everyone, and there is no single right way to do it.
2. **Most people respond with kindness and curiosity.** Awkward questions are usually about ignorance, not cruelty.
3. **Casual confidence is your best tool.** If you treat diabetes as normal and manageable, others will follow your lead.
4. **It is OK to correct misconceptions.** "It is not about eating too much sugar — it is an autoimmune condition" is a one-liner worth practising.
5. **It gets easier every time.** The first conversation is the hardest. After that, you will find your rhythm and your words.
