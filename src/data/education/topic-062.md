# Protein and Fat — The Slow Glucose Release Most People Ignore

## The Forgotten Macronutrients

Most people with Type 1 Diabetes learn early that carbohydrates raise blood glucose. That is true and important. But here is something that catches many people off guard: protein and fat also affect blood glucose — just more slowly and less predictably.

If you have ever eaten a low-carb, high-fat meal and been puzzled by a glucose rise that appears three, four, or even six hours later, you have experienced this firsthand. Understanding why this happens — and what to do about it — is a genuine step forward in glucose management.

## How Protein Affects Blood Glucose

Protein does not raise blood glucose the way carbohydrates do. Carbohydrates are broken down into glucose relatively quickly — within about 15 minutes to 2 hours, depending on the type. Protein, on the other hand, is broken down into amino acids, and a portion of those amino acids can be converted into glucose through a process called gluconeogenesis.

This conversion is slow. It typically begins 3–4 hours after eating and can continue for up to 5–6 hours. The glucose rise from protein is also smaller in magnitude than the rise from an equivalent weight of carbohydrate — roughly 30–50% of protein may eventually convert to glucose, depending on the individual and the context.

**Practical example:** A 200g steak contains approximately 50g of protein and virtually no carbohydrate. You might see no glucose impact in the first 2 hours, then a gradual rise of 2–4 mmol/L over the following 3–5 hours.

This matters most when you eat large amounts of protein with relatively few carbohydrates — a common scenario for those following lower-carb approaches, including the Bernstein method.

## How Fat Affects Blood Glucose

Fat does not convert to glucose in any meaningful way. So why does it affect blood glucose? Two reasons:

1. **Fat slows gastric emptying.** When you eat fat alongside carbohydrates, the carbs take longer to be absorbed. This means the glucose rise from the carbs is delayed and often extended. Instead of a sharp peak at 1 hour, you might see a slow rise that starts at 2 hours and continues for 4–5 hours.

2. **Fat can cause insulin resistance.** High-fat meals — particularly those rich in saturated fat — may temporarily reduce how effectively insulin works. This means you may need more insulin coverage for a high-fat meal than for an equivalent-carb meal that is lower in fat, even though the carb content is the same.

**Practical example:** Two slices of plain bread (about 30g carb) will produce a different glucose curve than two slices of bread thickly spread with butter and topped with cheese. The carbs are similar, but the fat changes the timing and potentially the magnitude of the glucose response.

## The Pizza Effect — A Perfect Case Study

The "pizza effect" is perhaps the most famous example of fat and protein influencing glucose in T1D. Pizza is high in carbohydrate (the base), high in fat (the cheese and oil), and moderate in protein (the cheese and toppings). This combination creates a unique glucose pattern:

- **First 1–2 hours:** A moderate glucose rise from the carbohydrate in the base.
- **Hours 3–6:** A prolonged, often stubborn glucose rise from the delayed absorption caused by fat, plus the protein contribution.

Many people find that covering pizza with a single pre-meal approach leaves them with excellent glucose readings for the first 2 hours, followed by persistent highs for the next 4–6 hours. This is the pizza effect in action.

**Other foods that produce similar effects:**
- Fish and chips (battered fish with hot chips)
- Creamy pasta dishes (carbonara, alfredo)
- Curry with rice and naan bread
- Fried chicken with sides
- Meat pies and sausage rolls
- Full English or cooked breakfasts with toast

## Extended Bolusing Concepts

For those using insulin pumps, the concept of an extended or dual-wave bolus was developed specifically for meals like these. The idea is to split the insulin delivery: a portion is delivered upfront to cover the immediate carbohydrate, and the remainder is delivered gradually over the next few hours to cover the delayed glucose impact of fat and protein.

For those on multiple daily injections (MDI), the principle is the same even if the tools differ. Your healthcare team can discuss strategies for managing these extended glucose responses. Some people find that a well-timed check and correction approach works effectively for high-fat, high-protein meals.

The key concept to understand is that **some meals need coverage that extends beyond the usual carb-counting window.** Recognising which meals fall into this category is half the battle.

## Real Meal Scenarios

Let us look at how fat and protein change the glucose picture across different meals:

**Meal 1 — Grilled chicken breast with steamed vegetables and rice:**
- Carbs: ~45g (mostly from rice)
- Fat: moderate
- Protein: high (~40g)
- **Expected pattern:** A standard glucose rise from the rice within 1–2 hours, with a possible gentle tail from the protein at 3–5 hours.

**Meal 2 — Lamb curry with basmati rice, naan bread, and raita:**
- Carbs: ~90g (rice + naan)
- Fat: high (oil, ghee, lamb fat)
- Protein: high (~35g)
- **Expected pattern:** A delayed and extended glucose rise. The fat slows absorption, so the peak may come later and last longer than expected for 90g of carb.

**Meal 3 — Cheese omelette with avocado (no bread):**
- Carbs: ~5g
- Fat: very high
- Protein: high (~30g)
- **Expected pattern:** Minimal immediate glucose rise, but a slow climb starting at 3–4 hours from protein conversion. This is especially relevant for those following Bernstein or other low-carb approaches.

These patterns apply equally whether you are eating within Halal, Kosher, or any other dietary framework. The macronutrient composition — not the cultural context of the meal — determines the glucose response.

## Meals During Ramadan — A Special Consideration

During Ramadan, the Iftar meal that breaks the fast is often rich in both carbohydrates and fats. Traditional dishes like samosas, fried foods, dates, and rice-based meals combine quick-acting carbs with significant fat content. This makes the glucose response after Iftar particularly complex — an initial rise from carbohydrates and dates, followed by a prolonged elevation from fat and protein.

Understanding the fat and protein effect helps explain why glucose management during Ramadan requires thoughtful planning. See the Ramadan Fasting Module for detailed guidance.

## Practical Tips for Managing Fat and Protein Effects

1. **Learn your personal patterns.** Not everyone responds identically. Use your continuous glucose monitor or regular finger-prick checks to observe how high-fat, high-protein meals affect you specifically.

2. **Keep a food and glucose diary for high-fat meals.** Note what you ate, how much, when you ate it, and what your glucose did over the next 6 hours. Patterns will emerge.

3. **Discuss extended meal strategies with your diabetes team.** They can help you develop an approach for pizza nights, braais, and celebratory meals.

4. **Do not fear fat and protein.** These macronutrients are essential for health, satiety, and enjoyment. Understanding their glucose impact is about management, not avoidance.

5. **Be patient with yourself.** Fat and protein effects are genuinely harder to manage than pure carbohydrate meals. Even experienced T1D veterans find pizza challenging.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Protein can convert to glucose slowly over 3–6 hours** — this is especially noticeable in high-protein, low-carb meals.
2. **Fat slows carbohydrate absorption and may reduce insulin sensitivity** — leading to delayed and extended glucose rises.
3. **The "pizza effect" is a classic example** — high-carb, high-fat, moderate-protein meals produce a prolonged, multi-phase glucose response.
4. **Extended bolus concepts exist to address these patterns** — talk to your healthcare team about strategies for your specific therapy.
5. **Tracking your personal response to high-fat, high-protein meals is the most valuable tool** — patterns are individual, and your data tells the real story.
