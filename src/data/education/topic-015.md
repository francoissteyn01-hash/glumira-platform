# Toddler Tantrums and Glucose — The Hidden Connection

**Age range:** 2-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 15
**Category:** Behaviour and blood sugar

---

Your toddler is lying face-down on the supermarket floor screaming because you broke their banana. This is a completely normal toddler thing. Every parent in the shop is looking away because they've been there.

But you're not just thinking about the banana. You're thinking: is this a tantrum, or is this a hypo?

That question — the one that lives in the back of your mind every time your child's behaviour changes — is exhausting. And you're not being paranoid. You're being a well-informed parent of a child with Type 1 diabetes.

You're doing great. Let's talk about what's happening in that little body and how to tell the difference.

## Why Blood Sugar Affects Behaviour

Blood glucose levels have a direct impact on brain function. The brain runs on glucose — it's the primary fuel — and when levels swing high or low, behaviour changes. This is true for adults too, but adults have language to describe feeling shaky or foggy. Toddlers do not.

**During hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar):**
- Irritability and sudden mood changes
- Crying that seems disproportionate to the trigger
- Clinginess or withdrawal
- Aggression — hitting, biting, throwing
- Difficulty concentrating or appearing "zoned out"
- Lethargy or sudden sleepiness
- Shakiness or poor coordination (which might look like clumsiness)

**During hyperglycaemia (high blood sugar):**
- Irritability and frustration
- Increased thirst and frequent urination (which is uncomfortable and annoying for a toddler)
- Fatigue and lethargy
- Difficulty focusing
- General crankiness that doesn't resolve with usual comforts

The overlap between "normal toddler behaviour" and "blood sugar-related behaviour" is enormous. This is what makes it so hard.

## The Check-First Approach

Many paediatric diabetes teams recommend a simple strategy: **check blood sugar before responding to challenging behaviour.**

This doesn't mean you need to wrestle a screaming child onto the floor with a finger prick every time they're upset. But it does mean developing a habit of considering glucose as a possible factor before jumping to behavioural conclusions.

**Practical steps:**

- If your child has a CGM, glance at it. A quick look at the current reading and trend arrow can tell you a lot in two seconds.
- If you're using finger pricks, and the behaviour is unusual or escalating, a check is reasonable.
- If blood sugar is low, treat the low first. The tantrum may resolve entirely once glucose is back in range.
- If blood sugar is high, the irritability may ease once levels come down. Be patient and provide comfort.
- If blood sugar is in range, congratulations — you have a normal toddler having a normal tantrum. Proceed with your usual parenting approach.

## The Guilt Cycle

Here's where it gets emotionally complicated.

Many parents of children with T1D describe a painful guilt cycle:

1. Child has a meltdown.
2. Parent responds with discipline or firm boundaries (because that's normal parenting).
3. Parent checks blood sugar afterwards and discovers it was low.
4. Parent feels crushing guilt for disciplining a child who was having a medical event.
5. Parent becomes hesitant to set any boundaries, fearing the behaviour might always be glucose-related.
6. Child learns that extreme behaviour gets a different response, regardless of the cause.
7. Everyone is confused and exhausted.

If this sounds familiar, you are not alone. This cycle is incredibly common in families managing T1D in toddlers, and it's not a sign that you're doing anything wrong. It's a sign that you're dealing with two overlapping challenges — normal child development and a chronic medical condition — and the boundaries between them are blurry.

**Breaking the cycle:**

- **Check first, respond second.** Make glucose checking part of your response to difficult behaviour, not an afterthought.
- **Forgive yourself for the times you got it wrong.** You will sometimes discipline a low. You will sometimes comfort a tantrum that was purely behavioural. Neither of these will damage your child.
- **Maintain boundaries.** Children with diabetes still need consistent boundaries. Having T1D doesn't mean a child never has a genuine tantrum, and treating every meltdown as medical undermines the structure that toddlers actually need.
- **Talk to your diabetes team.** If you're struggling to tell the difference between glucose-related behaviour and developmental behaviour, this is exactly the kind of thing your team can help with. Many teams include or can refer to psychologists who specialise in paediatric diabetes.

## What "Normal" Toddler Behaviour Looks Like

It helps to remember what's developmentally typical for this age group, because a lot of it looks alarming regardless of diabetes:

- **Tantrums are normal** between ages 1 and 4. They peak around 2-3 years.
- **Big emotions with no regulation skills** are the definition of being a toddler. They feel everything at maximum volume.
- **Testing boundaries** is healthy. It's how children learn about the world and their place in it.
- **Irregular eating** is normal. Toddlers graze, refuse meals, and then eat everything in sight.
- **Sleep disruption** is common and affects mood dramatically.

Not everything is diabetes. Sometimes a broken banana is just a broken banana.

## Tracking Patterns

Over time, you may start to notice patterns in your child's behaviour that correlate with blood sugar trends. This is valuable information.

Consider keeping a simple log (even just notes on your phone) that captures:

- Time of day
- Blood sugar reading
- What the behaviour looked like
- What was happening beforehand (meal, nap, play, transition)
- What helped resolve it

After a few weeks, patterns may emerge. Perhaps your child is consistently irritable at 4pm when blood sugar tends to drift high. Perhaps morning clinginess coincides with overnight lows. These patterns give your diabetes team something concrete to work with.

## When to Seek Additional Support

Sometimes the emotional and behavioural challenges of managing T1D in a toddler go beyond what parents can navigate alone. Consider asking for additional support if:

- You're finding it impossible to distinguish between medical and behavioural causes of meltdowns
- Your own anxiety about blood sugar is affecting how you parent
- Your child's behaviour is significantly different from their peers, even when blood sugar is in range
- You or your partner are experiencing burnout, depression, or persistent guilt
- Siblings are being affected by the intensity of diabetes management

Paediatric diabetes psychology services exist for exactly these situations. If your team doesn't have an in-house psychologist, ask for a referral. ISPAD guidelines specifically recommend that psychological support be integrated into paediatric diabetes care.

## A Gentle Reminder

You are not a glucose monitor with legs. You are a parent — a tired, dedicated, loving parent who is doing their best with incomplete information in real time.

Some days you'll check before you react. Some days you'll react before you check. Some days the tantrum will be a hypo and some days it will be because their sock feels funny.

All of those days count. All of those days, you showed up.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Blood sugar swings directly affect toddler behaviour**, and the symptoms of hypo and hyperglycaemia overlap heavily with normal developmental behaviour.
2. **Check glucose before responding to challenging behaviour** whenever possible — a quick CGM glance or finger prick can guide your response.
3. **The guilt cycle is common and understandable**, but it can be broken by checking first, forgiving yourself, and maintaining consistent boundaries.
4. **Not every meltdown is diabetes-related.** Toddlers have big emotions regardless of blood sugar. Normal parenting still applies.
5. **Track behaviour alongside glucose readings** to identify patterns, and seek psychological support if the emotional burden becomes overwhelming.
