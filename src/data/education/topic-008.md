# The First Night Home — A Parent's Survival Guide

## Hello from Mira

Dear parent,

You are home. Your child has been diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes, you have had some training at the hospital, you have supplies, and now — you are on your own. The front door has closed behind you, and the weight of what lies ahead may feel crushing.

I want to be honest with you: the first night home is one of the most frightening experiences parents describe in the entire diabetes journey. But I also want to tell you something equally true: **you will get through this.** Thousands of families have stood exactly where you are standing right now, felt exactly what you are feeling, and they made it to the other side. You will too.

---

## Why the first night feels so terrifying

In the hospital, there were nurses. There were monitors. There were people who knew what they were doing. Now it is just you, your child, a blood glucose meter, and a brain full of new information that you are not sure you have absorbed properly.

The fear is not irrational. You have just learned that your child has a condition that requires constant management, that blood glucose levels can drop dangerously low — including during sleep — and that you are now the primary person responsible for managing this. Of course you are afraid.

According to research aligned with ISPAD guidelines, parental fear of nocturnal hypoglycaemia (nighttime lows) is one of the most significant sources of anxiety for families living with Type 1 Diabetes. You are not overreacting. This fear is well-documented, well-understood, and shared by virtually every parent in your position.

---

## What to check and when

Your care team will have given you specific guidance for your child's overnight monitoring. What follows is general educational information — **always follow the plan your care team has provided.**

In the early days after diagnosis, many care teams recommend checking blood glucose at least once during the night — often around 2-3am, as this is when blood glucose is frequently at its lowest. Some teams may recommend additional checks depending on your child's age, insulin regimen, and individual risk factors.

**Before bed:**
- Check your child's blood glucose level.
- Compare it to the range your care team has given you for bedtime.
- Ensure your child has had their bedtime snack if one has been recommended.
- Make sure fast-acting glucose (juice box, glucose tablets) is easily accessible — by the bed, not in the kitchen cupboard.

**During the night (at the time your care team has recommended):**
- Check blood glucose.
- If the level is within the range you were given, breathe. Everything is fine.
- If it is below the target, follow the treatment your care team taught you. Treat with fast-acting glucose, wait, and recheck.
- If it is above the target, follow the guidance your care team has provided for high readings overnight.

**Record the numbers.** In the early days, writing down each reading with the time helps your care team make adjustments at your next appointment.

If your child is using a **Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM)**, you may have alarms set to alert you to highs and lows. Your care team will have helped you set these up. Even with a CGM, many parents choose to physically check during the first few nights for their own reassurance — and that is perfectly reasonable.

## Setting up your nighttime station

Many experienced diabetes parents recommend setting up a small "station" by your child's bed (or by yours) for overnight checks. This might include:

- Blood glucose meter and test strips
- Lancet device
- Fast-acting glucose (juice boxes, glucose tablets)
- A torch or low light (so you do not have to turn on bright lights)
- A notebook and pen for recording readings
- Your phone (with your care team's emergency contact number easily accessible)
- A glass of water for yourself

Having everything within arm's reach reduces fumbling in the dark and makes the process calmer for everyone.

## Tips for managing the anxiety

**Set an alarm.** Do not try to stay awake all night. You need sleep too, and exhaustion will make everything harder. Set your alarm for the check time your care team recommended, do the check, and go back to sleep.

**Accept that the first few nights will be hard.** Do not expect to sleep well. That is normal. It will improve as your confidence grows and as you begin to see that the numbers are manageable.

**Share the load.** If there are two parents or caregivers in the home, take turns doing the nighttime checks. Alternate nights. Your wellbeing matters — you cannot care for your child effectively if you are running on empty for weeks.

**Talk to someone.** The anxiety you are feeling tonight is real and valid. If you have a partner, talk about it. If you are a single parent, text a friend. Call a diabetes helpline. Post in an online support group at 3am — someone there will be awake, guaranteed, because they are doing a nighttime check too.

**Remember: you were trained for this.** The hospital team would not have sent you home if they did not believe you were capable. You have the skills. You have the supplies. You have the emergency numbers. You are more prepared than you feel.

## When to call your care team (or emergency services)

Call your care team or seek urgent medical help if:

- Your child's blood glucose is very low and not responding to treatment (still below target after two rounds of fast-acting glucose)
- Your child is difficult to wake, unusually drowsy, or seems confused
- Your child is vomiting and you cannot keep glucose levels stable
- You see signs of DKA: fruity breath, rapid or laboured breathing, nausea, tummy pain
- Your child has a seizure
- **You are unsure and something does not feel right.** Trust your instincts. It is always better to call and be reassured than to wait and worry.

Your care team expects calls in the early days. You are not bothering them. This is exactly what they are there for.

## The emotional reality of tonight

You might cry tonight. You might stand over your child's bed watching their chest rise and fall, unable to look away. You might feel rage at the unfairness of it all. You might feel numb. You might sit in the dark Googling things you know you should not be Googling.

All of this is normal. You are processing a major life change under the worst possible conditions — exhaustion, fear, and the crushing sense of responsibility.

But here is what I need you to hear: **this is the hardest night.** Not because the danger is highest (it is not — your child is on insulin now, and that is what they needed), but because your fear is highest. Every night after this one, you will know a little more, trust yourself a little more, and sleep a little better.

Some parents of children diagnosed years ago look back on the first night home and barely recognise the terrified person they were. That terrified person became an expert. You will too.

---

## A message for right now, at 2am

If you are reading this in the dark, phone in one hand, having just checked your child's blood glucose — I see you. What you are doing right now is an act of extraordinary love and dedication. Your child is lucky to have you, even though luck feels like a strange word when you are in the middle of this.

Check the number. Record it. Follow your care team's guidance if action is needed. Put the phone down. Close your eyes. And know that tomorrow, you will be one day stronger than you are tonight.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **The first night home is the hardest night** — not because the danger is greatest, but because your fear and uncertainty are at their peak. It gets easier from here.
2. **Follow your care team's specific guidance** for overnight blood glucose checks. Do not guess — follow the plan they gave you.
3. **Set up a nighttime station** with everything you need within arm's reach: meter, strips, fast-acting glucose, torch, and phone with emergency numbers.
4. **Set an alarm and sleep between checks.** Do not try to stay awake all night. Share the load with another caregiver if possible.
5. **Call your care team if you are unsure about anything.** They expect calls in the early days. You are never bothering them by asking.
