# Telling School About Your Child's Diagnosis

**Age Range:** 5-18 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 009
**Related Topics:** [topic-021](/education/topic-021), [topic-028](/education/topic-028), [topic-088](/education/topic-088)

---

## Hello from Mira

Dear parent,

One of the first questions many families face after a Type 1 Diabetes diagnosis is: "What do we tell the school?" It is a question loaded with worry — about your child's safety, their independence, whether staff will understand, and whether your child will be treated differently.

I want to help you navigate this conversation with confidence. Schools have a legal and moral obligation to support children with medical conditions, and with the right communication, most schools step up well. The key is knowing what to ask for, what to provide, and what your child's rights are.

---

## When to tell them

As soon as possible. Ideally, contact the school before your child returns after diagnosis, or very soon after if they are already attending. The sooner the school knows, the sooner they can put support in place.

Request a meeting — not just a phone call or email. This conversation deserves time, attention, and the presence of the right people. Ask for the **headteacher or principal, the school nurse (if there is one), your child's class teacher or form tutor, and any other relevant staff** (PE teachers, lunch supervisors, etc.).

---

## What the school needs to know

Schools need to understand the following. You do not have to be an expert — your care team can provide written information, and many diabetes organisations offer school information packs. But here is what to communicate:

**The basics of Type 1 Diabetes:**
- It is an autoimmune condition, not caused by diet or lifestyle.
- Your child's body cannot produce insulin.
- Insulin is given via injections or an insulin pump.
- Blood glucose must be monitored throughout the day.
- It is not contagious, and your child is not "sick" in the traditional sense.

**What hypoglycaemia (hypo) looks like and what to do:**
- This is the most urgent safety information. A hypo can happen at any time and requires immediate treatment.
- Staff must know the symptoms: shakiness, pallor, confusion, irritability, sweating.
- Staff must know where fast-acting glucose is stored and how to administer it.
- Staff must know **never** to leave a child experiencing a hypo alone, and **never** to send them to walk somewhere unaccompanied to get treatment.
- Staff should know when to call emergency services.

**What hyperglycaemia (hyper) looks like:**
- Thirst, frequent need for the toilet, tiredness, irritability.
- Your child must be allowed unrestricted access to water and the toilet.
- Staff should know when to contact you.

**Daily management needs:**
- Blood glucose checking (when, where, and who will supervise if your child is young)
- Insulin administration (if needed during school hours — who will give it, where, and how)
- Carb counting at lunchtime and for snacks
- The need for snacks outside of normal break times
- Adjustments for PE and physical activity
- What to do on school trips and during exams

## The Individual Healthcare Plan

In many countries, schools are required to have a written plan for children with medical conditions. In the UK, this is often called an **Individual Healthcare Plan (IHP)**. In the US, it may be a **504 Plan** or an **Individualized Education Program (IEP)** depending on the child's needs. In other countries, similar frameworks exist.

This document should include:

- Your child's name, age, and diagnosis
- Emergency contact details (yours and your care team's)
- A description of the condition and daily management needs
- Specific instructions for hypo and hyper management
- A list of trained staff members and their roles
- Arrangements for blood glucose monitoring and insulin administration
- Arrangements for school meals, snacks, and carb counting support
- Adjustments for PE, sport, and physical activity
- Plans for school trips, exams, and non-routine days
- Emergency procedures, including when to call an ambulance
- Review dates — the plan should be updated at least annually or whenever management changes

Your care team can help you draft or review this plan. Many diabetes specialist nurses are willing to attend school meetings or provide written guidance directly to the school.

## Legal rights

Children with Type 1 Diabetes have legal protections in most countries:

**In the UK:** Type 1 Diabetes is classified as a disability under the Equality Act 2010. Schools have a duty to make reasonable adjustments and to support pupils with medical conditions (as outlined in the Department for Education's statutory guidance, "Supporting pupils at school with medical conditions").

**In the US:** The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act protect students with diabetes. Schools must provide reasonable accommodations, which are documented in a 504 Plan.

**Internationally:** ISPAD guidelines emphasise that children with diabetes should have full access to education without discrimination, and that schools should provide appropriate support for diabetes management during school hours.

Your child has the right to:
- Manage their diabetes safely at school (or have adults do so if they are too young)
- Access food and water as needed
- Use the toilet without restriction
- Participate fully in all school activities, including PE, trips, and exams
- Not be excluded, penalised, or treated differently because of their condition

If the school is resistant to providing support, discuss the situation with your care team and, if necessary, seek advice from a diabetes advocacy organisation or legal advisor.

## The diabetes kit

Your child should have a diabetes kit at school that is:

- **Accessible at all times** — not locked in a cupboard that only one person has the key to
- **Clearly labelled** with your child's name
- **Stocked and regularly checked** — someone (you, the school nurse, a designated staff member) should be responsible for ensuring supplies do not run out or expire

The kit typically contains:
- Blood glucose meter and test strips
- Lancet device and spare lancets
- Fast-acting glucose (juice boxes, glucose tablets, or equivalent)
- Longer-acting snacks (biscuits, cereal bars)
- Ketone testing supplies (if recommended by your care team)
- Insulin and injection supplies (if your child takes insulin at school)
- Spare batteries for the meter
- Your contact details and your care team's contact details
- A copy of the healthcare plan

If your child uses an insulin pump or CGM, the school also needs basic instructions for those devices — at minimum, what the alarms mean and what to do.

## Training staff

Asking a school to support a child with Type 1 Diabetes is asking staff to take on a medical responsibility that most of them have no training for. They will need education and support — just as you did.

Many care teams offer school training sessions where a diabetes specialist nurse visits the school and trains relevant staff. This might cover:

- What Type 1 Diabetes is
- Recognising and treating hypos and hypers
- How to use a blood glucose meter
- How to support carb counting at lunch
- How to use a glucagon kit (if provided)
- What to do in an emergency

Ideally, **more than one staff member should be trained**. If the only trained person is absent, your child still needs support. NICE guidelines specifically recommend that schools identify several members of staff who are trained and willing to help.

---

## Helping your child feel supported, not singled out

One of the biggest worries children have about school knowing is that they will be treated differently — singled out, fussed over, or seen as fragile. Work with the school to find a balance:

- Your child should have a **private space** to check blood glucose or inject if they prefer, but should also have the option to do it in class if they are comfortable.
- Discuss with your child (if they are old enough) **how much they want their classmates to know** and who should explain it.
- Some children benefit from a brief, age-appropriate class explanation (with their permission). Others prefer to tell friends individually. Follow your child's lead.
- Ensure that support is provided **discreetly and respectfully** — no announcing blood glucose numbers across the classroom.

---

## When to call your care team

Discuss school arrangements with your care team if:
- You are unsure what the school needs to know or provide
- The school is resistant to creating a healthcare plan or providing training
- You want the diabetes specialist nurse to visit the school
- There is a change in your child's management that the school needs to be updated on
- Your child is experiencing difficulties at school related to their diabetes (bullying, exclusion, lack of support)

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Tell the school as soon as possible** and request an in-person meeting with key staff. The sooner they know, the sooner support is in place.
2. **A written healthcare plan is essential.** It should cover daily management, emergency procedures, and be reviewed regularly. Your care team can help you create it.
3. **Your child has legal rights** to manage their diabetes at school, access food and water, use the toilet, and participate fully in all activities without discrimination.
4. **Multiple staff members should be trained** in recognising hypos and hypers, using the blood glucose meter, and knowing what to do in an emergency.
5. **A well-stocked, accessible diabetes kit** must be at school at all times, clearly labelled and regularly checked.
