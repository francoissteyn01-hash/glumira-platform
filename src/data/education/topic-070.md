## Why Cooking Matters More Than You Think

Here is something Mira believes deeply: learning to cook is one of the most empowering things a teenager with Type 1 Diabetes can do. Not because you need to become a chef. Not because takeaways are forbidden. But because knowing how to prepare your own food gives you something priceless — control.

When you cook, you know exactly what went into your plate. You know the carbs because you measured the ingredients. You know the portion size because you served it yourself. You know whether there is hidden sugar in the sauce because you made the sauce. That knowledge translates directly into better glucose management, more confidence, and genuine independence.

Plus, cooking is a life skill that will serve you well whether or not you have T1D. Consider this your head start.

## Before You Start — Kitchen Safety

A few basics that matter for everyone, but especially for someone managing T1D:

- **Always have your hypo treatment within reach in the kitchen.** Glucose tablets or a juice box on the counter, not buried in your school bag. Cooking involves standing, moving, and sometimes waiting — if a low hits, you need fast glucose immediately.
- **Check your glucose before you start cooking.** If you are trending low, treat the low first. Cooking with a blood glucose of 3.5 mmol/L is not safe — you are handling heat, sharp objects, and making decisions.
- **Set timers.** Timers are your friend for both cooking and pre-bolusing (see Topic 64). If you take insulin before you start cooking, set a timer so you do not lose track of time and delay eating.
- **Wash your hands before checking glucose.** Food residue on fingers — especially sugary or starchy food — can give falsely high finger-prick readings.

## Carb Counting While You Cook

Cooking gives you an enormous advantage for carb counting: you control the ingredients. Here is how to use that advantage:

### Weigh Ingredients Before Cooking

The most accurate time to count carbs is when the ingredients are raw and separate. Weigh your rice before cooking. Weigh your pasta before boiling. Measure your flour before mixing.

**Key raw-to-cooked conversions:**
- Rice: roughly triples in weight when cooked (100g dry → ~300g cooked)
- Pasta: roughly doubles in weight when cooked (100g dry → ~200g cooked)
- Lentils: roughly double in weight when cooked

### Calculate the Total, Then Divide

If you are making a dish that serves multiple people, calculate the total carbs for the entire recipe, then divide by the number of servings.

**Example:** You make a pasta dish using 400g dry pasta (280g carb total), 500g tomato-based sauce (25g carb), and vegetables (10g carb). Total carbs for the whole dish: approximately 315g. If it serves 4 people equally, each serving is approximately 79g of carbohydrate.

### Keep a Recipe Carb Log

Write the carb count on your recipes. Next time you make that meal, the calculation is already done. A simple notebook, a note on your phone, or a label stuck to the recipe page all work perfectly.

## Five T1D-Friendly Recipes to Get You Started

These recipes are designed to be simple, delicious, and easy to carb count. Each one teaches a different cooking skill.

---

### Recipe 1: One-Pan Chicken and Vegetable Stir-Fry with Rice

**What you will learn:** Using a pan, cooking rice, and carb counting a simple meal.

**Ingredients (serves 2):**
- 150g dry basmati rice (~110g carb)
- 2 chicken breasts, sliced (~0g carb)
- 1 tablespoon oil (0g carb)
- 1 cup mixed vegetables — capsicum, broccoli, snap peas (~8g carb)
- 2 tablespoons soy sauce (~2g carb)
- 1 teaspoon garlic (minced) (0g carb)

**Total carbs for full recipe:** ~120g
**Per serving:** ~60g carb

**Method:**
1. Cook rice according to packet instructions.
2. Heat oil in a large pan over medium-high heat.
3. Cook chicken slices until golden and cooked through (about 6–7 minutes).
4. Add vegetables and garlic. Stir-fry for 3–4 minutes until tender-crisp.
5. Add soy sauce and stir through.
6. Serve over rice.

**T1D tip:** The basmati rice has a lower GI than jasmine rice (see Topic 63). The protein from the chicken and the fibre from the vegetables help moderate the glucose rise.

---

### Recipe 2: Loaded Omelette

**What you will learn:** Cracking eggs, using a non-stick pan, and making a low-carb high-protein meal.

**Ingredients (serves 1):**
- 3 eggs (~1g carb)
- 30g grated cheese (~0g carb)
- 1/4 cup diced vegetables — mushrooms, tomato, spinach (~3g carb)
- 1 slice whole wheat toast (~15g carb)
- Butter or oil for the pan (0g carb)

**Total carbs:** ~19g

**Method:**
1. Whisk eggs in a bowl with a pinch of salt and pepper.
2. Heat butter or oil in a non-stick pan over medium heat.
3. Pour in eggs. Let them set for about 1 minute.
4. Add vegetables and cheese to one half.
5. When the bottom is set but the top is still slightly wet, fold the omelette in half.
6. Cook for another minute. Slide onto a plate. Serve with toast.

**T1D tip:** This is a great low-carb option for days when your glucose is running high and you want a filling meal without a big carb load. The protein and fat from eggs and cheese provide sustained energy.

---

### Recipe 3: Quick Bean and Vegetable Wrap

**What you will learn:** Using tinned foods, assembling a balanced meal, and working with tortillas.

**Ingredients (serves 1):**
- 1 large flour tortilla (~35g carb)
- 1/2 cup tinned kidney beans, rinsed and drained (~18g carb)
- 1/4 avocado, sliced (~2g carb)
- Small handful of lettuce and tomato (~2g carb)
- 1 tablespoon salsa or hot sauce (~2g carb)
- Squeeze of lime juice (0g carb)

**Total carbs:** ~59g

**Method:**
1. Warm the tortilla in a dry pan for 30 seconds each side (or microwave for 15 seconds).
2. Warm the beans in a small pot or microwave. Lightly mash half of them with a fork.
3. Spread the bean mixture along the centre of the tortilla.
4. Add avocado, lettuce, tomato, and salsa.
5. Fold the bottom up, then roll the sides in to form a wrap.

**T1D tip:** Kidney beans are high in fibre (see Topic 68) and low GI (see Topic 63), which means this wrap produces a slower, more gradual glucose rise than a wrap filled with only refined carbs. The fibre and protein from the beans are doing real work here.

### Recipe 4: Simple Pasta with Tomato and Basil Sauce

**What you will learn:** Boiling pasta al dente, making a basic sauce, and portioning.

**Ingredients (serves 2):**
- 160g dry pasta (~112g carb)
- 1 tin (400g) chopped tomatoes (~16g carb)
- 1 tablespoon olive oil (0g carb)
- 2 cloves garlic, minced (0g carb)
- Handful of fresh basil (0g carb)
- Salt, pepper, pinch of sugar (1g carb)
- Grated parmesan for serving (~0g carb)

**Total carbs for full recipe:** ~129g
**Per serving:** ~65g carb

**Method:**
1. Cook pasta in salted boiling water until al dente (follow packet time, test 1 minute early).
2. While pasta cooks, heat oil in a pot. Add garlic and cook for 30 seconds.
3. Add tinned tomatoes, salt, pepper, and a small pinch of sugar.
4. Simmer for 10 minutes, stirring occasionally.
5. Drain pasta. Toss with sauce. Tear basil over the top. Serve with parmesan.

**T1D tip:** Al dente pasta has a lower GI than well-cooked, soft pasta. That extra bite is not just better texture — it is genuinely better for your glucose curve. Weigh the dry pasta before cooking for the most accurate carb count.

---

### Recipe 5: Overnight Oats (No Cooking Required)

**What you will learn:** Meal prep, measuring, and making a low-GI breakfast with zero morning effort.

**Ingredients (serves 1):**
- 40g rolled oats (~28g carb)
- 150ml milk (~7g carb)
- 1 tablespoon chia seeds (~1g carb)
- 1 tablespoon peanut butter (~2g carb)
- Small handful of berries (~5g carb)

**Total carbs:** ~43g

**Method:**
1. The night before, combine oats, milk, and chia seeds in a jar or container.
2. Stir well. Seal and refrigerate overnight.
3. In the morning, stir in peanut butter and top with berries.
4. Eat cold, straight from the jar.

**T1D tip:** Overnight oats are lower GI than cooked oats because the oats are not heat-processed. The chia seeds add fibre (Topic 68), and the peanut butter adds protein and fat (Topic 62), further slowing glucose absorption. This is an excellent Suhoor option during Ramadan — low GI, high fibre, and sustaining through fasting hours.

## Meal Prep — Your Future Self Will Thank You

Meal prep is simply cooking in larger batches so you have ready-made meals for the next few days. For a teen with T1D, it offers two huge advantages:

1. **Carbs are already counted.** You calculated once, portioned into containers, and labelled them. Tuesday's lunch requires zero maths.
2. **You are less likely to reach for high-GI convenience food** when a good meal is already waiting in the fridge.

**Start small:** Make a double batch of one recipe on Sunday. Portion it into two or three containers. Label each one with the carb count. That is three meals sorted.

## Cooking Within Dietary Frameworks

If your family observes Kosher dietary laws, cooking skills include understanding meat and dairy separation, which naturally shapes meal planning. If your family eats Halal, you are already familiar with sourcing appropriate ingredients. These frameworks are entirely compatible with T1D-friendly cooking — the carb-counting principles are universal regardless of dietary practice.

For teens interested in the Bernstein low-carb approach, the omelette recipe and stir-fry (with reduced rice) are good starting points. Discuss any significant dietary changes with both your family and your healthcare team.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cooking gives you the most accurate carb counting possible** — you control every ingredient, every measurement, and every portion.
2. **Weigh ingredients before cooking for the most reliable carb counts** — use raw weights and standard conversion factors for rice, pasta, and lentils.
3. **Five basic recipes — stir-fry, omelette, wrap, pasta, overnight oats — cover most meal situations** and teach foundational cooking skills.
4. **Meal prep eliminates daily carb-counting effort** — cook once, portion, label, and eat with confidence all week.
5. **Always keep hypo treatment in the kitchen and check your glucose before you start cooking** — safety comes first when you are handling heat and sharp tools.
