# Financial Burden of T1D — Resources and Assistance Programmes

**Topic ID:** 089
**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people living with T1D, parents, caregivers, and family members
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## It's not just a health condition. It's a financial condition.

No one tells you this at diagnosis. They tell you about insulin and carb counting and sick-day rules. But no one sits you down and says: "This is going to cost you. Every month. For the rest of your life. And some months, it will cost more than you can afford."

The financial burden of T1D is real, relentless, and profoundly unfair. It affects decisions that should have nothing to do with money — whether to use a CGM, how often to change a sensor, whether to ration test strips, whether to skip an appointment because of the parking fee. And for many families, it is the unspoken stress that sits beneath every other challenge.

If money is tight and you're managing T1D, you are not irresponsible. You are navigating a system that was not designed with you in mind. And there are resources that can help — though finding them often requires the kind of energy that T1D has already depleted.

This topic aims to make that search a little easier.

## The real cost of T1D

The costs of T1D extend far beyond insulin. A comprehensive picture includes:

**Direct medical costs:**
- Insulin (rapid-acting and long-acting)
- Blood glucose test strips and lancets
- Continuous glucose monitor (CGM) sensors and transmitters
- Insulin pump supplies — infusion sets, reservoirs, batteries
- Glucagon emergency kits
- Ketone testing strips
- Syringes or pen needles

**Technology costs:**
- CGM devices (initial purchase and ongoing sensors)
- Insulin pumps (purchase, warranty, and consumables)
- Smartphone or reader devices for CGM
- Apps and data management subscriptions

**Healthcare costs:**
- Endocrinologist appointments (consultation fees, co-pays, or private fees)
- Diabetes nurse educator visits
- Dietitian consultations
- Psychology or counselling sessions
- Blood tests (HbA1c, thyroid, coeliac screening, kidney function)
- Eye screening
- Podiatry

**Indirect costs:**
- Time off work for appointments (yours or your child's)
- Reduced working hours to accommodate caregiving
- Travel costs to specialist centres
- Childcare for siblings during hospital visits
- Emotional labour — the unpaid, unquantified cost of managing a chronic condition around the clock

Studies estimate the annual cost of T1D management ranges from several thousand to tens of thousands of dollars, pounds, or rands — depending on the country, insurance status, and level of technology use. For many families, this is the difference between managing well and barely managing.

## Insurance navigation — know what you're entitled to

If you have health insurance — whether private, employer-sponsored, or government-funded — understanding your cover is essential.

**Key questions to ask your insurer:**
- Is insulin covered? Which brands and formulations? Is there a preferred list (formulary)?
- Are CGM devices and sensors covered? What about specific brands?
- Is there a cap on monthly or annual diabetes supply costs?
- Are pump supplies covered separately from the pump itself?
- Do I need prior authorisation for specific devices or medications?
- Are psychological services covered when related to chronic illness?
- Is there a maximum out-of-pocket limit?

**Tips for navigating insurance:**
- **Appeal denied claims.** Many initial denials are overturned on appeal. Your diabetes team can provide supporting letters.
- **Request formulary exceptions.** If your prescribed insulin or device isn't on the formulary, your endocrinologist can submit a medical necessity letter.
- **Keep records.** Document every call, every claim, every denial. Dates, names, reference numbers. This paper trail is your protection.
- **Ask about patient assistance programmes** offered by your insurer — some have hardship provisions that are not widely advertised.

In countries with public healthcare systems (UK's NHS, South Africa's public sector, many EU nations), insulin and basic supplies are generally covered. However, access to technology — CGMs, pumps, hybrid closed-loop systems — varies enormously by region, postcode, and funding body. If you've been told "no" to technology access, ask why, ask what the criteria are, and ask how to meet them. Sometimes persistence is the difference.

## Charity programmes and assistance funds

Numerous organisations exist specifically to help people with T1D access the supplies and support they need.

**Global and multi-country:**
- **Life for a Child (International Diabetes Federation):** Provides insulin, supplies, and education to children with T1D in under-resourced countries.
- **Insulin for Life:** Collects and distributes unused, unexpired insulin and supplies to people in need globally.
- **T1International:** Advocates for insulin affordability and access worldwide. Their website includes country-specific resource lists.

**United Kingdom:**
- **NHS prescriptions** cover insulin and most supplies. If you're in England and paying prescription charges, apply for a **Medical Exemption Certificate** — people with diabetes are entitled to free prescriptions.
- **Diabetes UK** offers a helpline that can guide you through financial support options and technology access.
- **Turn2Us** and **Citizens Advice** can help identify benefits you may be entitled to, including **Disability Living Allowance (DLA)** or **Personal Independence Payment (PIP)** for T1D.

**United States:**
- **Manufacturer patient assistance programmes:** Eli Lilly (Lilly Cares), Novo Nordisk (NovoCare PAP), and Sanofi (Patient Connection) all offer programmes for uninsured or underinsured patients.
- **GetInsulin.org:** Connects people to emergency insulin and supplies.
- **Beyond Type 1:** Maintains an insurance and financial resources hub.
- **JDRF (Breakthrough T1D):** Offers financial resource guides and advocacy tools.
- **Rx Assist and NeedyMeds:** Databases of patient assistance programmes and discount coupons.
- **340B Drug Pricing Programme:** Federally Qualified Health Centres (FQHCs) can provide medications at significantly reduced prices.

**South Africa:**
- **Diabetes South Africa (DSA):** Offers support and can connect families with resources.
- **Sweet Life Diabetes Community:** Provides advocacy, education, and community support.
- Public sector patients can access insulin and basic supplies through government hospitals and clinics, though stock shortages and limited technology access remain significant challenges.
- **Discovery Health, Bonitas, GEMS**, and other medical aids have varying levels of chronic medication cover — check your plan's Prescribed Minimum Benefits (PMBs), which must cover T1D as a chronic condition.

**European Union:**
- Most EU countries provide insulin and supplies through public healthcare systems, though co-payment requirements vary.
- Contact your national diabetes association for country-specific financial assistance programmes.

## Generic vs branded — what you need to know

The conversation around generic and biosimilar insulin is evolving. Key points:

- **Biosimilar insulins** are available in many markets and are often significantly cheaper than originator brands. They have undergone rigorous regulatory approval and are considered clinically equivalent.
- Switching from a branded insulin to a biosimilar should **always** be done in consultation with your diabetes team — never unilaterally by a pharmacist or insurer without clinical review.
- For non-insulin supplies (test strips, lancets, pen needles), generic or store-brand options can offer substantial savings without compromising safety.
- Some pharmacies offer discount programmes or loyalty pricing for diabetes supplies — ask directly.

## Applying for financial support — practical steps

1. **Start with your diabetes team.** Nurses and social workers attached to diabetes clinics often know about local resources that aren't widely advertised.
2. **Contact your national diabetes organisation.** They maintain up-to-date resource lists and can often advocate on your behalf.
3. **Check your entitlements.** Disability benefits, tax credits, medical exemption certificates, and chronic illness provisions exist in many countries — but you often have to apply proactively.
4. **Document your costs.** Keeping a record of what you spend makes it easier to apply for assistance, claim tax deductions (where available), and demonstrate financial need.
5. **Don't ration supplies in silence.** If you are stretching sensors, reusing lancets beyond safety, or rationing insulin, tell your diabetes team. They need to know. They may be able to help. And if they can't, they can connect you with someone who can.

## You shouldn't have to fight for this

The fact that any person, anywhere, has to choose between insulin and rent is a systemic failure — not a personal one. If you are struggling financially because of T1D, the shame belongs to the system, not to you.

You deserve access to the medication and technology that keeps you alive. And while we work towards systems that provide that universally, the resources above can help bridge the gap.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **The financial burden of T1D is significant and ongoing** — encompassing insulin, technology, supplies, healthcare, and substantial indirect costs that are rarely acknowledged.
2. **Understanding your insurance cover and appealing denied claims can unlock access** — persistence and documentation are essential tools.
3. **Manufacturer assistance programmes, charities, and government benefits exist in most countries** — your diabetes team and national diabetes organisation can help you find them.
4. **Biosimilar insulins are clinically equivalent and often cheaper** — but any switch should be guided by your diabetes team.
5. **If you are rationing supplies or insulin, tell your healthcare team immediately** — they can connect you with resources, and no one should manage T1D in financial silence.
