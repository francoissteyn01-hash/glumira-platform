# Teen Mental Health and T1D — Depression, Anxiety, Eating Disorders

**Topic ID:** 47
**Age Range:** 13–18
**Audience:** All (teens with T1D, parents, carers)
**Category:** Mental Health & Wellbeing
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17 / NICE CG19

## The Part Nobody Talks About Enough

Here is a fact that should be said more often: having Type 1 Diabetes significantly increases your risk of depression, anxiety, and eating disorders. This is not a maybe. It is not a theory. It is a well-established finding backed by decades of research across multiple countries and populations.

Teens with T1D are two to three times more likely to experience depression than their peers without diabetes. Anxiety disorders are similarly elevated. And eating disorders — particularly a pattern called diabulimia — occur at rates that should alarm anyone paying attention.

If you are struggling with your mental health, you are not weak, you are not dramatic, and you are not alone. You are experiencing something that the disease itself makes more likely. Understanding this is the first step toward getting help.

## Why T1D Takes a Mental Health Toll

Managing Type 1 Diabetes is relentless. There is no day off. There is no holiday from carb counting, from checking your blood glucose, from thinking about whether that spike was the food or the stress or the infusion site or something else entirely. Every day, multiple times a day, you make decisions that directly affect your health. And the feedback is often frustrating — you can do everything "right" and still get readings that make no sense.

This constant cognitive load is called **diabetes distress**, and it is a recognised clinical concept, distinct from depression but often coexisting with it. Diabetes distress is the emotional burden of living with a demanding chronic condition. It can manifest as frustration, guilt, burnout, feeling overwhelmed, or simply wanting a break from something that never gives you one.

Add to this the normal challenges of being a teenager — identity formation, social pressure, academic stress, physical changes — and you have a recipe for mental health difficulties.

The hormonal chaos of puberty makes blood glucose harder to control, which increases frustration. Social situations draw attention to your difference. The desire to fit in can conflict with the need to manage your health openly. And the pervasive myth that diabetes is about willpower or discipline can make you feel like every out-of-range reading is a personal failure.

It is not. Your pancreas stopped working. That is not a character flaw.

## Depression and T1D

Depression in teens with T1D can look like:

- Persistent low mood that does not lift
- Loss of interest in things you used to enjoy
- Withdrawing from friends and family
- Changes in sleep — too much or too little
- Changes in appetite
- Difficulty concentrating
- Feelings of hopelessness, worthlessness, or guilt
- **Stopping or reducing diabetes self-care** — this is a critical warning sign. Skipping insulin, not checking BG, ignoring high readings, missing clinic appointments

That last point is vital. When someone with T1D starts disengaging from their diabetes management, it is often a sign of depression or burnout, not laziness. Missed insulin doses in a depressed teenager are a mental health emergency, not a compliance problem.

Depression also directly affects blood glucose through physiological mechanisms. It alters cortisol levels, disrupts sleep, reduces physical activity, and affects eating patterns — all of which worsen glycaemic control. Poor glycaemic control then increases feelings of failure and hopelessness, creating a vicious cycle.

## Anxiety and T1D

Living with a condition where low blood sugar can cause you to collapse and high blood sugar can lead to long-term complications provides fertile ground for anxiety. Common anxiety patterns in teens with T1D include:

- **Fear of hypoglycaemia:** Keeping BG deliberately high to avoid lows. Checking obsessively. Avoiding exercise, sleep, or situations where hypos have occurred before.
- **Fear of complications:** Fixating on the possibility of future eye disease, kidney problems, or other long-term complications, sometimes to the point of paralysing anxiety.
- **Social anxiety:** Fear of having a hypo in public, of being seen testing or injecting, of being judged as different.
- **Generalised anxiety:** A pervasive sense of worry and tension that extends beyond diabetes-specific concerns.

Some level of concern about your health is rational and adaptive — it keeps you checking your BG and taking your insulin. But when that concern becomes debilitating, when it prevents you from living your life, when it keeps you awake at night, it has crossed into territory that needs professional support.

## Diabulimia — The Eating Disorder You Need to Know About

Diabulimia is not an official diagnostic term, but it describes a very real and very dangerous pattern: deliberately restricting or omitting insulin to lose weight.

Here is the biology: without adequate insulin, your body cannot use glucose for energy, so it breaks down fat and muscle instead. The glucose is excreted through your urine. The result is weight loss — rapid, dramatic weight loss. And for a teenager in a culture that glorifies thinness, this can feel like a powerful tool.

It is not a tool. It is a medical emergency in slow motion.

Insulin restriction causes sustained hyperglycaemia and recurrent DKA. The short-term consequences include nausea, fatigue, dehydration, blurred vision, and cognitive impairment. The long-term consequences are devastating: accelerated retinopathy, neuropathy, nephropathy, and significantly increased mortality. Studies show that diabulimia triples the risk of death in young women with T1D.

Diabulimia is more common than most people realise. Research suggests that up to 30–40% of young women with T1D report some degree of insulin manipulation for weight control. It is less studied in young men, but it occurs there too.

Warning signs include:

- Unexplained weight loss
- Persistently high HbA1c despite apparent engagement with care
- Recurrent DKA episodes
- Secrecy around insulin use
- Preoccupation with body image and weight
- Avoidance of blood glucose checks or clinic appointments
- Finding unused insulin or discarded supplies

If you recognise any of this in yourself, please hear this clearly: **this is treatable and you deserve help.** Diabulimia thrives in secrecy and shame. Telling someone — your diabetes team, a parent, a counsellor, a trusted adult — is the hardest and most important step.

If you recognise this in someone you care about, do not ignore it. Raise it gently but directly. Offer to help them access support. Do not try to manage it alone.

## How to Get Help

**Talk to your diabetes team.** Modern diabetes care increasingly recognises the psychological dimension of T1D. Many diabetes clinics have psychologists or counsellors as part of the team. If yours does not, they can refer you. Tell them how you are feeling. You do not need to have a diagnosis — "I'm struggling" is enough to start the conversation.

**Talk to your GP.** If your diabetes team does not offer psychological support, your GP can refer you to mental health services. In the UK, you can also self-refer to NHS Talking Therapies (formerly IAPT).

**School or university counsellors.** Many schools and universities have counselling services that are free, confidential, and accessible without a GP referral.

**Crisis support.** If you are in crisis — if you are thinking about harming yourself, if you have stopped taking your insulin, if you feel you cannot keep yourself safe — contact emergency services or a crisis line immediately.

- UK: Samaritans — 116 123 (24/7); SHOUT — text 85258
- US: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988
- Australia: Lifeline — 13 11 14; Beyond Blue — 1300 22 4636

**Peer support.** Organisations like Diabetes UK, JDRF, Beyond Type 1, and others offer peer support groups, online communities, and events where you can connect with other young people who genuinely understand what living with T1D is like. This is not therapy, but feeling less alone is powerful.

## For Parents and Carers

If your teenager has T1D and is struggling with their mental health, the most important thing you can do is listen without judgement. Do not respond to disengagement from diabetes care with anger or punishment. Recognise that it may be a symptom of something deeper. Ask how they are feeling, not just what their last BG reading was. Help them access professional support. And take care of your own mental health too — caring for a child with a chronic condition is hard on you as well.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Teens with T1D are two to three times more likely to experience depression and anxiety** than their peers — this is a known consequence of living with a demanding chronic condition.
2. **Disengagement from diabetes self-care** (skipping insulin, not checking BG, missing appointments) is often a sign of depression or burnout, not laziness.
3. **Diabulimia — deliberate insulin restriction for weight loss — is dangerous and more common than most people realise**, particularly in young women with T1D. It requires specialist treatment.
4. **Help is available** through diabetes teams, GPs, school counsellors, crisis lines, and peer support organisations. You do not need a diagnosis to ask for help.
5. **Mental health and blood glucose control are directly connected** — looking after your mind is not separate from managing your diabetes. It is part of it.
