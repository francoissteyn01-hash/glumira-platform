# The Overnight Check Routine — How to Survive Without Losing Your Mind

**Age range:** 0-5 years
**Audience:** Parents and caregivers
**Topic ID:** 19
**Category:** Overnight management and parent wellbeing

---

It's 2am. Your alarm goes off. You lie there for a moment, trying to remember what day it is and why you set an alarm that isn't for work. Then you remember: blood sugar check.

You get up. You find the meter. You creep into your child's room trying not to wake them (or the dog, or the sibling, or the floorboard that creaks). You prick a tiny finger on a sleeping child who may or may not wake up screaming. You read the number. You make a decision. You go back to bed. You stare at the ceiling for 40 minutes wondering if the decision was right. Your alarm goes off again in two hours.

This is your life now. And it's relentless.

You're doing great. And this topic is less about the clinical details of overnight monitoring and more about how to survive it as a human being.

## Why Overnight Checks Matter

Let's start with the why, because understanding the reason helps when the alarm goes off and every cell in your body wants to ignore it.

Young children with Type 1 diabetes are at particular risk of overnight hypoglycaemia for several reasons:

- **They can't tell you they feel low.** An adult can wake up with hypo symptoms and eat something. A baby or toddler cannot.
- **Nocturnal hypoglycaemia can be prolonged.** Without intervention, a low that starts at midnight can persist for hours.
- **Counter-regulatory responses are immature.** Young children's bodies are less effective at self-correcting lows than adult bodies.
- **Activity and eating patterns are unpredictable.** A toddler who ran around all afternoon and barely ate dinner is at higher risk overnight.

ISPAD guidelines acknowledge the importance of overnight monitoring in young children with T1D, particularly those on multiple daily injection therapy and those with a history of nocturnal hypoglycaemia.

## How Often to Check

Your diabetes team will give you specific guidance tailored to your child. Common overnight check schedules include:

- **Once overnight** (typically between midnight and 3am)
- **Twice overnight** (for example, 11pm and 3am)
- **More frequently** during illness, after unusual activity, or when doses have recently changed

Some families find that a single check at 2-3am catches most overnight lows. Others need more frequent monitoring during certain periods. There is no universal schedule — it depends on your child's individual pattern, their insulin regimen, and whether they use a CGM.

## CGM: A Partial Solution

Continuous glucose monitors have transformed overnight management for many families. Instead of setting alarms for finger pricks, you can receive alerts when glucose goes below (or above) a threshold.

**The benefits are real:**
- Alerts for actual lows rather than scheduled checks regardless of glucose level
- Trend arrows show direction — is glucose falling, stable, or rising?
- Some systems share data to your phone, so you can check without entering the room
- Reduces (though rarely eliminates) the number of finger pricks needed

**But CGMs are not a complete solution:**
- **Alarm fatigue is real.** When the alarm goes off multiple times per night — sometimes for readings that self-correct — you start sleeping through them. Or you start wanting to throw the receiver through the window. Both are normal responses.
- **Compression lows.** If your child lies on the sensor, it can give falsely low readings, triggering alarms for lows that aren't real. This is maddening at 3am.
- **Signal loss.** If the receiver is out of range or the sensor has a gap, you're back to finger pricks.
- **Trust takes time.** Many parents, particularly those who've experienced a severe hypo, find it hard to trust the CGM and still wake to check manually. This is understandable, not irrational.

If you don't have a CGM and would like one, talk to your diabetes team about access. If you have one and it's causing more stress than it's relieving, that's also worth discussing.

## Alarm Fatigue — The Silent Problem

Alarm fatigue deserves its own section because it's dangerous and nobody talks about it enough.

When alarms go off repeatedly — night after night, sometimes multiple times per night — your brain starts tuning them out. This is a well-documented phenomenon in both healthcare and parenting contexts. The alarm becomes background noise, and you sleep through it.

This is not laziness. This is neurology. Your brain is designed to habituate to repeated stimuli. It's trying to protect you from sleep deprivation, and it doesn't understand that this particular alarm might be important.

**Strategies for managing alarm fatigue:**

- **Change your alarm tone regularly.** A novel sound is harder to sleep through than a familiar one.
- **Place the receiver or phone across the room** so you have to physically get up to silence it.
- **Adjust alarm thresholds with your team.** If you're getting 5 alarms a night for glucose levels that aren't dangerously low, the thresholds may need widening. Discuss what's clinically necessary versus what's creating unnecessary disruption.
- **Use vibration and sound together.** A phone vibrating on a hard surface plus an alarm sound is harder to ignore than either alone.
- **Acknowledge the problem.** If you're sleeping through alarms, tell your diabetes team. They need to know, and they can help you find a solution rather than judging you for it.

## Sharing the Burden

If you have a partner, sharing overnight checks is not optional — it's essential. This isn't about fairness (though it is fair). It's about safety. A perpetually exhausted parent makes worse medical decisions, drives less safely, and is at higher risk of depression and burnout.

**Models that work for different families:**

- **Alternate nights.** One person is "on" Monday, Wednesday, Friday. The other is "on" Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday. Alternate Sundays.
- **Split the night.** One person covers 10pm-2am, the other covers 2am-6am. Each person gets one unbroken block of sleep.
- **Weekday/weekend split.** If one parent works outside the home, the working parent may take weekends while the other covers weekdays. (This only works if both people actually get recovery sleep.)
- **Designated sleep nights.** One night per week, one parent sleeps in another room with earplugs and is completely off duty. The on-duty parent handles everything. This guarantees at least one night of proper sleep per week.

**For single parents:** This is harder. Substantially harder. If you're managing overnight checks alone every night, you are carrying an enormous load. Explore every support option available to you — family, friends, respite care, diabetes camp programmes, online communities. You cannot pour from an empty cup indefinitely.

## Self-Care Is Not a Luxury

You've probably heard this before and rolled your eyes. Fair enough. "Self-care" has been co-opted by bath bomb marketing to the point where it sounds trivial.

But here's the clinical reality: chronic sleep deprivation is associated with depression, anxiety, impaired immune function, increased risk of accidents, relationship breakdown, and poor decision-making. All of which directly affect your ability to manage your child's diabetes.

Taking care of yourself is not selfish. It is a medical necessity.

**Practical self-care for exhausted parents:**

- **Nap.** Whenever your child naps, consider napping too. The dishes will survive.
- **Say yes to help.** When someone offers, don't say "we're fine." Say "yes, can you come on Thursday?"
- **Protect one thing.** Maybe it's a 20-minute walk. Maybe it's a cup of tea in silence. Maybe it's a phone call with a friend. Protect one small thing each day that is just for you.
- **Talk to your GP.** If you're not sleeping even when you have the opportunity, if you're crying more than feels normal, if you're feeling hopeless — talk to your doctor. Parental mental health in the context of childhood chronic illness is a recognised and treatable concern.

## When Overnight Checks Might Reduce

As your child gets older and their diabetes management stabilises, overnight checks may become less frequent. Factors that can reduce the need include:

- Stable CGM data showing consistent overnight trends
- Insulin pump therapy with predictive low-glucose suspend or hybrid closed-loop technology
- Growing out of the very insulin-sensitive early years
- Better understanding of your child's individual patterns

This is a conversation to have with your diabetes team over time. The goal is always to balance safety with sustainability.

## A Word to the Parent Reading This at 3am

If you're reading this right now because you're awake for a check and can't fall back to sleep — you are seen. This is hard. It's harder than people who don't live it can understand.

You are not failing. You are showing up, in the dark, in the middle of the night, for a child who won't remember any of this but who is safer because of every single alarm you answered.

That matters. You matter.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Overnight checks are clinically important** for young children with T1D due to their risk of undetected nocturnal hypoglycaemia and immature counter-regulatory responses.
2. **CGMs can reduce but not eliminate overnight burden** — alarm fatigue and compression lows are real challenges that need proactive management.
3. **Share the overnight load** with a partner or support person wherever possible. Split schedules protect both the on-duty and off-duty parent.
4. **Alarm fatigue is a neurological response, not laziness.** Change alarm tones, adjust thresholds with your team, and be honest about the problem.
5. **Your wellbeing is not separate from your child's diabetes management** — it is part of it. Chronic sleep deprivation impairs the very skills you need to keep your child safe.
