# Fever and Infection — Why Glucose Goes Haywire When You're Sick

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 074

## Hello from Mira

You have probably noticed it already. Your child — or you — come down with a cold, a chest infection, a fever, or even a minor ear infection, and suddenly blood glucose numbers look like they have lost their mind. Readings that were steady yesterday are now soaring. The insulin that worked perfectly last week seems to have stopped working. Nothing makes sense.

I want to help you understand why this happens. Because when you understand the "why," the chaos becomes manageable. Illness does not break the rules of diabetes management — it changes the playing field. And once you can see the new field, you can navigate it.

---

## What happens inside the body during illness

When the body detects an infection — whether it is a virus, bacteria, or any inflammatory process — it activates its defence systems. Part of that defence involves releasing a group of hormones known as **counter-regulatory hormones**. These include:

- **Cortisol** — the body's primary stress hormone
- **Adrenaline (epinephrine)** — the "fight or flight" hormone
- **Glucagon** — signals the liver to release stored glucose
- **Growth hormone** — rises during physiological stress

These hormones are designed to flood the body with energy so it can fight the infection. They do this by telling the liver to dump glucose into the bloodstream and by making cells more resistant to insulin. In a body that produces its own insulin, the pancreas simply ramps up insulin production to compensate. In a body with Type 1 Diabetes, that compensation cannot happen — the pancreas cannot respond.

The result: **blood glucose rises, sometimes dramatically, even if the person is eating less than usual.**

This is one of the most confusing aspects of illness with T1D. A child who is lying on the sofa, barely eating, with a fever — and their BG is 18, 20, 22 mmol/L. It feels wrong. But it is the body's stress response doing exactly what it was designed to do, in a body that lacks the insulin to match it.

---

## Fever specifically amplifies the problem

Fever is the body's way of creating an inhospitable environment for pathogens. But raising body temperature is metabolically expensive. It requires enormous amounts of energy, which means even more glucose mobilisation and even more insulin resistance.

Research aligned with ISPAD guidelines shows that for every 1 degree Celsius of fever, insulin requirements may increase by approximately 10–20 percent in some individuals. A child with a temperature of 39 degrees Celsius may need significantly more insulin than their usual doses — and yet they may be eating far less.

This mismatch — increased insulin need but decreased food intake — is why sick-day management is one of the most important skills in T1D care.

## The double-edged sword of reduced appetite

When someone with T1D is sick, they often lose their appetite. This creates a dilemma:

- **If they eat less, you might expect BG to be lower.** But counter-regulatory hormones are pushing it up.
- **If insulin is reduced because they are eating less, ketones may start to rise** — because the body still needs insulin to prevent fat breakdown.
- **If they vomit or have diarrhoea**, the situation becomes the SUBJ-NAM-001 Scenario (see Topic 073), where the risk of both hypos and DKA escalates rapidly.

The answer is not to stop insulin. The body needs basal insulin even when not eating — often more of it, not less, during illness. However, dosing adjustments require guidance from your diabetes care team. Contact them early in any illness, not when things have already deteriorated.

## More frequent monitoring — the non-negotiable rule

During illness, standard monitoring schedules are not enough. ISPAD, ADA, and NICE all recommend increased frequency of both blood glucose and ketone monitoring during any illness.

### Blood glucose
- Check every **2–3 hours** during illness, including overnight if the person has a fever.
- If using a CGM, watch trend arrows closely. But be aware that **dehydration can affect sensor accuracy** — confirm unexpected readings with a finger-prick test.
- High glucose readings during illness do not necessarily mean something has gone wrong with your management. They are an expected consequence of the stress response. The key is recognising them and working with your care team to respond appropriately.

### Ketones
- Check **blood ketones** at least every 4 hours during illness, and more often if BG is persistently above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL).
- Ketones during illness are a red flag that insulin supply may not be meeting demand. Rising ketones must never be ignored — see Topic 077 for detailed ketone thresholds and Topic 072 for DKA warning signs.

### Fluid intake
- Track how much the person is drinking. Dehydration worsens hyperglycaemia and accelerates ketone production.
- Encourage frequent small sips. If BG is high, use sugar-free fluids. If BG is low or dropping, use fluids with sugar.

## Specific types of infection and what to expect

### Respiratory infections (colds, flu, chest infections)
- Typically cause significant hyperglycaemia due to strong cortisol and adrenaline release.
- Coughing and congestion can disrupt sleep, leading to additional stress hormone release and erratic overnight readings.

### Urinary tract infections
- Particularly common in girls and women with T1D. Persistent unexplained high BG can sometimes be the first sign of a UTI before other symptoms appear.
- If BG is running inexplicably high for more than 24 hours with no other explanation, consider the possibility of an infection and consult a doctor.

### Ear infections and tonsillitis
- Pain and fever drive the stress response. Reduced eating due to a sore throat compounds the management challenge.

### Gastroenteritis
- This is the highest-risk illness scenario for T1D. See Topic 073 (The Gastroenteritis Crisis Protocol) for detailed step-by-step guidance.

## When illness resolves — the rebound

As the infection clears and counter-regulatory hormones return to normal levels, insulin sensitivity improves — sometimes quite suddenly. This means the increased insulin that was appropriate during illness can become too much, leading to **hypoglycaemia during recovery**.

Watch for dropping BG levels in the 24–48 hours after fever breaks and appetite returns. This recovery period requires just as much vigilance as the illness itself.

---

## When to seek medical help

Contact your diabetes care team if:
- BG remains above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) for more than 4–6 hours despite following your sick-day plan
- Ketones rise above 1.5 mmol/L
- The person cannot keep fluids down for more than 2 hours
- Fever persists above 38.5 degrees Celsius for more than 48 hours
- You are unsure how to adjust management during illness

**Go to the emergency department if:**
- Ketones are above 3.0 mmol/L
- There is persistent vomiting with rising ketones
- The person is drowsy, confused, or breathing rapidly
- You cannot contact your diabetes team and the situation is worsening

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Illness raises blood glucose** even when eating less — counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, adrenaline, glucagon, growth hormone) flood the body with glucose and increase insulin resistance.
2. **Do not stop basal insulin during illness** — the body typically needs more insulin, not less, even with reduced food intake. Contact your diabetes team for guidance.
3. **Monitor blood glucose every 2–3 hours and ketones every 4 hours** during any illness — dehydration can affect CGM accuracy, so use finger-prick confirmation when needed.
4. **Hydration is critical** — dehydration worsens hyperglycaemia and accelerates ketone production; encourage small, frequent sips throughout the illness.
5. **Watch for hypoglycaemia during recovery** — as the infection clears, insulin sensitivity returns quickly, and doses that were right during illness may now be too much.
