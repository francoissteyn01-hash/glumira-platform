# Relationship With Your Endo — How to Be Heard in a 15-Minute Appointment

**Topic ID:** 087
**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people living with T1D, parents, and caregivers
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## You waited three months for this appointment. It lasted twelve minutes.

You had questions. You had concerns. You had a list in your head — things that had kept you awake, things you'd Googled at 2am, things your child had said that worried you. But the appointment started with numbers, moved through an HbA1c review, touched briefly on pump settings, and ended with "Any questions?" just as the endocrinologist was already reaching for the door.

You said, "No, I think we're good."

You were not good. You just ran out of time and courage in the same moment.

This experience is so common in T1D care that it has almost become accepted. But it shouldn't be. Your relationship with your endocrinologist — and your broader diabetes team — is one of the most important partnerships in your life. When it works well, it transforms your management and your confidence. When it doesn't, it can leave you feeling dismissed, judged, and alone.

The good news is that there are concrete, practical things you can do to make that partnership work better. Not because the burden should fall on you — in an ideal system, it wouldn't — but because in the reality of overstretched clinics and limited appointment times, self-advocacy is a survival skill.

## Why the relationship matters

Your endocrinologist is not just someone who adjusts your insulin. They are the gatekeeper to referrals, technology access, psychological support, and specialist input. The quality of your relationship with them directly affects:

- Whether you feel safe raising concerns
- Whether management changes are collaborative or dictated
- Whether your emotional wellbeing is addressed alongside your numbers
- Whether you leave the appointment feeling empowered or defeated

Research shows that people with T1D who feel heard by their healthcare team have better engagement with management, lower rates of burnout, and — importantly — are more likely to return for follow-up appointments. The relationship is clinical, but it is also deeply personal.

## Preparing for appointments — the work before the work

The most effective way to be heard in a short appointment is to arrive prepared. This doesn't mean you need a medical degree. It means organising your thoughts so that nothing important gets lost.

**1. Write your questions beforehand.**
Take a piece of paper or open a note on your phone. Over the week before your appointment, jot down every question, concern, or observation that comes to mind. Don't filter. Just capture.

Then, the night before, review the list. Circle the three most important items. These are your non-negotiables — the things you will raise even if the appointment is running late, even if you feel rushed, even if your voice shakes.

**2. Bring data — but curate it.**
Your diabetes team lives in data. Downloads, graphs, averages — this is the language they speak most fluently. If you can bring a CGM report, a logbook summary, or a pump download, do so. But don't just dump a month of data and hope they find the patterns. Highlight what you've noticed:

- "Tuesday nights consistently go low — I think it's related to football training."
- "Time in range has dropped since we changed the breakfast ratio."
- "There's been a pattern of highs after lunch at school."

This does two things: it shows your team that you're engaged and observant, and it directs the conversation towards what matters to you rather than what the algorithm flags first.

**3. Prepare a brief update.**
Before the appointment, mentally rehearse a 60-second summary of how things have been: "Overall management has been okay. We've had trouble with overnight lows on active days. I'm also worried about [specific concern]. And I'd like to discuss [specific topic]."

This frames the appointment from the start and signals to your clinician what you need from this visit.

**4. Bring someone with you if it helps.**
A partner, a parent, a friend — someone who can take notes, remember details, and provide moral support. It is remarkably easy to forget half of what was said the moment you leave the room. An extra set of ears is a practical tool, not a sign of weakness.

## During the appointment — advocating for yourself

**Ask for clarification.**
If your endocrinologist says something you don't understand, say so. "Can you explain that differently?" or "What does that mean in practical terms?" are not annoying questions. They are essential ones. You cannot act on advice you don't understand.

**Redirect if needed.**
If the conversation is spending too long on something that isn't your priority, it's okay to gently redirect: "That's helpful, thank you. I also really wanted to talk about [your concern] — can we spend a few minutes on that?"

**Name your emotional experience.**
If you're struggling — with burnout, anxiety, grief, motivation — say it. Many clinicians won't ask about mental health unless prompted. A simple "I'm finding things really hard right now" opens a door that your HbA1c cannot.

**Challenge respectfully.**
If a recommendation doesn't feel right — if it doesn't account for your daily reality, your child's school schedule, your work constraints — say so. "I understand the clinical rationale, but that doesn't work for our family because..." is a reasonable, respectful way to push back. Good clinicians welcome this. It leads to better, more realistic plans.

**Ask about what's available.**
Technology access, psychological support, dietitian reviews, peer support programmes — your team may have resources they don't routinely offer unless asked. "Is there anything else available that might help us?" is a question worth asking at every visit.

## When the relationship isn't working

Sometimes, despite your best efforts, the relationship with your endocrinologist is not right. Maybe they're dismissive. Maybe they lecture rather than listen. Maybe they use language that makes you feel judged — "non-compliant," "out of control," "you need to try harder."

If this is your experience, know this: **you are allowed to request a different clinician.** You are allowed to seek a second opinion. You are allowed to change clinics. Your relationship with your healthcare team should be collaborative, respectful, and safe. If it is not, the problem is not you.

In some healthcare systems, changing providers is straightforward. In others — particularly public systems with limited capacity — it may be more difficult. In those cases, consider:

- Requesting a different team member (a diabetes nurse educator or registrar may be more accessible and communicative)
- Writing a letter or email to the team outlining your concerns
- Bringing an advocate — a family member, a patient advocate, or a support worker — to your appointment
- Contacting your local diabetes organisation for guidance on navigating the system

You deserve a healthcare partnership that works. Settling for one that doesn't is not an option — your life depends on this relationship functioning well.

## Advocating for your child

If you are a parent attending appointments for your child, everything above applies, with an additional layer: you are also modelling self-advocacy for your child. As they grow, they will need to navigate these appointments independently. Showing them that it's okay to ask questions, to push back, and to expect respect is one of the most valuable things you can teach them.

As your child enters adolescence, begin including them in the conversation. Let them ask their own questions. Let them describe their own experience. Gradually shift from speaking for them to speaking alongside them to stepping back and letting them lead.

This transition takes years. It requires patience, trust, and the willingness to let go — which, for a parent who has been managing every detail, is its own kind of grief. But it is essential. Your child's long-term relationship with healthcare is being shaped in these rooms, in these moments.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Preparation is the single most effective tool for a productive appointment** — write your questions beforehand, bring curated data, and rehearse a brief summary of how things have been.
2. **Three prioritised concerns ensure your most important issues are addressed** — even if the appointment is short or rushed.
3. **Naming your emotional experience opens doors** — clinicians often won't ask about mental health unless you raise it. A simple "I'm struggling" is enough.
4. **You are allowed to change clinicians if the relationship is not working** — a collaborative, respectful partnership is not a luxury, it is a clinical necessity.
5. **For parents, modelling self-advocacy teaches your child to navigate healthcare independently** — gradually include them in appointments and let them lead as they grow.
