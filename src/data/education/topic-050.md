# Transitioning from Paediatric to Adult Diabetes Care

**Topic ID:** 50
**Age Range:** 16–18
**Audience:** All (teens with T1D, parents, carers)
**Category:** Healthcare Navigation
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17 / NICE NG18

---

## The Handover Nobody Prepares You For

At some point between the ages of 16 and 25 — depending on where you live and how your local health system works — you will be transferred from paediatric (children's) diabetes services to adult diabetes services. This transition is one of the most significant changes in your diabetes care, and it happens at a time when you are also dealing with exams, leaving home, starting university or work, and generally navigating the chaos of becoming an adult.

Here is the problem: this transition is consistently identified in research as a high-risk period for people with T1D. HbA1c levels often deteriorate. Clinic attendance drops. Some young people fall off the radar entirely, sometimes for years. The reasons are not mysterious — the change is disorienting, the adult system works differently, and no one always explains what to expect.

This topic explains what actually changes, why it matters, and how to make the transition work for you rather than against you.

## What Changes — And Why It Feels Different

Paediatric diabetes care is typically designed around you and your family. Your parents are involved. Your team knows you — often they have known you since diagnosis. Appointments may be more frequent. There is usually a dedicated children's diabetes nurse you can call. The environment is familiar, sometimes literally — you have been going to the same clinic for years.

Adult diabetes services operate differently. Not worse — differently. Here is what to expect:

**Less hand-holding.** Adult services assume you are the primary manager of your diabetes. They will give you information, answer your questions, adjust your treatment plan, and support you — but they will not chase you if you miss appointments or remind you to order your prescriptions. The responsibility shifts firmly onto you.

**Less frequent appointments.** Paediatric teams often see you every 2–3 months. Adult services may offer appointments every 3–6 months, or less frequently if your management is stable. This is not neglect — it is how adult healthcare works. But it means you need to be proactive about booking appointments and reaching out if things are not going well.

**Different faces.** You will have a new consultant, a new diabetes specialist nurse, possibly a new dietitian and psychologist. You will need to build new relationships and re-explain your history. This can feel exhausting and impersonal after years with a team who knew you.

**Different environment.** Adult clinics may be in a general hospital setting rather than a children's hospital. You may be in a waiting room with older adults who have Type 2 Diabetes, which can feel alienating. The tone may be more clinical, less warm. This is a common complaint, and it is valid.

**Your parents step back.** In paediatric care, your parents or carers were often part of the conversation. In adult care, you are the patient. Your parents can still attend appointments if you want them to, but the team will primarily address you. For some teens, this is liberating. For others, it is terrifying. Both reactions are normal.

## Why This Transition Period Is Risky

The data is clear: the transition from paediatric to adult care is associated with worsening glycaemic control, increased rates of DKA, and higher rates of disengagement from services. Several factors drive this:

**Loss of continuity.** The relationship with your paediatric team provided a safety net. Losing it, especially if the transition is abrupt, removes that net.

**Competing priorities.** The transition typically coincides with exams, leaving home, starting university or work, and the general upheaval of late adolescence. Diabetes management can slide down the priority list.

**System gaps.** In some areas, there is a genuine gap between paediatric and adult services. You are discharged from one before you are fully established in the other. Prescriptions may lapse. Referrals may not arrive. Supplies may run out.

**Emotional factors.** Leaving a team you have known for years can feel like a loss. Starting with a new team feels impersonal. Some young people respond by simply not engaging.

Understanding these risks is not about scaring you. It is about recognising that this is a known vulnerability, so you can plan around it.

## Self-Advocacy — The Skill That Changes Everything

The single most important skill you can develop before, during, and after this transition is **self-advocacy**: the ability to speak up for your own needs, ask questions, challenge decisions you do not agree with, and navigate the healthcare system on your own terms.

In paediatric care, your parents often did this for you. In adult care, you need to do it yourself. Here is what that looks like in practice:

**Know your own diabetes.** By the time you transition, you should be able to answer: What type of insulin do I use? What are my doses? What is my most recent HbA1c? Do I have any complications? What does my typical day look like in terms of BG management? If you cannot answer these questions, start learning now — your paediatric team can help you build this knowledge before you leave.

**Ask questions.** If your new team suggests a change you do not understand, ask why. If a treatment plan does not make sense to you, say so. "Can you explain why?" is not confrontational — it is engaged.

**Challenge respectfully but firmly.** If a healthcare professional makes assumptions, gives advice that does not fit your life, or does not listen to your experience, you have every right to push back. You are the expert on your own daily management. They are the expert on clinical diabetes care. Good care happens when both perspectives are heard.

**Book your own appointments.** Before the transition, start taking ownership of the administrative side: booking appointments, ordering prescriptions, chasing referrals. These tasks are boring but essential, and in adult services, nobody will do them for you.

**Keep records.** Have your own copy of your medical summary, your current prescriptions, your HbA1c history, and any letters from your care team. A simple folder — digital or physical — means you are never starting from scratch with a new provider.

## Building Your Own Care Team

In adult services, your diabetes team typically includes:

- **Consultant endocrinologist or diabetologist:** The doctor who oversees your care and makes major treatment decisions.
- **Diabetes specialist nurse (DSN):** Often your main day-to-day contact. They can adjust insulin, troubleshoot problems, and provide ongoing support. Learn their name and contact details. Use them.
- **Dietitian:** Can help with carb counting, meal planning, and nutritional questions.
- **Psychologist:** Available in many (though not all) adult diabetes services. If mental health support is available, use it — there is no threshold of distress required.
- **GP:** Your general practitioner handles your prescriptions and can be a useful first contact for non-urgent issues. Make sure your GP knows your diabetes history.

You may also benefit from peer support — organisations like Diabetes UK, JDRF, and Beyond Type 1 run events and communities for young adults with T1D. Connecting with people who are going through the same transition can be remarkably helpful.

## How to Prepare — Starting Now

The best transitions are gradual. If you are currently in paediatric care, you can start preparing well before the actual transfer:

**Start attending appointments independently.** Go in without your parents for at least part of the appointment. Get used to being the person who talks to the team.

**Take over your own prescription management.** Know what you are on, how to order repeats, and how to check that everything has been dispensed correctly.

**Ask your paediatric team to prepare a transfer summary.** This should include your diagnosis date, current regimen, HbA1c history, any complications or co-morbidities, and key aspects of your management. You should have a copy.

**Visit the adult service before you transfer, if possible.** Some areas offer joint clinics or introductory appointments where you can meet the adult team while still under paediatric care. If this is available, take it.

**Know who to contact after the transfer.** Get the direct contact details for the adult diabetes team before you leave paediatric services. Know the number for advice, the number for emergencies, and how to book appointments.

## For Parents and Carers

Your role changes during this transition, but it does not disappear. Moving from manager to supporter is hard. You may need to step back from appointments, let your teenager make their own decisions (including imperfect ones), and resist the urge to take over when things get difficult.

The most helpful thing you can do is gradually transfer responsibility in the years before the transition, rather than handing everything over at once. Let them order their own prescriptions at 15. Let them attend part of their appointment alone at 16. Let them manage their own sick days at 17. By the time the formal transition happens, the practical skills are already in place.

And if you are worried about your teenager's readiness, raise it with the paediatric team. Transitions can be delayed if clinically appropriate.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Adult diabetes services operate differently from paediatric services** — less frequent appointments, more personal responsibility, and new clinical relationships. Understanding this in advance reduces the shock.
2. **The transition period is a known high-risk time** for deteriorating BG control and disengagement from services. Awareness of this risk is the first step to mitigating it.
3. **Self-advocacy is the most important skill you can develop** — knowing your own diabetes, asking questions, booking your own appointments, and speaking up for your needs.
4. **Start preparing before you transition:** attend appointments independently, manage your own prescriptions, and get a comprehensive transfer summary from your paediatric team.
5. **Know your new team's contact details before you leave the old one** — the gap between services is where people get lost.
