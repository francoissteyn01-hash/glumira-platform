# Field Trips and School Camps — Extended Absence Planning

**Topic ID:** 029
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 6 minutes

---

Hello, it's Mira. Field trips and school camps are the adventures your child will remember for years — the overnight stay in a nature reserve, the museum visit, the outdoor education week. These experiences build independence, resilience, and friendships, and your child with Type 1 diabetes absolutely deserves to be part of every single one. But extended time away from home, often with staff who are less familiar with diabetes management, requires careful planning. Let's walk through how to prepare so your child can focus on the adventure, not the diabetes.

## The Principle: Plan for the Worst, Expect the Best

Most field trips and camps go smoothly. Blood glucose might run a little higher from excitement or a little lower from extra activity, but serious problems are rare when preparation is solid. Your job is to make sure that if something does go wrong — a severe hypo, a pump failure, a lost supply bag — the staff have everything they need to handle it without you being physically present.

ISPAD guidelines are clear: children with T1D should not be excluded from school excursions. The school has a responsibility to provide adequate support, and your role is to make that support as easy as possible.

## Step 1 — Meet with the Trip Organisers Early

As soon as you receive the permission slip, contact the trip organiser (usually the class teacher or a senior staff member) and request a planning meeting. Do this weeks before the trip, not the night before.

At the meeting, cover:

### Logistics

- **Duration:** Is this a half-day trip, a full-day excursion, or a multi-night camp?
- **Location:** How far from medical facilities? Is there mobile phone reception? Is there a first-aid station?
- **Activities:** What will the children be doing? High-energy outdoor activities (hiking, kayaking, obstacle courses) require different planning than a museum visit.
- **Meals:** Will food be provided, or does your child need packed lunches? If food is provided, can you get the menu in advance for carb counting?
- **Sleeping arrangements (for camps):** Where will your child sleep? Is there an adult nearby overnight? Where will the diabetes supplies be stored?

### Staffing

- **Who will be the designated diabetes-aware adult?** This person should have received basic diabetes training (see Topic 28) and should carry your child's emergency kit.
- **Is there a backup?** What happens if the designated person is ill on the day?
- **Can the designated adult contact you at all times?** Confirm that mobile phone coverage exists at the site, or establish an alternative communication plan.

## Step 2 — Prepare the Extended Supplies Kit

For a multi-day camp, you need to pack significantly more than the daily school kit. The rule of thumb: pack for double the expected duration, plus emergency extras.

### Monitoring

- Blood glucose meter, plus a spare meter
- Test strips — at least 10 per day of the trip, plus 20 extra
- Lancing device and lancets
- Spare CGM sensors (at least 1 extra beyond what is expected to last)
- CGM reader or receiver (with charger if rechargeable)
- Ketone testing strips

### Hypo Treatment

- Fast-acting glucose: juice boxes, glucose tablets, or glucose gel — at least 6 servings per day of the trip
- Follow-up snacks: muesli bars, crackers, small sandwiches — at least 4 per day
- Glucagon emergency kit (check expiry date before packing)

### Insulin and Delivery

- Insulin pens with cartridges or pre-filled pens — enough for the trip plus one spare of each type (if on MDI)
- Pen needles — at least 6 per day plus extras
- If on insulin pump: spare infusion sets (at least 2 extra), spare reservoirs (at least 2 extra), pump batteries, insertion device
- A backup insulin pen and needles even if your child is on a pump — pumps can fail
- Insulated pouch or bag for insulin storage (insulin degrades in heat and freezing temperatures)

### Paperwork

- A copy of the Individual Healthcare Plan / care plan
- A one-page emergency instruction sheet (as described in Topic 25)
- Emergency contact numbers: yours, your partner's, your diabetes team's, the nearest hospital with paediatric services
- A letter from your child's diabetes team confirming the diagnosis and the supplies your child carries (useful if travelling by coach or if supplies are questioned)

### Extras for Multi-Day Camps

- Spare batteries for everything
- Waterproof bags for supplies (camp environments can be wet and muddy)
- A written daily schedule: "Check glucose before breakfast, before lunch, before dinner, before bed. If doing intense activity, check before and every 30–45 minutes during."
- A list of your child's usual meals with carb counts, to help staff estimate unfamiliar camp food

Label every single item with your child's name. Use a permanent marker on supply bags and a luggage tag on the main kit.

## Step 3 — Staff Training

If the designated diabetes-aware adult has not already received training (see Topic 28), arrange a training session before the trip. This does not need to be long — 30 to 45 minutes is usually sufficient.

The training should cover:

1. **Hypo recognition and treatment:** Signs to watch for, how to give fast-acting glucose, when to use glucagon, when to call an ambulance.
2. **Hyper recognition:** Signs of high blood glucose, when to check ketones, when to call you.
3. **Exercise adjustments:** The child may need extra carbs before and during physical activities. Provide a simple written guide: "Before hiking, check glucose. If below 7 mmol/L (126 mg/dL), give a snack of 15–20 g carbohydrate."
4. **Meal management:** How to support the child with carb counting using the pre-prepared meal list. Staff do not need to be expert carb counters — they just need to facilitate what the child (and your written guide) tells them.
5. **Overnight monitoring:** For camps, discuss how overnight glucose will be managed. If your child wears a CGM with sharing, you can monitor remotely and text the designated adult if action is needed. If CGM sharing is not possible, agree on a schedule for overnight checks (e.g., check at 10 p.m. and 2 a.m.).

## Step 4 — Prepare Your Child

Talk to your child about the trip in a positive, empowering way:

- **"You are going to have an amazing time."** Lead with excitement, not anxiety.
- **"Mr. Smith knows about your diabetes and has all your supplies."** Reassure them that an adult is prepared.
- **"Check your CGM before meals and before any big activity, and tell Mr. Smith the number."** Give them a simple routine.
- **"If you feel low, tell an adult immediately — even if you are in the middle of something fun."** Reinforce this message.
- **"I will have my phone on. You can call me any time."**

For children aged 8–10, camp can be a wonderful step toward diabetes independence. They may check their own glucose, count their own carbs, and take ownership of their routine in a way they do not do at home. Encourage this, while ensuring adult oversight is in place.

## Step 5 — Communication During the Trip

Agree with the school on a communication plan:

- **Daily check-in:** A text or call at a set time each day, with a quick glucose update. Keep it brief and low-stress: "All good today, glucose between 6–12, had a great time kayaking."
- **Immediate contact for:** Severe hypo requiring glucagon, persistent high glucose with ketones, any illness, pump failure with no backup, or any situation where the designated adult is uncertain.
- **CGM sharing:** If your system supports it, set up sharing so you can see your child's glucose remotely. Set alarms judiciously — you do not want to call the camp leader at 3 a.m. over a brief glucose dip that self-corrects.

## What If the School Resists?

Occasionally, schools may suggest that your child should not attend a camp or field trip because of their diabetes, or they may ask you to attend as a chaperone. While offering to chaperone is a personal choice, it should not be a requirement.

In the UK, the Children and Families Act 2014 and DfE statutory guidance require schools to ensure children with medical conditions can access all educational activities, including trips. In the US, Section 504 provides the same protection. In South Africa, the right to education under the Constitution supports your child's inclusion.

If the school is reluctant, involve your child's diabetes team. A letter from a consultant or DSN stating that the child is fit to attend with appropriate support often resolves the issue.

## When Your Child Returns

Expect some glucose variability for a day or two after camp. Disrupted routines, different foods, physical exhaustion, and the emotional comedown from an exciting experience can all affect blood glucose. Be patient, check more frequently, and return to your normal routine gently.

Most importantly, celebrate the adventure. Ask about the campfire, the funny thing that happened at breakfast, the friend they made. The diabetes was just one small part of the story.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Meet with trip organisers weeks in advance** to discuss logistics, staffing, meals, activities, and communication plans.
2. **Pack supplies for double the expected trip duration** plus emergency extras, including a backup insulin pen even if your child uses a pump.
3. **Ensure the designated diabetes-aware staff member has received training** in hypo treatment, glucagon use, exercise adjustments, and overnight monitoring.
4. **Set up a daily communication plan and CGM sharing** so you can support the staff remotely without needing to be physically present.
5. **Your child has a legal right to attend** field trips and school camps — schools cannot exclude a child because of T1D, and your diabetes team can provide supporting documentation if needed.
