# Severe Hypoglycaemia — What It Looks Like and What to Do

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, teachers, friends

## Hello from Mira

This is one of the most important topics I will ever share with you. Severe hypoglycaemia — a dangerously low blood glucose level — is a medical emergency. It can happen to anyone living with Type 1 Diabetes, at any age, at any time. It can happen even when you are doing everything right.

I am not telling you this to frighten you. I am telling you because knowing what severe hypoglycaemia looks like, and knowing exactly what to do, can save a life. That is not an exaggeration. This is the kind of knowledge that turns a terrifying moment into one you can manage.

Let us walk through it together — clearly, calmly, and step by step.

---

## What is severe hypoglycaemia?

Blood glucose (BG) normally sits between approximately 4.0 and 7.0 mmol/L (72–126 mg/dL) before meals. A mild hypo is generally defined as a BG below 4.0 mmol/L (72 mg/dL). You may feel shaky, sweaty, or irritable, but you can treat it yourself with fast-acting glucose.

**Severe hypoglycaemia** is different. It is defined by the International Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Diabetes (ISPAD) and the American Diabetes Association (ADA) as any episode where the person is **unable to treat themselves** and requires assistance from another person. The blood glucose level is often below 3.0 mmol/L (54 mg/dL), and sometimes far lower.

The key distinction is not just a number — it is the loss of the ability to self-rescue.

---

## How symptoms progress

Hypoglycaemia does not always announce itself loudly. Sometimes it creeps. Understanding the stages can help you act before a mild hypo becomes a severe one.

### Stage 1 — Mild (BG approximately 3.5–3.9 mmol/L / 63–70 mg/dL)
- Shakiness, trembling hands
- Sweating (often cold and clammy)
- Hunger — sudden, intense
- Tingling around the lips or fingers
- Heart pounding or racing

### Stage 2 — Moderate (BG approximately 2.8–3.4 mmol/L / 50–62 mg/dL)
- Confusion, difficulty concentrating
- Slurred speech — words come out wrong
- Clumsiness, poor coordination
- Behaviour changes — irritability, tearfulness, aggression (especially in children)
- Blurred or double vision
- Feeling "far away" or disconnected

### Stage 3 — Severe (BG often below 2.8 mmol/L / 50 mg/dL)
- Inability to eat or drink safely
- Loss of consciousness
- Seizures (convulsions)
- Extreme pallor (very pale skin)
- Unresponsiveness

In young children, the signs may be harder to spot. A toddler cannot tell you they feel shaky. Watch for sudden silence in a normally active child, glassy eyes, unusual clinginess, limpness, or refusal to eat. In teenagers, severe hypos can be mistaken for intoxication — confusion, slurred speech, and unsteadiness may look like alcohol or drug use to people who do not know.

**Important:** Some people experience **impaired hypoglycaemia awareness**, meaning they lose the early warning signs over time. They may skip straight from feeling normal to Stage 2 or Stage 3 with little warning. This is more common in people who have had T1D for many years. If this applies to you or your child, discuss it with your diabetes team — strategies exist to help restore awareness.

## What to do — step-by-step emergency response

### If the person is conscious and can swallow safely

1. Give **fast-acting glucose** immediately — glucose tablets, glucose gel, or a sugary drink (not diet).
2. Stay with them. Do not leave them alone.
3. Recheck BG after 10–15 minutes. If still below 4.0 mmol/L (72 mg/dL), repeat the fast-acting glucose.
4. Once BG is above 4.0 mmol/L, give a longer-acting carbohydrate snack to sustain levels.

### If the person is unconscious, semi-conscious, seizing, or CANNOT swallow safely

**DO NOT attempt to put food, drink, or glucose gel into their mouth.** There is a serious risk of choking or aspiration.

1. **Call emergency services immediately** (ambulance — see thresholds below).
2. If you have **glucagon**, administer it now:
   - **Injectable glucagon:** Follow the instructions in the kit. Inject into the thigh or upper arm.
   - **Nasal glucagon:** Place the device in one nostril and press the plunger. The person does not need to inhale.
   - Glucagon works by telling the liver to release stored glucose. It typically raises BG within 10–15 minutes.
3. Place the person in the **recovery position** (on their side, with the upper leg bent forward for stability and the head tilted slightly to keep the airway open). This prevents choking if they vomit.
4. Do NOT restrain them if they are having a seizure. Move objects away to prevent injury. Protect their head if possible.
5. Stay with them and keep talking calmly, even if they seem unresponsive.
6. When they regain consciousness, give small sips of a sugary drink, then a carbohydrate snack once they can swallow safely.

## When to call an ambulance

Call emergency services (ambulance) **immediately** if ANY of the following apply:

- The person is **unconscious** and you do NOT have glucagon available
- The person has had a **seizure**
- The person **does not respond** to glucagon within 15 minutes
- The person **vomits after glucagon** and cannot keep glucose down
- The person **remains confused or drowsy** for more than 20 minutes after treatment
- You are **unsure** whether they can swallow safely
- You are **alone with a child** and feel unable to manage the situation
- This is the person's **first severe hypo** and you do not know their history

**Do not hesitate.** Paramedics would always rather attend a hypo that resolves than arrive too late for one that does not. You will never be criticised for calling.

## After the episode — recovery

Severe hypoglycaemia is exhausting. After an episode, the person may feel drained, emotional, nauseous, and headachy for hours — sometimes for the rest of the day. This is normal.

- Ensure they eat a proper meal with carbohydrate and protein when they are ready.
- Monitor BG closely for the next 12–24 hours. Recurrent hypos can follow a severe episode because the liver's glucose stores have been depleted.
- Record what happened — time, suspected cause, treatment given, response time. Share this with the diabetes care team.
- Talk about it. Severe hypos are frightening for everyone involved. It is okay to feel shaken.

---

## Prevention — reducing the risk

- Check BG regularly, especially before driving, before sleep, and during illness.
- Always carry fast-acting glucose. Every pocket, every bag, every bedside table.
- Ensure glucagon is available, in date, and that at least two people know how to use it.
- If hypo awareness is reduced, speak with your care team about adjusting targets or using continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) with low-glucose alarms.
- Educate the people around you — family, friends, teachers, colleagues. A two-minute explanation today could save a life tomorrow.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **Severe hypoglycaemia means the person cannot help themselves** — they need someone else to act.
2. **Never put food or liquid into the mouth of someone who is unconscious or seizing** — use glucagon and call for help.
3. **Glucagon is the emergency treatment** — know where it is, check the expiry date, and make sure more than one person knows how to use it.
4. **Place an unconscious person in the recovery position** to protect their airway.
5. **Call an ambulance without hesitation** if there is no glucagon, if glucagon does not work within 15 minutes, or if a seizure occurs.
