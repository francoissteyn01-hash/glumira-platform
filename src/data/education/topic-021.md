# Packing the School Diabetes Kit — The Complete Checklist

**Topic ID:** 021
**Age Range:** 5–10 years
**Audience:** Parent / Caregiver
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

Hello there, it's Mira. Let's talk about one of those things that sounds simple but makes an enormous difference to your child's school day — the diabetes kit. A well-packed, well-maintained kit means your child's teachers and school staff can respond quickly and confidently to whatever the day throws at them. And it means you can breathe a little easier when the school bell rings.

## Why a Dedicated School Kit Matters

Many families start by sending supplies back and forth each day, but this is where things get forgotten, run out, or expire without anyone noticing. The gold standard — recommended by ISPAD (International Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Diabetes) and supported by ADA and NICE guidelines — is to keep a dedicated, clearly labelled diabetes kit at school at all times, separate from what your child carries at home.

Think of it as a small emergency department that lives in your child's classroom or the school office. It should be accessible, not locked away in a cupboard that only one staff member can open. Every adult who supervises your child should know exactly where it is.

## The Complete Kit — What Goes In

Here is your master checklist. Print it, laminate it, and tape it inside the kit bag or box.

### Blood Glucose Monitoring

- Blood glucose meter (even if your child wears a CGM — sensors can fail)
- Spare test strips (at least 20, check expiry dates monthly)
- Lancing device and spare lancets
- Spare CGM sensor (if applicable) and the applicator
- CGM reader or receiver if your child does not use a phone

### Hypoglycaemia (Low Blood Glucose) Treatment

- Fast-acting glucose: glucose tablets, small juice boxes (150–200 ml), or glucose gel sachets — at least 4 servings
- Follow-up carbohydrate snacks: small crackers, a muesli bar, or a small sandwich
- Glucagon emergency kit (nasal glucagon such as Baqsimi, or injectable glucagon) — clearly labelled with expiry date and simple instructions for staff

### Insulin Delivery

- Spare insulin pen or pen with cartridge (if on MDI — multiple daily injections)
- Spare pen needles (at least 5)
- Spare pump supplies if on insulin pump therapy: infusion set, reservoir, insertion device, and batteries
- A note from you or your diabetes team confirming which insulin your child uses (but remember, school staff should never adjust doses without guidance)

### General Supplies

- Alcohol swabs or hand sanitiser
- Sharps container (a small, hard-sided one is fine)
- Spare batteries for the meter
- Medical ID bracelet or card
- A current photo of your child (for emergency services, in case it is ever needed)
- Ketone testing strips (blood or urine, as directed by your diabetes team)

### Paperwork

- Your child's individual diabetes management plan (also called a care plan or health management plan)
- Emergency contact numbers: yours, your partner's, your diabetes team's clinic number, and your child's GP
- A simple one-page instruction sheet: "If blood glucose is below X, do this. If above Y, do this. If in doubt, call [parent's number]."
- Any relevant legal documentation for your region (Section 504 plan in the US, Individual Healthcare Plan in the UK, or equivalent in South Africa)

## Duplicates — Classroom and Office

Here is a tip that experienced diabetes families swear by: keep a mini duplicate kit in the actual classroom, not just in the nurse's office. A child with a blood glucose of 3.2 mmol/L (58 mg/dL) should not have to walk down two corridors and up a flight of stairs to get a juice box.

The classroom mini kit can be simple:

- 3–4 fast-acting glucose treatments (juice boxes or glucose tablets)
- A follow-up snack
- A copy of the one-page instruction sheet

The full kit stays in the school office or nurse's room. The mini kit stays within arm's reach of the teacher.

## Expiry Dates — The Silent Saboteur

This is the part that catches everyone out. Supplies expire, and expired supplies can give inaccurate readings or fail to work when you need them most.

Set a recurring reminder — the first Monday of every month works well — to check the following:

- **Test strips:** These expire, and they also degrade if the container has been opened for a long time. Replace open containers every 3–4 months even if strips remain.
- **Insulin:** Unopened insulin pens stored at room temperature are typically good for 28–30 days (check the specific product information). In a school environment, temperature can fluctuate. If the classroom gets very hot in summer, insulin may degrade faster.
- **Glucagon:** Check the expiry date printed on the box. Expired glucagon may still have some effect in a true emergency, but you never want to rely on that.
- **Juice boxes and glucose tablets:** Yes, these expire too. Check the dates.
- **CGM sensors:** These have a printed expiry date. Rotate stock so the oldest sensor is used first.

A simple trick: write the "replace by" date on a piece of tape stuck to each item. When you do your monthly check, anything past its date gets swapped out immediately.

## Keeping the Kit Organised

Use a clear, hard-sided container or a brightly coloured bag that is easy to spot. Label it with your child's name, the word "DIABETES," and your phone number on the outside. Inside, use zip-lock bags to group items: one bag for hypo treatment, one for monitoring, one for insulin supplies.

Some families use a traffic-light system: a green card on top means "all stocked and current," a yellow card means "running low on something — please notify parent," and a red card means "something critical is missing or expired." Teach the school staff to swap the cards when they use supplies.

## Seasonal Adjustments

As the school year progresses, think about:

- **Summer terms:** Heat can affect insulin and test strips. Consider an insulated pouch.
- **Winter terms:** Cold can also affect insulin. Keep it at room temperature, not in an unheated storage room.
- **Term transitions:** Restock fully at the start of each new term. Do not assume leftover supplies from last term are still good.

## Getting the School on Board

Drop off the kit in person at the start of the year. Walk the teacher and any support staff through each item. Show them where the hypo treatment is, show them the glucagon, and show them the emergency contact sheet. A five-minute walk-through builds confidence and trust — and it is much more effective than a note in the schoolbag.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Keep a dedicated, fully stocked diabetes kit at school** — separate from your home supplies — so nothing is forgotten on a rushed morning.
2. **Place a mini hypo treatment kit in the classroom itself**, not just in the nurse's office, so fast-acting glucose is always within reach.
3. **Check expiry dates monthly** on test strips, insulin, glucagon, and even juice boxes, and replace anything that is past its date.
4. **Include clear, simple written instructions and emergency contacts** inside the kit so that any adult can act quickly, even if they are unfamiliar with diabetes.
5. **Walk school staff through the kit in person** at the start of each year and after any major restocking, building their confidence to support your child.
