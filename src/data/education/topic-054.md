# The Danger Window — When Overlapping Doses Create Risk

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with diabetes, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 054

## Hello from Mira

In the previous topic on insulin stacking, we explored why taking a new dose before the previous one has finished can create compounding effects. Now I want to take you deeper into the specific **window of time** where that risk is highest — the period I call the **danger window**.

This is not about making you afraid. It is about making you aware. Because when you can see the window, you can navigate it with confidence rather than stumbling through it in the dark.

---

## What is the danger window?

The danger window is the period — roughly **2 to 4 hours** after a dose of rapid-acting insulin — when the previous dose is still significantly active and a new dose would create the most pronounced overlap.

Let me be more specific. When you inject rapid-acting insulin (such as NovoRapid, Humalog, or Fiasp), it follows a predictable activity curve:

- **0-15 minutes:** The insulin is being absorbed. Activity is low but rising.
- **30-90 minutes:** The insulin reaches its **peak** — this is when it is working hardest, lowering blood glucose most aggressively.
- **90 minutes to 3 hours:** Activity is declining but still substantial. This is the heart of the danger window.
- **3-5 hours:** The **tail** — activity is low but not zero. The insulin is still gently lowering blood glucose.

The danger window sits in that middle zone, roughly 1.5 to 4 hours after injection, where there is still enough active insulin to matter significantly — but enough time has passed that you might believe the dose has "worn off." It has not. It is still there, still working, still pulling your blood glucose downward.

---

## Why corrections in the danger window are risky

Let us walk through the mechanics. Say you take a meal bolus at noon. By 2:00 pm, your blood glucose is higher than you would like. You feel frustrated. You want to act. So you calculate a correction dose.

But here is the problem. At 2:00 pm, your noon dose is right in the middle of its activity curve. Depending on the insulin and your personal physiology, somewhere between **30% and 50%** of that dose is still active. That is not a trivial amount.

When you add a correction dose on top, the combined insulin activity curve jumps. You are not just adding the correction — you are adding it to a system that is already working harder than you might realise. The total effective insulin in your body is now significantly more than the correction dose alone.

The result can be a steep, unexpected drop in blood glucose several hours later — often arriving at exactly the wrong time, such as late afternoon, during exercise, or as you are settling down for the evening.

---

## The bridge analogy

Imagine you are building a bridge across a river. The first dose is the first section of the bridge, extending from one bank. It has not reached the other side yet — it is still in progress. The danger window is the period where that first section is still being built.

If you start building a second section from the same bank before the first one is complete, they overlap in the middle. The bridge becomes structurally overloaded in that section. The weight is not distributed — it is concentrated where the two sections meet.

That concentrated weight is the combined insulin activity at the point of maximum overlap. And just like an overloaded bridge, the system can buckle — in this case, with a hypoglycaemic event.

## Real-world patterns

Let me describe some of the most common real-world scenarios where the danger window catches people out.

**The post-lunch correction:** You eat lunch at 12:00 and bolus. At 2:00 or 2:30, you check your blood glucose and it is high. You correct. By 4:00 or 5:00 pm, both doses are working simultaneously, and your blood glucose drops sharply. This is perhaps the single most common stacking scenario in daily diabetes management.

**The party or celebration meal:** You eat a large meal and bolus for it. The meal was larger than expected, or the carbohydrate content was underestimated, so blood glucose rises. You correct early, during the danger window. The combined doses send blood glucose crashing later in the evening.

**The anxious correction at school or work:** A parent or carer checks a child's blood glucose mid-morning and sees a high reading. Wanting to protect the child, they give a correction — but the breakfast bolus was only two hours ago. The child goes low before lunch.

**The pre-bedtime correction:** Blood glucose is high before bed. A correction is given. But there is still IOB from the evening meal bolus. Overnight, the combined effect causes a nocturnal hypo — one of the most feared scenarios in diabetes management.

In each case, the mechanism is the same: a new dose was given inside the danger window of the previous dose.

## Patience is a clinical skill

I want to acknowledge something. Waiting is hard. When your blood glucose is high — or your child's blood glucose is high — and you know insulin can bring it down, choosing to wait feels counterintuitive. It feels like doing nothing.

But waiting for the previous dose to work is not doing nothing. It is doing something very important: it is allowing the insulin already in your body to finish its job. It is respecting the pharmacokinetics of the drug. It is prioritising safety over speed.

This does not mean you should never correct during the danger window. There are situations where correction is clinically appropriate, even with IOB present — your care team will help you understand those nuances. What it means is that **any correction given during the danger window should account for the insulin already on board**. The correction is reduced, not full. The overlap is acknowledged, not ignored.

---

## The window closes eventually

The good news is that the danger window is finite. For most rapid-acting insulins, once 4 to 5 hours have passed since a dose, the active insulin has been largely absorbed and utilised. At that point, a new dose stands on its own — there is minimal overlap and minimal compounding risk.

Understanding this timeline helps you plan. If you know your insulin's duration of action (your DIA setting, discussed in [topic-051](/education/topic-051)), you can identify when the danger window opens and when it closes. IOB Hunter reflects this in its calculations.

## Five key takeaways

1. **The danger window is roughly 2 to 4 hours after a dose of rapid-acting insulin,** when significant insulin activity remains and a new dose would create pronounced overlap.
2. **Corrections given inside the danger window without accounting for IOB are a leading cause of unexpected hypoglycaemia.** The combined effect of overlapping doses exceeds what either dose would do alone.
3. **Common real-world stacking scenarios include post-lunch corrections, party meals, school or work corrections, and pre-bedtime corrections.** Each involves dosing inside the danger window.
4. **IOB Hunter in GluMira visualises the danger window** by showing the activity curve of your previous dose and your current IOB, helping you see the overlap before you act.
5. **Patience is a clinical skill.** Allowing a previous dose to finish its work before adding more insulin is not doing nothing — it is respecting the pharmacokinetics of the drug.
