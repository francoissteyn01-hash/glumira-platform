# Basal vs Bolus — The Two Jobs Insulin Does

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with diabetes, parents, caregivers
**Topic ID:** 052

## Hello from Mira

If someone asked you what insulin does, you might say "it lowers blood sugar." And you would be right — but that is a bit like saying a house has walls. It is true, but it does not tell you much about the architecture. Insulin actually performs two very different jobs in your body, and understanding those two roles is one of the most important foundations of diabetes management.

Let me introduce you to the concept of **basal** and **bolus** insulin — the background worker and the mealtime responder. Two roles, one hormone, and a partnership that keeps everything in balance.

---

## The background worker — basal insulin

Your body needs insulin every single moment of every single day, even when you are asleep, even when you have not eaten a single thing. Why? Because your liver is constantly releasing small amounts of glucose into your bloodstream. This is called **hepatic glucose output**, and it is your body's way of keeping your brain, your organs, and your cells fuelled around the clock.

In a body without diabetes, the pancreas handles this automatically — releasing tiny, steady pulses of insulin throughout the day and night. This constant, low-level supply is what we call **basal insulin**.

Think of basal insulin like the central heating in your house. It runs quietly in the background, keeping the temperature steady. You do not really notice it when it is working well, but the moment it stops, the house gets cold very quickly. In the same way, without basal insulin, blood glucose would rise steadily throughout the day and night, even without eating.

For people using insulin therapy, basal coverage is provided by **long-acting insulin analogues** — medications designed to work slowly and steadily over many hours. Common examples include Tresiba (insulin degludec), Lantus (insulin glargine), and Levemir (insulin detemir). Each has a slightly different profile, which we explore in detail in [topic-056](/education/topic-056). Some people achieve basal coverage through an insulin pump, which delivers tiny, continuous pulses of rapid-acting insulin instead.

The goal of basal insulin is beautifully simple: **keep blood glucose stable when you are not eating.** If your basal rate is well matched to your body's needs, your blood glucose should remain relatively flat overnight and between meals. If it is rising or falling during those periods, that is often a sign that basal needs adjusting — a conversation to have with your care team.

---

## The mealtime responder — bolus insulin

Now imagine you sit down and eat a meal. Carbohydrates in that meal are broken down into glucose, which enters your bloodstream. This creates a surge — a wave of glucose that basal insulin alone cannot handle. You need a bigger, faster response.

This is the job of **bolus insulin**. It is the rapid-response team. In a body without diabetes, the pancreas detects the rising glucose and releases a burst of insulin to match it. In insulin therapy, this is replicated by taking a dose of **rapid-acting insulin** (such as NovoRapid, Humalog, or Fiasp) at mealtimes.

If basal insulin is the central heating, bolus insulin is the kettle. You switch it on when you need it, it works quickly, and then it is done. It is not running all day — it is a targeted response to a specific event: food.

Bolus insulin is also used for **corrections** — when blood glucose is higher than your target range and needs to be brought down. A correction bolus is calculated based on how far above target you are and your individual **insulin sensitivity factor** (how much one unit of insulin is expected to lower your blood glucose). Your care team will help you understand your personal correction parameters.

## Why the distinction matters

Understanding the difference between basal and bolus is not just academic — it directly affects how you think about your insulin, your food, and your blood glucose patterns.

Here is a practical example. You wake up in the morning and your blood glucose is higher than expected. Where do you look first? If it has been rising steadily through the night, that is likely a **basal** issue — perhaps your long-acting insulin dose needs reviewing, or perhaps the timing of your basal injection needs adjusting. But if your blood glucose was stable overnight and only rose after breakfast, that is likely a **bolus** issue — perhaps the mealtime dose did not match the carbohydrate content of the meal.

Different problems, different solutions. If you conflate the two, you end up adjusting the wrong thing, and the pattern continues.

This is one of the reasons GluMira encourages you to log and review your glucose patterns across different times of day. When you can see the shape of your glucose over 24 hours, the basal-bolus distinction becomes visible. Flat lines between meals suggest good basal coverage. Spikes after meals suggest the bolus needs attention. The pattern tells the story.

---

## The ratio between basal and bolus

In many insulin regimens, approximately **40-60% of total daily insulin** is provided by basal, and the remainder by bolus doses throughout the day. This is sometimes called the **basal-bolus split**. But this is a general guideline — individual needs vary enormously based on diet, activity levels, insulin sensitivity, and many other factors.

What matters is not hitting a perfect ratio, but understanding that both components need to work well for overall glucose management to be effective. If your basal is too low, your bolus doses are fighting an uphill battle. If your bolus is poorly timed or sized, even perfect basal cannot prevent post-meal spikes.

## How this connects to IOB

Here is where basal and bolus connect to the concept of **Insulin On Board** explored in [topic-051](/education/topic-051). When IOB Hunter calculates your active insulin, it is primarily tracking your **bolus** insulin — the doses you took for meals or corrections. These are the doses with a defined activity curve that rises, peaks, and falls over a few hours.

Basal insulin, because it works slowly and steadily over a much longer period, is generally handled differently in IOB calculations. It is always present, always working, but it does not create the same sharp peaks and valleys that bolus doses do. Understanding this distinction helps you interpret what IOB Hunter is showing you and why it focuses on the insulin most likely to cause short-term glucose changes.

## A word about insulin pumps

If you or your child uses an insulin pump, the basal-bolus distinction still applies — but the delivery method is different. Instead of a long-acting injection for basal, the pump delivers tiny, continuous pulses of rapid-acting insulin throughout the day. Bolus doses are delivered on demand when you eat or need a correction. The two jobs remain the same; only the tools change.

## The partnership

Basal and bolus are not competitors. They are partners. One keeps the foundation steady; the other responds to the events of the day. When both are working well together, blood glucose management becomes more predictable, more understandable, and — crucially — less exhausting.

Your care team is the best resource for helping you fine-tune both components. GluMira is here to help you understand the concepts, see the patterns, and feel confident in the conversations you have with your clinical team.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Basal insulin is the background worker** — it provides a steady, low-level supply of insulin to manage the glucose your liver releases around the clock, even when you are not eating.
2. **Bolus insulin is the mealtime responder** — it is the rapid burst of insulin taken to cover food or to correct a high blood glucose reading.
3. **Different glucose problems point to different insulin roles.** Rising glucose overnight often suggests a basal issue; spikes after meals suggest a bolus issue.
4. **IOB Hunter primarily tracks bolus insulin activity,** because these are the doses with defined peaks and tails that most affect short-term glucose movement.
5. **Basal and bolus work as a partnership.** Both need to be well matched to your body's needs for effective glucose management.
