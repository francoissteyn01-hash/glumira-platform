## Hello from Mira

Dear parent,

There is a moment that almost every parent of a child with Type 1 Diabetes remembers vividly: the first injection. Not the one the nurse gave in the hospital — the one you had to give yourself, with your own hands, to your own child. If that moment is approaching, or if it just happened and you are still shaking, I want you to know: what you are feeling is completely normal, and you are going to be okay.

Let me walk you through this, gently and honestly.

---

## The fear is real — and it is universal

Let's name it. You are afraid of hurting your child. You may be afraid of needles yourself. You might feel physically sick at the idea of pressing a needle into your baby's skin. Some parents describe the first injection as one of the hardest things they have ever done — harder than the diagnosis itself, because this requires you to take an active step that feels counterintuitive to every protective instinct you have.

Research aligned with ISPAD guidelines acknowledges that parental injection anxiety is extremely common and can significantly affect early diabetes management. You are not being weak. You are being human.

Here is the truth that every experienced diabetes parent will tell you: **it gets easier.** Not immediately. Not overnight. But it does. The tenth injection is easier than the first. The hundredth barely registers. Your hands will stop shaking. Your confidence will grow. And your child will adapt far more quickly than you expect — children are remarkably resilient.

## What the injection actually involves

Your care team will have shown you the technique during your diabetes education sessions, but here is a general overview of what the process looks like, so you know what to expect:

**The needle is small.** Modern insulin pen needles are typically 4mm to 6mm in length — far shorter than what you might picture when you think of an injection. Many parents are surprised by how tiny they are.

**The common injection sites** include the upper arms, thighs, abdomen, and buttocks. Your care team will advise you on the best sites for your child's age and body. Rotating sites is important to prevent the skin from developing lumps (called lipohypertrophy), which can affect insulin absorption.

**The general steps** (as taught by your care team) typically involve:
1. Preparing the insulin pen or syringe as instructed
2. Choosing and cleaning the injection site if needed
3. Gently pinching the skin (your care team will show you when and how)
4. Inserting the needle at the angle you were taught
5. Pressing the plunger and waiting the recommended time before withdrawing
6. Disposing of the needle safely in a sharps bin

**Important:** GluMira does not provide specific dosing instructions or injection protocols. The exact technique, needle size, injection sites, and insulin types are clinical decisions that must be made with your care team based on your child's individual needs.

When learning about how insulin works after injection, GluMira's **IOB Hunter** feature can help you visualise the concept of insulin on board — how much insulin is still active in your child's body over time. This is an educational concept, not a clinical tool. Always discuss insulin management with your care team.

## Helping your child through it

How you approach the injection emotionally has a significant impact on how your child experiences it. Here are some strategies that many families find helpful:

**For babies and toddlers (0-3):**
- They will not understand why this is happening, and that is okay. Your calm energy matters more than words.
- Distraction works: a favourite toy, a song, a funny face.
- Some parents find that giving the injection during a feed or while the child is engaged in something else reduces distress.
- A brief cry is normal and does not mean you have done something wrong.

**For preschoolers (3-5):**
- Simple, honest language: "This is your insulin. It helps your body use the food you eat for energy."
- Let them choose which arm or leg (giving a sense of control).
- A reward system (sticker charts, for example) can help build positive associations.
- Never use the injection as a threat or punishment.

**For older children (6+):**
- Explain what is happening and why, at their level.
- Some children want to watch; others prefer to look away. Both are fine.
- Involve them gradually — letting them push the plunger, then eventually guiding the process.
- Praise their bravery, but also normalise the moment. It is not a big dramatic event — it is just part of the routine.

## The guilt

Many parents carry guilt about giving injections. "I am hurting my child." "They are crying because of me." "What if I do it wrong?"

I want to reframe this for you: **you are not hurting your child. You are keeping them alive.** Insulin is not optional for someone with Type 1 Diabetes. Every injection you give is an act of love, even when it does not feel like one. The discomfort is brief. The benefit is everything.

If the guilt feels overwhelming, or if injection anxiety is affecting your ability to manage your child's diabetes, please discuss this with your care team. They can offer additional training, support, and sometimes referral to a psychologist who specialises in medical procedures. This is a common and well-understood challenge — there is no shame in asking for help.

## Practical tips from experienced parents

- **Have everything prepared before you begin.** Fumbling with equipment while a toddler squirms increases stress for everyone.
- **Stay calm and matter-of-fact.** Children pick up on anxiety. If you are tense, they will be tense.
- **Ice or numbing cream** can help in the early days — discuss options with your care team.
- **Consistency helps.** The more routine it becomes, the less remarkable it feels.
- **Accept imperfect days.** Some injections will go smoothly. Some will not. Both are normal.
- **Connect with other parents.** Hearing "I felt exactly the same way" from another parent who has been through it is incredibly reassuring.

## When to call your care team

Reach out to your care team if:
- You are unsure about any aspect of the injection technique
- You notice bruising, lumps, or skin changes at injection sites
- Your child is having extreme distress reactions that are not improving over time
- You are experiencing significant anxiety that is affecting your ability to give injections
- You have any questions at all — no question is too small

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **The first injection is one of the hardest moments for parents** — fear, guilt, and anxiety are completely normal and universally experienced.
2. **It gets easier.** Your hands will steady, your confidence will grow, and your child will adapt faster than you expect.
3. **Modern insulin needles are very small** (typically 4-6mm). The discomfort is brief, and children are remarkably resilient.
4. **Your calm energy matters.** Distraction, choice, and routine all help your child cope — and your emotional state directly influences theirs.
5. **Every injection is an act of love.** You are not hurting your child — you are giving them the insulin their body needs to thrive.
