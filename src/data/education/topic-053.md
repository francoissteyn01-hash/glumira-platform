## Hello from Mira

Here is something that trips up even the most experienced insulin users: you take a dose, wait a couple of hours, check your blood glucose, and it is still high. So you take another dose. Logical, right? Two plus two equals four. Except with insulin, it does not.

Welcome to the concept of **insulin stacking** — one of the most common and most preventable causes of unexpected hypoglycaemia. It sounds technical, but the principle is surprisingly simple once you see it. And once you see it, you will never unsee it.

---

## What is insulin stacking?

Insulin stacking occurs when you take a new dose of insulin **before the previous dose has finished working**. Because rapid-acting insulin has an activity duration of roughly 3 to 5 hours (varying by individual and insulin type), there is a long window during which that first dose is still lowering your blood glucose — even if it does not feel like it.

When you take a second dose during that window, the two doses **overlap**. Their effects compound. You do not simply have the effect of the second dose — you have the combined effect of both doses working simultaneously. And that combined effect can be significantly more than you intended.

This is why 2+2 does not equal 4 with insulin. If you take 2 units, and two hours later take another 2 units, you do not have 2 units working. You might have 3 or even 3.5 units of effective insulin activity at that moment — because the first dose still has insulin on board. The total glucose-lowering power in your system is greater than the second dose alone.

---

## The traffic analogy

Think of a motorway. Cars are entering at a steady rate and exiting at a steady rate. Traffic flows smoothly. Now imagine a second on-ramp opens and starts feeding more cars onto the same stretch of road before the first batch has cleared. Suddenly the motorway is congested — not because either flow of cars is too large on its own, but because they are overlapping.

Insulin stacking works the same way. Each dose, on its own, might be perfectly appropriate. But when they overlap in time, the total insulin activity exceeds what was intended, and blood glucose can drop further and faster than expected. The result? An unexpected hypo, sometimes a severe one.

---

## Why it happens so easily

Insulin stacking is common because it follows what feels like perfectly reasonable logic. Here is a scenario that plays out thousands of times every day:

**12:00 pm** — You eat lunch and take your bolus dose. Everything is as expected.

**2:00 pm** — You check your blood glucose. It is 14 mmol/L (252 mg/dL). That is too high. You calculate a correction dose and take it.

**What you may not have considered:** Your lunch bolus is only two hours old. Depending on your personal insulin profile, somewhere between 40% and 60% of that original dose is still active. It is still working. It has not failed — it simply has not finished yet.

By adding a full correction dose on top of it, you have effectively doubled down. The correction assumes you have no insulin working for you, but you do. The result is more active insulin than intended, and a blood glucose that may plummet in the hours that follow.

This is not a mistake born of carelessness. It is a mistake born of not having visibility into what is already happening beneath the surface.

---

## The emotional pull

Let me be honest about something. High blood glucose does not feel good — physically or emotionally. It makes you tired, thirsty, foggy, and frustrated. When you see a high number on your meter or CGM, the desire to fix it immediately is powerful. Taking another dose feels like taking action, taking control.

But insulin is not instant. It is not paracetamol. It is a hormone with a defined activity curve that cannot be rushed. And the emotional urgency to "fix it now" is one of the biggest drivers of stacking. Recognising that impulse — and pausing — is one of the most valuable habits you can build.

## Example scenarios

Let us look at three scenarios to make this concrete. These are illustrative and use simplified numbers for educational purposes only — your personal parameters will be determined by your care team.

**Scenario A — No stacking risk:**
You took a bolus at 12:00. At 5:00 pm, your blood glucose is high. Your insulin from lunch has been fully absorbed (five hours have passed). A correction dose now carries minimal overlap risk.

**Scenario B — Moderate stacking risk:**
You took a bolus at 12:00. At 3:00 pm, your blood glucose is high. Three hours have passed, but depending on your DIA (Duration of Insulin Action), 20-30% of your lunch dose may still be active. A full correction dose without subtracting IOB could result in more insulin activity than intended.

**Scenario C — High stacking risk:**
You took a bolus at 12:00. At 1:30 pm, your blood glucose is high. Only 90 minutes have passed — the first dose is likely still near its peak activity. Taking a full correction now means you are stacking at the point of maximum overlap. This is the scenario most likely to lead to an unexpected hypo.

In each case, the key variable is the same: **how much insulin is still on board?**

---

## Stacking is not just about corrections

While correction doses are the most common stacking scenario, it is worth noting that stacking can also occur with meal boluses taken close together — for example, a snack bolus taken shortly after a meal bolus. The principle is identical: overlapping doses compound.

---

## What to do instead

The most important step is simply **awareness**. Before taking any dose, ask: "When was my last dose, and how much of it is still active?" IOB Hunter in GluMira can help answer that question visually. Your care team can help you understand how to factor IOB into your decision-making.

This is not about being afraid to take insulin when you need it. It is about taking it **with full information**, rather than reacting to a number without context.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Insulin stacking occurs when a new dose overlaps with a previous dose that is still active.** The effects compound, creating more glucose-lowering power than intended.
2. **Two plus two does not equal four with insulin.** Because the first dose is still working, adding a second dose on top creates a combined effect greater than the second dose alone.
3. **The emotional urge to correct a high reading immediately is one of the biggest drivers of stacking.** Pausing to check IOB before acting is a powerful habit.
4. **IOB Hunter in GluMira visualises overlapping insulin activity,** helping you see the combined curve before you make a decision.
5. **Stacking is preventable.** The key is knowing how much insulin is still on board before taking more.
