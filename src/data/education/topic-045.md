# Exam Stress and Glucose — Cortisol Is Not Your Friend

**Topic ID:** 45
**Age Range:** 13–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** School & Academic Life
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17

## The Exam Season BG Rollercoaster

You have studied for weeks. You know the material. You sit down in the exam hall, open the paper, and your blood glucose decides to do its own thing. Levels climbing for no obvious reason. Or crashing mid-paper. Or swinging wildly between the two.

This is not your imagination. This is not bad management. This is stress physiology, and it is well documented. Exam periods are one of the most common times teens with T1D report difficulty controlling their blood glucose — and there is a clear biological reason why.

## What Stress Actually Does to Your Blood Glucose

When you are stressed — physically or psychologically — your body activates its stress response. This involves several hormones, but the two big players for blood glucose are **cortisol** and **adrenaline** (epinephrine).

**Cortisol** is a slow-burn stress hormone. During prolonged stress (like weeks of exam revision, sleep deprivation, or sustained anxiety), cortisol levels stay elevated. Cortisol directly increases insulin resistance, meaning your cells respond less effectively to the insulin you take. The result: higher blood glucose readings, even when you are eating and dosing exactly as usual.

**Adrenaline** is the acute stress hormone — the "fight or flight" response. Walking into an exam hall, opening a paper, realising you do not know question one — these can trigger adrenaline surges. Adrenaline prompts your liver to dump stored glucose into your bloodstream, giving you a rapid BG spike. This is your body preparing to run from a predator. Unfortunately, your body does not know the difference between a predator and a maths exam.

The combined effect: during exam season, you may see persistently higher readings, more variability, and patterns that do not match your usual experience. Your time-in-range may drop. You may feel like your diabetes is "broken." It is not. Your hormones are doing exactly what they are designed to do — it is just unhelpful when the threat is a chemistry paper rather than a sabre-toothed tiger.

## The Revision Period — Weeks of Low-Grade Stress

Exam stress does not start on the day of the exam. It starts weeks before, during revision season. This is often a period of disrupted routines: irregular sleep, changed eating patterns, more caffeine, less exercise, more sitting, more anxiety.

Every single one of these factors affects blood glucose:

- **Irregular sleep** reduces insulin sensitivity and disrupts hormone cycles.
- **Changed eating patterns** — skipping meals, snacking constantly, eating at odd hours — make it harder to predict BG responses.
- **Caffeine** can raise blood glucose and increase anxiety.
- **Reduced exercise** means less glucose uptake by muscles and less insulin sensitivity.
- **Increased sitting** has a similar effect.
- **Sustained anxiety** keeps cortisol elevated.

This is why many teens with T1D find that their BG management goes sideways during revision, not just on exam days. Understanding that this is a physiological response — not a personal failure — is genuinely important.

## Strategies for the Revision Period

You cannot eliminate stress. But you can manage the factors that compound it.

**Maintain a basic routine.** You do not need a military schedule, but eating roughly at the same times, sleeping roughly at the same times, and building some movement into your day makes a measurable difference to BG stability. Your body likes predictability, especially when everything else feels chaotic.

**Check more often.** During exam season, increase your monitoring. If you use a CGM, pay attention to trends, not just point readings. If you use a meter, check more frequently than usual. Knowledge reduces surprises, and surprises during revision add unnecessary stress.

**Move your body.** Even 20 minutes of walking, stretching, or any physical activity helps reduce cortisol levels and improve insulin sensitivity. This is not about fitness — it is about physiology. A study break that involves movement is more effective for both your BG and your memory consolidation than sitting staring at notes for another hour.

**Eat properly.** Revision snacking is a trap. Constant grazing on high-carb snacks creates a continuous glucose management challenge. Plan your meals and snacks. Aim for balanced options that give sustained energy rather than spikes and crashes. This is not about restriction — it is about choosing fuel that helps rather than hinders.

**Watch the caffeine.** Coffee and energy drinks are revision staples, but caffeine can raise blood glucose and increase anxiety. If you use them, be aware of the impact and factor it into your management. Water is less exciting but does not mess with your levels.

**Sleep is non-negotiable.** The evidence is unambiguous: sleep deprivation worsens insulin resistance, impairs cognitive function, and increases stress hormones. Pulling all-nighters is not heroic. It makes your diabetes harder to manage AND your exam performance worse. Eight hours is the target. Seven is the minimum.

## Exam Day Protocol

The day of an exam needs specific planning. Here is a framework:

**The night before:** Set your alarm with plenty of time. Lay out your diabetes supplies alongside your exam supplies. Get to bed at a reasonable time.

**Morning:** Eat a proper breakfast — something you know and trust, not something new or unusual. Check your BG. Give yourself time for levels to stabilise before the exam.

**Before the exam:** Check your BG again. If you are trending low, eat something. If you are high, note it but do not panic-correct — high BG during an exam is less dangerous than going low mid-paper. You want to enter the exam with a stable, in-range reading, ideally slightly higher than your usual target rather than lower.

**Take supplies into the exam.** In most countries, you have a legal right to bring glucose tablets, snacks, water, your meter, and your phone (for CGM if applicable) into an exam. This usually requires prior arrangement through your school's exam access arrangements or your university's disability service. **Set this up well in advance.** Do not assume it will be allowed on the day without paperwork.

**During the exam:** If you feel low, stop writing and treat. No exam question is worth a severe hypo. Eat your glucose, wait for recovery, then continue. You may be entitled to extra time to compensate for medical interruptions — check your access arrangements.

**After the exam:** Check your BG. The adrenaline comedown can cause a BG drop. Eat something. Do not immediately dive into worrying about the next exam.

## What About Extra Time and Exam Adjustments?

In the UK, the Joint Council for Qualifications (JCQ) allows access arrangements for candidates with diabetes, including rest breaks and supervised BG management time. In the US, Section 504 plans and ADA accommodations provide similar protections. In Australia, SACE and equivalent bodies have medical provisions.

These are your legal rights. Using them is not cheating. A non-diabetic student does not have to stop mid-exam to treat a hypo. You do. Extra time compensates for that. Apply for these arrangements early in the academic year, not the week before exams.

## When Stress Becomes Something More

Exam stress is normal. But if you are experiencing persistent anxiety that does not go away between exams, panic attacks, inability to eat or sleep, hopelessness, or a feeling that you simply cannot cope, that may be more than ordinary stress. Mental health conditions are more common in people with T1D than in the general population, and exam season can be a trigger.

Talk to someone. Your school counsellor, your GP, your diabetes team, a parent, a trusted friend. There is no exam result that is worth your mental health, and help is available.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cortisol and adrenaline from stress directly raise blood glucose and increase insulin resistance** — erratic readings during exam season are physiology, not failure.
2. **Maintain basic routines during revision:** regular meals, adequate sleep, and some physical activity measurably improve both BG control and academic performance.
3. **Check your blood glucose more frequently during exam periods** and expect higher variability than usual.
4. **Set up exam access arrangements well in advance** — you have a legal right to bring supplies into exams and may be entitled to extra time for medical management.
5. **Enter exams slightly above target rather than below** — high BG is uncomfortable but manageable; a hypo mid-exam disrupts your performance and your safety.
