# DKA Warning Signs — The Emergency You Must Never Miss

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, school staff

## Hello from Mira

I need to be direct with you. Diabetic ketoacidosis — DKA — is the single most dangerous acute complication of Type 1 Diabetes. It is the leading cause of death in children and young people with T1D. That sentence is hard to read, and I do not write it lightly.

But here is the other truth: DKA is almost always preventable when you know the warning signs and act on them quickly. That is why this topic exists. Not to scare you, but to arm you with knowledge that genuinely saves lives.

Please read this carefully. Share it with the people in your life. And if you recognise these signs in yourself or someone you care for — act immediately.

---

## What is DKA?

In Type 1 Diabetes, the body cannot produce insulin. Insulin is the hormone that allows glucose (sugar) to leave the bloodstream and enter cells to be used as energy. Without enough insulin, glucose builds up in the blood — but the cells are starving.

When cells are starved of glucose, the body turns to an emergency backup: it starts breaking down fat for fuel. This process produces chemicals called **ketones**. In small amounts, ketones are manageable. But when insulin is severely lacking, ketone production accelerates out of control. Ketones are acidic. As they accumulate, the blood becomes dangerously acidic — this is **ketoacidosis**.

DKA is not simply "high blood sugar." It is a metabolic crisis. The body's chemistry becomes so disrupted that organs begin to fail. Without treatment, it is fatal.

According to ISPAD, DKA is defined by three things occurring together:
- **Blood glucose above 11 mmol/L (200 mg/dL)** — though DKA can occasionally occur at lower glucose levels (euglycaemic DKA)
- **Blood pH below 7.3** or **serum bicarbonate below 15 mmol/L** (measured in hospital)
- **Ketones elevated** — blood ketones above 3.0 mmol/L or significant ketonuria

---

## Why does DKA happen?

DKA develops when there is not enough working insulin in the body. This can happen because:

- **Missed insulin doses** — even one or two missed doses can trigger it in some people
- **Pump failure** — if an insulin pump stops delivering (kinked cannula, empty reservoir, site failure), insulin levels drop rapidly because pumps only deliver fast-acting insulin with no long-acting reserve
- **Illness or infection** — the body releases stress hormones that increase glucose and insulin demand; the usual doses may not be enough
- **New diagnosis** — DKA is sometimes the first sign that someone has T1D, before they have started insulin treatment
- **Deliberate insulin omission** — this occurs in some adolescents and adults, sometimes linked to disordered eating or diabetes distress, and must be treated with compassion and specialist support

## The warning signs — know them by heart

### Early signs (hours)
- Blood glucose persistently above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) despite correction
- Increased thirst — drinking much more than usual
- Frequent urination — the body is trying to flush excess glucose
- Blood ketones rising above 0.6 mmol/L
- Feeling generally unwell — fatigue, malaise

### Progressing signs (hours to a day)
- **Nausea and vomiting** — a critical warning sign; do not dismiss this as "just a stomach bug"
- **Abdominal pain** — can be severe; in children, DKA is sometimes misdiagnosed as appendicitis
- **Blood ketones above 1.5 mmol/L** — this is firmly in the danger zone
- **Fruity or acetone smell on the breath** — caused by ketones being exhaled
- Rapid or deep breathing — called **Kussmaul breathing**, the body's attempt to blow off acid
- Dehydration — dry mouth, sunken eyes, reduced skin elasticity

### Late and critical signs
- **Blood ketones above 3.0 mmol/L**
- Severe confusion or drowsiness
- Rapid, laboured breathing
- Unable to keep fluids down
- Falling level of consciousness
- Shock — cold extremities, rapid weak pulse

## Ketone thresholds — what the numbers mean

If you are checking **blood ketones** (the most reliable method):

| Blood ketone level | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Below 0.6 mmol/L | Normal | Continue routine management |
| 0.6–1.4 mmol/L | Mildly elevated | Recheck in 1–2 hours. Ensure insulin is active and being delivered. Increase fluid intake. Contact diabetes team if not falling |
| 1.5–2.9 mmol/L | Significantly elevated — risk of DKA | Contact your diabetes care team urgently. Follow your sick-day plan. Do NOT wait to see if it improves on its own |
| 3.0 mmol/L or above | DKA range | **Go to the emergency department immediately.** Do not wait. |

**Urine ketone testing** is less precise. If urine ketones read "moderate" or "large," treat this as serious and act as you would for blood ketones above 1.5 mmol/L.

---

## When to go to hospital — no exceptions

**Go to the emergency department or call an ambulance IMMEDIATELY if:**

- Blood ketones are **3.0 mmol/L or above**
- The person is **vomiting repeatedly** and cannot keep fluids down, with ketones rising
- There is **rapid or laboured breathing** (Kussmaul breathing)
- The person is **drowsy, confused, or losing consciousness**
- The person is a **young child** and you cannot reach your diabetes team
- You are **unsure** — when in doubt, go to hospital

DKA requires **intravenous fluids and insulin** administered in a hospital setting, with careful monitoring of blood chemistry. It cannot be treated at home once it has progressed to moderate or severe stages.

---

## This is not something to "wait and see"

I want to be very clear about this. DKA can progress from early signs to a life-threatening emergency in a matter of hours — sometimes faster in young children or people using insulin pumps (because there is no long-acting insulin depot).

Every year, people die from DKA that was caught too late. Not because they did not have access to care, but because the early signs were dismissed. Vomiting was attributed to a stomach bug. High blood glucose was assumed to come down on its own. Ketones were not checked.

Please do not let this happen to you or someone you love. Check ketones whenever blood glucose is persistently high. Check ketones whenever someone with T1D is vomiting. And act on the numbers.

## Prevention — the daily habits that prevent DKA

- **Never skip insulin.** If you are struggling with insulin adherence, please speak to your diabetes team without shame — they are there to help, not to judge.
- **Check ketones** when BG is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) for more than 2–3 hours, during illness, or when vomiting.
- **Have a sick-day plan** agreed with your care team and keep it accessible.
- **Pump users:** Always carry a backup insulin pen and know how to switch to injections if your pump fails.
- **Know the signs.** Teach them to your family, your school, your workplace.

---

## Five Key Takeaways

1. **DKA is a life-threatening emergency** caused by severe insulin deficiency — the body breaks down fat, producing toxic levels of ketones that make the blood dangerously acidic.
2. **Key warning signs are persistent high BG, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath, and rapid breathing** — do not dismiss vomiting as "just a bug" when someone has T1D.
3. **Blood ketones of 3.0 mmol/L or above mean go to hospital immediately** — DKA requires intravenous treatment that cannot be given at home.
4. **DKA can develop in hours**, especially in children and pump users — check ketones early and often during illness or persistent highs.
5. **DKA is almost always preventable** — never skip insulin, always check ketones when glucose is high, and follow your sick-day plan.
