# Time in Range vs HbA1c — The Modern Metric Explained

**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people with T1D, parents, caregivers, and care teams

## Hello from Mira

For decades, diabetes management revolved around one number: HbA1c. It was the metric that clinics tracked, that studies measured, and that determined whether management was considered "good" or "not quite there yet." And while HbA1c remains valuable — as we explored in [topic-091](/education/topic-091) — there is a newer metric that many in the diabetes community believe paints a far more honest picture of daily life with T1D.

It is called **Time in Range**, often shortened to **TIR**. And understanding it might change the way you think about your glucose data.

---

## What is Time in Range?

Time in Range refers to the **percentage of time** during a given period that your glucose level stays within a defined target window. The internationally agreed target range for most people with T1D is:

- **70 to 180 mg/dL** (3.9 to 10.0 mmol/L)

This range was established through international consensus — including input from ISPAD, the ADA, and the Advanced Technologies & Treatments for Diabetes (ATTD) group — and is now widely referenced in clinical guidelines.

If you wear a continuous glucose monitor (CGM), the device samples your glucose approximately every five minutes. Over a 24-hour period, that produces roughly 288 readings. Time in Range simply calculates what proportion of those readings fall within the 70-180 mg/dL window.

For example, if 70% of your readings in a day are between 70 and 180 mg/dL, your TIR for that day is 70%.

---

## Why 70% is the gold standard

The international consensus report published in 2019 — endorsed by the ADA and EASD among others — recommended that most people with T1D aim for a **Time in Range of at least 70%**. This translates to approximately **16 hours and 48 minutes per day** within range.

Why 70%? Research has shown that a TIR of 70% correlates roughly with an HbA1c of approximately 7.0% (53 mmol/mol). But more importantly, studies have demonstrated that higher TIR is independently associated with reduced risk of microvascular complications — retinopathy in particular. In other words, TIR is not just a proxy for HbA1c. It carries its own predictive value.

The consensus targets also address the other parts of the day:

- **Time below range** (below 70 mg/dL / 3.9 mmol/L): less than 4% of the day — roughly under one hour
- **Time significantly below range** (below 54 mg/dL / 3.0 mmol/L): less than 1% — roughly under 15 minutes
- **Time above range** (above 180 mg/dL / 10.0 mmol/L): less than 25% of the day
- **Time significantly above range** (above 250 mg/dL / 13.9 mmol/L): less than 5% of the day

For older adults, those with high hypoglycaemia risk, or young children, targets may be adjusted. For example, ISPAD recognises that the target range for very young children may be discussed with the care team in the context of developmental stage and hypoglycaemia risk.

## What TIR captures that HbA1c misses

Imagine two people, both with an HbA1c of 7.0%.

**Person A** has relatively stable glucose throughout the day. Their CGM trace shows gentle undulations — a modest rise after meals, a smooth return to range. Their TIR is 75%. They spend very little time below range.

**Person B** lives on a glucose rollercoaster. They spike to 300 mg/dL (16.7 mmol/L) after meals, then crash to 50 mg/dL (2.8 mmol/L) in the early hours of the morning. The highs and lows average out to the same HbA1c. Their TIR might be only 45%. They spend significant time both above and below range.

HbA1c cannot distinguish between these two people. TIR can — and so can the complementary metrics of time below range and glycaemic variability.

This is why TIR has become so important. It gives visibility to the **texture** of daily glucose management, not just the average. It honours the reality that spending three hours high and three hours low is not the same experience as spending six hours in range — even if the maths produces the same mean.

## The ambulatory glucose profile (AGP)

When you download your CGM data or view it in your clinic's software, you may see a standardised report called the **Ambulatory Glucose Profile** or AGP. This report — recommended by the international consensus — presents:

- Time in Range percentages broken down by range category
- A visual median glucose trace across 24 hours
- Variability bands showing the spread between the 25th and 75th percentiles
- A coefficient of variation (CV) indicating glycaemic variability — a CV below 36% is generally considered a marker of more stable glucose levels

The AGP is designed to make CGM data clinically useful at a glance. It allows you and your care team to spot patterns — for example, a consistent post-lunch spike, or a predictable overnight drop — and to have focused, productive conversations about management.

---

## TIR is motivating in a way HbA1c often is not

One of the most profound differences between TIR and HbA1c is how quickly it responds. HbA1c reflects two to three months and moves slowly. You might make meaningful changes to your management and wait weeks to see the number shift.

TIR, by contrast, updates with every new CGM reading. You can see improvement within a single day. If you pre-bolus before a meal and your post-meal spike is smaller than yesterday's, that shows up immediately in your TIR. This responsiveness creates a tighter **feedback loop** — you can see the effect of your choices in near real-time, which can be deeply motivating.

Of course, this immediacy also has a shadow side. It is important not to become obsessive about minute-to-minute numbers or to feel defeated by a single "bad" day. TIR is most useful when viewed over periods of **14 days or more**, which is why the AGP report typically uses a two-week window.

---

## TIR does not replace HbA1c — it complements it

The international consensus is clear: TIR and HbA1c are **complementary metrics**. HbA1c remains essential — it has decades of outcome data behind it and is available to everyone, not just those using CGM. TIR adds depth and granularity for those with access to continuous glucose data.

Together, they provide a far richer picture than either can alone. And alongside them, time below range deserves particular attention — because avoiding hypoglycaemia is not just a management goal, it is a safety imperative.

---

## A note on access

Not everyone has access to a CGM, and therefore not everyone can measure TIR. This is an important equity consideration that the diabetes community continues to advocate around. If you do not currently use a CGM, HbA1c and structured blood glucose monitoring remain valuable tools. Your management is not less valid because you lack access to a particular technology.

---

## Five key takeaways

1. **Time in Range (TIR) measures the percentage of time glucose stays between 70-180 mg/dL (3.9-10.0 mmol/L)**, providing a real-time, granular view of glucose management that HbA1c cannot offer.
2. **The international consensus recommends a TIR of at least 70%** for most people with T1D — equivalent to roughly 16 hours and 48 minutes per day within range — alongside targets for time below and above range.
3. **TIR captures glycaemic variability that HbA1c hides.** Two identical HbA1c values can represent very different daily experiences — stable and in range, or swinging between highs and lows.
4. **TIR provides a faster feedback loop than HbA1c**, responding to management changes within days rather than months, which can be motivating — though it is best evaluated over a 14-day period.
5. **TIR complements HbA1c rather than replacing it.** Together with time below range and glycaemic variability, these metrics give the most complete picture of T1D management currently available.
