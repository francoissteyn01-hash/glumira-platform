# Anxiety and Glucose Checking — When Vigilance Becomes Harmful

**Topic ID:** 084
**Age Range:** All ages
**Audience:** All — people living with T1D, parents, and caregivers
**Reading Time:** Approximately 5 minutes

---

## You checked five minutes ago. You need to check again.

The number said 6.2. That should feel safe. But what if it's dropping? What if the sensor is lagging? What if it was wrong? You pick up the phone again. You scan again. You prick again. The number says 6.1. That's fine. That's normal.

But you don't feel fine. And you won't feel fine until you check again.

If this sounds familiar, you are not alone. And you are not irrational. You are a person whose life — or whose child's life — depends on a number, and your brain has decided that the only way to stay safe is to never stop watching it.

The problem is that hypervigilance, past a certain point, stops protecting you and starts harming you. And the line between the two is not always easy to see.

## The line between careful and obsessive

T1D requires monitoring. That is a medical fact. Checking blood glucose, responding to trends, adjusting insulin — these are necessary, life-sustaining behaviours. No one is suggesting you stop paying attention.

But there is a difference between attentive management and anxiety-driven compulsion. Here's how to tell them apart:

**Attentive management** looks like checking at clinically recommended times — before meals, before driving, before bed, when symptoms suggest a high or low, and when your CGM alerts you. It involves responding to data with proportionate action and then moving on with your day.

**Anxiety-driven checking** looks different. It looks like:

- Checking 20, 30, or 40+ times a day despite wearing a CGM
- Scanning every few minutes, unable to tolerate not knowing the exact number
- Finger-pricking to "verify" the CGM, then doing it again because you don't trust the finger prick
- Waking multiple times per night to check, even when the CGM is set to alarm at appropriate thresholds
- Being unable to concentrate on work, conversation, or activities because part of your brain is permanently fixed on the number
- Feeling panic — genuine, physical panic — when the number moves even slightly out of range
- Experiencing relief after a check that lasts only seconds before the anxiety returns
- Restricting your child's activities because you cannot monitor them closely enough

If you are a parent, it might also look like calling the school repeatedly, requesting photo evidence of glucose readings, or being unable to leave your child with anyone else because no one checks frequently enough.

These behaviours come from a place of love and fear. They are understandable. But they are also unsustainable — and they can cause real harm.

## The damage of constant checking

**Physiological harm:**
Excessive finger-pricking causes callusing, nerve damage, and pain in the fingertips. In children, it can create a lasting aversion to glucose monitoring.

**Psychological harm:**
Hypervigilant monitoring keeps your nervous system in a state of chronic activation — the fight-or-flight response running constantly at a low hum. Over time, this leads to anxiety disorders, insomnia, difficulty concentrating, irritability, and physical symptoms like headaches, muscle tension, and gastrointestinal problems.

**Relational harm:**
When a parent's anxiety drives excessive monitoring, it can damage the parent-child relationship. Children and teenagers may feel surveilled rather than cared for. They may associate diabetes management with their parent's distress, which can contribute to their own burnout and disengagement later.

**Paradoxical harm to management:**
Counterintuitively, checking too often can actually make management worse. When you respond to every small fluctuation — correcting a 7.5 that would have come down on its own, treating a 4.2 that was stable — you introduce more variables and create more instability. Good diabetes management often requires tolerating imperfect numbers and trusting the trend.

## Why T1D breeds health anxiety

It would be strange if T1D didn't create anxiety. Consider what the condition teaches your brain:

- A number on a screen determines whether you are safe or in danger
- That number changes constantly, often unpredictably
- Getting it "wrong" can result in seizures, loss of consciousness, or hospitalisation
- You have experienced or witnessed these outcomes
- The responsibility is entirely yours

Your brain has learned, correctly, that glucose levels matter. The problem is that it has generalised this into "I must know the number at all times or something terrible will happen." This is a trauma response. It is your mind trying to protect you using the only tool it has — vigilance.

But vigilance without rest is not protection. It is imprisonment.

## When to seek help

Consider reaching out to a mental health professional if:

- Your checking behaviour is increasing over time rather than stabilising
- You experience physical symptoms of anxiety (racing heart, shortness of breath, nausea) related to glucose monitoring
- You are unable to sleep even when the glucose level is in range and the CGM is alarming appropriately
- Your monitoring behaviour is causing conflict in relationships
- You recognise that your checking is excessive but feel unable to reduce it
- Your child is expressing distress about the frequency of checks
- You have begun avoiding situations (social events, travel, work) because they limit your ability to monitor

A psychologist experienced in health anxiety, OCD, or chronic illness can help you develop strategies to manage the anxiety without abandoning the monitoring. This is not about caring less. It is about suffering less while still caring well.

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) has strong evidence for health anxiety and compulsive checking behaviours. Some diabetes centres now offer integrated psychological support — ask your team if this is available.

## Practical steps towards healthier monitoring

**1. Set defined checking times.**
Work with your diabetes team to establish a monitoring schedule that is clinically appropriate. Write it down. When the urge to check outside these times arises, acknowledge it — "That's anxiety, not a medical need" — and delay for 10 minutes. Often the urge passes.

**2. Trust your CGM thresholds.**
If your CGM is set to alarm below 4.0 and above 13.0, and it is not alarming, you have clinical evidence that you are safe. Practice sitting with that evidence even when your anxiety says otherwise.

**3. Notice the relief cycle.**
Check → relief → anxiety returns → check again. This is a compulsive loop, and each check reinforces it. Breaking the cycle means tolerating discomfort in the short term to reduce it in the long term.

**4. Separate your anxiety from your child's needs.**
If you are a parent, ask yourself honestly: "Am I checking because my child needs it, or because I need it?" Both answers are valid, but they require different responses.

**5. Be compassionate with yourself.**
You developed this pattern because you were trying to keep someone alive. That is not pathology. That is devotion. But devotion that destroys you is not sustainable, and you deserve better than survival mode.

## You are allowed to put the phone down

Somewhere between neglect and obsession is a space where good management lives alongside peace of mind. You may not find it immediately. You may need help getting there. But it exists. And you deserve to live in it.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **There is a clinically meaningful difference between attentive monitoring and anxiety-driven compulsive checking** — the latter causes psychological, physiological, and relational harm.
2. **T1D creates conditions that naturally breed health anxiety** — hypervigilance is a trauma response, not a character flaw.
3. **Excessive checking can paradoxically worsen glucose management** — reacting to every fluctuation introduces instability.
4. **CBT and specialist psychological support are effective** — seek help if checking behaviour is escalating, causing distress, or impairing daily life.
5. **Breaking the check-relief-anxiety cycle requires tolerating short-term discomfort** — with support, this becomes easier over time.
