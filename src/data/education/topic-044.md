# University/College Preparation — Self-Management Away from Home

**Topic ID:** 44
**Age Range:** 16–18
**Audience:** Teens with Type 1 Diabetes
**Category:** Independence & Lifestyle
**Clinically Aligned:** ISPAD 2024, ADA Standards of Care 2025, NICE NG17

---

## The Biggest Transition You Have Done So Far

Leaving home for university or college is a massive change for anyone. For you, it comes with an extra layer: for the first time, there may be no one around who knows your diabetes routine, no one reminding you to check your levels, no one who stocks the fridge with hypo supplies. Your parents or carers, who may have been a quiet safety net your entire life, are suddenly not in the next room.

This is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to prepare. People with T1D go to university every year and do brilliantly. But the ones who do it well tend to be the ones who sorted certain things out before they arrived — not the ones who figured it out during Freshers' Week while sleep-deprived and surrounded by chaos.

## Setting Up Your Diabetes Care Before You Go

This is the most important practical task, and you should start it months before you leave — not the week before.

**Register with a new diabetes team near your university.** If you are moving to a new city, you will need a local diabetes team or endocrinologist. Your current team can refer you, but waiting lists exist, so start early. Ask your current clinic to send a referral and your records. Do not assume this happens automatically.

In the UK, you will need to register with a GP near your university first, then get referred to local diabetes services. In other countries, the process varies, but the principle is the same: you need a local team who can see you, prescribe your supplies, and handle emergencies.

**Sort your prescriptions.** Make sure you know how your insulin, test strips, pump supplies, sensors, and any other prescriptions will work in your new location. If you are crossing state lines in the US, or moving between NHS regions in the UK, there can be administrative gaps. Carry enough supplies to cover at least the first few weeks, plus a buffer.

**Get a letter from your current team.** A summary letter with your diagnosis, current insulin regimen, recent HbA1c, and any complications or special considerations. Keep a copy digitally on your phone and a printed copy in your room. If you end up in A&E at 3am, this letter saves time and prevents errors.

**Register with the student health service.** Most universities have an on-campus health service. Register with them and let them know you have T1D. They can be a useful first point of contact for non-urgent issues and can often fast-track you to specialist services if needed.

**Notify the university disability/accessibility service.** In most countries, T1D qualifies for reasonable adjustments in exams and coursework — extra time, permission to have food and glucose in exams, flexibility around deadlines during medical episodes. You need to register with the relevant service (often called Disability Services or Student Support) to access these, and documentation takes time. Do it before term starts.

## The Flatmate Conversation

You are going to be living with people who probably know nothing about Type 1 Diabetes. That is fine. They do not need to become experts. But they need to know enough to keep you safe.

Within the first few days, have a straightforward conversation with your closest flatmates. Keep it brief and practical:

- "I have Type 1 Diabetes. My body doesn't make insulin, so I take it through injections/a pump."
- "If I ever seem really confused, shaky, or pass out, I'm probably having a low blood sugar. Give me something sugary — juice, sweets, whatever. If I can't swallow or don't respond, call an ambulance."
- "There's a glucagon kit in [specific location] for emergencies. Here's how it works." (Show them. A 30-second demo could save your life.)
- "My supplies are in [specific place]. Please don't move them, and please don't eat my hypo food."

That last point is not trivial. There is nothing worse than reaching for your emergency glucose at 3am and finding your flatmate ate it as a midnight snack. Label your hypo supplies clearly and keep a backup stash somewhere private.

## Building a Routine in an Unstructured Environment

University life is spectacularly unstructured compared to school. Lectures at random times. Late nights. Irregular meals. Social events constantly. This is wonderful for your personal development and challenging for your blood glucose management.

The people who manage T1D well at university tend to build a few non-negotiable anchors into their day:

**Set alarms.** For checking BG, for meals, for supply restocking. Your phone is your best friend here. The freedom of university can mean you genuinely forget to eat lunch because you were in the library for six hours. Alarms catch these gaps.

**Keep your room stocked.** Have a supply station in your room: insulin stored correctly, hypo supplies, spare batteries or chargers for your pump/CGM, test strips, ketone strips. Treat it like your base camp. Restock regularly before you run out, not after.

**Learn to cook — or at least to assemble.** University catering varies hugely in quality and predictability. If you can cook a few basic meals, you have much better control over what you eat and when. This is not about being a chef. It is about being able to feed yourself predictably when the cafeteria is closed or the options are terrible.

**Protect your sleep where you can.** Chronic sleep deprivation affects insulin sensitivity, increases stress hormones, and makes BG management harder across the board. You do not need to be in bed by 9pm, but consistently running on four hours of sleep will make your diabetes harder to manage and your academic performance worse. Both.

## Alcohol, Social Life, and All-Nighters

University social culture often involves alcohol, late nights, and irregular eating — all of which interact directly with T1D management. Read Topic 42 for detail on alcohol specifically.

The key principle: you can participate in social life fully. You just need to do it with slightly more preparation than your friends. Eat before you go out. Carry supplies. Tell someone where your glucose is. Check your levels before bed. These are not restrictions — they are the price of admission, and they become automatic quickly.

All-nighters for studying are a university tradition. They are also terrible for blood glucose control. If you are going to do one, keep snacks available, check your BG more often, and recognise that your readings will probably be erratic. A 3am essay crisis is not improved by a 3am hypo.

## Sick Days Away from Home

At some point, you will get ill at university. Everyone does. But for you, illness can escalate faster because of T1D. You need a sick day plan — and you need to know it before you need it.

Make sure you know: how to check for ketones, when to call your diabetes team, when to go to A&E, and what to do if you are vomiting and cannot keep food or insulin down. Have the number for your local diabetes team, your university health service, and your nearest emergency department saved in your phone.

If you are too unwell to manage your diabetes safely, get help. Do not lie in bed hoping it gets better. DKA can develop within hours during illness, and it is far easier to treat early than late.

## Homesickness and Mental Health

Moving away from home is emotionally hard, and managing a chronic condition alone for the first time adds to that. It is completely normal to feel overwhelmed, anxious, or down in your first weeks or months. The transition is genuinely difficult.

If diabetes management feels like too much on top of everything else, talk to someone — your diabetes team, the university counselling service, your GP, a trusted friend. Struggling is not failure. Asking for help is not weakness. And poor mental health directly affects diabetes management, so looking after your mind is looking after your diabetes.

## Before You Leave Home — A Checklist Mindset

Think of preparation as layers. Before you go, make sure you have:

- New diabetes team referral in progress
- GP registered near campus
- Disability services registered for exam adjustments
- Enough supplies for at least 4–6 weeks
- A medical summary letter (digital and paper)
- Emergency contacts saved and shared
- A plan for teaching flatmates the basics
- Your own sick day protocol written down

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start setting up local diabetes care months before you move** — referrals and GP registration take time, and you cannot afford gaps in your supply chain.
2. **Brief your flatmates in the first week** — they need to know the basics of hypo recognition, where your supplies are, and when to call for help.
3. **Register with disability/accessibility services** for exam adjustments and deadline flexibility — this is your right, not a favour.
4. **Build a few non-negotiable routines** (meal alarms, supply restocking, regular BG checks) to anchor your management in an unstructured environment.
5. **Have a sick day plan before you need it** — know when and how to check for ketones, who to call, and when to go to hospital.
