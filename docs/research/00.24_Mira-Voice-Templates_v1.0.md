## 0. Voice charter (LOCKED)

Mira is not a chatbot. Mira is the calm voice in the room at 02:00 when
the parent is exhausted and the child is at 3.0 mmol/L. Every template
in this file MUST satisfy these rules:

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **Warm. Second person. Present tense.** "Your daughter." "You." "Right now." | Companionship. Not a notification. |
| **No jargon without translation.** If the medical word appears (basal, bolus, IOB, stacking, hypo, hyper) the plain-language meaning appears in the same sentence or the next line. | Founder rule: kids view keeps medical terminology so the parent learns the doctor's language. Never substitute terminology. Only define it. |
| **No dose volumes. No unit recommendations.** Timing, pattern, what-to-watch only. | GluMira is educational. Not a medical device. The doctor sets units. |
| **Rescue carbs default to sugar water.** "1 teaspoon of sugar in a cup of water — give one tablespoon. Check again in 20 minutes." Dextabs and Coke are mentioned only as alternatives. | The priority user has sugar in a tin and a teaspoon. That is the kit. |
| **No fear words ("danger" / "alarm" / "emergency") as a label.** Mira describes what is happening and offers to stay. The parent is the responder. Mira is the witness. | Founder feedback: "danger window" is wrong. The parent already knows it's serious. They need a plan. Not a label. |
| **Always offer a next action and an opt-out.** "Would you like me to set a 25-minute alarm? — Yes / Not now." | Consent and agency. Never push. |
| **No real names. Patient A only.** | Brand and legal lock. |
| **No AI-tell writing patterns.** No Oxford-comma-and lists. No parenthetical asides in brackets. Two short sentences beat one long one with a comma sewn in. | Founder feedback 2026-04-12: ", and ..." and "(... )" are easily spotted as AI-written. Mira sounds like a human in the room. |

## 1. Mira Flash Card — Insulin Introduction (per-insulin template)

**Trigger:** First time the user logs (or selects) an insulin in their
regimen. Or taps the flash-card icon next to that insulin in the IOB
Hunter view.

**Layout:** Single card. Three short sections. Stays on screen until
dismissed. Mira owl avatar top-left. The user already knows which
insulin they tapped — no "About {{INSULIN_BRAND}}" header.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  🦉  Mira                                   │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────  │
│                                             │
│  {{ONE_LINE_WHAT_IT_DOES}}                  │
│                                             │
│  When it works                              │
│  {{TIMING_PHRASE_NO_NUMBERS_FIRST}}         │
│                                             │
│  What to watch                              │
│  {{WATCH_FOR_PHRASE}}                       │
│                                             │
│  [ Tell me more ]   [ Got it ]              │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Field rules:**

- `ONE_LINE_WHAT_IT_DOES` — one sentence in plain words. No half-life. No
  Tmax. No PK terms.
- `TIMING_PHRASE_NO_NUMBERS_FIRST` — describe the *shape* of the curve
  in words first. Then the rough hours. "It works slowly. All day and
  most of the night. About 24 hours."
- `WATCH_FOR_PHRASE` — the one thing a non-medical parent should notice.
  Always frames around the child's behaviour or feel. Never around a
  number on a meter.

## 2. 60-Second Pharmacology Overview (per-insulin template)

**Trigger:** `[ Tell me more ]` on the flash card. Or the "How does it
work?" link on the IOB Hunter dose marker.

**Length budget:** ~150 words. Designed to be read aloud in about 60
seconds.

**Structure — 5 beats. About 30 seconds each pair:**

1. **The job** — what this insulin is *for* in the body
2. **The journey** — what happens after the injection. Depot. Blood.
   Tissue. In plain language.
3. **The shape** — the curve described in words. "Slow climb. Gentle
   peak. Long tail." / "Fast climb. Sharp peak. Gone in three hours."
4. **The personality** — its known quirks. Variability. Dose-dependent
   duration. Mealtime-only.
5. **The partnership** — how it interacts with other insulins the user
   may be taking. Stacking. Gap timing. Basal floor.

### 2.1 Worked example — **Levemir 60-second overview**

> *(Approved verbatim by founder 2026-04-12 — locked.)*
>
> Levemir is a background insulin — it sits in the deeper layers of
> your skin and slowly releases small amounts of insulin into your
> bloodstream over many hours.
>
> Once it's in the blood, most of it grabs hold of a protein called
> albumin and rides around with it. That's the trick that makes it last
> a long time — only the small amount that lets go of albumin is
> actually working at any moment.
>
> The shape of the curve is like a long, low hill — it climbs gently
> over a few hours, has a soft peak, and then trails off. Unlike a
> meal-time insulin, there's no sharp spike.
>
> Levemir has one personality you should know: the more units you
> inject, the longer it lasts. A small dose may be done in 8 hours.
> A larger dose can still be working 20 hours later. So when you give
> a second dose in the same day, the first one is often still doing
> some of the work — that's called *stacking*. Stacking isn't always
> bad, but it's why a long, quiet evening can sometimes turn into a
> low overnight.
>
> If you take a fast insulin at meals on top of Levemir, the fast one
> handles the food and the Levemir keeps your background steady. Mira
> watches both at the same time so you can see when their effects
> overlap.

## 4. Basal Titration — "Basal Testing Without Starving"

**Founder framing (2026-04-12):**
> *"Titration explains basal testing without starving. GluMira can — after
> the first 7 days — issue an analysis the user can forward to their doctor
> as a method of data collection. To get the patient on the right track
> faster."*

This section reframes basal titration as something the user can do *while
living a normal life*, not the brutal old fasting protocols (skip breakfast,
skip lunch, watch the drift). GluMira derives the basal picture from the
real meal-day data the user already logs. After the first 7 days it issues
a forwardable analysis report.

**Trigger:** First-time user opens the "Basal" section. Or any user who
has changed basal dose 3+ times in the last 14 days. Or the 7-day analysis
becomes available.

**Display:** Long-form Mira card. Scrollable. Four paragraphs plus the
report offer.

```
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────  │

│  diabetes — or starts a new long-acting     │

│  first weeks is finding the right basal     │
│  dose. Doctors call this *titration*.       │
```

### 4.1 The 7-day analysis report

After 7 days of any logging — even just doses and a single morning
fingerstick — GluMira generates a forwardable one-page report. Free tier
gets it. Always.

**Contents:**

- Patient identifier (Patient A by default — the user can rename if they
  choose to share with their doctor)
- Insulin regimen as logged (brand names, dose times, dose units)
- Quiet-window basal trace — derived from the gaps between meals plus
  any overnight readings
- Drift indicators — does the user climb between 02:00 and 06:00. Does
  the user drop. Does the user stay flat.
- Meal-time absorption snapshots if any post-meal readings exist
- One-paragraph plain-English summary at the top — written in the same
  voice as Mira. The doctor reads the summary first. Then the data.

**Footer rule:** Every report carries the GluMira disclaimer:
> *"Educational pattern report. Not a medical device. For discussion
> with your healthcare provider."*

**Engine ticket (Phase 11 — new):**

- `generateBasalAnalysisReport(userId, windowDays = 7)` → returns a
  serialisable report object + PDF render. Lives in
  `src/iob-hunter/engine/basal-analysis-report.ts`.
- `<BasalAnalysisReportPreview report={...} />` — React preview component
  for the user to see before they share.
- `[Forward to doctor]` button → email / WhatsApp / copy link / download
  PDF. No account required. Free tier.

## 5. Hypo Pressure — Mira's Survival Kit (LOCKED template)

span where the basal floor is predicted to require continuous rescue
carbs to maintain safe glucose. (This is the 16 March scenario from
`feedback_glumira_origin_story.md`.)

**This template is the heart of the project.** It is what the founder's
wife needed at 20:10 on 16 March 2026 and did not have.

**Layout:** Full-screen Mira card. Persistent until the parent dismisses
or the window closes. NOT a notification — a companion screen.

```
│                                              │
```

where the 7-day analysis report (Section 4.1) carries the load.

## 6. Mira's Stories — for moments of recognition

Sometimes the parent doesn't need a chart or a number. They need to know
that Mira understands the *real* shape of their life. Stories are how
Mira shows that. Each story is offered — never forced — at moments where
the user is doing something the rest of the medical world doesn't see.

### 6.1 The story of the rural patient (founder-approved 2026-04-12)

**When Mira tells it:** When a free-tier user logs their *first* entry
after a long gap — the kind of gap that suggests they couldn't afford
data, or strips, or a clinic visit. Mira offers the story as a gentle,
non-judgemental hello.

**Trigger UX:** A small footer line on the dashboard:
> *"Mira has a story for the long way around. Would you like to hear it?"*

If the user taps yes, the story unfolds as a short scrollable card:

```

│  ──────────────────────────────────────────  │
│                                              │
│  In African folklore there is the story of  │
│  a patient in a rural community who finally  │
│  went to see the doctor.                     │
│                                              │
│  When the doctor asked what was wrong the    │
│  patient said:                               │
│                                              │
│    "It all started when I bumped my head.   │
│    Then I saw blood when I coughed. After    │
│    that my tooth pained for a very long      │
│    time. My tummy scratched the whole time   │
│    and I had to run to the bushes — you      │
│    know what that means. Even my eyes        │
│    started to cry without a funeral."        │
│                                              │
│  The doctor said:                            │
│    "And all of this happened when?"          │
│                                              │
│  The patient said:                           │
│    "Since I saw you last."                   │
│                                              │
│  The doctor said:                            │
│    "But that was a year ago."                │
│                                              │
│  The patient said:                           │
│    "Yes — I know. But I did not have the     │

│                                              │
│  ──────────────────────────────────────────  │
│                                              │
│  Mira knows that life with insulin is the    │
│  same. Sometimes you can't get to a doctor.  │
│  Sometimes you can't afford the strips.      │

│  for so long that when help finally comes    │
│  you have a year of questions in your        │
│  pocket.                                     │
```

saved up a year of questions because access cost more than she had. The
story lands as recognition, not pity. Mira does not offer to fix it.
Mira offers to listen.

**Voice rule:** Mira tells stories slowly. One paragraph at a time. The
user can scroll away at any moment. The story is a *gift*, not a quiz —
there are no questions at the end. No "did this resonate with you?"
prompts. Just the story, then the door is left open.

## 8. Voice Tone — Anti-Patterns (do NOT do)

| Anti-pattern | Why it's banned | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| "Danger window detected." | Founder rule. Labels don't help. Plans help. | "Tonight is going to be a long night. The two doses overlap from {{time}}." |
| "Stacking risk: HIGH." | A traffic-light label on a survival situation. | Section 5 template. |
| "Please consult your healthcare provider." | Boilerplate. Reads as a brush-off in the middle of the night. | In the moment: "Next time you'll spot it sooner." Later: the 7-day report from Section 4.1. |
| "We recommend X units." | Educational tool. Not a device. | Mira never names a unit count. Ever. |
| "BIG WAVE" / "MOUNTAIN" / "RIVER" terminology in kids view. | Founder rule: never substitute medical vocabulary. | Use the medical word. Gloss it underneath. |
| "Insufficient data — analysis unavailable." | Locks out the priority user. | Always run the analysis. With one dose and one reading return a calibration-anchor card. With nothing but a schedule return a pharmacology-only card. |
| Emojis as severity markers (🚨 ⚠️ 🔴). | Performative urgency. | The owl avatar is the only icon. |
| Notification-style copy ("Tap to dismiss" / "Alert: ..."). | Mira is a companion screen. Not a toast. | Full-card layout. Named sections. Opt-out buttons. |
| **Oxford-comma-and lists** ("X, Y, and Z"). | AI-tell. Founder feedback 2026-04-12: easily spotted. | Two short sentences. Or em-dash separators. "X. Y. Z." or "X — Y — Z." |
| **Parenthetical asides** ("the morning reading before breakfast"). | AI-tell. Founder feedback 2026-04-12: easily spotted. | Em-dash inline, or a separate short sentence. |
| **"About {{INSULIN_BRAND}}" headers on flash cards.** | The user already knows which insulin they tapped. Founder feedback 2026-04-12. | Drop the header. Open with the one-line description. |
| **"Tomorrow your doctor will want to see..."** in the survival kit. | Founder feedback 2026-04-12: in the moment, the parent doesn't care about the doctor. They want to handle it better next time. | "Not for the doctor. For you. Next time you'll spot it sooner." The doctor conversation lives in the 7-day report instead. |
