## 1. Universal safety notice

**Required on every insulin card, every dose-entry screen, every medication-change workflow:**

> **⚠️ CHECK YOUR INSULIN LABEL BEFORE EVERY INJECTION**
>
> Before you start to prepare your injection, check the label to make sure you are taking the right type of insulin. This is especially important if you use more than 1 type of insulin. Rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting insulins look similar but behave very differently in your body. A mix-up can cause a serious low or high.
>
> *GluMira™ is an educational platform, not a medical device.*

**Source:** Tresiba FDA label NDA 203314 (2022 revision), Patient Package Insert. **Applies to all 13 canonical insulins** — this wording is standard across insulin product labels globally.

**Engine implementation (Phase 1):** Add a `checkLabelNotice` component that renders above every dose-entry form. For users with >1 insulin type in their regimen, the notice is **bold red** and the user must acknowledge it once per session before entering doses.

## 2. Adverse reactions

### 2.1 Class-wide adverse reactions (apply to ALL 13 insulins)

Per FDA class labeling and Tresiba FDA label NDA 203314:

| Reaction | Frequency (Tresiba T1DM) | Frequency (Tresiba T2DM) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hypoglycemia** | Most common | Most common | THE single most important adverse reaction. Can be life-threatening. |
| **Hypersensitivity** (swelling of tongue/lips, diarrhoea, nausea, tiredness, itching, urticaria) | 0.9% | 0.9% | Generalised anaphylaxis is rare but documented. |
| **Lipodystrophy** (lipohypertrophy or lipoatrophy at injection sites) | 0.3% | 0.3% | Caused by failure to rotate injection sites. |
| **Pruritus / rash at injection site** | Common | Common | Usually transient. |
| **Edema** | 0.9% | 3.0% | More common in T2DM; fluid retention on insulin initiation. |
| **Weight gain** | Common | Common | Associated with improved glycaemic control. |
| **Hypokalaemia** (low potassium) | Rare but serious | Rare but serious | Insulin shifts potassium intracellularly. **Warn especially for users on diuretics or with renal impairment.** |

### 2.2 Severe hypoglycaemia — GluMira's primary concern

> Severe hypoglycaemia can cause seizures, unconsciousness, or death. Symptoms include: shakiness, sweating, confusion, fast heartbeat, hunger, dizziness, blurred vision, headache, and weakness. Treat immediately with fast-acting glucose (glucose tablets, juice, sugar water, honey). **If the person cannot swallow, do not give oral glucose — seek emergency help immediately.**

**This is the event GluMira exists to prevent.** The 60-second insight + hypo pressure callout + Mira survival kit are built around this.

### 2.3 Insulin-specific adverse reaction notes

**Levemir:** Same class profile. No dose-specific adverse reaction data beyond the class.

**Lantus / Basaglar / Toujeo:** Injection site pain slightly more common due to the acidic (pH 4) formulation causing a stinging sensation on injection. Documented in FDA label NDA 021081.

**Humulin N / Insulatard (NPH):** Weight gain slightly more common than with analogs due to broader action profile causing more between-meal insulin exposure.

**Fiasp:** Early post-injection reactions (redness, urticaria at site) reported in ~1-2% more than NovoRapid, attributed to the niacinamide + arginine excipients.

**Lyumjev:** Similar profile to Humalog; injection site reactions (pain, burning) slightly more common due to treprostinil + citrate excipients causing local vasodilation.

**Apidra:** No specific notes — same class profile.

**Actrapid / Humulin R:** Due to slower onset, users may inject and then **eat too soon**, causing immediate post-meal high followed by 2-3h low when the regular insulin peaks. This is a **user-education issue**, not a product defect — but GluMira must flag it.

## 3. Drug interactions

### 3.1 The insulin drug-interaction framework (FDA class labeling)

Every insulin FDA label lists drug interactions under three categories:

1. **Drugs that may INCREASE the blood glucose-lowering effect** (→ hypoglycaemia risk)
2. **Drugs that may DECREASE the blood glucose-lowering effect** (→ hyperglycaemia risk)
3. **Drugs that may INCREASE OR DECREASE the blood glucose-lowering effect** (→ monitoring required)

### 3.2 Drugs that INCREASE insulin effect (more hypo risk)

| Drug class | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Other antidiabetic drugs** | Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide), meglitinides (repaglinide), DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin), GLP-1 agonists (liraglutide, semaglutide), SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin), metformin | Additive glucose-lowering |
| **ACE inhibitors** | Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril | Improve insulin sensitivity, favour hypoglycaemia |
| **ARBs** | Losartan, valsartan | Similar to ACE inhibitors |
| **Fibric acid derivatives** | Fenofibrate, gemfibrozil | Increase insulin sensitivity |
| **Fluoxetine, MAO inhibitors** | SSRI antidepressants | Increase insulin sensitivity |
| **Pentoxifylline** | (vasodilator) | Enhances glucose uptake |
| **Salicylates (high dose)** | Aspirin >3g/day | Pancreatic effect |
| **Sulfonamide antibiotics** | Sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim) | Displace protein binding |
| **Somatostatin analogs** | Octreotide, lanreotide | Suppress counter-regulatory hormones |

### 3.3 Drugs that DECREASE insulin effect (more hyper risk)

| Drug class | Examples | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Atypical antipsychotics** | Olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine, risperidone | Cause insulin resistance, weight gain |
| **Corticosteroids** | Prednisone, dexamethasone, hydrocortisone | Directly elevate blood glucose, cause insulin resistance |
| **Danazol** | (hormonal) | Insulin antagonism |
| **Diuretics (thiazides, loop)** | Hydrochlorothiazide, furosemide | Impair insulin sensitivity |
| **Estrogens and progestogens** | Oral contraceptives, HRT | Cause insulin resistance |
| **Glucagon** | (emergency hypo treatment) | Direct glucose elevation — intentional |
| **Isoniazid** | TB antibiotic | Interferes with insulin secretion |
| **Niacin** | (high dose, for dyslipidaemia) | Reduces insulin sensitivity |
| **Phenothiazines** | Chlorpromazine | Reduce insulin secretion |
| **Protease inhibitors** | Ritonavir, atazanavir (HIV meds) | Cause insulin resistance |
| **Somatropin** | Growth hormone | Insulin antagonism |
| **Sympathomimetics** | Epinephrine, albuterol, terbutaline | Stress-response glucose elevation |
| **Thyroid hormones** | Levothyroxine (if over-replaced) | Accelerated glucose metabolism — complex effect |

### 3.4 Drugs that INCREASE OR DECREASE insulin effect (monitoring)

| Drug class | Examples | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Alcohol** | Ethanol | **Biphasic** — initial hypo (especially fasting) then rebound hyper. Very dangerous; commonly causes severe overnight hypos. |
| **Beta-blockers** | Propranolol, metoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol | **BLUNT HYPO SYMPTOMS** — users cannot feel their low coming. This is why beta-blockers are a **critical flag** for GluMira. Also slightly impair insulin release. |
| **Clonidine** | (blood pressure) | Variable, blunts hypo symptoms similar to beta-blockers |
| **Lithium salts** | (bipolar disorder) | Unpredictable, both directions reported |
| **Pentamidine** | (antiprotozoal) | Hypo then hyper biphasic (similar to alcohol) |

### 3.5 The "hypoglycaemia unawareness" high-risk combination

**If a user reports being on a beta-blocker AND insulin:** GluMira must flag this in the profile as "hypoglycaemia unawareness risk." The user's normal hypo symptoms (shakiness, sweating, fast heartbeat) are suppressed by the beta-blocker. This makes **continuous glucose monitoring or more frequent fingerstick testing essential**, and makes the engine's predictive hypo pressure detection **even more critical** because the user cannot rely on feel.

This is a module-level rule. The profile captures it; the engine's narrative generator calls it out in the 60-second insight when relevant.

## 4. Use in specific populations

Structure per FDA label Section 8. Template derived from Tresiba NDA 203314 (most recent comprehensive populations data).

### 4.1 Pregnancy (Section 8.1)

**Class-wide:** Insulin is the treatment of choice for diabetes in pregnancy because it does not cross the placenta in clinically meaningful amounts. **All human insulins and most analogs are considered safe for pregnancy** based on extensive clinical use.

**Specific analog data:**

| Insulin | Pregnancy category (legacy) / current data |
|---|---|
| **Regular human insulin (Actrapid, Humulin R)** | Gold standard for pregnancy; most experience |
| **NPH (Humulin N, Insulatard)** | Gold standard for pregnancy; most experience |
| **Humalog (lispro)** | Class B → current: adequate data, considered safe |
| **NovoRapid (aspart)** | Class B → current: adequate data, considered safe |
| **Apidra (glulisine)** | Class C → limited human data; use if benefit outweighs risk |
| **Fiasp (faster aspart)** | Limited pregnancy data; the niacinamide excipient adds uncertainty |
| **Lyumjev (ultra-rapid lispro)** | Limited pregnancy data; the treprostinil + citrate excipients add uncertainty |
| **Lantus / Basaglar (glargine U-100)** | Observational data reassuring; no teratogenicity signal |
| **Toujeo (glargine U-300)** | Limited data; generally treated same as Lantus |
| **Levemir (detemir)** | **Approved for pregnancy use** — EMA and FDA. Reassuring observational and randomised data (Mathiesen et al. 2012) |
| **Tresiba (degludec)** | **Limited pregnancy data.** Per 2022 FDA label: "Available data from published studies and postmarketing case reports have not reported a clear association with insulin degludec and major birth defects or miscarriage risk. There are risks to the mother and fetus associated with poorly controlled diabetes in pregnancy." |

**GluMira module trigger:** If user has `pregnancy_flag: true`, the IOB Hunter module switches to the **Pregnancy Module** (existing module `PregnancyModule.tsx`) which adjusts:
- Insulin sensitivity expectations (increases in T1, may decrease in T2/T3)
- ISF recalculation based on trimester
- Flagged warnings for Fiasp, Lyumjev, Apidra, and Tresiba (limited data)
- Preferred analog recommendations (Levemir, NPH, Humalog, NovoRapid have the most data)

### 4.2 Labor and delivery (Section 8.2)

Insulin requirements typically drop dramatically during active labor and can drop by 50% or more in the first 24-48h postpartum. **This is a danger window for severe hypo** — the mother's own glucose utilisation during labor, the immediate drop in placental hormones post-delivery, and breastfeeding-related glucose expenditure all compound.

**Engine implication:** If `pregnancy_flag: true` AND estimated delivery date is within 2 weeks, the IOB Hunter displays a persistent warning: "Insulin needs often drop 30-50% during labor and the first days postpartum. Discuss a dose-reduction plan with your care team before labor begins."

### 4.3 Lactation (Section 8.3)

Insulin does not pass into breast milk in clinically meaningful amounts. **All insulins are considered compatible with breastfeeding.** However, breastfeeding itself lowers glucose, so users should be counselled to eat carbohydrate before or during a feed to prevent hypo.

### 4.4 Paediatric use (Section 8.4)

**Tresiba FDA label (2022 revision):** Population PK analysis from 199 paediatric subjects (1 to <18 years) with T1DM. Body weight was the only significant covariate. After body-weight adjustment, exposure at steady state was **independent of age**.

**Class-wide paediatric guidance:**
- **Dosing is weight-based** (typically 0.4-1.0 U/kg/day total daily dose, split between basal and bolus)
- **Small doses require half-unit precision** — hence the need for the NovoPen Echo or similar half-unit pen
- **Young children** (<6) often require <2U per bolus — syringes with 0.5U markings are essential if pens are not available
- **Absorption** is faster than in adults due to thinner subcutaneous tissue
- **Variability** is higher than in adults
- **Hypoglycaemia recognition** may be impaired in young children who cannot articulate symptoms; caregivers must monitor closely
- **Growth and puberty** cause significant insulin requirement changes — dose review every 2-3 months is typical

**Insulins specifically approved for paediatric use (FDA):**

| Insulin | Minimum age |
|---|---|
| **Humalog** | ≥3 years |
| **NovoRapid / NovoLog** | ≥2 years |
| **Apidra** | ≥4 years |
| **Fiasp** | ≥1 year (T1DM) |
| **Lyumjev** | ≥18 years (no paediatric approval as of 2024) |
| **Humulin R / Novolin R** | all ages |
| **Humulin N / Novolin N** | all ages |
| **Lantus** | ≥6 years |
| **Basaglar** | ≥6 years |
| **Toujeo** | ≥6 years |
| **Levemir** | ≥2 years |
| **Tresiba** | ≥1 year |

**GluMira paediatric module:** The existing `PaediatricModule.tsx` should be extended to:
- Flag insulin choices that are off-label for the patient's age
- Enforce half-unit dosing for patients under 12 years
- Display Mira in "kid-friendly" visual variant (bigger, more prominent) — but **do NOT change medical terminology** per founder instruction 2026-04-11
- Surface the caregiver-aware narrative (acknowledging the parent is the operator, not the child)

### 4.5 Geriatric use (Section 8.5)

Per Tresiba FDA label: Pharmacokinetics at steady state were similar in younger adult and geriatric subjects, **but with greater between-subject variability among geriatric subjects**. Caution warranted — initial dosing, dose increments, and maintenance dosage should be conservative to avoid hypoglycaemia. **Hypoglycaemia may be more difficult to recognise in the elderly** due to:

- Blunted counter-regulatory response
- Concurrent beta-blocker use (common in elderly for hypertension/arrhythmia)
- Cognitive impairment
- Living alone (no witnesses)
- Polypharmacy interactions

**GluMira elderly module (not yet built):** When `patient_age >= 65`, the engine should:
- Widen the target glucose range by ~1 mmol/L upward
- Increase the hypo pressure sensitivity (lower threshold for calling a window)
- Add "Is there someone you can call if you feel unwell?" to the Mira survival kit opener
- Flag concurrent beta-blocker use as doubly critical for hypo masking

### 4.6 Renal impairment (Section 8.6)

**Per Tresiba FDA label:** PK studied in 32 subjects across normal (CrCl ≥90), mild (60-89), moderate (30-59), severe (<30), and ESRD. **Hemodialysis did not affect clearance** in ESRD.

**Class-wide implication:** As renal function declines, **insulin clearance slows** — meaning the same dose lasts longer and creates more hypo risk. Most guidelines recommend:

- **CrCl 30-59 (moderate CKD):** Reduce total daily insulin dose by ~25% as a starting point
- **CrCl <30 (severe CKD / ESRD):** Reduce total daily insulin dose by ~50% as a starting point
- **Dialysis days:** Insulin needs often drop further; monitor closely

**GluMira engine flag:** `renal_function` field on patient profile with values `normal | mild | moderate | severe | esrd`. When set to moderate or worse, the engine:
- Extends the DOA estimate for that patient by ~20-40%
- Increases hypo pressure sensitivity
- Surfaces a flag on every dose entry: "Your kidney function affects how long insulin stays active. Discuss dose adjustments with your care team."

### 4.7 Hepatic impairment (Section 8.7)

**Per Tresiba FDA label:** Studied in 24 subjects (mild, moderate, severe hepatic impairment, Child-Pugh 5-15). **No differences in PK identified** between healthy subjects and subjects with hepatic impairment. However, **"glucose monitoring should be intensified and dosage adjusted on an individual basis."**

**Class-wide implication:** The liver is the primary site of insulin clearance for most insulins. Severe hepatic impairment can:
- Prolong insulin action
- Impair gluconeogenesis (the body's natural counter-regulation during a low)
- Cause unpredictable swings

**GluMira engine flag:** `hepatic_function` field with values `normal | mild | moderate | severe`. When moderate or worse:
- Warn about impaired counter-regulation
- Suggest more frequent glucose monitoring
- Increase hypo pressure sensitivity

## 5. Injection technique — drawing from vial

### 5.1 Step-by-step (from ADCES / MedlinePlus / UW Health patient education)

**Supplies:** insulin vial, insulin syringe (matching concentration — U-100 syringe for U-100 insulin, etc.), alcohol wipe, sharps disposal container.

1. **Wash hands** thoroughly with soap and water; dry well.
2. **Check the label** on the insulin vial — right name, right concentration, right expiry date, right appearance (clear for rapid/short/glargine/detemir/degludec; cloudy for NPH only).
3. **If using NPH (cloudy):** Roll the vial gently between your palms 10-20 times to mix. **Do NOT shake** — shaking causes foaming and clumping.
4. **Clean the vial top** with an alcohol wipe if the rubber stopper is not freshly exposed.
5. **Remove the cap** from the syringe needle.
6. **Pull back the plunger** to draw in an amount of **air equal to your insulin dose** (e.g., pull to 10U marking if you need 10U). The tip of the black plunger should line up with the dose number.
7. **Insert the needle** through the rubber stopper on the vial. Hold the syringe like a pencil.
8. **Push the plunger down** — injecting the air into the vial. This equalises the pressure so the insulin flows out easily.
9. **Turn the vial upside down** (syringe below, vial above) with the needle still inserted.
10. **Pull the plunger back** slowly until the black tip reaches the number of units you need. **Check for air bubbles.**
11. **If there are air bubbles:** push the plunger back up to push the air back into the vial, then pull down again to the correct dose. Repeat until no bubbles.
12. **Remove the needle** from the vial. **Do not let the needle touch anything** until you inject.
13. **Inject** per the site selection protocol in Section 7.

### 5.2 Mixing two insulins in one syringe (NPH + Regular)

**Only regular human insulin and NPH can be mixed in the same syringe.** Rapid-acting analogs and long-acting analogs should NEVER be mixed. **Tresiba, Levemir, Lantus, Basaglar, and Toujeo must be in separate syringes, always.**

Order (for NPH + Regular mix):
1. Draw air for NPH dose, inject into NPH vial, withdraw needle (no insulin yet)
2. Draw air for Regular dose, inject into Regular vial, then draw the Regular insulin
3. Insert needle into NPH vial (now already pressurised from step 1) and draw the NPH
4. Inject within 5 minutes to prevent the insulins from destabilising each other

**Most modern guidance:** use pens or pumps where possible. Mixing syringes is a technique from the 1980s-90s that is increasingly unnecessary except in low-resource settings where analog pens are unaffordable.

## 6. Injection technique — pens (full and half-unit)

### 6.1 Standard insulin pen (1-unit increments)

1. **Wash hands.**
2. **Check the pen label** — right insulin, right expiry, right appearance.
3. **If NPH or premix (cloudy):** roll the pen gently 10-20 times to mix.
4. **Attach a new needle** — twist on until snug, then remove outer and inner caps. **Never reuse needles** — they dull, cause pain, and increase lipohypertrophy risk.
5. **Prime the pen** ("air shot"): dial up 2 units, hold the pen with the needle pointing up, press the plunger fully. You should see a drop of insulin at the needle tip. If not, repeat. **Priming clears air from the needle and cartridge and confirms the pen is working.**
6. **Dial your dose.** Double-check the number in the dose window.
7. **Inject** at a 90° angle (or 45° if very lean) into a rotated injection site (Section 7).
8. **Hold the button down for 10 seconds** after the plunger reaches zero. This ensures the full dose is delivered and prevents insulin leaking back out.
9. **Remove the needle from the skin.**
10. **Remove the needle from the pen** and discard in a sharps container. **Do not leave the needle on the pen** between injections — it allows air in, contaminates the cartridge, and can cause dose errors.

### 6.2 Half-unit pen (NovoPen Echo, HumaPen Luxura HD)

**NovoPen Echo** (Novo Nordisk — the "C-Twist" pen the founder referenced):

- **Half-unit increments** (0.5 U) up to 30 U per dose
- **Memory function** — displays the time and amount of the last dose
- **Uses NovoFine, NovoFine Plus, or NovoTwist needles** (NovoTwist attaches by twisting, designed for easier use by children and those with dexterity issues)
- **Target user:** paediatric, small-dose adults, and users who need precise fine-tuning
- **Studies:** 80% of users preferred NovoPen Echo to other paediatric insulin pens

**How to dial half-units on NovoPen Echo:**
1. Each click of the dial is 0.5 U
2. The dose display shows halves as a separator (e.g., "3" then "3.5" then "4")
3. Priming uses 1.0 U (2 clicks), not 0.5 U
4. If you dial past the correct dose, turn the dial **back** — the pen does not dispense while dialling back

**HumaPen Luxura HD** (Eli Lilly — uses Humalog cartridges):
- Half-unit increments, up to 30 U per dose
- No memory function
- Uses BD Ultra-Fine needles

## 7. Injection site selection

### 7.1 The four approved injection sites

From the Forum for Injection Technique (FIT) guidelines and ADA Standards of Care:

1. **Abdomen** — **fastest absorption.** Inject across the abdomen from just below the ribcage to well below the belt line. **Stay two finger widths away from the belly button** (navel area has altered absorption).
2. **Upper outer arms** — second fastest. Use the fleshy area on the **back and side** of the upper arm, not the inner surface. Requires a second person's help to reach safely unless the user has good arm flexibility.
3. **Outer upper thighs** — third fastest. Use the **top and outer** areas only; **avoid the inner thigh and the back of the thigh** (near arteries and nerves). Stay **three to four finger widths away from both the knee and the groin.**
4. **Upper outer buttocks** — slowest absorption. Inject in the area where a pant belt would sit, on the upper outer quadrant.

### 7.2 Absorption-rate ordering (fast → slow)

> **Abdomen → Arms → Thighs → Buttocks**

This matters because **the same dose behaves differently** depending on where it's injected:
- **Rapid-acting meal bolus** → abdomen (fastest onset, aligns with meal peak)
- **Basal insulin** → thigh or buttock (slower, steadier release matches basal intent)
- **Avoid switching sites mid-regimen** without checking dose effectiveness — a Lantus dose that "worked" in the thigh may hit harder in the abdomen.

### 7.3 Site selection for newly diagnosed

**Founder instruction (2026-04-12): "Add picking the bolus site for new diagnosed. Apply to paediatric."**

**Newly-diagnosed educational flow (Mira voice):**

> "You'll be giving yourself (or your child) several injections a day. Over time you'll develop preferences, but here's the place to start:
>
> **For your mealtime (bolus) insulin** — start with the **abdomen**. It absorbs fastest, which matches your meal. Inject at least two finger widths away from your belly button, anywhere from your ribcage down to your belt line.
>
> **For your long-acting (basal) insulin** — start with the **outer thigh** or the **upper outer buttock**. These absorb more slowly, which matches the job of a basal insulin (slow, steady background coverage for the full day).
>
> **The biggest mistake new users make** is injecting in the same spot every time. The skin and fat underneath get damaged over weeks and months, creating lumps called 'lipohypertrophy' that make the insulin absorb unpredictably. **Rotate every single injection.** We'll show you how in the next card."

### 7.4 Paediatric site selection notes

**For children under 6:** Thighs and buttocks are preferred because the skin is thinner on the abdomen and the needle insertion angle matters more. **Use a shorter needle** (4mm) to avoid intramuscular injection.

**For children 6-12:** All four sites acceptable but rotate carefully. Caregivers should use the **pinch-up technique** (lift a fold of skin between thumb and finger) to avoid IM injection in lean children.

**For adolescents:** Adult protocol applies but watch for **injection avoidance** — teens sometimes use the same site repeatedly because it "hurts less," leading to lipohypertrophy. Encourage rotation via the structured week-by-section approach below.

## 8. Site rotation protocol

**FIT UK / ADA recommendation:** The **weekly quadrant method**:

1. **Divide each injection area into 4 quadrants.** For the abdomen: upper-left, upper-right, lower-left, lower-right.
2. **Use one quadrant per week.** All injections that week go into that one quadrant.
3. **Within the quadrant, rotate systematically** — each injection at least 1cm (one finger width) from the previous, working in a spiral or grid pattern.
4. **Next week, move to the next quadrant.** Continue rotating through all four before returning to the first.
5. **This gives each skin section ~3 weeks of rest** before the next injection in that area, which is enough for healing.

**Engine implementation:** Track `injection_site` + `injection_quadrant` + `timestamp` across recent doses. If the same quadrant has been used for >7 consecutive days, suggest rotation to the next quadrant. If a specific square-inch area has been used >3 times, specifically flag that.

## 9. Paediatric-specific

### 9.1 The C-Twist (NovoTwist needle) for NovoPen Echo

**Founder instruction:** "Add how to use C twist pen for insulin Full and 0.5 dosage - Apply to paediatrics."

**NovoTwist needle attachment:**

1. **Remove** the protective paper tab from a new NovoTwist needle.
2. **Align** the NovoTwist's twist mechanism with the pen's needle port.
3. **Twist until you hear or feel a click.** The needle is now attached. You do NOT need to push and screw like with threaded needles — the twist is the only motion required.
4. **Remove** the outer and inner needle caps.
5. **Prime** the pen with a 1U air shot.
6. **Inject** per standard technique.
7. **After injection:** twist the needle off in the opposite direction and drop it into the sharps container.

**The twist mechanism was designed specifically for:**
- Children's smaller hands and grip strength
- Adults with arthritis or limited dexterity
- Speed (no fumbling with threaded caps)

### 9.2 Half-unit dosing on NovoPen Echo (the paediatric precision need)

**Why half-units matter for kids:**

A 30kg child on a 0.1 U/kg-per-meal correction factor needs **3U per meal**. But a 25kg child needs **2.5U**. A 20kg child needs **2U**. With a 1-unit pen, the 2.5U dose would have to round to either 2 or 3 — a 20% error in either direction, which matters enormously for young children where total daily insulin might be only 8-12U.

**Half-unit precision:**

- 0.5U increments eliminate rounding error
- Particularly valuable for bolus (meal) doses where carb ratios produce decimal results (e.g., 15g carbs ÷ 10g/U carb ratio = 1.5U)
- Also valuable for correction doses (e.g., BG 8.5 → target 5.5 with ISF 2.0 = 1.5U correction)

**How to dial 2.5U on NovoPen Echo:**

1. Starting from 0 on the dose display, turn the dial **clockwise**.
2. Each click = 0.5U. You will hear/feel 5 clicks to reach 2.5U.
3. The display shows: 0 → 0.5 → 1 → 1.5 → 2 → 2.5.
4. If you overshoot, turn the dial **counter-clockwise** — the pen does NOT dispense while dialling back. You can correct safely.
5. Press the plunger to deliver.

### 9.3 Paediatric module additions

The existing `PaediatricModule.tsx` should be extended with:
- **Weight-based dosing calculators** (basal, ICR, ISF) that output decimal values
- **Half-unit rounding enforcement** — all dose outputs round to nearest 0.5U, never to nearest 1U
- **Pen model field** in the patient profile — if `pen_model === "NovoPen_Echo"`, the UI accepts and displays half-units; if `pen_model === "standard"`, warn on decimal entries
- **Growth-related dose review reminder** — every 90 days, prompt "Has {child name}'s weight changed since the last review?"
- **Caregiver narrative mode** — Mira speaks to the parent about the child, not to the child directly (e.g., "your daughter's afternoon insulin is still working...")
