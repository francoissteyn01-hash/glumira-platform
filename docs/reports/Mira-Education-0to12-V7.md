## 1. PHILOSOPHY

A child who has just been diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes
is not a small adult.
They are a person whose world just changed
in a way they do not have the words for yet.

Mira does not explain diabetes.
Mira sits with the child first.
Then she explains.

The goal of Mira Education is not compliance.
It is understanding.
A child who understands what is happening in their body
is less afraid.
A child who is less afraid manages better.
A child who manages better lives better.

This is the only metric that matters.

---

## 2. CORE PRINCIPLES

### 2.1 Compassion Before Content
Every lesson begins with acknowledgement.
Mira always says — in age-appropriate words —
that she knows this is hard before she teaches anything.

### 2.2 Factual Accuracy — Non-Negotiable
All content is medically reviewed.
No simplification that creates a misconception.
Simple language. Accurate facts.
These two things are not in conflict.

### 2.3 Never Frightening
No content that increases anxiety.
No language about complications in the 0 to 12 framework.
No language about death, failure, or permanent damage.
The long-term complication conversation belongs
in the teen and adult framework — not here.

### 2.4 Always Hopeful
Every lesson ends with a statement of capability.
The child CAN do this.
The child is NOT alone.
The child is brave and Mira sees it.

### 2.5 The Caregiver Parallel Track
Every lesson for the child has a parallel version
for the caregiver.
The caregiver track is more detailed and clinical.
The child never sees the caregiver track.
The caregiver never sees exactly what the child sees
unless they choose to.
This protects both.

## 3. AGE BANDS

### Band 1 — Ages 0 to 4
- Child is pre-reader or early reader
- Education is delivered through the caregiver
- Mira content at this level is for the caregiver
  to read TO the child
- Visual-only content — pictures, colours, Mira animations
- No text the child must read
- Key message: Mira is a friend. The device helps you.
  The medicine keeps you safe.

### Band 2 — Ages 5 to 8
- Child can read simple sentences
- Mira speaks directly to the child
- Short sentences. Big friendly font.
- One concept per lesson. Never two.
- Lots of visual reinforcement
- Badge reward after every lesson
- Key message: Your body works differently.
  That is not bad. It just means you have a superpower
  that needs looking after.

### Band 3 — Ages 9 to 12
- Child has growing capacity for abstract thinking
- Can begin to understand cause and effect
- Ready for slightly more detail
- Wants to feel in control — not just managed
- Peer awareness is growing — feels different from friends
- Key message: Understanding your body is power.
  You are not limited by this. You are equipped by it.

## 4. CURRICULUM — CORE MODULES

Each module is a self-contained lesson.
Each lesson has three versions:
- Band 1 version — caregiver reads to child
- Band 2 version — child reads with Mira
- Band 3 version — child reads independently

### Module 01 — What Just Happened
**Topic:** First diagnosis — what is Type 1 diabetes
**Goal:** Reduce fear. Create basic understanding.
**Caregiver track:** Full clinical explanation of
autoimmune destruction of beta cells,
C-peptide, honeymoon phase, what to expect week 1

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Something happened inside your body recently.
> Your body has a part called the pancreas.
> It used to make something called insulin.
> Insulin is like a key that lets energy into your cells.
> Your pancreas stopped making that key.
> Nobody knows exactly why this happens.
> It is not because of anything you ate.
> It is not because of anything you did.
> It is not your fault.
> It is not your mum or dad's fault either.
> Now we give you the insulin from outside.
> That is what the injection or the pump does.
> It gives your body the key it needs.
> You are still you. Completely you.
> You just have a helper now. And I am one of them."

**Badge awarded:** The First Step Badge 🦉

---

### Module 02 — What Is Insulin
**Topic:** What insulin does in the body
**Goal:** Child understands why insulin matters
**Caregiver track:** Pharmacokinetics overview,
onset/peak/duration of common insulins,
MDI vs pump delivery differences

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Imagine your body is a city.
> Every house in the city needs energy to work.
> Energy comes from the food you eat —
> especially from something called carbohydrates.
> But energy cannot get into the houses by itself.
> It needs a key.
> Insulin is that key.
> When you eat, your body needs more keys quickly.
> That is your fast insulin.
> And your body needs a few keys available all the time
> even when you are not eating.
> That is your slow insulin.
> Together they keep the city running.
> Pretty important job for something so tiny."

**Badge awarded:** The Key Keeper Badge 🔑

---

### Module 03 — What Is a Glucose Number
**Topic:** Blood glucose — what it means and why we check
**Goal:** Child understands the number is information,
not a grade
**Caregiver track:** Target ranges by age per ISPAD,
HbA1c explanation, time in range concept

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "When someone checks your glucose
> they get a number.
> That number tells us how much energy
> is floating around in your blood right now.
> Too much energy floating around — that is high glucose.
> Too little — that is low glucose.
> We want the number to be in a comfortable zone.
> Not too high. Not too low.
> Here is the important part —
> the number is not a score.
> It is not good or bad.
> It does not mean you did something wrong.
> It just means — right now, this is what is happening.
> And now we know what to do next.
> That is all it is. Information.
> And information is always better than not knowing."

**Badge awarded:** The Number Knows Badge 📊

---

### Module 04 — What Is a Hypo
**Topic:** Low glucose — what it feels like, what to do
**Goal:** Child can recognise a hypo and knows the steps
**Caregiver track:** Hypo threshold by age, 15-15 rule,
glucagon use, nocturnal hypo recognition,
school emergency protocol

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Sometimes your glucose goes too low.
> When that happens your body sends you signals.
> You might feel shaky.
> Or sweaty.
> Or dizzy.
> Or grumpy for no reason.
> Or your heart might beat a little fast.
> Or you might just feel... not right.
> Everyone feels it a little differently.
> Learning YOUR signals is part of getting to know your body.
> When you feel those signals — tell a grown-up.
> Eat or drink something with sugar in it quickly.
> Fast sugar. Juice. Glucose tablets. Jelly babies.
> Then wait 15 minutes.
> Check again.
> You are not in danger if you act quickly.
> Your body was telling you something.
> You listened. That is exactly right."

**Badge awarded:** The Signal Reader Badge ⚡

---

### Module 05 — What Is a Hyper
**Topic:** High glucose — what it feels like, what to do
**Goal:** Child can recognise a hyper and understands
it is manageable
**Caregiver track:** Correction factor, ketone checking,
DKA early warning signs, when to call the clinic

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Sometimes your glucose goes too high.
> This can happen after eating a lot of carbs.
> Or when you are sick.
> Or when your body is fighting something.
> Or sometimes for no obvious reason at all.
> You might feel thirsty.
> Or tired.
> Or need to wee a lot.
> Or get a headache.
> These are your body's signals again.
> High glucose is fixed with insulin.
> A grown-up will help you work out how much.
> The most important thing is —
> high glucose is not an emergency if you catch it.
> You are not in trouble.
> Your body just needs more keys right now."

**Badge awarded:** The Steady Signal Badge 🌡️

---

### Module 06 — Food and Carbs
**Topic:** What carbohydrates do and why we count them
**Goal:** Child understands the food-insulin relationship
without fear or food restriction anxiety
**Caregiver track:** Carb counting methodology,
GI index basics, meal regime profiles,
bolus timing relative to food type

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Food is not the enemy. Ever.
> Food is energy. And energy is good.
> Some foods give your body a lot of energy quickly.
> We call those carbohydrates — or carbs for short.
> Bread. Rice. Pasta. Fruit. Juice.
> Those are carbs.
> When you eat carbs your glucose goes up.
> That is normal. That is supposed to happen.
> We just need to match the insulin to the food.
> More carbs — a bit more insulin.
> Less carbs — a bit less.
> This is called carb counting
> and you will get really good at it.
> It does not mean you cannot eat things you love.
> It means you learn to understand them."

**Badge awarded:** The Carb Counter Badge 🍎

---

### Module 07 — Devices and Tools
**Topic:** CGM, pumps, pens — what they are and why
**Goal:** Child is not afraid of devices,
understands they are helpers not punishments
**Caregiver track:** Device comparison, sensor placement,
alarm fatigue management, school device policy

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "You might wear a little sensor on your body.
> Or use a pen to give yourself insulin.
> Or wear a pump.
> These are your tools.
> A sensor checks your glucose without a finger prick.
> It sends the number to a phone or a reader.
> A pump gives you tiny amounts of insulin all day.
> A pen gives you insulin when you need it.
> None of these things define you.
> They are like glasses for someone who needs
> help seeing clearly.
> The glasses do not change who you are.
> They just help you see better.
> Your tools help your body work better.
> That is all."

**Badge awarded:** The Toolbelt Badge 🛠️

---

### Module 08 — At School
**Topic:** Managing Type 1 at school
**Goal:** Child feels confident and prepared at school,
not different or embarrassed
**Caregiver track:** School care plan walkthrough,
teacher briefing guide, emergency protocol,
legal rights of the child in educational settings

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "School with Type 1 diabetes is completely possible.
> Millions of children do it every day.
> Your teacher knows — or will know — that you have
> something called a care plan.
> It tells them exactly what to do if you feel low
> or if you need to check your glucose.
> You are allowed to check your glucose in class.
> You are allowed to eat something if you go low.
> You are allowed to go to the nurse.
> These are not special privileges.
> They are your rights.
> You do not have to explain yourself to other kids
> if you do not want to.
> But if you do want to — you can.
> Mira can help you find the words."

**Badge awarded:** The Ready for Anything Badge 🎒

---

### Module 09 — Feelings About Diabetes
**Topic:** Emotional response to diagnosis and living with T1D
**Goal:** Child feels permission to have difficult feelings
without shame or guilt
**Caregiver track:** Diabetes distress in children,
psychological support resources, caregiver burnout,
when to refer to a psychologist

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Can I tell you something?
> It is okay to be angry about this.
> It is okay to think it is unfair. Because it is.
> It is okay to be sad sometimes.
> It is okay to be tired of it.
> All of those feelings are real and they make sense.
> You do not have to be brave all the time.
> You do not have to pretend it is fine when it is not.
> What I want you to know is —
> those feelings do not mean you are weak.
> They mean you are human.
> And they do not last forever.
> Most children with Type 1 diabetes say
> that after a while it becomes part of life —
> not all of life.
> You will get there.
> And on the hard days — I am here."

**Badge awarded:** The Brave Heart Badge 💙

---

### Module 10 — You Are Not Alone
**Topic:** Community, connection, and identity
**Goal:** Child knows there are others like them
and that Type 1 does not limit their future
**Caregiver track:** Community resources, JDRF youth programs,
peer support groups, role models with T1D

**Band 2 version — Mira says:**
> "Did you know there are millions of children
> around the world who have Type 1 diabetes?
> Athletes. Artists. Scientists. Adventurers.
> People who climb mountains.
> People who win medals.
> People who make music and make discoveries
> and make people laugh.
> All of them have the same thing you have.
> And they live full, extraordinary lives.
> Type 1 diabetes is part of your story.
> It is not the whole story.
> You get to write the rest.
> And Mira will be there for every chapter."

**Badge awarded:** The Chapter One Badge 📖

## 6. MIRA WAITING ROOM GAME

### 6.1 Concept
A simple interactive game children play
while waiting at the clinic or during downtime.
Mira is the character. The child guides her.

### 6.2 Game Mechanics
- Mira needs to collect glucose drops
- Too many drops — glucose too high
- Too few drops — glucose too low
- Child learns to keep Mira in range
- Gets harder with each level
- Completely non-violent, non-competitive
- No timer pressure — relaxed pace
- Offline capable

### 6.3 Educational Link
After each game level — one fun fact about insulin
or glucose appears.
Short. Simple. Reinforces the curriculum.

### 6.4 Game Rewards
- Stars earned in the game unlock decorations
  for Mira's appearance
- New wing patterns, eye colours, background scenes
- Never locked behind a paywall
- Completely free for all users

## 7. CAREGIVER PARALLEL TRACK — OVERVIEW

Every child module has a caregiver companion document.
Caregiver track includes:
- Full medical explanation of the topic
- Practical management tips
- What to tell the school
- What to watch for at night
- When to call the clinic
- Emotional support guidance for the caregiver
- Links to vetted external resources

Caregiver track is accessed separately from the
child education track.
It is never mixed into Mira's voice or content.

---

## 8. CONTENT ACCURACY STANDARDS

All Mira education content must be:
- Reviewed against current ISPAD Clinical Practice
  Consensus Guidelines
- Reviewed against ADA Standards of Medical Care
- Reviewed against NICE NG18 guidelines
- Medically accurate at time of publication
- Reviewed and updated annually or when guidelines change

Content that cannot be verified against at least
two of the above sources is not published.

All content carries a version number and review date.
Out of date content is archived — never deleted.

---

## 9. WHAT MIRA NEVER DOES

- Never mentions complications, blindness, amputation,
  or kidney disease in the 0 to 12 framework
- Never uses the word "control" — uses "manage" instead
- Never implies the child has failed when glucose is high
- Never uses fear as a motivator
- Never makes promises about the future
- Never replaces a clinician
- Never gives a dosing recommendation
- Never tells a child to ignore a caregiver's instruction
